NARRATOR: Last episode we put a charged particle into an electric field, and watched it move. Let me bring that back, because today's topic is the mirror image of it, and the contrast is where most of the marks live.

NARRATOR: In an electric field, the force on a charge is the charge q multiplied by the field strength E. That force is constant, because in a uniform field the field strength is the same everywhere. For a positive charge the force points along the field; for a negative charge it points the opposite way. A constant force means a constant acceleration, exactly like gravity in projectile motion — so a charge fired sideways across the field traces a parabola. The component of velocity along the field grows steadily; the component across it stays the same. And the energy bookkeeping was clean: the work done by the field, the charge q times the voltage V it falls through, equals the kinetic energy it gains, one half m v squared. We said the charge "falls through a potential difference." Keep that whole picture warm, because today we change one word — electric becomes magnetic — and almost everything flips.

NARRATOR: Here's the single most important difference, and I want you to hear it before anything else. An electric field pushes a charge whether or not it's moving. A stationary charge in an electric field still feels the force q E. A magnetic field is completely different: it only pushes a charge that is already moving. A charge sitting still in a magnetic field feels nothing at all. Motion is the price of admission. That one fact is the seed of everything we do today.

QUESTION: So if the magnetic force needs motion, and the electric force doesn't — does that change the kind of path the particle takes?

NARRATOR: Completely. The electric field gave us a parabola. The magnetic field is going to give us a circle. Same particle, same kind of field region, utterly different trajectory — and the reason why is the heart of this lesson. By the end of this episode you'll be able to do four things in an exam: state the force law for a moving charge and predict its direction; explain — in proper marker's language — why a magnetic force does no work and never changes the particle's speed; derive the radius of the circular path by equating two forces you already know; and explain the cyclotron insight, that the time to go round is independent of how fast the particle is going. Let's build it.

NARRATOR: Start with the force itself. A charge moving through a magnetic field experiences a force whose magnitude is the charge q, multiplied by its speed v, multiplied by the magnetic field strength B, multiplied by the sine of the angle theta between the velocity and the field. Say that as a sentence: F equals q v B sine theta. The full statement is in Listing 1 if you want to read it later.

NARRATOR: Now read what that sine theta is telling you, because the HSC tests it directly. When the velocity is perpendicular to the field — theta is ninety degrees — the sine is one, and the force is at its maximum: just q v B. When the velocity is parallel to the field — theta is zero, or one hundred and eighty degrees — the sine is zero, and the force vanishes. A charge fired straight along the field lines feels nothing; it sails through undisturbed. And of course if the speed v is zero, the whole thing is zero — there's that "must be moving" rule falling straight out of the equation. For the rest of today, unless I say otherwise, assume the charge enters at right angles to the field, so theta is ninety degrees and the force is simply q v B. That's the standard exam setup.

NARRATOR: There's a trap baked into that angle, and it's worth a sentence. Theta is the angle between the velocity and the field. Some students reflexively measure the angle from the perpendicular instead, and end up with the force biggest when it should be zero and zero when it should be biggest. So: maximum force when motion is across the field, zero force when motion is along the field. If a question gives you a charge moving at, say, thirty degrees to the field, you genuinely need the sine of thirty in there — it's not always the simple q v B.

QUESTION: Okay — that's the size of the force. How do I get the direction? That's where I always tie myself in knots.

NARRATOR: Right, and direction is where careless marks are lost, so let's be precise. The defining property of the magnetic force is that it's perpendicular to both the velocity and the field at the same time. Not along the field, like the electric force was — perpendicular to it, and perpendicular to the motion as well. To find that direction we use the right-hand push rule, sometimes called the right-hand palm rule. Hold your right hand flat. Point your fingers in the direction of the external field — that's from north to south, the way field lines run. Point your thumb in the direction the positive charge is moving — which is the direction of conventional current. Then the force pushes straight out of your palm. Palm faces the way the charge gets shoved.

NARRATOR: Now the part that catches everyone. That rule is built for positive charges. The most common single error in this entire topic is using it unchanged for an electron. An electron is negative, so the force on it is the exact opposite of what the right hand gives you. You've got three ways to handle that, pick one and stick to it: do the right-hand rule and then reverse your answer; or point your thumb in the direction opposite to the electron's motion — that is, along the conventional current, which runs backwards to electron flow; or just use your left hand for negative charges. Any of those works. What does not work is forgetting the charge is negative and reading the palm straight off. Markers see that mistake constantly.

NARRATOR: And don't confuse this with the other right-hand rule. The right-hand grip rule — thumb along the current, fingers curl round to give the field around a wire — that's for the field a current makes, and we'll need it later in the motor block. The push rule, the flat palm, is for the force on a moving charge. Different rules, different jobs. Mixing them up is a classic.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the textbook's own picture, because it's a clean memory anchor and it's exam-relevant. Imagine alpha and beta particles fired into the same magnetic field. The alpha particle is a helium nucleus — heavy, and positive, charge plus two e. The beta particle is just an electron — light, and negative. Apply the push rule honestly: the positive alpha gets pushed one way, say upwards, curving along a wide circular arc. The negative beta, opposite charge, gets pushed the other way, downwards — and because it's so much lighter, it whips round a far tighter arc. Same field, two particles, curving in opposite directions and with wildly different radii. Hold that image: alpha up and wide, beta down and tight. It tells you the sign of the charge sets the direction, and the mass sets the size of the circle. We'll justify the radius part properly in a moment.

QUESTION: Here's what's bugging me. If there's a force on the particle the whole time it's in the field, why doesn't it speed up? A force causes acceleration. So why doesn't the magnetic field accelerate it?

NARRATOR: That is the question of the episode, and getting the answer phrased correctly is worth real marks. Pause the player for a moment and try to answer it yourself: a constant force acts on the particle, yet its speed never changes — how can both be true? Have a genuine go, then play on.

NARRATOR: Here's the resolution. The force does cause acceleration — but acceleration in physics means any change in velocity, and velocity is a vector. You can change a velocity by changing its size, its speed; or you can change a velocity by changing only its direction, leaving the speed alone. The magnetic force does the second kind. It's a direction-changer, not a speed-changer. So when a weak answer says "the magnetic force accelerates the particle" and leaves it there, the marker can't tell whether the student understands that the speed is untouched. The strong answer says the force produces a centripetal acceleration — it continually changes the direction of the velocity — while the speed, and therefore the kinetic energy, stay constant.

NARRATOR: And here's the deeper reason the speed can't change, the line that separates a band-six answer from the rest. Work is force multiplied by the displacement in the direction of that force. The magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity — and the velocity is the direction of motion, the direction of displacement. A force at right angles to the displacement does no work. None. Zero, every instant. So the magnetic force does no work on the charge. By the work-energy theorem, no work means no change in kinetic energy, which means no change in speed. The magnetic force can turn the particle, but it can never give it or take energy. Write that down and underline it: a magnetic force does no work, so it never changes the particle's speed — only its direction. That sentence alone earns marks across this whole module.

NARRATOR: Contrast that with last episode one more time, because the HSC loves to make you compare them. The electric force is along the field, so it generally is along the motion, so it does do work — that's how the electron in an electron gun speeds up, q V becomes kinetic energy. The magnetic force is across the motion, does no work, changes nothing about the speed. Electric field: changes speed, gives a parabola. Magnetic field: changes direction only, gives a circle. Listing 10 lays that whole contrast out point by point for you. We'll devote a whole episode to that comparison next time — it's one of the most-examined extended responses in the module — but plant the seed now.

QUESTION: So how does "always perpendicular to the velocity" actually turn into a circle? I can see it bends the path, but why a perfect circle and not some other curve?

NARRATOR: Beautiful question, and the logic is tight. Picture the particle moving, with the force at ninety degrees to its velocity, pulling it sideways. That sideways pull bends the velocity a little — the particle now points in a slightly new direction. But the force law says the force must stay perpendicular to the velocity, so as the velocity rotates, the force rotates too, keeping that perfect right angle. The result is a force that's constant in magnitude — it's q v B, and neither q, v, nor B is changing — and always pointing at ninety degrees to the motion, which means always pointing toward one fixed centre. A force of constant size, always aimed at a fixed central point, perpendicular to the velocity — that is the exact definition of the centripetal force that produces uniform circular motion. The magnetic force isn't like a centripetal force; it is the centripetal force here. It's playing precisely the role that tension plays for a ball on a string, or gravity plays for a satellite.

NARRATOR: And that link is not decoration — it's your route to the next result. You met centripetal force back in Module 5, Advanced Mechanics: for anything moving in a circle, the net force toward the centre is the mass m, times the speed v squared, divided by the radius r. Same equation, whether it's a car on a roundabout, the Moon round the Earth, or our charge in the field. The physics of the circle doesn't care what's supplying the force. So here's the move the syllabus actually names as a dot-point — "derive the relationship" — and markers want to see the derivation, not just the final formula.

NARRATOR: Watch the logic; the full algebra is in Listing 2. We have one force doing the work of holding the particle in its circle, and it's the magnetic force, q v B. That force is supplying the centripetal force, which is m v squared over r. The whole derivation is just the courage to write that those two are the same thing. So: q v B equals m v squared over r. Now there's a v on both sides — one factor cancels. That leaves q B equals m v over r. Rearrange for the radius, and you get r equals m v over q B. The radius of the circular path is the mass times the speed, divided by the charge times the field strength. That's the equation the whole topic is built around.

NARRATOR: I want to stress the one step students skip, because skipping it loses derivation marks. You must explicitly state that the magnetic force provides the centripetal force, and you must write the line where you set q v B equal to m v squared over r. If you just produce r equals m v over q B out of nowhere, you've quoted a formula, not derived it. The mark is in the equating step. Say it in words on the page: "the magnetic force provides the centripetal force, so q v B equals m v squared over r." That sentence is the derivation.

QUESTION: Now read me the equation. What's each variable actually doing to the size of the circle?

NARRATOR: This is where understanding beats memorising, and where a nasty trap hides. Radius equals m v over q B. So the radius grows with mass — heavier particles curve in bigger circles, they're harder to turn. The radius grows with speed — faster particles curve in bigger circles too; counterintuitively, going faster makes the bend gentler, not sharper. And the radius shrinks when you increase the charge or the field — bigger charge, tighter circle; stronger field, tighter circle. More charge or more field means more force, and more force means a tighter turn.

NARRATOR: That last point is the trap, so let me kill it directly. A weak intuition says "more force means a bigger radius" — it feels like a stronger push should fling the particle out wider. It's the opposite. Look at the equation: B is on the bottom. Stronger field, stronger force, smaller radius. The force is centripetal — it pulls inward — so a bigger inward pull means a tighter curl, not a wider one. Reason it straight off r equals m v over q B every time and you won't get burned: radius up with mass and speed, down with charge and field.

NARRATOR: Now look back at that alpha-and-beta picture with the equation in hand. Why did the electron — the beta — curl so tightly while the alpha swept round wide? Same speed, same field, so the radius is governed by mass over charge. The electron's mass is about nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms. The proton, and the alpha's built from protons and neutrons, is about one point six seven times ten to the minus twenty-seven kilograms — the proton is roughly one thousand eight hundred times heavier than the electron. So even though the alpha carries double the charge, its enormous mass wins, and its radius is thousands of times bigger than the electron's. The light particle whips round tight; the heavy one barely bends. Listing 9 sets the three particles side by side — their masses, charges, and how their radii compare. Mass over charge — that ratio is exactly what a mass spectrometer measures, and it's how the spectrometer tells isotopes apart. We do that device in two episodes' time; file it away.

NARRATOR: Let's make this concrete with numbers, because this equation is examinable as a straight calculation and the marks are in the method. First example — the textbook's own. An electron travelling at five point nine times ten to the six metres per second enters a magnetic field of six point zero millitesla, perpendicular to its motion. Find the radius of its circular path. The full working is in Listing 3.

NARRATOR: Method first, always. The radius is mass times speed over charge times field. Now — the trap that catches people before they even start: the field is given in millitesla. Six point zero millitesla is six point zero times ten to the minus three tesla, not six. One millitesla is a thousandth of a tesla. Get that conversion wrong and your answer is out by a factor of a thousand. Substitute: the electron's mass, nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms, times the speed, five point nine times ten to the six metres per second, on the top. On the bottom, the charge, one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, times the field, six point zero times ten to the minus three tesla. Notice I used the magnitude of the electron's charge — positive one point six times ten to the minus nineteen — because the radius equation only cares about the size of the charge; the sign only told us which way the circle curls. Work it through and the radius comes out as five point six times ten to the minus three metres — five point six millimetres. A tiny circle, which makes sense: the electron is feather-light, so it turns easily and tightly.

QUESTION: Can we do one where I have to find the force first, then the radius? That feels like a two-part exam question.

NARRATOR: It's exactly a two-part exam question, so let's do it. A stream of electrons with speed one point two times ten to the six metres per second enters a uniform magnetic field of two point six times ten to the minus three tesla, at right angles. Part a: the force on each electron. Part b: the radius of its path. Working's in Listing 4.

NARRATOR: Part a. The charge enters at right angles, so theta is ninety degrees, sine is one, and the force is simply charge times speed times field — q v B. Substitute the charge, one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, the speed, one point two times ten to the six metres per second, and the field, two point six times ten to the minus three tesla. Multiply through and you get five point zero times ten to the minus sixteen newtons. A tiny force — but acting on a tinier mass, so it bends the path hard. Part b: radius is mass times speed over charge times field. Top: nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one times one point two times ten to the six. Bottom: one point six times ten to the minus nineteen times two point six times ten to the minus three. That gives two point six times ten to the minus three metres — two point six millimetres. Notice the structure of a good answer: state the formula, show the substitution with units, give the final answer with units. Every line of that is a potential mark.

NARRATOR: Now a reverse problem, because the HSC loves to rearrange the equation on you. Find the speed of an electron that moves in an arc of radius five point zero zero millimetres in a field of five point zero zero millitesla. Listing 5 has it. You're given the radius and asked for the speed, so rearrange r equals m v over q B into v equals r times q times B, all over m. Convert both quantities: five point zero zero millimetres is five point zero zero times ten to the minus three metres; five point zero zero millitesla is five point zero zero times ten to the minus three tesla — two conversions, two chances to slip, so be careful. Substitute the radius, the charge, and the field on top, the electron mass on the bottom, and you get a speed of about four point four times ten to the six metres per second. Same equation, just solved for a different unknown. If you can rearrange it three ways — for r, for v, and for m — you can handle anything they throw at you, including the mass-spectrometer questions later, which solve it for the mass.

QUESTION: You promised something about a cyclotron, and about time. Where does that come in?

NARRATOR: Here's the elegant bit — and it's a genuine "aha" that the syllabus likes you to appreciate, even though, fair warning, the cyclotron itself is properly a Module 8 application and the period formula isn't a quoted Module 6 dot-point. Treat what I'm about to say as insight that illuminates r equals m v over q B and links forward — not as a formula to drill for this module. The textbook flags it the same way: "we'll use this later in the course."

NARRATOR: Ask how long the particle takes to go round once — the period. Period is the distance round the circle, which is two pi r, divided by the speed v. Now substitute our radius, r equals m v over q B, into that. The full cancellation is in Listing 6, and it's worth seeing: the period becomes two pi m over q B. Look at what dropped out. The speed v cancelled completely. It's gone. The period — the time to complete one loop — does not depend on the speed of the particle at all. It depends only on the mass, the charge, and the field.

NARRATOR: Sit with how strange that is. Take a slow particle and a fast particle in the same field. The fast one travels a much bigger circle — bigger radius, because r grows with v. But it's also covering that bigger circle at higher speed. The two effects cancel exactly, and both particles come round in precisely the same time. Speed up the particle, and it just draws a proportionally larger circle in the same period. That's the principle the cyclotron accelerator exploits: it gives charged particles a kick of energy every half-turn, timed to a fixed rhythm — and because the period never changes as the particle speeds up and spirals outward, that single fixed timing keeps working all the way out. You'll meet the cyclotron and the synchrotron properly in Module 8, with particle accelerators; this is the seed of how they work.

NARRATOR: Quick number to make it real — Listing 7. A proton, mass one point six seven times ten to the minus twenty-seven kilograms, charge one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, in a field of one point five tesla. The period is two pi times the mass, over the charge times the field. That works out to about four point four times ten to the minus eight seconds — forty-four nanoseconds to go round once. And the punchline: that forty-four nanoseconds is the same whether the proton is dawdling or screaming round at near the speed of a bigger circle. The speed never entered the answer.

NARRATOR: One trap to flag on this, because it's easy to garble under pressure: it's the period that's independent of speed, not the radius. The radius absolutely does depend on speed — faster means bigger circle. Don't swap those in an exam. Period: fixed, set by mass, charge and field. Radius: grows with speed. Two different statements.

NARRATOR: Let me also nail down the path itself, because there's a comparison trap waiting. In a uniform magnetic field, with the velocity across the field, the path is a circle. Not a parabola. The parabola belonged to the electric field and to projectile motion in gravity — those are constant-force situations. The magnetic field gives a circle because the force constantly reorients to stay perpendicular. If you ever write "parabola" for a charge in a uniform magnetic field, that's a lost mark. Circle for magnetic, parabola for electric and gravitational. And as a quick aside for the curious — if the particle has a bit of velocity along the field as well as across it, that along-field part feels no force and just coasts, so the circle stretches into a helix, a spiral like a stretched spring. The HSC sticks to the clean perpendicular case, but knowing the helix is there shows you understand why: only the across-field motion gets bent.

NARRATOR: Now let me build you the model extended-response answer, because this is exactly the kind of "explain why a charged particle moves in a circle" question that shows up, and the gap between a weak and a strong answer is pure phrasing. A weak answer — the band-four version — says: "The particle moves in a circle because of the magnetic force." True, but it earns almost nothing. It doesn't say why the force makes a circle, it doesn't mention work or speed, and a marker can't tell whether the student really understands it.

NARRATOR: Here's the strong answer — say it along with me, this is the band-six version. "The magnetic force on a moving charge acts perpendicular to both the velocity and the field at every instant. Because the force is always perpendicular to the velocity, it does no work on the charge — so the particle's speed, and its kinetic energy, remain constant. However, the direction of the velocity continuously changes, and since the force is constant in magnitude and always directed toward a fixed centre, it provides the centripetal force required for uniform circular motion. The particle therefore travels in a circle of radius r equals m v over q B." Hear everything that does: perpendicular, no work, constant speed, the direction changing, centripetal force, circle, and the radius relationship. Every clause is a mark. The difference between the two answers isn't knowledge — it's that the strong one names the mechanism step by step.

NARRATOR: Before the closing questions, the recap — the handful of things to walk into the exam with. One: a magnetic field only exerts a force on a moving charge, and the force is the charge, times its speed, times the field, times sine theta — maximum across the field, zero along it. Two: the force is perpendicular to both the velocity and the field; find its direction with the right-hand push rule, and reverse it for negative charges like electrons. Three — the headline — the magnetic force does no work, so it never changes the particle's speed, only its direction; "accelerates" here means centripetal acceleration, a change of direction, not of speed. Four: that perpendicular force is centripetal, so the path is a circle, and equating q v B to m v squared over r gives radius equals m v over q B — bigger with mass and speed, smaller with charge and field. Five: the period of that circle is independent of speed — the cyclotron insight — a faster particle just makes a bigger circle in the same time. Five facts. The third one is the one markers reward most, so say it twice on the page if you have to.

NARRATOR: Now let's finish where it counts — exam-style questions in real NESA format. Pause after each, actually attempt it, then play on for the model answer. These are your last and best retrieval practice for the whole episode.

QUESTION: Question one. "Account for the fact that a magnetic field can change the direction of a charged particle's motion but cannot change its speed." Three marks. Pause the player, write a real answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The magnetic force on a moving charge is always directed perpendicular to the velocity of the charge. Because work is done only by a force component along the displacement, and this force has no component along the direction of motion, the magnetic force does no work on the particle. With no work done, the kinetic energy is unchanged, so the speed is constant. However, the force does continually change the direction of the velocity — that perpendicular force is a centripetal force — so the particle's direction of motion changes even though its speed does not. That hits all three marks: perpendicular force, does no work so speed constant, but direction changes.

QUESTION: Question two. "A proton and an electron enter the same uniform magnetic field with the same speed, perpendicular to the field. Compare the radii of their circular paths and explain the difference." Three marks. Pause, attempt, play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The radius of the circular path is given by r equals m v over q B. Both particles have the same speed and are in the same field, and the magnitude of their charge is identical — one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs each. So the radius depends only on the mass. The proton is about one thousand eight hundred times more massive than the electron, so its radius is about one thousand eight hundred times larger; the electron follows a much tighter circle. The strong phrasing is "the radius is proportional to the mass when speed, charge and field are equal, so the more massive proton has the larger radius." And note — because their charges are opposite in sign, they curve in opposite directions, which is worth a mark if the question asks about direction too.

QUESTION: Question three. "Derive an expression for the radius of the circular path of a charged particle moving at right angles to a uniform magnetic field." Three marks. This is a "show your derivation" question — pause and write the full thing.

NARRATOR: Model answer, and the markers want the steps, not just the destination. A charged particle moving at right angles to the field experiences a magnetic force of magnitude q v B. This force acts perpendicular to the velocity, so it provides the centripetal force required for circular motion, which is m v squared over r. Equating the two — and this line is where the marks are — q v B equals m v squared over r. Cancelling one factor of v from both sides gives q B equals m v over r. Rearranging for the radius gives r equals m v over q B. The mark-earning move is explicitly writing "the magnetic force provides the centripetal force" and showing the q v B equals m v squared over r line. Skip that and you've quoted the formula, not derived it.

QUESTION: Question four. "An electron moving at three point zero times ten to the seven metres per second enters a magnetic field of zero point twenty tesla at right angles. Calculate the radius of its circular path." Two marks. Pause and crunch it.

NARRATOR: Model answer — the full working is in Listing 8. Radius equals mass times speed over charge times field. Substitute: nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms, times three point zero times ten to the seven metres per second, on top; one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs times zero point twenty tesla on the bottom. The top is about two point seven three times ten to the minus twenty-three; the bottom is three point two times ten to the minus twenty. Divide, and the radius is about eight point five times ten to the minus four metres — roughly zero point eight five millimetres. Two marks: one for correct substitution including the right units, one for the correct final answer with its unit. No millitesla trap this time — the field was given in tesla — but always check.

QUESTION: Question five. "Explain why the period of a charged particle's circular motion in a uniform magnetic field is independent of its speed, and state one practical application of this property." Four marks. This is the harder one — pause and give it a real go.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The period is the circumference of the circle divided by the speed — two pi r over v. Substituting the radius, r equals m v over q B, the period becomes two pi m over q B. The speed v cancels completely, leaving the period dependent only on the mass, the charge, and the field strength. Physically: a faster particle follows a proportionally larger-radius circle, but travels that larger circumference at the higher speed, so the time for one revolution is unchanged. The practical application is the cyclotron — a particle accelerator that gives the particle an electrical kick once per half-revolution at a fixed frequency; because the period stays constant as the particle gains energy and spirals outward, that single fixed timing keeps accelerating it all the way out. Four marks: the derivation that v cancels, the statement that period depends only on mass, charge and field, the physical reasoning about radius and speed compensating, and the cyclotron application. That's a full-mark answer — and it ties this whole episode forward to Module Eight, where particle accelerators live.
