# Supplementary Materials

### Listing 1 — Quark content and electric charge of the proton and neutron

Quark electric charges (in units of the elementary charge \(e\)):

- up quark \(= +\tfrac{2}{3}\)
- down quark \(= -\tfrac{1}{3}\)

Proton \(=\) up \(+\) up \(+\) down:

$$
\text{charge} = \left(+\tfrac{2}{3}\right) + \left(+\tfrac{2}{3}\right) + \left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right) = +\tfrac{4}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} = +\tfrac{3}{3} = +1
$$

(matches the observed proton charge).

Neutron \(=\) up \(+\) down \(+\) down:

$$
\text{charge} = \left(+\tfrac{2}{3}\right) + \left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right) + \left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right) = +\tfrac{2}{3} - \tfrac{2}{3} = 0
$$

(matches the observed neutron charge).

Key point: fractional quark charges combine to give whole-number charges for every observed particle.

### Listing 2 — The de Broglie wavelength: why high energy resolves small structure

de Broglie wavelength of a moving particle (NESA syllabus formula):

$$
\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{m v}
$$

where:

- \(\lambda\) = wavelength of the probe particle (m)
- \(h\) = Planck's constant \(= 6.63\times10^{-34}\ \mathrm{J\,s}\)
- \(p\) = momentum of the particle (kg·m/s)
- \(m\) = mass of the particle (kg)
- \(v\) = speed of the particle (m/s)

Accelerated-electron form (from the accelerating voltage \(V\)): an electron accelerated through voltage \(V\) gains kinetic energy \(E_k = q_e V\), so its momentum is \(p = \sqrt{2 m_e q_e V}\), and:

$$
\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2 m_e q_e V}}
$$

so higher accelerating voltage \(\to\) higher momentum \(\to\) shorter \(\lambda\).

Rule of probing: to resolve a structure of size \(d\), you need \(\lambda \le d\) (roughly). The proton radius is \(d \approx 1\times10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}\) (1 femtometre). Smaller \(\lambda\) \(\to\) higher momentum \(p\) \(\to\) higher energy electrons.

This is why SLAC needed electrons up to \(20\ \mathrm{GeV}\): high energy \(\to\) short wavelength \(\to\) resolves sub-proton structure. (At these energies electrons are ultra-relativistic, so the simple non-relativistic voltage form is only a guide; use \(E \approx p\,c\) instead.)

### Listing 3 — The Bjorken scaling variable

In deep inelastic scattering the key dimensionless variable is:

$$
x = \frac{Q^{2}}{2 M \nu}
$$

where:

- \(Q^{2}\) = squared 4-momentum transfer of the virtual photon (large \(Q^{2}\) = short-wavelength probe = "deep")
- \(M\) = mass of the proton
- \(\nu\) = energy lost by the electron in the lab frame
- \(x\) = fraction of the proton's momentum carried by the struck quark (parton), ranges from 0 to 1

Bjorken scaling: if the proton contains POINT-LIKE constituents, the measured structure functions depend only on \(x\) — NOT on \(Q^{2}\) separately.

- Soft diffuse proton \(\to\) scattering falls rapidly with \(Q^{2}\)
- Point-like quarks \(\to\) scattering depends only on \(x\) (scaling)

The SLAC data showed scaling \(\to\) evidence for point-like quarks.

### Listing 4 — Colour confinement and string breaking (energy → matter)

The strong-force flux tube between two quarks stores energy:

$$
E \approx \kappa\,r
$$

where:

- \(E\) = energy stored in the flux tube (J or GeV)
- \(\kappa\) = string tension \(\approx 1\ \mathrm{GeV}\) per femtometre (1 GeV/fm)
- \(r\) = separation between the quarks (fm)

Unlike gravity or electromagnetism, the force does NOT fall with \(r\). The stored energy keeps GROWING as you pull the quarks apart.

String breaking (pair production): when stored energy reaches \(E \ge 2 m c^{2}\) for a new quark pair, the vacuum produces a new quark + antiquark at the break point.

Result: pulling 1 quark away makes 2 new colour-neutral particles instead of freeing a single quark. So no free quark is ever produced. This is colour confinement.

### Listing 5 — Worked example: minimum electron energy to "see" a quark

GOAL: estimate the electron momentum/energy whose de Broglie wavelength can resolve structure inside a proton (size \(\approx 1\times10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}\)).

STEP 1 — set \(\lambda\) equal to the proton size:

$$
\lambda = 1\times10^{-15}\ \mathrm{m}
$$

STEP 2 — find the required momentum from \(p = h/\lambda\):

$$
p = \frac{6.63\times10^{-34}}{1\times10^{-15}} = 6.63\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{kg\,m/s}
$$

STEP 3 — convert to energy (ultra-relativistic, \(E \approx p\,c\)):

$$
E = p\,c = (6.63\times10^{-19})(3.0\times10^{8}) = 1.99\times10^{-10}\ \mathrm{J}
$$

STEP 4 — convert joules to electron volts (\(1\ \mathrm{eV} = 1.602\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}\)):

$$
E = \frac{1.99\times10^{-10}}{1.602\times10^{-19}} = 1.24\times10^{9}\ \mathrm{eV} \approx 1.2\ \mathrm{GeV}
$$

(order-of-magnitude minimum).

INTERPRETATION: probing INSIDE the proton (well below its radius) needs energies of several GeV and beyond — which is exactly why SLAC ran electrons from about 6 GeV up to 20 GeV.
