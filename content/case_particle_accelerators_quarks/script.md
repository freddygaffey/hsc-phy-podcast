NARRATOR: There is a particle inside you that has never been alone. Not once, in the entire history of the universe. It has never been seen by itself, never measured on its own, never held up to the light. And here is the strange part. It is not that we lack the technology to find it. It is not that our instruments are too crude or our accelerators too weak. It is that the laws of physics themselves forbid it. This particle is condemned, forever, to exist only in the company of others. Pull it away from its companions, try to drag it out into the open, and the very act of pulling will conjure new particles out of empty space to keep it hidden. You can never win. You will never see one alone. Ever.

NARRATOR: That particle is called a quark. And in the nineteen sixties, nobody believed it was real. The man who proposed it called it, in his own published paper, something close to a mathematical fiction. The greatest physicists of the age dismissed it. One of them, shown the idea, said it was beautiful, but that he did not believe a word of it. And then a team of physicists aimed a machine two miles long at a single proton, and what bounced back changed everything. This is the story of how we discovered the smallest pieces of matter, by looking at the way electrons scattered off something hard hidden deep inside the proton, something nobody expected to be there.

QUESTION: So let us start at the beginning. In the nineteen sixties, what did physicists think the world was made of?

NARRATOR: For a while, the answer had been beautifully simple. By the nineteen thirties, the atom had been cracked open. You had the proton and the neutron packed into the nucleus, and the electron orbiting around it. Three particles. With those three, plus the photon, the particle of light, you could in principle build the entire periodic table, every element, every molecule, all of chemistry, all of biology, all of the visible world. It was elegant. It was clean. It felt like physics was nearly finished.

NARRATOR: And then the accelerators came. After the Second World War, physicists built bigger and bigger machines to smash particles together at higher and higher energies. And out of those collisions poured a flood of new particles. Not three. Not ten. By the early nineteen sixties, physicists had catalogued more than a hundred so-called fundamental particles, with hundreds more fleeting resonances flickering in and out of existence in the detectors. There were pions and kaons, lambdas and sigmas, xis and deltas, a whole alphabet soup of Greek letters. Each one seemed to be a particle in its own right. And every single one of them claimed to be elementary, a basic building block of nature, with no parts inside.

NARRATOR: It was an embarrassment. Physicists had a name for it. They called it the particle zoo. The whole point of fundamental particles is that there should be a few of them, a small clean set from which everything else is built. You do not expect a hundred fundamental things. A hundred fundamental things is not an answer. It is the absence of an answer. The physicist Willis Lamb captured the mood with a famous quip. Accepting his own Nobel Prize in nineteen fifty-five, he joked that the discoverer of a new particle used to be rewarded with a Nobel, but that such a discovery now ought to be punished with a hefty fine. Nobody believed that all of these things could truly be fundamental. But nobody had a convincing scheme to organise them, to explain why this particular zoo existed and not some other.

QUESTION: So who first found a pattern in the chaos? Who started to bring order to the zoo?

NARRATOR: His name was Murray Gell-Mann. And he was, by almost any measure, one of the most formidable intellects in the history of physics. He was born in Manhattan in nineteen twenty-nine, the son of immigrants. He was a child prodigy of an almost intimidating kind, a boy who devoured languages, archaeology, ornithology, and natural history with the same hunger he brought to mathematics. He entered Yale at fifteen. He earned his doctorate from M.I.T. at the age of twenty-one. By nineteen fifty-six he was a full professor at Caltech, the youngest in the institution's history. He was brilliant, and he knew it, and he was not always gentle about reminding others.

NARRATOR: Gell-Mann looked at the chaos of the particle zoo and asked a mathematician's question. Was there a hidden pattern? He took the known particles, the heavy ones called hadrons, and he began sorting them. He sorted them by their electric charge, and by another property called strangeness, a quantum number that had been invented to explain why certain particles lived far longer than they should. And when he plotted them out, by charge and by strangeness, something astonishing emerged. The particles fell into neat geometric patterns. Groups of eight. Groups of ten. Beautiful, symmetrical arrangements, like crystals forming out of a solution.

NARRATOR: Gell-Mann called this scheme the Eightfold Way, borrowing the phrase, with a touch of wit, from the Buddhist path to enlightenment, because the number eight kept appearing in the patterns. Underneath the playful name was serious mathematics, a branch of group theory called S U three symmetry. And at almost exactly the same time, in nineteen sixty-one, an Israeli physicist named Yuval Ne'eman arrived at the very same classification, entirely independently. Two minds, oceans apart, had found the same hidden order in nature.

QUESTION: And did this pattern actually predict anything? Or was it just a clever way of tidying up particles people already knew about?

NARRATOR: This is exactly the right question, and it has a thrilling answer. Because a pattern that only organises what you already know is just bookkeeping. But a pattern that predicts something new, something nobody has ever seen, that is science at its most powerful. And the Eightfold Way did exactly that.

NARRATOR: One of Gell-Mann's geometric arrangements was a group of ten, a triangular pattern called the baryon decuplet. When Gell-Mann sketched it out, nine of the ten slots were filled by known particles. But one corner of the triangle, the very tip, was empty. There was a gap. And the logic of the pattern was so tight that Gell-Mann could read off, directly from the geometry, exactly what the missing particle had to be. He predicted it would have an electric charge of minus one. He predicted it would have a strangeness of minus three, more than any particle then known. He even estimated its mass. And he gave it a name. He called it the omega-minus.

NARRATOR: This was a bold, naked prediction. Either this strange new particle existed with these precise properties, or the whole beautiful Eightfold Way was wrong. The test came at Brookhaven National Laboratory on Long Island, where physicists pointed a beam of particles into a giant tank called a bubble chamber and hunted for the omega-minus. They captured the tell-tale event on the thirty-first of January, nineteen sixty-four, and reported it weeks later in a paper received on the eleventh of February. There it was. A single track in the chamber, one solitary event, with exactly the charge and exactly the strangeness Gell-Mann had predicted. The discovery paper carried thirty-three authors and reported that one observed event. It was front-page news in the world of physics. The Eightfold Way was confirmed beyond reasonable doubt. There really was a deep pattern underneath the zoo.

QUESTION: But a pattern is not an explanation. Why did the particles fall into these groups of eight and ten? What was causing it?

NARRATOR: And that is the question that led Gell-Mann to the most radical idea of his career. He asked himself, what kind of underlying objects would naturally produce these S U three patterns? What deeper structure could generate groups of eight and ten as if by magic? And when he worked through the mathematics, the answer that fell out was almost too strange to believe. The patterns would arise naturally if all of these hadrons, the protons, the neutrons, the whole zoo, were not fundamental at all. If instead they were each built from a small number of more basic objects. Three of them, to be precise. Three kinds of truly elementary particle, combining in different ways to produce everything else.

NARRATOR: But these basic objects had a property so bizarre that it had never been seen in nature. They had to carry fractional electric charge. Every particle ever observed, without exception, had carried a charge that was a whole-number multiple of the electron's charge. Minus one for the electron. Plus one for the proton. Zero for the neutron. Always a whole number. But Gell-Mann's basic objects had to carry charges of plus two-thirds and minus one-third of the fundamental charge. Two-thirds of an electron's charge. A third of it. Fractions that had never been observed in the entire history of experimental physics.

QUESTION: And what did he call these strange fractional things?

NARRATOR: He called them quarks. And the name itself is one of the great stories in physics. Gell-Mann, who loved words and languages as much as he loved physics, had been carrying a sound in his head. A sound like kwork. He needed a spelling for it. And then he was reading James Joyce's notoriously difficult novel Finnegans Wake, and he came across the line, three quarks for Muster Mark. Three quarks. The word was spelled exactly as he wanted, and the number, three, matched the number of quarks he needed to build a proton. It was perfect. There was just one problem. In Joyce, the word quark was meant to rhyme with Mark, which would make it sound like kwark, not kwork. Gell-Mann, ever the scholar, constructed an elaborate justification. He argued that the line in Joyce might be a pub landlord's call for drinks, three quarts for Mister Mark, which let him defend the kwork pronunciation he had wanted all along. A particle named from a pun inside a pun inside one of the hardest books ever written.

NARRATOR: Gell-Mann proposed the quark in a short, two-page paper. He submitted it in January of nineteen sixty-four, and it was published in the journal Physics Letters in February. Three quarks, which he called up, down, and strange. The proton was two up quarks and one down quark. Add up their fractional charges, two-thirds plus two-thirds minus one-third, and you get exactly plus one, the charge of the proton. The neutron was one up quark and two down quarks. Two-thirds minus one-third minus one-third, which gives exactly zero, the charge of the neutron. The arithmetic was flawless. The fractions, impossible as they seemed, conspired to produce exactly the whole-number charges we actually observe.

QUESTION: That sounds like a triumph. So why did people not believe him?

NARRATOR: Because of those fractional charges, and because of one stubborn, glaring fact. Nobody had ever seen a free quark. If protons and neutrons were really made of quarks, then surely, somewhere, in some experiment, you should be able to knock one loose and catch it on its own. And a free quark would be unmistakable. It would carry a fractional charge, something never observed before. Physicists searched. They looked in cosmic rays. They looked in seawater. They looked in moon rocks, in oyster shells, in deep-sea sediment. And they found nothing. Not a single fractional charge anywhere. The quarks, if they existed at all, simply refused to come out.

NARRATOR: And so the physics community treated quarks as a kind of accounting trick. A convenient bookkeeping device that made the symmetry mathematics work, but with no claim to physical reality. Even Gell-Mann himself hedged. In his own published paper, he wrote a line suggesting that a search for stable quarks would help reassure us of the non-existence of real quarks. Read that again. The man who invented quarks wrote, in the paper proposing them, about reassuring ourselves they did not really exist. He was protecting himself. If quarks were never found, he could say he had only meant them as a mathematical convenience. Years later he would explain that what he really believed was subtler. The quarks were real, he said, but permanently trapped, confined inside the proton and neutron, never to be seen alone. But in nineteen sixty-four, he kept that conviction quiet.

NARRATOR: He was not the only one to have the idea. At almost the same moment, at the laboratory called CERN in Geneva, a young physicist named George Zweig arrived at the very same model, completely independently. Zweig called his particles aces, after playing cards, because he thought there might be four of them. But Zweig had worse luck. His paper, written up as a CERN preprint in January of nineteen sixty-four, was blocked from publication by a senior theorist who reportedly thought the idea was nonsense, and even cancelled a seminar Zweig had been scheduled to give. Zweig's detailed papers went unpublished in a journal for years. Gell-Mann's name for the particles, quark, is the one that survived. But Zweig deserves equal credit for the idea.

QUESTION: So the experts were sceptical. How sceptical, really? Give me their actual words.

NARRATOR: The scepticism was deep and it came from the very top. When the physicist Oscar Greenberg showed a paper on the quark model to Robert Oppenheimer in nineteen sixty-four, the father of the atomic bomb reportedly looked at it and said, Greenberg, it is beautiful, but I don't believe a word of it. Steven Weinberg, who would go on to win his own Nobel Prize for unifying the forces of nature, later admitted that as late as nineteen sixty-seven he did not have any faith in the existence of quarks. Victor Weisskopf, the director-general of CERN, reportedly discouraged Gell-Mann from even discussing quarks in public. The core problem was always the same. Nobody had ever seen a fractional charge, and free quarks stubbornly refused to appear in any experiment, no matter how hard anyone looked. To most physicists, quarks were a clever fiction. A trick of the algebra. Not a statement about what the world is actually made of.

NARRATOR: And that is where matters stood in the middle of the nineteen sixties. A beautiful idea, dismissed. A name from a novel, attached to particles most people thought were imaginary. The proton was still, as far as anyone could prove, a fundamental, structureless point. To find out whether quarks were real, somebody was going to have to look inside the proton itself. And to do that, they were going to need a machine of almost unimaginable scale.

QUESTION: So what was the machine? How do you look inside a proton?

NARRATOR: To understand how, you have to remember a beautiful idea from half a century earlier, from Ernest Rutherford. In nineteen oh nine, Rutherford's team had discovered the atomic nucleus by firing alpha particles at a thin sheet of gold and watching how they scattered. Most went straight through, because the atom is mostly empty space. But a few bounced back at wild angles, and that told Rutherford there had to be something small, hard, and dense hidden at the centre of each atom. The nucleus. You learn what is inside something by firing a probe at it and watching how the probe scatters. The scattering pattern is a map of the structure within.

NARRATOR: Now the physicists wanted to do Rutherford's experiment again, but one level deeper. Instead of firing alpha particles at an atom to find the nucleus, they would fire electrons at a proton to see if the proton had anything inside it. And there was a deep principle that made this possible. Every particle, when it moves, behaves like a wave, with a wavelength that gets shorter as the particle's energy gets higher. This is the de Broglie wavelength, the wavelength associated with a moving particle, which equals Planck's constant divided by the particle's momentum. To resolve something tiny, you need a wave with a wavelength smaller than the thing you are trying to see. To peer inside a proton, which is about one femtometre across, one quadrillionth of a metre, you need electrons of staggeringly high energy, electrons with wavelengths short enough to resolve structures far smaller than the proton itself. And to give electrons that much energy, you need an accelerator like nothing ever built before.

NARRATOR: They built it at Stanford University, in California. The Stanford Linear Accelerator Center, known as SLAC. And it was a monster. A straight-line accelerator, a linac, two miles long. Three point two kilometres of dead-straight machine, buried nine metres underground, running so straight and so far that it passes directly beneath Interstate Highway two-eighty. It was deliberately designed to be exactly two miles, the longest, straightest building of its kind on Earth. Down that two-mile tube, electrons were accelerated to energies of up to twenty billion electron volts, twenty G E V. These were the most energetic electrons ever produced by human beings, electrons whose wavelengths were short enough to probe deep inside a single proton. Construction began in nineteen sixty-two, the first beam fired in May of nineteen sixty-six, and it was, at the time, the largest civilian science project ever undertaken by the United States government, at a cost of over a hundred million dollars.

QUESTION: So who actually ran the experiment, and what exactly did they do?

NARRATOR: Three men led it. Jerome Friedman and Henry Kendall, both from M.I.T., and Richard Taylor, from SLAC itself. Friedman, Kendall, and Taylor. Three names worth remembering, because in nineteen ninety they would share the Nobel Prize for what they were about to find. They began their program in nineteen sixty-seven, and the procedure was, in spirit, exactly Rutherford's. Fire a beam of electrons at a target of liquid hydrogen, which is essentially a tank of pure protons. Then, on the far side, station enormous magnetic spectrometers, instruments the size of houses, to measure precisely how many electrons came out at each angle, and with how much energy. They measured at six degrees, ten degrees, eighteen degrees, and on out to wide angles beyond.

NARRATOR: There are two ways an electron can scatter off a proton. The first is called elastic scattering. The electron bounces off and the proton recoils but stays intact, like one billiard ball striking another. The second is called deep inelastic scattering, and this is the heart of the whole story. In deep inelastic scattering, the electron strikes the proton so hard, and probes so deep, that the proton does not survive. It shatters, spraying out a fountain of new particles. The word deep means the probe is reaching deep inside, to very short distances. And it was in the deep inelastic collisions that the secret of the proton was hiding.

QUESTION: So what did they expect to see, and what did they actually see?

NARRATOR: Here is what they expected. If the proton were what most people thought, a soft, diffuse, squishy blob of charge, smeared out smoothly through its volume, then the predictions were clear. As you looked at harder and harder collisions, at larger and larger angles, the scattering should fall away rapidly. A soft, fuzzy object cannot deflect a probe by a large angle. Just as in Rutherford's experiment, a smeared-out charge gives only gentle deflections. The deep, violent, large-angle scattering should have been vanishingly rare. The numbers should have plummeted as the collisions got harder.

NARRATOR: That is not what they saw. When Friedman, Kendall, and Taylor looked at the deep inelastic collisions, the hard scatters did not fall away. The electrons were bouncing off at large angles far, far more often than anyone had predicted. The proton was deflecting electrons violently, again and again. And there was only one way to explain it. The same way Rutherford had explained the alpha particles bouncing back from the gold foil. There had to be something small, and hard, and point-like inside the proton. The electrons were not striking a soft diffuse cloud. They were ricocheting off tiny hard kernels buried deep within. The proton, it turned out, was not a smooth ball at all. It was more like a bag of marbles.

NARRATOR: At first, even the experimenters did not fully grasp what they had. The results were anomalous, puzzling, hard to interpret. They knew the inelastic scattering was strangely large, strangely persistent, but the meaning was not obvious. They needed a theorist to tell them what the data were screaming.

QUESTION: And who was that theorist? Who understood what the scattering meant?

NARRATOR: There were two, and they came at it from different directions. The first was a SLAC theorist named James Bjorken, known to everyone as B J. Bjorken was a quiet, deep thinker who had been turning the problem over in his mind for years. He had even, by some accounts, used the lab's mountain-climbing trips with the Stanford Alpine Club as a chance to lobby the experimental team to look hard at deep inelastic scattering in the first place. And Bjorken had made a remarkable theoretical prediction. He had worked out that if the proton really did contain point-like constituents, then the way the electrons scattered should follow a very particular rule.

NARRATOR: Normally, you would expect the scattering to depend on two separate things. On the energy the electron loses, and on the momentum it transfers, as two independent quantities. But Bjorken predicted that if there were hard point-like objects inside, the scattering would not depend on those two things separately. It would depend only on a single combination of them, a dimensionless ratio. Plot the data against that one ratio, and the curves for completely different energies should all collapse onto one single universal curve. This property is called Bjorken scaling. It is a fingerprint. It is the signature that says, there are point-like particles inside. And when the SLAC team plotted their data the way Bjorken told them to, the scaling was there. The curves collapsed together, just as he had predicted. The proton contained points.

QUESTION: You said there were two theorists. Who was the second?

NARRATOR: The second was one of the most famous physicists of the twentieth century. Richard Feynman. The Caltech legend, the bongo-playing, safe-cracking, Nobel-winning genius who had helped build the theory of light and matter. In August of nineteen sixty-eight, Feynman visited SLAC, and he saw the deep inelastic data. And it struck him like lightning. Feynman had been developing his own way of picturing high-energy particles. He imagined the proton not as a single object but as a swarm of many tiny constituents, which he called, with deliberate vagueness, partons. Just, parts. Parts of the proton.

NARRATOR: Feynman's picture was vivid and physical. Imagine the proton moving toward you at very nearly the speed of light. Because it moves so fast, time inside it slows down, frozen by relativity, so that during the brief instant of the collision the partons inside are essentially frozen in place, drifting freely. The incoming electron does not interact with the whole proton at once. Instead, it strikes one single parton, one tiny piece, and scatters off it cleanly, as if that piece were a free, isolated particle. The whole complicated collision becomes simple. It is just an electron bouncing off one small, hard, point-like parton, which happens to be carrying some fraction of the proton's total momentum. And when Feynman worked out what his parton model predicted, it predicted Bjorken scaling exactly. The two pictures, Bjorken's abstract scaling and Feynman's physical partons, were describing the same reality. There were hard points inside the proton. And the way they scattered electrons told you they carried fractional charge. Bjorken and Feynman both recognised that the numbers pointed to objects with fractional charge and fractional properties. The partons were quarks.

QUESTION: So this was the moment. They had proof that quarks were real. Did the world finally accept it?

NARRATOR: Not yet. And this is the part of the story that students of science should never forget. The truth did not arrive in a single triumphant flash. The SLAC results were first presented to the world at a great international physics conference in Vienna, in the late summer of nineteen sixty-eight. And the reaction was muted. Many physicists in the audience did not immediately grasp what they were seeing. The connection to quarks was subtle, buried in the numbers. The structure functions, the quantities they measured, were numerically small in a way that, if you did the careful analysis, pointed precisely to fractional charges. But that was a quiet, technical argument, not a thunderclap. Plenty of physicists remained unconvinced. The quarks were still, to many, a mathematical convenience that happened to fit some scattering data.

NARRATOR: The full acceptance took six more years, and it took one more shock. The decisive blow came on the eleventh of November, nineteen seventy-four, a date physicists still call the November Revolution. On that single weekend, two teams, working completely independently, discovered the same brand-new particle. Samuel Ting's group at Brookhaven, firing a beam of protons into a beryllium target, found a sharp, startling spike in their data at a mass of about three billion electron volts. They named it the J particle. At the very same time, Burton Richter's group at SLAC, colliding electrons with their antimatter twins, positrons, found the exact same particle and named it the psi. Because both names stuck, we call it the J-psi to this day.

NARRATOR: And the J-psi was the smoking gun. It was a brand-new kind of particle made from a brand-new kind of quark, a fourth quark called charm, bound together with its own antimatter twin. Charm had actually been predicted, on theoretical grounds, by Bjorken and another physicist named Sheldon Glashow. And here it was, exactly as predicted, in two laboratories at once. After the November Revolution, the scepticism finally collapsed. If you could predict a new quark and then find the particle it builds, in two places simultaneously, the quarks had to be real. The fiction had become fact.

QUESTION: Let us go back to the deepest mystery. Why can you never see a single quark on its own? What actually stops you?

NARRATOR: This is the most beautiful and counterintuitive physics in the whole story, and it goes by the name colour confinement. To understand it, you have to understand that quarks are held together by a force we had never properly seen before. Not gravity. Not electromagnetism. The strong nuclear force, the strongest force in nature. And the strong force behaves in a way that is the exact opposite of everything your intuition expects.

NARRATOR: Think about the forces you know. Gravity gets weaker as things move apart. The gravitational pull of the Earth fades the higher you climb. Electromagnetism is the same. The electric force between two charges weakens rapidly as you separate them. Every force you have ever met gets weaker with distance. The strong force does not. When two quarks are close together, almost on top of each other, the strong force between them is feeble. They drift around almost freely, which is exactly why Feynman's partons looked like free particles in the SLAC collisions. But as you try to pull two quarks apart, the force between them does not fade. It stays constant. It even grows. The further you separate them, the harder the force pulls them back.

NARRATOR: The reason is that the strong force is carried by particles called gluons, and gluons are unlike the photons that carry electromagnetism in one crucial respect. Photons ignore each other. But gluons grip each other. They attract one another, and this self-attraction pinches the force field between two quarks into a narrow, tense tube. A string. A flux tube, physicists call it, stretching between the two quarks, with a tension of roughly one billion electron volts of energy for every femtometre of length. That is an enormous tension on the scale of a single particle, the equivalent of tonnes of force concentrated in a thread far thinner than an atom.

QUESTION: So what happens if you just pull harder? Surely, with enough energy, the string has to break and let a quark go free?

NARRATOR: It does break. But here is the magic, the thing that makes nature so endlessly clever. When you pull the two quarks apart, you pour energy into the stretching string. The longer it gets, the more energy it stores. And there comes a point where the string holds so much energy that something extraordinary happens. That energy converts directly into matter, through Einstein's most famous equation, energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. The energy in the string is enough to create a brand-new quark and antiquark pair, conjured straight out of the vacuum. And those new particles appear right in the middle of the stretching string, at the point where it snaps.

NARRATOR: So picture it. You start with a quark and an antiquark, joined by a string. You pull them apart, hoping to free the single quark. The string stretches, stores energy, and snaps, and at the break point a new quark-antiquark pair pops into being. Now, instead of two free quarks, you have two new pairs. Two new particles, each one a quark bound to an antiquark, each one perfectly respectable, each one with its quark still hidden inside. You pulled, hoping to liberate a quark, and all you did was manufacture two new particles that each keep their quarks locked away. It is like cutting a magnet in half hoping to isolate a single pole, and finding that each half is a complete magnet again. You can never win. The harder you pull, the more particles you make, and the quark stays forever hidden. This is why no isolated quark has ever been seen, and why none ever will be. It is not a limitation of our machines. It is a fundamental law of nature.

QUESTION: You mentioned colour. What does colour have to do with any of this? Quarks are far too small to have a colour.

NARRATOR: They are, and this is one of the most misunderstood ideas in all of physics, so let us be clear. Quarks carry a kind of charge that has nothing whatsoever to do with the colours your eyes can see. Physicists, with their sense of humour, named it colour charge, and they labelled its three varieties red, green, and blue. But there is no actual redness or blueness involved. It is a pure analogy, nothing more. The reason for the names is that the three colour charges combine to cancel out, to become neutral, in exactly the way that red, green, and blue light combine to make white. A particle is allowed to exist freely in nature only if its colour charges cancel to neutral, to white.

NARRATOR: And this is the deep reason for confinement. A single quark always carries one unit of colour charge, red, or green, or blue. It is never neutral on its own. So it can never exist on its own. To make something neutral, you need to combine quarks so their colours cancel. And nature, it turns out, allows essentially two recipes, which gives us the two great families of particles built from quarks. The first family is the baryons, particles made of three quarks bound together by the strong force, one red, one green, one blue, which together make white, neutral, allowed. The proton and the neutron are baryons. Every proton is two up quarks and a down quark, every neutron an up quark and two downs, three quarks apiece, glued tight by the strong force. The second family is the mesons, each one a quark paired with an antiquark, a colour and its matching anticolour, which cancel to neutral, allowed. A pion is a meson. So when the syllabus asks you to describe baryons in terms of quark combinations and the role of the strong force, this is the answer in a single sentence. A baryon is three quarks, held together by the strong force, in a colour-neutral bundle that nature will permit to exist. But notice what is never on the menu. A single quark, on its own, with its one stubborn unit of colour, can never be neutral, and so nature never lets it stand alone. The mathematics of all this is called quantum chromodynamics, chromo meaning colour, the theory of the strong force, worked out in the early nineteen seventies by Harald Fritzsch, Murray Gell-Mann himself, and Heinrich Leutwyler.

QUESTION: And the strange behaviour, the force getting weaker when quarks are close, did anyone explain that?

NARRATOR: They did, and it earned a Nobel Prize. The property is called asymptotic freedom, the idea that quarks become almost free, almost unbound, when they are very close together at very high energies. It was the missing piece that explained why Feynman's partons had looked like free particles in the SLAC collisions. At the short distances and high energies of those violent scatters, the strong force between the quarks nearly vanishes, leaving them to rattle around freely inside the proton. It was proven, through some difficult mathematics, in nineteen seventy-three, by David Gross and Frank Wilczek at Princeton, and independently by David Politzer at Harvard. Gross later described the moment the calculation came out, the moment he realised the strong force did the exact opposite of every other force. He said that, like an atheist who had just received a message from a burning bush, he became an immediate true believer. The pieces had finally locked together. The strong force was weak up close, which is why the partons looked free, and strong at a distance, which is why the quarks can never escape.

QUESTION: So once the quarks were accepted, what came next? How did the picture get completed?

NARRATOR: What followed was one of the great triumphs of human thought, the construction of the Standard Model of particle physics, the most precisely tested theory in the history of science. The hunt for quarks did not stop at three, or four. The November Revolution had revealed the charm quark in nineteen seventy-four. A fifth, the bottom quark, was found at Fermilab in Illinois in nineteen seventy-seven. And then physicists waited, for eighteen long years, for the sixth and final quark, the top quark, which theory demanded but which proved fantastically heavy and hard to make. It was finally discovered at Fermilab's Tevatron collider in nineteen ninety-five. The top quark turned out to weigh about a hundred and seventy-three billion electron volts, which means a single top quark is roughly as heavy as an entire atom of tungsten, one of the densest metals there is. Six quarks in all. Up, down, strange, charm, bottom, and top. The set was complete.

NARRATOR: And it was not only quarks. The same era of great accelerators filled in the rest of the picture. The gluons, the carriers of the strong force, were directly confirmed in nineteen seventy-nine at a German laboratory called DESY, in Hamburg, where collisions produced three sprays of particles, three jets, exactly as you would expect if a gluon had been knocked loose. The W and Z bosons, the carriers of the weak nuclear force, the force behind radioactive decay, were discovered at CERN in nineteen eighty-three. And the final piece, the Higgs boson, the particle that gives the others their mass, was discovered at CERN's Large Hadron Collider on the fourth of July, twenty twelve, completing the Standard Model nearly fifty years after Gell-Mann first sketched his fractional charges.

NARRATOR: The final tally is beautifully small. Six quarks. Six lighter particles called leptons, a family that includes the familiar electron, its heavier cousins the muon and the tau, and three ghostly, nearly massless neutrinos. A handful of force-carrying bosons, the photon, the gluons, the W and the Z. And the Higgs. And here is the point that brings the whole story back home to the atom you are made of. Every atom in your body is, at bottom, just quarks and leptons. The nucleus is a cluster of protons and neutrons, and each of those is a baryon, a bundle of three quarks held by the strong force. Around that nucleus orbit the electrons, and the electron is a lepton, one of the truly fundamental particles, with no smaller parts inside it at all. Quarks in the nucleus, leptons in orbit. That is the entire architecture of ordinary matter. From the chaos of the nineteen sixties, with its hundred-strong particle zoo, physics had distilled the entire visible universe down to a few dozen truly fundamental particles, all governed by one elegant mathematical framework. The zoo had been tamed. The hundred particles were revealed to be nothing more than different combinations of a few quarks, held together by gluons.

QUESTION: Before we close, is there an injustice in this story? Did everyone who deserved credit get it?

NARRATOR: There is, and it is worth naming. The Nobel Prize for the SLAC experiments went, in nineteen ninety, to the three experimentalists, Friedman, Kendall, and Taylor, and they earned it. The Nobel for asymptotic freedom went to Gross, Wilczek, and Politzer in two thousand and four. Gell-Mann had received his own Nobel back in nineteen sixty-nine, though, intriguingly, his citation was for the classification of particles and the Eightfold Way, and it did not even mention the word quark. But one man, whose contribution was arguably the single most important theoretical step in the whole story, never received the prize at all. James Bjorken. It was Bjorken's prediction of scaling that turned the puzzling SLAC data into proof of point-like particles, the bridge between the experiment and the quarks. He was honoured with other great awards, but never the Nobel, and many physicists consider that one of the genuine injustices in the history of the prize. A SLAC colleague, Michael Peskin, put Bjorken's achievement simply. He called it a huge moment in particle physics, the beginning of our understanding that quarks are real. Bjorken died in twenty twenty-four. The proton has been giving up its secrets ever since, but it gave the first of them to him.

NARRATOR: And so we end where we began, with that particle inside you that has never been alone. Not because we are not clever enough to catch one, but because the universe is built so that we cannot. The quarks are real. We proved it by watching electrons ricochet off something hard, deep inside the proton, in a machine two miles long. But they are real in a way that no other particle is.
