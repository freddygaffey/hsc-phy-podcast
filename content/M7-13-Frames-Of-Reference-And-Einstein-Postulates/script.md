NARRATOR: Maxwell's equations predicted a single fixed speed for light, set by the permittivity and permeability of free space, with no frame of reference specified. And an electromagnetic wave travels through nothing, through vacuum, it needs no medium at all. Both of those become load-bearing today. If light needs no medium and has one fixed speed, then the obvious next question is: one fixed speed relative to what? That question breaks classical physics. Let's build up to it properly, starting with something you already know cold: relative motion.

NARRATOR: You've handled relative velocity since the early modules, in kinematics and dynamics. Here's the standard picture. A sports car drives down the road at ninety kilometres an hour. A police officer parked on the roadside measures it at ninety. But a second police car, driving toward the sports car at sixty, measures it approaching at a hundred and fifty. So what is the sports car's speed? The answer is that all the measurements are correct. Speed is relative; it depends on the motion of the observer doing the measuring. There's no single true speed of the car floating in the universe, there's only its speed relative to some chosen observer. That's Galilean relativity, named after Galileo, and you've used it for years. Velocity is relative.

NARRATOR: To talk about this precisely, physicists use the idea of a frame of reference. A frame of reference is just a coordinate system, a set of rulers for measuring positions in space and a clock for measuring time, attached to a particular observer. Everything you measure, you measure within some frame. The sports car driver has his frame; each police officer has theirs; and they disagree about the car's speed because they're each measuring in their own frame. That's all a frame of reference is: somebody's space-and-time measuring system.

NARRATOR: Now the distinction that's worth real marks: inertial versus non-inertial frames. An inertial frame of reference is one that is not accelerating, it's either at rest or moving at constant velocity in a straight line. The defining feature is that Newton's first law holds: a free object, with no net force on it, stays at rest or keeps moving in a straight line at constant speed. A non-inertial frame is one that is accelerating, speeding up, slowing down, or turning. In a non-inertial frame, Newton's first law appears to break: things seem to accelerate with no real force behind them, and you have to invent so-called fictitious forces to explain it. You feel the difference in your body. In a car cruising at a steady hundred on a straight, smooth highway, you feel nothing, you could pour a drink, that's an inertial frame. The instant the car brakes, accelerates, or turns, you're thrown against the seatbelt or the door, you feel a force, that's a non-inertial frame. The test for inertial is simple: do free objects stay put or move in straight lines with no force? If yes, inertial. If you feel a phantom push, non-inertial. That comparison, inertial means constant velocity and Newton's first law holds, non-inertial means accelerating and you feel a force, is a classic short-answer question, and Listing one lays it out side by side.

NARRATOR: Here's the deep point that flows from inertial frames, the principle of relativity, and it's older than Einstein, it's Galileo's. The laws of physics are the same in every inertial frame. There is no experiment you can do inside a sealed, smoothly moving cabin to tell how fast you're going, or even whether you're moving at all, without looking outside. Galileo's version was a ship below decks on a calm sea: fish swim, flies buzz, a dropped coin falls straight down, exactly as they would in port. You cannot feel constant velocity. On a smooth aeroplane at eight hundred kilometres an hour you pour your drink and drop your pencil just as on the ground. There is no special, privileged frame that's truly "at rest", all inertial frames are equally valid. That's the principle of relativity, and remember it, because it becomes Einstein's second postulate almost word for word.

NARRATOR: Now here's the part that matters most for understanding what Einstein overturned. In classical, Galilean physics, velocity is relative, we've established that, but time, length, and mass were all assumed to be absolute. Absolute means every observer agrees on them, no matter how they're moving. My ruler and your ruler measure the same metre. My clock and your clock tick at the same rate. A kilogram is a kilogram for everyone. Two events that happen at the same time for me happen at the same time for you. For two hundred years, nobody questioned this, it's just common sense. Hold that list close, time, length, mass, and simultaneity, all assumed absolute, because Einstein is about to demolish every single one of them. Today we knock over the first domino; next episode they all fall.

QUESTION: Before we get to the crisis, lock in the key distinction. In classical physics, which of these is relative, and which are absolute: velocity, time, length, mass? Pause the player, sort them, then play.

NARRATOR: In classical physics, velocity is relative, it depends on the observer, but time, length, and mass are all absolute, the same for everyone. That's the classical worldview in one sentence, and it's exactly the worldview special relativity overturns. The thing classical physics thought was relative, Einstein keeps relative; but the things it thought were absolute, time and length especially, Einstein makes relative too. Now, what forced such a radical change? A crisis about light.

NARRATOR: Here's the crisis. Every other wave we knew, sound, water, waves on a string, travels through a medium, and its speed is fixed relative to that medium. Sound travels at about three hundred and forty metres per second relative to the air. So nineteenth-century physicists naturally assumed light, being a wave, must also travel through a medium. They named this invisible medium the luminiferous aether, and they imagined it filling all of space. Light, they said, travels at c relative to the aether. And that solves the "speed relative to what?" question: c is the speed of light relative to the aether, the one true stationary frame of the universe.

NARRATOR: But that idea makes a testable prediction, and this is a gorgeous piece of physics. If the aether is the fixed stage of the universe and the Earth is hurtling around the Sun through it at thirty kilometres a second, then there's an "aether wind" blowing past the Earth. Light sent in the direction of Earth's motion through the aether should travel at a slightly different speed, relative to us, than light sent across it, just as sound from the front of a moving plane reaches you at a different speed than sound from the side. So we should be able to detect the Earth's motion through the aether by carefully comparing the speed of light in different directions. Measure the aether wind, and you've found the universe's absolute rest frame.

NARRATOR: In eighteen eighty-seven, Albert Michelson and Edward Morley built an instrument precise enough to do exactly that. It used the interference of light, the same wave interference from episodes five and six, sending two beams along perpendicular paths and recombining them, so that even a tiny difference in travel time would shift the interference fringes. It was sensitive enough to easily detect the expected aether wind from Earth's orbital motion. And the result, the most famous null result in the history of physics, was nothing. No shift. No aether wind. The speed of light came out exactly the same in every direction, no matter how the apparatus was oriented or how the Earth was moving. It was as if the Earth's motion through the aether simply didn't exist. We've got a fuller account of that experiment as a separate story, the Michelson-Morley experiment, but the headline is all you need here: they looked for the aether, and it wasn't there. The full logic of what they expected versus what they found is in Listing four.

NARRATOR: So physics was stuck. Light is a wave with no detectable medium, and its speed comes out the same no matter how you're moving. Some physicists tried to patch the aether with ever more elaborate fixes. Einstein did something braver: he threw the aether out entirely and asked, what if there is no medium, no absolute rest frame, and the speed of light really is the same for every observer? What if the null result isn't a failure to detect the aether, but nature telling us the aether doesn't exist and light's speed is simply invariant? From that one bold move, all of special relativity follows. He built it on two statements, his two postulates.

NARRATOR: Here are the two postulates, and you must be able to state them precisely, because markers are strict about the exact wording. Postulate one: the speed of light in a vacuum is an absolute constant, it is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer. Postulate two: all inertial frames of reference are equivalent, the laws of physics are the same in every inertial frame. That second one is just Galileo's principle of relativity, promoted to a fundamental law. The first one is the bombshell. The precise wording of both is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: A quick but important note on numbering, because it trips students up. Different textbooks number these two the other way around, some call the constancy of light postulate one, some call it postulate two. NESA's syllabus lists the constancy of the speed of light first, so that's the order I've given you. But honestly, the marker does not care which one you number first. What the marker cares about is that you state both, and that you state them correctly. So don't waste a brain cell worrying about the order; spend it on getting the words right. And the single most common mark-loser is dropping the qualifier on postulate one. It is not enough to say "the speed of light is constant." You must say it is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source or the observer. That qualifier is the entire revolutionary content of the postulate. Without it, you've said something trivially true; with it, you've said something that breaks classical physics.

NARRATOR: Now let me show you why postulate one is so shocking, because this is where the marks and the understanding are. It breaks the most obvious rule in physics: adding velocities. Picture a spacecraft flying toward Earth at half the speed of light, and it sends out a radio signal ahead of it. Radio is just low-frequency light, so the signal travels at c. Now, every instinct you have, every bit of Galilean relativity, says: the ship is doing half light speed, the signal leaves it at light speed, so the signal must hit Earth at one and a half times the speed of light. Add the velocities. That's how the sports car worked. But postulate one forbids it. The radio signal hits Earth at exactly c, the speed of light, not one and a half c. The astronaut measures the signal leaving at c; Earth measures it arriving at c; they're moving at half light speed relative to each other, and they both get the same c. Velocities do not add the way they always have. The comparison, what Galileo predicts versus what actually happens, is laid out in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Sit with how violent that is. Something has to give. If two observers in relative motion both measure the same light beam at the same speed, and speed is distance over time, then they cannot agree on both distance and time. One or both of those, the metres and the seconds, must be different for the two observers. The constancy of the speed of light can only be saved by sacrificing the absoluteness of space and time. That is the whole of special relativity in one breath, and it's why this episode is the foundation: once you accept that c is the same for everyone, time and length are forced to become relative. Next episode we cash that out as time dilation and length contraction.

NARRATOR: There's one consequence we can see right now, today, without any equations, and it's an explicit syllabus dot-point: the relativity of simultaneity. Simultaneous just means "at the same time." Classically, if two events are simultaneous for me, they're simultaneous for everyone. Einstein showed that's false. Here's the thought experiment, and thought experiments, reasoning carefully through an imagined setup, are how relativity is taught, so get comfortable with them. Picture a train carriage moving at high speed, with a lamp at the exact centre. The lamp flashes once. Inside the train, the light spreads out and reaches the back wall and the front wall at the same instant, because the lamp is dead centre and light travels at c in both directions, equal distances, equal times. So for a passenger on the train, the two events, light hitting the back wall and light hitting the front wall, are simultaneous.

NARRATOR: But now watch from the platform as the train rushes past. The light still travels at c in both directions, that's postulate one, the same c for the platform observer too. But during the tiny time the light is travelling, the train moves forward. So the back wall is rushing toward the point where the flash happened, and the front wall is racing away from it. The light therefore reaches the back wall first, it had less distance to cover, and the front wall second. For the platform observer, the two events are not simultaneous, the back one happens first. Same two events, two observers in relative motion, and they genuinely disagree about whether the events happened at the same time. Neither is wrong. Simultaneity is relative, not absolute. That walk-through is in Listing five. And notice what made it happen: the only reason the two observers disagree is that the light's speed is the same for both of them. Relax that, let the light speed add to the train's speed, and the disagreement vanishes. Simultaneity becomes relative precisely because the speed of light is absolute. That's the first domino, and it's the seed of everything next episode.
