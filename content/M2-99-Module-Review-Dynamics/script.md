NARRATOR: This is the module review for Dynamics, Module Two, and the job is to see the four episodes as one connected framework, because the exam hands you a scenario and expects you to choose between forces, energy and momentum, often using two of them in one answer. Here's the spine of the whole module in one sentence: forces cause changes in motion, and across any interaction two quantities, energy and momentum, are conserved. Newton's laws explain the change; the conservation laws constrain it. Four episodes, one spine. Let's reassemble them, and I'll lean on you to retrieve.

NARRATOR: Episode one gave us forces and Newton's three laws, the hook I, F, A. Inertia: an object's motion only changes under a net force. F equals m a: the net force equals mass times acceleration, with the acceleration in the direction of the net force, and you feed that acceleration into Module One's suvat equations. Action-reaction: forces come in equal and opposite pairs acting on different objects, so they never cancel. And the distinction that's tested relentlessly: mass is the scalar amount of matter in kilograms, constant; weight is the gravitational force on it, a vector, equal to m g.

NARRATOR: Episode two took the net force into two dimensions with friction and the inclined plane. Tilt your axes along and into the slope; the weight resolves into m g sine theta down the slope and m g cosine theta into it; the normal force equals m g cosine theta, less than the full weight; and the net force along the slope is m g sine theta minus friction, giving an acceleration of g sine theta for a frictionless incline, independent of mass. Friction opposes motion, static adjusts up to a maximum, kinetic acts while sliding.

NARRATOR: Episode three was the energy lens: work is force times displacement in the direction of the force, the net work equals the change in kinetic energy, gravitational potential energy change is m g delta h, and in the absence of friction mechanical energy is conserved, so potential lost equals kinetic gained, m g h equals one half m v squared, independent of mass. Energy is transformed, never destroyed. And power is the rate of energy transfer, energy over time, or force times velocity.

NARRATOR: And episode four added momentum: momentum is mass times velocity, a vector; impulse is the change in momentum, force times time, so extend the time to cut the force; total momentum is conserved in any closed-system collision; and momentum is conserved in all collisions while kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic ones. NARRATOR: Now let's see how the module connects, because that's what the integrated questions test. The deepest connection is that there are three ways to analyse the same motion, and they must all agree: forces and Newton's second law, the work-energy approach, and momentum. They're not separate theories, they're linked. The work-energy theorem is Newton's second law integrated over distance, force times distance equals change in kinetic energy. Impulse is Newton's second law integrated over time, force times time equals change in momentum. So from F equals m a you get energy by multiplying through by distance and momentum by multiplying through by time. That's why a strong student picks the quickest tool: forces when you need acceleration or a force or a time; energy when you have heights and speeds and no time; momentum for collisions and explosions.

NARRATOR: Let me flag the traps that span the module in one pass, because a review is where you inoculate yourself. Trap one: confusing mass and weight, kilograms versus newtons, weight equals m g. Trap two: thinking action-reaction forces cancel, they're on different objects. NARRATOR: Now a worked weak-versus-strong on the most common integrated question, "a moving trolley collides with a stationary one and they stick together; analyse the collision". QUESTION: Retrieval before the final questions. A box is pushed at constant velocity across a rough floor. Using Newton's laws, what is the net force on it, and how does the applied force compare with friction? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Constant velocity means no acceleration, so by Newton's first and second laws the net force is zero, the forces are balanced. That means the applied force exactly equals the friction force in magnitude, opposite in direction, so they cancel. People wrongly think you need a bigger force than friction to keep something moving, but no, to move at constant velocity you only need to match friction; a bigger force would cause acceleration. Constant velocity equals zero net force equals applied force balancing friction. That's the first law applied to everyday motion, and it's a guaranteed mark.

NARRATOR: Let me make the decision explicit, because choosing the right lens is half the skill, and it's laid out in Listing four. You have three tools for any mechanics problem. Use Newton's laws and forces when the question asks for an acceleration, a force, a normal force, a friction force, or anything involving time, and on inclined planes where you resolve the forces. Use energy when the question involves heights and speeds with no mention of time, when you only care about the start and end states, or when you're asked about work, power, or energy transformed, conservation of energy connects two points in one line. Use momentum for any collision, explosion, or recoil, where it's conserved regardless of the messy forces during contact, and pair it with an energy comparison to classify the collision. The decision tree: collision or explosion, start with momentum; heights and speeds without time, start with energy; need a force, an acceleration, or a time, start with Newton's laws. NARRATOR: Let me do one more integrated example, braking distance, because it shows energy beating forces neatly, and it's in Listing two. A one-thousand-kilogram car travelling at thirty metres per second brakes to a stop; the braking force is six thousand newtons. How far does it travel before stopping? The energy way: the kinetic energy to be removed is one half times one thousand times thirty squared, four hundred and fifty thousand joules. The braking force does negative work over the stopping distance, and the work-energy theorem says the work done by the brakes equals the change in kinetic energy, so braking force times distance equals the kinetic energy: six thousand times the distance equals four hundred and fifty thousand, so the distance is seventy-five metres. Notice we never found the acceleration or the time, the work-energy theorem went straight from force and energy to distance. And a useful insight falls out: kinetic energy depends on the speed squared, so doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy and quadruples the braking distance, which is exactly why speed limits matter so much, stopping distance grows with the square of the speed. That's a band-six insight, the work-energy theorem revealing why a small speed increase greatly lengthens braking.

NARRATOR: Let me say a word about why the two conservation laws are so powerful, because it lifts an answer. Conservation of energy and conservation of momentum are both consequences of deep symmetries of nature, and they hold even when the forces during an interaction are complicated and unknown. In a car crash you have no idea of the detailed forces as the metal crumples, microsecond by microsecond, yet you can still predict the velocities afterward, because momentum is conserved no matter what those messy internal forces do. That's the magic of a conservation law: it lets you connect the before and the after without knowing the chaos in between. Energy does the same for heights and speeds. So when a problem looks impossibly complicated to do with forces, ask whether a conserved quantity, momentum or energy, lets you skip the middle entirely. That's not just a trick, it's how physicists actually think, and it's the habit that carries you into the rest of the course.

QUESTION: One more retrieval. For a frictionless ramp, why does the speed at the bottom depend only on the vertical height dropped, not on the ramp's angle or length or the object's mass? Pause the player, explain it, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's pure energy reasoning. By conservation of mechanical energy, the gravitational potential energy lost equals the kinetic energy gained: m g times the vertical height equals one half m v squared. The mass cancels from both sides, and only the vertical height appears, not the angle or the slope length, because gravitational potential energy depends only on the change in height, not the path taken to descend it. So a steep short ramp and a gentle long ramp to the same vertical drop deliver the same speed at the bottom, and a heavy and a light object arrive together. The angle and length and mass all drop out; only the height survives. If you saw that the mass cancels and only the height matters, you've understood why energy is often the smarter tool than forces for these problems.
