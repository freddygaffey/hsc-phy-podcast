NARRATOR: Somewhere right now, in a hospital, a person is lying very still inside a large white ring of machinery. They were injected, about an hour ago, with a sugar. An ordinary-looking sugar, dissolved in saline, fed in through a vein in the arm. But this sugar is not quite ordinary. Tucked into each molecule, in place of one of its parts, is a single atom of a radioactive element. And that atom is, at this very moment, doing something that sounds like science fiction. It is spitting out antimatter. Tiny bullets of the stuff. The mirror-image of the matter that you and I and that patient are made of. The kind of matter that, the instant it touches ordinary matter, vanishes in a flash of pure energy.

NARRATOR: This is not a thought experiment. It is not a particle accelerator buried under a mountain in Switzerland. It is a Tuesday morning in a radiology department. Every single day, in thousands of hospitals around the world, doctors deliberately inject antimatter into living human beings. And the annihilation that follows, the destruction of matter and antimatter together, is not an accident or a danger to be avoided. It is the entire point. The flashes of energy that come pouring out of that patient's body are caught by the white ring, counted, traced backwards along invisible lines, and assembled, by a computer, into a glowing three-dimensional map. A map of where, inside that living body, the cells are hungriest. A map that can find a tumour the size of a grape hidden deep in a lung, or trace the dying of cells in a brain with Alzheimer's, before any other technique can see a thing.

QUESTION: So how on Earth did antimatter, the most exotic and destructive substance in physics, end up as a routine tool for finding cancer? And how does annihilating it build a picture?

NARRATOR: To answer that, we have to go back nearly a hundred years, to a quiet, intensely private young man in Cambridge, who wrote down an equation so beautiful that it forced the universe to contain a particle nobody had ever seen. This is the story of antimatter in medicine. It begins not in a hospital, but with a piece of pure mathematics that even its own author found, at first, deeply embarrassing.

NARRATOR: The year is nineteen twenty-eight. The place is Cambridge, England. The man is Paul Dirac, twenty-five years old, famously silent, a physicist who chose his words so sparingly that his colleagues half-jokingly defined a unit of one word per hour and called it a dirac. On the second of January, nineteen twenty-eight, Dirac submitted a paper to the Proceedings of the Royal Society. It was called The Quantum Theory of the Electron. And what he had done was something that the greatest physicists of the age had been trying and failing to do for years. He had married two of the most revolutionary ideas in all of science.

NARRATOR: On one side, there was quantum mechanics, the brand-new physics of the very small, which described electrons and atoms as waves of probability. On the other side, there was Einstein's special relativity, the physics of the very fast, which described how space and time themselves stretch and shrink as things approach the speed of light. Both theories were spectacularly successful. But they did not get along. The standard quantum equation, the Schrodinger equation, simply ignored relativity. It treated space and time differently, which relativity says you are absolutely not allowed to do. For a slow electron that was fine. But electrons inside atoms move fast, fast enough that relativity matters. Physics needed an equation for the electron that respected both theories at once. And nobody could find one that worked.

NARRATOR: Dirac found it. To do it, he had to be extraordinarily clever. He demanded that his equation be first order in both space and time, treating them on an equal footing the way relativity insists. But making that work with the strange algebra of quantum mechanics seemed impossible, until Dirac realised that his equation's coefficients could not be ordinary numbers. They had to be matrices, grids of numbers, four rows by four columns. When he wrote it all out, the equation did something miraculous. It automatically predicted that the electron must spin, and spin by exactly the amount that experiments had already measured but nobody had been able to explain. The spin just fell out of the mathematics, unbidden. It was one of those moments that makes physicists believe they are reading the mind of the universe.

NARRATOR: But the equation also did something else. Something that, at first, looked like a disaster.

QUESTION: What was the problem? If the equation predicted the electron's spin perfectly, what could possibly be wrong with it?

NARRATOR: The problem was this. When you solve Dirac's equation, it does not give you one kind of answer. It gives you two. One set of solutions described the ordinary electron, with positive energy, exactly as expected. But there was a second set of solutions, sitting right there in the same equation, that described particles with negative energy. Negative energy. Particles that, the faster they moved, the less energy they had. This made no physical sense at all. And worse, it was dangerous for the theory, because in quantum mechanics, things always fall toward lower energy. If negative-energy states existed, every ordinary electron in the universe should instantly tumble down into them, releasing a burst of light and disappearing. All the matter in the cosmos should collapse in a fraction of a second. Obviously, it doesn't. So what were these negative-energy solutions?

NARRATOR: Dirac, being Dirac, did not throw them away. He took the mathematics seriously, more seriously than his own intuition. His first idea, in nineteen thirty, was a strange and ingenious picture. He proposed that empty space, the vacuum, is not actually empty at all. It is completely filled, everywhere, with an infinite sea of negative-energy electrons, all the negative-energy states occupied right up to the brim. We don't notice this sea because it's uniform, it's everywhere, like a fish that doesn't notice the water. But, said Dirac, suppose you punched a hole in the sea. Suppose you lifted one negative-energy electron out, leaving a gap. That gap, that absence of a negative charge, would behave exactly like the presence of a positive charge. A hole in the sea would look like a particle, with positive charge, moving through the vacuum.

NARRATOR: So what was this hole? Dirac, cautious to a fault, first guessed that the hole might be the proton, the only positive particle anyone knew of at the time. But that couldn't be right, and a young American physicist proved it. In nineteen thirty, J. Robert Oppenheimer, the man who would later lead the atomic bomb project, published a sharp objection. If the hole were a proton, he showed, then an ordinary electron and a hole would annihilate each other almost instantly. Every hydrogen atom in existence, an electron sitting next to a proton, would self-destruct in a tiny fraction of a second. The universe would not survive. Oppenheimer's point was decisive. The hole could not be the proton. It had to have the same mass as the electron.

NARRATOR: And so, in nineteen thirty-one, Dirac took the final, breathtaking step. He accepted what his own equation had been telling him all along. The hole was not the proton. It was an entirely new particle, one that had never been seen, never been suspected. A particle with exactly the same mass as the electron, but the opposite charge. Dirac called it the anti-electron. He wrote, almost matter-of-factly, that it would be a new kind of particle, unknown to experimental physics, having the same mass and opposite charge to an electron. He had predicted antimatter. Predicted it not from any experiment, not from any observation, but purely from the internal logic of an equation. The mathematics demanded that this particle exist, and so Dirac declared that it must.

NARRATOR: Now, this was an enormous claim. A whole new particle, conjured from algebra. Most physicists were skeptical, and you can hardly blame them. The history of physics is littered with beautiful theories murdered by ugly facts. Dirac's anti-electron was a prediction waiting for an executioner, or a witness. And the witness, when it came, came from an entirely unexpected direction. From a man who wasn't even looking for it.

NARRATOR: Cross the Atlantic, and the continent, to Pasadena, California. The California Institute of Technology, Caltech. A young experimental physicist named Carl Anderson, working under the famous Robert Millikan, the man who had measured the charge of the electron. Anderson was not testing Dirac's theory. He later admitted he was only, in his own words, dimly aware of it. He was studying something quite different: cosmic rays. These are high-energy particles, mostly protons, that come raining down on the Earth from deep space, from exploding stars across the galaxy. When they smash into the upper atmosphere, they shatter into showers of new particles that cascade down to the ground. Anderson wanted to photograph those particles and measure them.

NARRATOR: His tool was a cloud chamber. A cloud chamber is a beautiful piece of apparatus. It is a sealed box filled with vapour, kept right on the edge of condensing. When a charged particle shoots through it, it leaves a trail, a thin line of tiny droplets marking its path, just like the white contrail of a jet against a blue sky. Anderson placed his cloud chamber inside a powerful magnet. And that is the crucial trick, because a magnetic field bends the path of a moving charged particle. A positive particle curves one way. A negative particle curves the other way. And the faster the particle, the gentler the curve; the slower it is, the tighter it spirals. From the direction and amount of curving, you could read off the charge and the energy of whatever had passed through.

NARRATOR: There was one more refinement. To tell which way a particle was travelling, up or down, Anderson inserted a plate of lead, six millimetres thick, straight across the middle of his chamber. The idea was simple. When a particle punches through the lead, it loses energy. So it comes out the other side moving more slowly than it went in. And a slower particle curves more tightly. So by comparing how tightly the track curved above the plate versus below it, Anderson could tell which side the particle entered from. Which way was it going.

NARRATOR: On the evening of the second of August, nineteen thirty-two, his camera caught a track that stopped him cold.

QUESTION: What did he see? What was so strange about one curving line in a cloud chamber?

NARRATOR: The track had the unmistakable signature of an electron. The same faint, wispy trail. The same density of droplets, telling him it had the same tiny mass as an electron. By everything Anderson could measure, this was an electron. But it was curving the wrong way. In his magnetic field, with the geometry he knew cold, an electron should bend in one particular direction. This track bent in the exact opposite direction. It was a particle as light as an electron, but carrying a positive charge. He could read the energies right off the photograph: the particle entered the lead plate carrying sixty-three million electron-volts of energy, punched through the six millimetres of lead, and emerged on the far side with twenty-three million electron-volts. The lead had slowed it, the curvature had tightened, and the direction of travel was now beyond doubt. A positive particle, with the mass of an electron.

NARRATOR: In his logbook he wrote, with the dry caution of a careful experimentalist: track seventy-five, through lead, showing energy change or double ejection. When he published, in the journal Science on the ninth of September, nineteen thirty-two, he gave the paper a title so cautious it almost hides the discovery: The Apparent Existence of Easily Deflectable Positives. But within a few months, the caution gave way. Anderson gave the new particle a name, stitched together from positive and electron. He called it the positron. It was Dirac's anti-electron, made real. The mathematics had been right. Antimatter existed, and here it was, leaving a trail of droplets in a box in California.

NARRATOR: There is a haunting footnote to this story, and it deserves telling. A few years earlier, another physicist at Caltech, a Chinese graduate student named Chung-Yao Chao, had been studying how gamma rays scatter off lead. And he had found something anomalous, an extra scattering that nobody could explain. We now know exactly what Chao was seeing. He was watching positrons being created and then annihilating, the very process at the heart of this whole story. He was looking straight at antimatter and its annihilation, two years before Anderson, without knowing what it was. He didn't have the cloud chamber to see the individual tracks, so he couldn't make the leap. Anderson, in fact, inherited some of Chao's experimental equipment. Chao came within a hair's breadth of the discovery, and history brushed right past him. He never received the Nobel Prize. Anderson did, in nineteen thirty-six, at the age of thirty-one. And Dirac had received his already, in nineteen thirty-three, sharing it with Erwin Schrodinger.

NARRATOR: At the Nobel ceremony, a member of the committee turned to Dirac and said, simply, the experimental discovery of the existence of the positron has in a brilliant way confirmed your theory. And in his Nobel lecture, Dirac let his imagination off its leash, just once. He speculated that if nature were truly symmetric between positive and negative charge, then it must be, in his words, an accident that the Earth, and presumably the whole solar system, contains more electrons and positive protons than the other way around. He wondered aloud whether there might be entire stars, entire worlds, made of antimatter. That question, why our universe is made of matter and not antimatter, remains one of the deepest unsolved mysteries in all of physics to this day.

NARRATOR: But let us come back to Earth, and to the human body, because we now have all the physics we need to understand the most extraordinary thing about that patient in the white ring. To understand it, we have to understand exactly what happens when matter meets antimatter. And this is where the syllabus physics lives, so let us be precise.

NARRATOR: When a positron, a piece of antimatter, meets an electron, a piece of ordinary matter, the two of them annihilate. They cease to exist as particles. Their mass is completely converted into energy, in the form of light, very high-energy light, gamma rays. Now, this is governed by the most famous equation in all of physics, Einstein's relationship: energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. The mass of the electron and the mass of the positron are both converted entirely into energy. So how much energy comes out?

NARRATOR: Each of these particles has a rest mass energy, the energy locked up in its mass, of about five hundred and eleven thousand electron-volts. Physicists write that as five hundred and eleven kilo-electron-volts, or zero point five one one million electron-volts. The electron and the positron have exactly the same mass, by Dirac's symmetry, so each contributes the same five hundred and eleven kilo-electron-volts. Add them together and you get one point zero two two million electron-volts of energy, released from nothing but the destruction of two tiny specks of matter and antimatter. The full numbers are in Listing one.

QUESTION: So the annihilation produces a burst of energy. But here is the thing that turns this into a medical scanner. That energy doesn't come out as one big flash. It comes out as two separate gamma rays. And those two gamma rays do something very specific. What is it, and why does it have to be that way?

NARRATOR: The two gamma rays fly off in exactly opposite directions. At one hundred and eighty degrees to each other. Back to back. And this is not a coincidence, it is not an approximation, it is forced by one of the deepest laws in physics: the conservation of momentum. Let me walk you through why, because this is the single most beautiful idea in the whole machine.

NARRATOR: Before the annihilation, picture the electron and positron. The positron, by the time it meets an electron, has slowed almost to a stop. So just before they annihilate, the pair is, near enough, sitting still. Their total momentum is essentially zero. Now, momentum is conserved. It cannot be created or destroyed. So whatever comes out of the annihilation must also have a total momentum of zero. But each gamma ray, each photon, carries momentum. A photon flying in some direction carries momentum in that direction. So if one photon flies off to the left, carrying momentum to the left, there must be a second photon flying off to the right, carrying exactly equal and opposite momentum to the right, so that the two momenta cancel and the total stays zero. That is why there are two photons and not one. A single photon could never have zero momentum. You need two, flying in precisely opposite directions, each carrying half the energy. Each gets five hundred and eleven kilo-electron-volts. They leave the scene of the annihilation back to back, one hundred and eighty degrees apart, travelling at the speed of light. The full reasoning is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: And there is a third conservation law quietly satisfied here, the conservation of charge. The electron carries a charge of negative one, the positron a charge of positive one, and added together they make exactly zero. The two gamma rays that come out are photons, and photons are electrically neutral, so the charge after the annihilation is also exactly zero. Charge in equals charge out. Nature balances her books in every currency at once: energy, momentum, and charge, all conserved in the same instant.

NARRATOR: So look at what conservation laws have given us, for free. Energy conservation tells us each photon carries exactly five hundred and eleven kilo-electron-volts, a sharp, precise, recognisable energy. And momentum conservation tells us the two photons fly out in exactly opposite directions. Two gamma rays, of known energy, born at the same instant, at the same place, heading in precisely opposite directions. Hold that image. That is the seed of the entire scanner. Two photons, back to back, that point straight back at the place they were born.

NARRATOR: Now we need to get those photons into a patient. And for that, we need the radioactive sugar I mentioned at the very start. Here is how it works, and it is genuinely ingenious. The element doing the work is fluorine-eighteen, a radioactive isotope of fluorine. Fluorine-eighteen is unstable. It has too many protons for its number of neutrons, and so it decays by a process called beta-plus decay. In beta-plus decay, one of the protons inside the nucleus transforms into a neutron, and in doing so, it spits out a positron, plus a tiny ghostly particle called a neutrino. And it is worth naming the force that drives this, because the syllabus asks for it. The conversion of a proton into a neutron is not the work of the strong nuclear force, the force that holds the nucleus together. It is the work of a different, far feebler force, the weak nuclear force. The weak force is the only one of nature's forces that can change one kind of particle into another in this way, turning a proton into a neutron and conjuring a positron and a neutrino out of the energy released. Fluorine-eighteen turns into oxygen-eighteen, and out flies a positron. There it is. There is your source of antimatter, manufactured one positron at a time, inside the patient, by a decaying atom.

NARRATOR: But you can't just inject loose fluorine into someone and hope for the best. The trick, the genius of it, is where you put that fluorine atom. Chemists take a molecule of glucose, ordinary blood sugar, and they modify it. They pluck off one part of the molecule and slot a fluorine-eighteen atom into its place. The result is a molecule called fluorodeoxyglucose, usually just called F-D-G. And here is the beautiful deception. To a living cell, F-D-G looks almost exactly like glucose. The cell's machinery grabs it, pulls it inside using the same transporter proteins it uses for real sugar, and begins to process it, exactly as if it were food.

QUESTION: So the cells eat the radioactive sugar, thinking it's food. But why does that help us find a tumour? What makes a cancer cell different from a normal one here?

NARRATOR: This is where biology hands physics a gift. Back in the nineteen-twenties, a German scientist named Otto Warburg discovered something strange about cancer cells. They are gluttons for glucose. Cancer cells consume sugar at a furious rate, far faster than normal, healthy tissue, even when they have plenty of oxygen. This is called the Warburg effect. A tumour is, metabolically, a fire. It burns sugar voraciously to fuel its own runaway growth. So when you inject F-D-G into the bloodstream, where does it go? It floods toward the cells that are eating the most sugar. And the hungriest cells of all, very often, are the cancerous ones.

NARRATOR: And then there's a second piece of cleverness, hidden in that modified molecule. When a normal glucose molecule enters a cell, the cell tags it with a phosphate group and then continues to break it down for energy. But F-D-G, because of that swapped fluorine atom, gets the phosphate tag and then gets stuck. The cell cannot process it any further. It is trapped inside the cell, accumulating, building up exactly in proportion to how hungry that cell was. So the most metabolically active tissues, the tumours, the busy brain, the working heart, become loaded with trapped, radioactive F-D-G. They become, quite literally, glowing hotspots of antimatter production. Each fluorine-eighteen atom, sitting trapped inside a hungry cell, ticking down, occasionally firing off a positron.

NARRATOR: Now let us follow one of those positrons on its very short, very dramatic life. It is born from a decaying fluorine nucleus, deep inside a cell, in the middle of the patient's body. It comes out moving fast. But it is surrounded, on every side, by ordinary matter, by the electrons of the patient's own tissue. The positron careens through the tissue, slowing down, jostling past atoms, for a tiny distance, on average about half a millimetre to a couple of millimetres at most. And then it finds an electron. Sometimes the two of them briefly pair up into a fleeting little atom-like system called positronium, half matter, half antimatter, orbiting each other for an instant. But it cannot last. Matter and antimatter cannot coexist. They annihilate. And in that instant, the two gamma rays are born, each carrying five hundred and eleven kilo-electron-volts, flying off in exactly opposite directions.

NARRATOR: Here is the crucial thing, and it clears up the single most common misunderstanding about these machines. The scanner does not detect the positrons. It never sees them. The positrons never leave the body. They annihilate within a millimetre or two of where they were born, buried in the tissue. What escapes the body, what the machine actually catches, is the pair of gamma rays. Because gamma rays of this energy are highly penetrating. They sail through ten centimetres of flesh on average before being absorbed, easily enough to punch out of the body and reach the detectors. The antimatter does its work and dies inside the patient. Only its light, its two back-to-back gamma rays, comes out to tell the tale.

QUESTION: So two gamma rays come flying out of the body, in opposite directions. How does catching them build a three-dimensional picture of where the sugar is?

NARRATOR: This is the masterstroke. This is why the patient lies inside a ring. The scanner is built as a complete circle of thousands of detectors, special crystals that flash with light whenever a gamma ray strikes them. The patient lies in the centre of the ring. Now, picture a single annihilation, happening at some unknown point inside the body. Two gamma rays shoot out, back to back, in opposite directions. One of them flies out and strikes a detector on, say, the upper left of the ring. The other, flying the exact opposite way, strikes a detector on the lower right of the ring. And here is the key: they arrive at almost exactly the same instant, because both are travelling at the speed of light, and they were born at the same moment.

NARRATOR: So the scanner watches for pairs. Two detectors lighting up at the same time, within a tiny window of a few billionths of a second of each other. When that happens, the machine flags it as a coincidence. And a coincidence tells you something powerful. If two opposite detectors fired at the same instant, then the annihilation that caused them must have happened somewhere along the straight line connecting those two detectors. Because the two gamma rays flew apart in a straight line, back to back. That line is called the line of response. The machine doesn't know exactly where on the line the annihilation happened, not from one event. But it knows the annihilation was somewhere on that particular line drawn across the body.

NARRATOR: One line isn't much. But the patient is not producing one annihilation. They are producing millions upon millions of them, every second, all over the regions where the F-D-G has pooled. Each one draws another line of response across the body. And where many, many lines cross, where they pile up and intersect again and again, that is where the annihilations are coming from. That is where the radioactive sugar is concentrated. That is where the hungry cells are. A computer takes all these millions of crisscrossing lines and, using reconstruction algorithms not unlike those in a CT scanner, works backwards to figure out the three-dimensional distribution of F-D-G that must have produced them. The result is a glowing map of metabolism inside the living body. A map built entirely out of the death of antimatter. This whole chain, from sugar to picture, is summarised in Listing three.

NARRATOR: And the name of this machine tells you exactly what it does. Positron Emission Tomography. P-E-T. Tomography, from the Greek for slice, meaning an image built up in slices. Positron emission, because it all begins with the emission of positrons, with antimatter, inside the body.

NARRATOR: Now, let me close one more loop, because the choice of fluorine-eighteen is not arbitrary, and it connects straight back to the syllabus idea of half-life. Fluorine-eighteen has a half-life of about one hundred and ten minutes. Just under two hours. That number is a Goldilocks compromise, and it had to be argued over. The radioactivity has to last long enough to be useful. F-D-G is made in a cyclotron, often at a separate facility, then shipped to the hospital, injected into the patient, and then you wait an hour or so for the sugar to be taken up by the cells before you even start the scan. If the half-life were too short, like carbon-eleven's twenty minutes, the radioactivity would be mostly gone before you could ship it anywhere. But the half-life also has to be short enough that the patient doesn't stay radioactive for days. With fluorine-eighteen's hundred and ten minutes, the activity drops by half every two hours. After about eleven hours, six half-lives, less than two percent remains. By the next morning, the patient is, for all practical purposes, no longer radioactive at all. Long enough to ship and scan, short enough to clear by tomorrow. That balance is why fluorine-eighteen won. In nineteen seventy-three, at a meeting between researchers at Brookhaven and the University of Pennsylvania, a chemist named Al Wolf argued for exactly this, for choosing fluorine over carbon precisely because of that longer, more practical half-life.

NARRATOR: And it is worth pausing on how recent all of this is. The positron was a curiosity of cosmic-ray physics in the nineteen-thirties. The first crude attempts to use positrons for medical imaging came in nineteen fifty-three, when Gordon Brownell and William Sweet at Massachusetts General Hospital used a positron-emitting form of arsenic to locate brain tumours with a pair of facing detectors. The first true ring-shaped scanner came in nineteen seventy-four, built by Michel Ter-Pogossian, Michael Phelps, and Edward Hoffman at Washington University in St Louis. The radioactive sugar F-D-G was first given to two human volunteers in August nineteen seventy-six, by Abass Alavi at the University of Pennsylvania. And the discovery that tumours light up with F-D-G, the thing that turned PET from a neuroscience curiosity into the most powerful cancer-imaging tool in medicine, came around nineteen eighty. Within a single human lifetime, antimatter went from an embarrassing term in an equation to a thing that finds cancer in your grandmother.

NARRATOR: Let me clear up a few things that people get wrong about all this, because the misconceptions are revealing. People often imagine the scanner detects the antimatter itself. It does not. The positrons die inside the body, within a millimetre or two; only their gamma-ray light escapes to be measured. People imagine the gamma rays come out of the nucleus, like ordinary gamma decay. They do not. These photons are born outside the nucleus entirely, from the annihilation of the positron with an electron in the surrounding tissue. People worry that injecting antimatter, or radioactivity, must be terribly dangerous. But the dose is tiny, the half-life is short, and the radiation a patient receives is comparable to a regular CT scan; by the next day it is essentially gone. And people sometimes think the two photons come out at exactly one hundred and eighty degrees in every circumstance, perfectly. Almost, but not quite. Because the electron and positron aren't perfectly at rest at the moment they annihilate, there's a tiny residual momentum, which means the photons come out a tiny fraction of a degree off from a perfect straight line. That tiny imperfection, called non-collinearity, is actually one of the fundamental physics limits on how sharp a PET image can ever be. The conservation laws are exact; it's just that the starting momentum isn't perfectly zero.

NARRATOR: Step back, and look at the whole arc of this story. It begins with Paul Dirac, refusing to flinch from his own equation, insisting that a particle must exist simply because the mathematics said so. It runs through Carl Anderson, catching that impossible track in a box in California, the wrong-way curve that proved antimatter was real. And it ends, for now, with a patient lying in a white ring, their cancer revealed by the annihilation of the very particle Dirac dreamed up at a desk in Cambridge. Every link in that chain is a conservation law made visible. Energy conservation sets the photon energy. Momentum conservation sets their direction. Mass-energy equivalence converts the particles into light. There is no magic here. There is only physics, followed faithfully, all the way from an equation to a diagnosis.

NARRATOR: Dirac's antimatter went on to do far more than this, of course. His equation predicted that every particle must have an antiparticle, and so it does: the antiproton was found in nineteen fifty-five, the antineutron a year later. His work laid the foundation for quantum field theory, the deepest description of reality we possess. And it left us with that aching question he raised in his Nobel lecture, the one we still cannot answer: in a universe that ought to be symmetric between matter and antimatter, why is there any matter left at all? Why are we here, and not annihilated into light at the dawn of time? We do not know. But we have learned to take the one small corner of that mystery we can control, the deliberate annihilation of a positron with an electron, and turn it into a window into the living human body.
