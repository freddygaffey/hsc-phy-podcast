NARRATOR: Welcome to Module 8 — Matter, Energy and the Cosmos. This is the module where physics zooms all the way in to the smallest pieces of matter, and all the way out to the stars that forge them, and shows you they're the same story. But before we start the new module, let's spend five minutes pulling the last block of Module 7 back into memory, because — believe it or not — the way Module 7 ended is exactly the door we walk through today.

NARRATOR: Module 7 was The Nature of Light, and it ended with relativity. Cast your mind back. Two episodes ago we did time dilation and length contraction. The key idea: because the speed of light is the same for every observer, a moving clock runs slow — the time measured equals the Lorentz factor, gamma, times the proper time. Proper time is the time measured in the frame where the two events happen at the same place, on the clock that's present at both. Length goes the other way: a moving object is contracted along its direction of motion, so the length you measure is the proper length divided by gamma. Gamma is always greater than or equal to one, and it only matters near the speed of light. And remember the evidence — the muon. Muons created high in the atmosphere should decay long before they reach the ground, but they arrive in droves, because from our frame their internal clock is dilated, and from their frame the atmosphere is length-contracted. Same answer, two frames. Remember the muon-lifetime story; we'll meet muons again in this module when we sort the fundamental particles.

NARRATOR: Then the final teaching episode of Module 7 took relativity to its famous conclusion: relativistic momentum and mass-energy. Momentum isn't simply mass times velocity anymore — it's gamma times the rest mass times velocity. As you push something toward the speed of light, gamma blows up, its inertia grows without limit, and you'd need infinite energy to actually reach light speed. So nothing with mass ever gets there — that's the cosmic speed limit. And out of the same mathematics falls the most famous equation in physics: energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. Mass and energy are two faces of one thing. Hold onto that equation. It's quiet right now, but in this module it becomes the engine of the stars, the source of nuclear power, and the reason a particle accelerator can manufacture matter out of pure kinetic energy.

NARRATOR: And here's the bridge. Module 7 also gave us the photon — light as a stream of energy packets — and the photoelectric effect, where light knocks electrons clean out of a metal surface. Notice the word in that sentence: electrons. We kept talking about electrons being knocked out, absorbing photons, carrying charge — but we never actually asked the obvious question. What is an electron? How does anyone know it exists? Nobody has ever seen one. So before we build atoms, before we split nuclei, before we read the spectra of stars, we have to do the thing that started all of twentieth-century physics: we have to discover the electron. That's today.

NARRATOR: So here's the hook. In the eighteen-nineties, in a sealed glass tube with almost no air in it and a huge voltage across it, physicists found an invisible something streaming from the negative electrode to the positive one. They called it a cathode ray. Nobody knew what it was. Was it a wave, like light? Or was it a stream of tiny particles? The argument ran for two decades. And the way it was settled — observation by observation, each one tied to a specific conclusion — is one of the most beautiful pieces of experimental reasoning in the whole syllabus. It's also, conveniently, exactly the kind of question NESA loves to ask.

NARRATOR: By the end of this episode you'll be able to do three things, because they're three named dot-points. One: account for the origin of cathode rays — explain where they come from and why. Two: describe what happens when a cathode ray meets a Maltese cross, a paddle wheel, an electric field, and a magnetic field. Three — and this is the big-mark one — analyse how those observations prove cathode rays are particles that carry mass and charge. Notice those three verbs: account for, describe, analyse. They're different jobs, and the marker wants the right job done for each. We'll come back to that.

NARRATOR: Let's start with the apparatus, because you can't answer any of these questions without picturing the tube clearly. It's called a discharge tube, or a cathode-ray tube. Picture a sealed glass tube. Inside it is gas — but at very low pressure, because a vacuum pump has sucked most of the air out. At each end is a metal electrode. One electrode is connected to the negative terminal of a high-voltage supply: that's the cathode, and cathode means negative — that's worth locking in now, because the whole topic is named after it. The other electrode is connected to the positive terminal: that's the anode, the positive one. The high voltage comes from an induction coil, which is just a step-up transformer producing tens of thousands of volts.

NARRATOR: Turn it on. A very high potential difference now sits across the tube, and something invisible streams from the cathode toward the anode. You can't see the ray itself, but you can see where it lands: when it strikes the glass at the far end, the glass glows with a pale green light. That glow is called fluorescence. Often the tube is coated with zinc sulphide or another phosphor so the beam shows up brightly, and sometimes there's a slit and a coated screen along the tube so you can see the beam's actual path as a glowing line. That green glow on the glass is your evidence that something is arriving — and it arrives at the anode end, having come from the cathode.

QUESTION: Why does the tube have to be at low pressure? Couldn't you just do this in ordinary air?

NARRATOR: Good — this is examinable, and it's a one-mark gift if you phrase it right. In ordinary air, or any gas at high pressure, the tube is packed with gas particles. The electrons streaming off the cathode would smash into those gas particles almost immediately — they'd have a very short mean free path, the average distance a particle travels before a collision. They'd lose their energy in collisions before they could ever travel the length of the tube. Lower the pressure, and you spread the gas particles out. Now the mean free path is long. The electrons can accelerate to high speed and travel the full length of the tube before they hit anything. So the mark-earning sentence is: at low pressure the gas particles are far apart, so the cathode-ray electrons can accelerate to high speed and cross the tube without losing their energy in collisions. The beautiful coloured bands and glows you sometimes see in these tubes — the striations, the positive column, the dark spaces — come from cathode rays exciting the remaining gas atoms, which then release photons of light. The colour depends on the gas. But that's flavour; the exam point is the mean free path.

NARRATOR: Now the first named dot-point: account for the origin of cathode rays. Account for means give the cause and effect — explain why they exist and where they come from. Here's the chain the marker wants, and I'll give it to you as a four-step story. Step one: that enormous potential difference across the tube sets up a very strong electric field inside it. Step two: that strong field, right at the cathode, rips electrons free — it ionises the low-pressure gas, stripping electrons off the gas atoms, and it also liberates electrons from the surface of the cathode itself. So now you've got a population of free, negatively charged electrons. Step three: the field pushes on those free electrons. A negative charge in a field feels a force, and these electrons are driven away from the negative cathode and toward the positive anode — they accelerate across the tube. Step four, the punchline: cathode rays are these accelerated electrons. That's it. Cathode rays are streams of fast-moving electrons that came off the cathode region and were accelerated by the field.

NARRATOR: Two traps live right here, so let me kill them now. Trap one: the rays originate at the cathode — the negative electrode — not the anode. The shadow experiments will prove this in a minute, because the shadows always point away from the cathode. Don't write that they're "emitted by the anode." Trap two: it's the electrons that travel — not "the glowing gas" moving down the tube. The glow is just gas atoms lighting up where the electrons pass; the thing in motion is the stream of electrons.

NARRATOR: And here's your first cross-link, and it's a big one. Everything I just described — electrons stripped off a cathode, accelerated by a field, formed into a beam — you have already met. In Module 6, Electromagnetism, we built the electron gun. Same idea: a heated cathode releases electrons, a potential difference accelerates them into a narrow beam. A cathode-ray tube is, physically, an electron gun followed by some deflection fields. When we get to the deflection experiments today, you'll find we're reusing the exact charged-particle physics from Module 6 — the force on a charge in an electric field, and the circular path of a charge in a magnetic field. So this isn't a new topic so much as a famous historical application of physics you already own. Keep Module 6 in the back of your mind; we're about to cash it in.

NARRATOR: Now the heart of the episode — and the heart of the exam question. There are four classic observations made inside these tubes, and each one was designed to answer a specific question about what the rays are. The skill the syllabus is testing, the skill this whole module runs on, is this: take an observation, and state precisely what it lets you conclude. Not "describe the experiment" — anyone can do that. The marks are in the link from observation to conclusion. So for each of the four, I'll give you the apparatus, the observation, and the conclusion, and I'll show you the difference between a weak answer and a strong one.

NARRATOR: Let me give you a memory hook for the four experiments first, because in the exam you want to be able to dump all four on the page in second one and then write a sentence about each. The four are: the Maltese Cross, the Paddle wheel, the Electric field, and the Magnetic field. Cross, Paddle, Electric, Magnetic. Remember it as "Cathode rays Make Particle Evidence Move" — Maltese, Paddle, Electric, Magnetic. Or just hold the picture: a cross casting a shadow, a little wheel rolling, plates pulling the beam sideways, a magnet curving it. Cross, Paddle, Electric, Magnetic. Four experiments, four conclusions. Write them down the instant you open the paper.

NARRATOR: Experiment one: the Maltese cross. Inside the tube there's a metal cross — shaped like a Maltese cross — standing in the path of the rays, between the cathode and the fluorescent glass at the far end. The observation: a sharp, well-defined shadow of the cross appears on the glowing glass. The cross blocks the rays, and behind it the glass stays dark in the exact shape of the cross. What does that tell you? Two things. First, the rays travel in straight lines — a sharp shadow can only form if whatever's casting it goes in straight lines, just like light and a solid object. Second, the rays are blocked by a solid, opaque object. And there's a bonus: the shadow points away from the cathode, which confirms the rays come from the cathode end. So the conclusion is: cathode rays travel in straight lines and are blocked by solid objects, consistent with a beam emitted from the cathode.

NARRATOR: Experiment two: the paddle wheel. This is the famous one, and it's the one that's most often mangled in exams, so listen carefully. Inside the tube is a tiny, very light paddle wheel — a little wheel with vanes, mounted on rails so it can roll along the tube. Turn the rays on. The observation: the wheel rolls along its rails, away from the cathode — and it'll roll even slightly uphill, against friction and against gravity. Now what does that prove? The rays are physically pushing the wheel and making it roll. To push a wheel along, to do mechanical work against friction and gravity, the rays must be delivering momentum and kinetic energy to it. A massless wave doesn't shove a wheel up a slope. So the conclusion is: cathode rays carry momentum and energy and can do mechanical work, which is evidence they are particles with mass.

QUESTION: I've read somewhere that the paddle wheel actually turns because of heating, not because it's being hit by particles. Should I mention that?

NARRATOR: That's a genuinely good piece of physics — and for the HSC, leave it out of your answer. You're right that modern analysis suggests heating effects, a radiometer-style mechanism, contribute to the motion. But the syllabus interpretation, the one that earns the marks, is the historical one: the wheel rolls away from the cathode because the rays transfer momentum and do work, so they carry mass. If you over-complicate it with the heating subtlety, you'll talk yourself out of the conclusion the question is asking for. Give the syllabus interpretation cleanly. Save the nuance for a dinner party.

NARRATOR: Let me show you the weak-versus-strong contrast right here, on the paddle wheel, because this is the single most important marking boundary in the whole topic. A weak answer says: "The paddle wheel turned, so cathode rays are particles." That's it. That answer earns almost nothing — maybe one mark — because it describes the observation and asserts a conclusion without the reasoning that connects them. It never says why turning a wheel implies mass. Now the strong answer: "When a light paddle wheel was placed in the path of the cathode rays, it rolled along its rails away from the cathode, even up a slight incline against friction and gravity. Since the rays were able to do mechanical work and transfer momentum to the wheel, they must carry kinetic energy and momentum, which is evidence that cathode rays are particles possessing mass rather than a massless electromagnetic wave." Hear the difference? The strong answer names the observation precisely, states the physical reasoning — work, momentum, energy — and then draws the conclusion, mass, and contrasts it with the alternative, a wave. Observation, reasoning, conclusion. That three-part structure is the whole game.

NARRATOR: Experiment three: the electric field. Now we put two parallel charged plates inside the tube — one positive, one negative — so there's an electric field across the beam's path, just like the deflection plates in the electron gun from Module 6. The observation: the beam bends — it deflects toward the positively charged plate. Now think about what that means. A charge that's attracted toward the positive plate must itself be negative — opposite charges attract. So the conclusion is: cathode rays carry negative electric charge. And here's the trap the lesson plan flags, so I'll say it plainly: it is not enough to write "the beam is attracted to the positive plate." You have to take the next step and say "therefore the rays carry negative charge." The observation is the deflection; the conclusion is the negative charge. The mark is in the word "negative." Don't stop at "attracted to positive."

NARRATOR: Experiment four: the magnetic field. Now bring a magnet up to the tube, putting a magnetic field across the beam. The observation: the beam curves — it follows a circular arc — and it curves in exactly the direction you'd predict for a negative charge moving through that field. This does two jobs. First, it confirms the rays are charged and moving, because a magnetic field only pushes on moving charges, and the curve direction matches a negative charge. The force on a moving charge in a magnetic field is the charge times the speed times the magnetic field strength, and because that force is always at right angles to the velocity, it acts as a centripetal force and bends the path into a circle — that's the Module 6 physics, identical. Second, and this is the subtle, high-value point: a magnetic field does not deflect light. Light and other electromagnetic waves sail straight through a magnet, undisturbed. So the fact that a magnet curves the cathode-ray beam is independent evidence that cathode rays are not simply light — they're charged particles. Add to that: cathode rays were later shown to travel much slower than light — slower than three times ten to the eight metres per second — which is another nail in the wave-of-light idea, because electromagnetic waves travel at light speed.

NARRATOR: Let me pause and pull the four together, because the way you assemble them is what separates a band-five answer from a band-six. Cross, Paddle, Electric, Magnetic. The Maltese cross shows straight-line travel and that the rays are blocked by solids. The paddle wheel shows mass — momentum, energy, work. The electric field shows negative charge — deflection toward positive. The magnetic field confirms charged-and-moving and rules out light. So when the exam asks you to analyse the evidence that cathode rays are particles with mass and charge, you sort your observations: paddle wheel for mass; electric and magnetic deflection for charge — specifically negative charge; and the Maltese cross as supporting evidence for the straight-line, particle-stream picture. Mass on one side, charge on the other, every observation tied to its conclusion. That sorting is the analyse verb in action.

QUESTION: Pause the player for this one — it's the highest-value retrieval in the episode. Without looking anything up: name all four experiments, and for each one, state the single conclusion it supports. Pause now, say all four out loud or write them down, then play.

NARRATOR: Right. Here they are. Maltese cross: a sharp shadow, so the rays travel in straight lines and are blocked by solids. Paddle wheel: it rolls away from the cathode doing work, so the rays carry momentum and have mass. Electric field: the beam deflects toward the positive plate, so the rays carry negative charge. Magnetic field: the beam curves like a negative charge would, so the rays are charged and moving — and since light isn't deflected by a magnet, they're not light. If you got all four conclusions, you can answer the central exam question of this topic. If you missed one, that's the one to drill.

NARRATOR: Now the historical wrinkle — and the HSC genuinely loves this, so it's worth two minutes. I told you the electric field deflects the beam toward the positive plate. But when scientists first tried it, the beam did not deflect in an electric field at all. Nothing. And that absence of deflection pushed a lot of clever people toward the wrong answer — they figured if it doesn't respond to an electric field, maybe cathode rays are a kind of wave, like light. Heinrich Hertz — yes, the same Hertz who confirmed Maxwell's electromagnetic waves, remember that case study — found in eighteen-ninety-two that cathode rays could penetrate thin metal foils, and he read that as wave-like behaviour. So for a while the wave model had real support, and it had it because of an experiment that seemed to show no electric deflection.

NARRATOR: Then, in eighteen-ninety-seven, J. J. Thomson — Joseph John Thomson — worked out what was actually going on. The tubes weren't evacuated well enough. There was too much residual gas left inside. The cathode rays were ionising that leftover gas, knocking it into charged ions, and those ions drifted over to the charged plates and neutralised them — they cancelled out the charge on the plates, which cancelled the electric field. No field, no deflection. The rays were negatively charged the whole time; the apparatus was just hiding it. So Thomson pumped the tube down to a much better vacuum, removed the residual gas, and the field was no longer being neutralised — and there it was: the beam deflected toward the positive plate, exactly as a stream of negative charges should. He was the first to successfully deflect cathode rays with an electric field, and that's a cornerstone of his discovery of the electron.

NARRATOR: There's a moral here that's worth holding for the whole module, and it's the kind of thing that earns you a mark in a "discuss the role of evidence" question: good evidence can mislead you until the apparatus is good enough. The absence of deflection wasn't telling you the rays were uncharged — it was telling you the vacuum was poor. The trap, and the lesson plan calls it out specifically, is to attribute that early no-deflection result to a real property of the rays. It wasn't. It was an artefact of the residual gas. Always blame the apparatus before you blame the physics. And for completeness: Jean-Baptiste Perrin had already shown in eighteen-ninety-five that cathode rays deposit negative charge when they hit a collector, which was strong independent evidence for the particle, negatively-charged picture.

NARRATOR: Now, I've been careful to call these "particles, later identified as electrons," and that's deliberate. The rays were studied for decades — Goldstein named them cathode rays back in eighteen-seventy-six — long before anyone knew what they were. It was Thomson, in eighteen-ninety-seven, who confirmed they were a single kind of particle, far lighter than any atom, present in all matter — the electron, the first subatomic particle ever found. But notice what we have and haven't done today. Today we've established that cathode rays are streams of fast-moving, negatively charged particles with mass. What we have not done is measure anything — we haven't found the charge, or the mass, or the ratio between them. That's the next episode. Here's your forward cross-link: in M8-02, Thomson takes this exact apparatus — same tube, same deflection plates, same magnet — and uses crossed electric and magnetic fields to measure the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron, about one point seven six times ten to the eleven coulombs per kilogram. He finds the particle is roughly one eighteen-hundredth the mass of a hydrogen ion — astonishingly light. And then Millikan pins down the charge itself. So everything we built today is the launch pad for the measurement that comes next. The whole story — the people, the rivalry, the 1897 breakthrough — is told in the case study, case J J Thomson cathode rays. Listen to it; it makes all of this stick.

So multiply: one point six times ten to the minus nineteen, times two point five times ten to the four, times two times ten to the minus two. Multiply the front numbers — one point six times two point five times two — that's eight. Add the powers of ten: minus nineteen, plus four, minus two, gives minus seventeen. QUESTION: Question one, two marks. Account for the origin of cathode rays in a cathode-ray tube. Pause now, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. A high potential difference is applied across the electrodes, producing a strong electric field in the tube. This field ionises the low-pressure gas and frees electrons from the gas atoms and the cathode itself. The field then accelerates these free, negatively charged electrons away from the negative cathode toward the positive anode. Cathode rays are these accelerated electrons. Notice that "account for" earns marks for the cause-and-effect chain — field, ionisation, acceleration — not just for naming the answer.

QUESTION: Question two, three marks. Describe the observation made when cathode rays interact with a magnetic field, and state what it demonstrates about the nature of the rays. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. When a magnetic field is applied across the beam, the cathode-ray beam is deflected into a curved, circular path, in the direction expected for a negatively charged particle moving through that field. This demonstrates that cathode rays are charged and in motion, because a magnetic field exerts a force only on moving charges, and the magnetic force acts perpendicular to the velocity, producing circular motion. Furthermore, because light and other electromagnetic waves are not deflected by a magnetic field, this observation shows cathode rays are not simply a form of light. That third sentence — the contrast with light — is the third mark; weak answers stop after "the beam curves."

QUESTION: Question three, six marks, the big one. Analyse how observations of cathode rays in a discharge tube provide evidence that cathode rays consist of particles with both mass and charge. Pause the player, plan and write a full response, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. When a light paddle wheel was placed in the path of the cathode rays, the wheel rolled along its rails away from the cathode, even up a slight incline against friction and gravity. Because the rays transferred momentum and did mechanical work on the wheel, they must carry kinetic energy and momentum, which is evidence that cathode rays are particles possessing mass rather than a massless electromagnetic wave. When the rays passed between two charged parallel plates, the beam deflected toward the positively charged plate; since only a negative charge is attracted toward a positive plate, this shows cathode rays carry negative electric charge. This was confirmed when a magnetic field was applied: the beam followed a curved, circular path in exactly the direction predicted for a negatively charged moving particle, again showing the rays are both charged and in motion — and since light is not deflected by a magnet, this also shows they are not simply light. Supporting these conclusions, a Maltese cross placed in the beam cast a sharp shadow on the fluorescent glass, showing the rays travel in straight lines and are blocked by a solid object, consistent with a stream of particles from the cathode. Initially the rays appeared not to deflect in an electric field, leading some to favour a wave model, but Thomson recognised the rays were ionising the residual gas and the resulting ions were neutralising the plates and cancelling the field; with a better vacuum the expected deflection toward the positive plate appeared. Taken together, the paddle wheel provides evidence of mass and momentum, while the electric-field deflection toward positive and the magnetic-field deflection as a negative charge provide evidence of negative charge. Cathode rays are therefore streams of fast-moving, negatively charged particles with mass — the particles we now call electrons.

NARRATOR: Hear how that answer is built. Every single observation is named, then explained, then tied to a conclusion — mass or charge — and at the end they're synthesised into one statement. QUESTION: Question four, two marks. Explain why a cathode-ray tube must contain gas at low pressure. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. At low pressure the gas particles are far apart, giving the electrons a long mean free path. This allows the cathode-ray electrons to accelerate to high speed and travel the full length of the tube without losing their energy in collisions with gas particles. At higher pressure the electrons would collide too soon and never reach the far end. Two clean marks: the mean free path, and the acceleration over the length of the tube.

QUESTION: Question five, three marks. Early experiments showed no deflection of cathode rays in an electric field, suggesting they might be uncharged. Explain why this observation was misleading and how it was resolved. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The absence of deflection was not a true property of the rays. The tube was imperfectly evacuated, so the cathode rays ionised the residual gas, and the resulting ions drifted to the charged plates and neutralised them, cancelling the electric field. With no field present, no deflection occurred. Thomson resolved this by improving the vacuum, removing the residual gas so the field was no longer neutralised; the beam then deflected toward the positive plate, confirming that cathode rays carry negative charge. The key marking point is attributing the early result to the apparatus — the poor vacuum and residual gas — not to the rays being uncharged.
