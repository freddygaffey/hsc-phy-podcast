# Supplementary Materials

Key equations, worked solutions, reference data, and an observation-to-conclusion summary for this episode. Nothing here is spoken in the audio — it is the read-along reference.

### Listing 1 — Magnetic force on a cathode-ray electron (worked example)

Force on a charge moving perpendicular to a magnetic field:

$$
F = qvB
$$

Given:

- \(q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}\)
- \(v = 2.5 \times 10^{4}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}\)
- \(B = 2.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{T}\) (\(v\) perpendicular to \(B\))

$$
F = (1.6 \times 10^{-19})(2.5 \times 10^{4})(2.0 \times 10^{-2})
$$

- Front numbers: \(1.6 \times 2.5 \times 2.0 = 8.0\)
- Powers of ten: \((-19) + (4) + (-2) = -17\)

$$
F = 8.0 \times 10^{-17}\ \mathrm{N}
$$

Path shape: circular. Reason: the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it acts as a centripetal force and bends the path into a circle.

### Listing 2 — Electric field between parallel plates (worked example)

Uniform field between parallel plates:

$$
E = \frac{V}{d}
$$

Given:

- \(V = 48\ \mathrm{V}\)
- \(d = 5.0\ \mathrm{mm} = 5.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m}\)

$$
E = \frac{48}{5.0 \times 10^{-3}} = 9.6 \times 10^{3}\ \mathrm{V\,m^{-1}} = 9600\ \mathrm{V\,m^{-1}}\ (= 9600\ \mathrm{N\,C^{-1}})
$$

Force on a cathode ray (negative charge) in this field:

$$
F = qE
$$

directed TOWARD the positive plate (a negative charge is attracted to the positive plate \(\to\) the observed deflection).

### Listing 4 — The four classic observations: observation → conclusion
| Apparatus | Observation | Conclusion (tie to mass / charge / straight-line) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Maltese cross | Sharp, well-defined shadow cast on the fluorescent glass | Rays travel in straight lines; blocked by solid objects; emitted from the cathode (shadow points away from it) |
| Paddle wheel | Wheel rolls along its rails away from the cathode, even uphill against friction and gravity | Rays carry momentum and kinetic energy and do mechanical work → particles with MASS |
| Electric field (charged plates) | Beam deflects toward the POSITIVE plate | Rays carry NEGATIVE charge (attracted to positive) |
| Magnetic field | Beam follows a curved, circular path matching a negative moving charge | Rays are charged and moving (\(F = qvB\)); and since light is NOT deflected by a magnet, they are not simply light |

### Listing 5 — Relevant equations (described in words in the narration)

Electric field between parallel plates (\(E\) in \(\mathrm{N\,C^{-1}}\) or \(\mathrm{V\,m^{-1}}\), \(V\) in volts, \(d\) in metres):

$$
E = \frac{V}{d}
$$

Force on a charge in an electric field (\(F\) in newtons, \(q\) in coulombs, \(E\) in \(\mathrm{N\,C^{-1}}\)):

$$
F = qE
$$

Force on a charge moving perpendicular to a magnetic field:

$$
F = qvB
$$

General case (\(\theta\) = angle between \(v\) and \(B\)):

$$
F = qvB\sin\theta
$$

Maximum when \(v \perp B\) (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)); zero when \(v \parallel B\) (\(\theta = 0\)); force is always perpendicular to \(v\) \(\to\) circular path.

Work done accelerating a charge through a voltage equals the kinetic energy gained:

$$
W = qV = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^{2}
$$

### Listing 6 — Key people and dates (use sparingly, only where valued)
| Year | Person | Contribution |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1855 | Heinrich Geissler | Refined the vacuum pump; enabled low-pressure tubes |
| ~1858 | Julius Plücker | Current through low-pressure gases; saw the green glow near the anode |
| 1875 | William Crookes | Improved (Crookes) tubes; hypothesised electric-field deflection |
| 1876 | Eugen Goldstein | Named "cathode rays" |
| 1892 | Heinrich Hertz | Rays penetrate thin metal foils → wrongly favoured a wave model |
| 1895 | Jean-Baptiste Perrin | Rays deposit negative charge on impact → particle nature |
| 1897 | J. J. Thomson | First to deflect cathode rays with an electric field; discovered the electron |

### Listing 7 — Mark-earning answer structure (the "analyse" skill)
```text
For every observation, write THREE parts:

  1. OBSERVATION   — state precisely what is seen
  2. REASONING     — the physics that links it (work / momentum / charge sign / field force)
  3. CONCLUSION    — tie explicitly to MASS or CHARGE (or straight-line travel)

Then SYNTHESISE: paddle wheel → mass; E-field + B-field → negative charge.

Weak answer (≈1 mark):
  "The paddle wheel turned, so cathode rays are particles."

Strong answer (full marks):
  "When a light paddle wheel was placed in the path of the cathode rays, it
   rolled along its rails away from the cathode, even up a slight incline
   against friction and gravity. Since the rays did mechanical work and
   transferred momentum to the wheel, they must carry kinetic energy and
   momentum — evidence that cathode rays are particles with mass, not a
   massless electromagnetic wave."

Memory hook for the four experiments (write down first in the exam):
  Cross · Paddle · Electric · Magnetic
  ("Cathode rays Make Particle Evidence Move")
```
