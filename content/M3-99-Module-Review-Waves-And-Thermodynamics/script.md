NARRATOR: Let me first replay the spine of the whole module in one breath, the toolkit's in Listing one. We began with wave properties: a wave transfers energy without transferring matter, described by wavelength, amplitude, period and frequency, tied by the wave equation, speed equals frequency times wavelength, with frequency the reciprocal of period. We classified waves two ways, transverse versus longitudinal by particle direction, mechanical versus electromagnetic by whether they need a medium. QUESTION: Let's start with pure retrieval, no calculation. Name the four behaviours that any wave can show. Then name the three modes of heat transfer. Pause the player, write all seven, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The four wave behaviours are reflection, refraction, diffraction, and superposition, and remember superposition is the parent of interference, standing waves, and resonance. Four behaviours, three heat modes. NARRATOR: Now an integrated wave problem, the kind that combines graphs with the wave equation, this is in Listing two. A sound wave is being studied. A displacement-time graph of one air particle shows a complete cycle every two milliseconds, that's zero point zero zero two seconds. The speed of sound in the air is three hundred and forty metres per second. Find the frequency, then the wavelength. Work it through: the period is zero point zero zero two seconds from the time graph, so the frequency is one over the period, one over zero point zero zero two, five hundred hertz. Then the wavelength is the speed over the frequency, three hundred and forty over five hundred, zero point six eight metres. NARRATOR: Let me push that one further, into the Doppler effect, because combining concepts is what the harder questions do. Suppose the source of that five-hundred-hertz sound now moves towards a stationary listener. Without calculating, what does the listener hear, and is the change in the source's emitted frequency or only in the observed frequency? The listener hears a higher frequency, a higher pitch, because the source's motion towards them compresses the wavefronts, shortening the received wavelength and raising the observed frequency. And crucially the source still emits five hundred hertz the whole time, it's only the observed frequency that rises. That distinction, emitted constant, observed shifted, is the single most marked point in Doppler questions, and it's in the registry in Listing three.

QUESTION: Now a ray-optics integrated question. Light travels from glass, refractive index one point five, towards a glass-air boundary. First, recall the critical-angle condition. Then state what happens to a ray striking the boundary at an angle of incidence greater than the critical angle. Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing two. The critical angle is found from the condition that the sine of the critical angle equals the ratio of the refractive indices, here one over one point five, which is about zero point six seven, giving a critical angle of about forty-two degrees. For a ray striking the boundary at an angle of incidence greater than that critical angle, the two conditions for total internal reflection are met, light going from the denser glass to the less dense air, and the angle exceeding the critical angle, so the ray undergoes total internal reflection: it is entirely reflected back into the glass, with none refracting out. That's the principle behind optical fibres. Remember both conditions, dense-to-less-dense and angle above critical, dropping either loses a mark.

NARRATOR: Let me show the weak-versus-strong contrast on a classic extended response, because how you write matters as much as what you know. The question: "explain how a standing wave forms on a string fixed at both ends". The weak answer says "the waves add up and make a standing wave". True but nearly empty, it names nothing and would earn almost nothing. The strong, band-six answer: two identical waves, the wave sent along the string and its reflection from the fixed end, travel in opposite directions and superpose; because the ends are fixed they must be nodes, so only certain wavelengths fit; at points where the two waves are always out of phase they cancel, forming nodes of zero displacement, and where always in phase they reinforce, forming antinodes of maximum displacement; the fundamental, the longest such wavelength, sets the pitch. The difference is naming the mechanism, opposite directions, superposition, the fixed-end node condition, and the in-phase and out-of-phase result. Mechanism and conditions, every time, that's in the registry in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Now let's cross fully into thermodynamics with the classic combined heat problem, the one that uses both equations, it's worked in Listing two. How much energy does it take to turn half a kilogram of ice at zero degrees Celsius completely into water at twenty degrees Celsius? This is a two-stage problem, and recognising that is the whole skill. Stage one, melt the ice at constant zero degrees, that's a change of state, so Q equals m L with the latent heat of fusion, zero point five times three hundred and thirty-four thousand, one hundred and sixty-seven thousand joules. Stage two, warm the resulting water from zero to twenty degrees, that's a temperature change, so Q equals m c delta T, zero point five times four thousand two hundred times twenty, forty-two thousand joules. Add them: one hundred and sixty-seven thousand plus forty-two thousand, two hundred and nine thousand joules. The key decision was spotting that melting needs m L and warming needs m c delta T, and that you sum the stages. That equation choice is in the decision guide in Listing four.

QUESTION: Here's that decision for you to make. For each of these, state whether you'd use Q equals m c delta T or Q equals m L: first, heating oil from twenty to ninety degrees; second, boiling water that's already at one hundred degrees into steam; third, cooling steam back to ninety degrees. Pause the player, decide all three, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, from Listing four. First, heating oil from twenty to ninety degrees is a temperature change with no change of state, so Q equals m c delta T. Second, boiling water already at one hundred degrees into steam is a change of state at constant temperature, so Q equals m L, with the latent heat of vaporisation. Third, cooling steam from one hundred down to ninety degrees, once it's already steam, is a temperature change, so Q equals m c delta T again. The rule that decides it: if the temperature is changing, m c delta T; if the state is changing at constant temperature, m L. Get that decision right and combined calorimetry problems fall apart cleanly.

NARRATOR: Let me re-surface the sharpest conceptual distinctions of the module, because the exam tests them relentlessly, and they're all in the registry in Listing three. Transverse versus longitudinal: particles perpendicular to travel versus parallel. Mechanical versus electromagnetic: needs a medium versus doesn't. Progressive versus standing wave: transports energy versus stores it in fixed nodes and antinodes. Constructive versus destructive interference: in phase and reinforcing versus out of phase and cancelling. Reflection, refraction, diffraction: bouncing back, bending from a speed change, spreading through a gap. Heat versus temperature: transferred energy versus average particle kinetic energy. And the latent-heat idea: during a change of state, energy goes into breaking bonds, not raising kinetic energy, so the temperature holds constant. Each of those is a one-mark discriminator, and knowing which side is which is what separates a band four from a band six.

QUESTION: A rapid-fire conceptual check, five of them. One: which single heat-transfer mode can cross a vacuum? Two: in refraction, which quantity stays constant at the boundary? Three: what does a displacement-position graph give you, period or wavelength? Four: is momentum, sorry, is kinetic energy of particles the measure of heat or of temperature? Five: does a closed pipe produce all harmonics or only odd ones? Pause the player, answer all five, then play.

NARRATOR: Here are the answers. One: radiation is the only mode that crosses a vacuum, which is how the Sun heats the Earth. Two: in refraction the frequency stays constant at the boundary, while speed and wavelength change. Three: a displacement-position graph, a snapshot, gives the wavelength; the period comes from a displacement-time graph. Four: the average kinetic energy of particles is the measure of temperature, not heat; heat is transferred energy. Five: a closed pipe produces only the odd harmonics, with a fundamental wavelength four times its length. If you nailed all five, your conceptual foundation is solid; any miss points to the episode to revisit. These are all in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Let me work one more integrated wave problem, this time in sound, combining a pipe with the wave equation and then with beats, because layered questions like this are common, it's in Listing two. An organ pipe open at both ends is zero point six eight metres long and the speed of sound is three hundred and forty metres per second. First find its fundamental frequency. For an open pipe the fundamental wavelength is twice the length, twice zero point six eight, one point three six metres, so the fundamental frequency is the speed over the wavelength, three hundred and forty over one point three six, two hundred and fifty hertz. Now suppose a tuning fork of two hundred and fifty-four hertz is sounded next to this pipe. What beat frequency is heard? The beat frequency is the magnitude of the difference, two hundred and fifty-four minus two hundred and fifty, four beats per second. See the layering: the pipe rule gave the wavelength, the wave equation gave the frequency, and then beats compared it with the fork. Each step is simple; the skill is sequencing them, and the chain is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: And one more on the thermal side, bringing in thermal conductivity and the inverse-square law together as a quick double, both in Listing two. First, conduction: heat is conducted through a glass window of area two square metres and thickness zero point zero zero four metres, with a thermal conductivity of about zero point eight, when the temperature difference across it is fifteen degrees. The rate of heat flow is the conductivity times the area times the temperature difference, divided by the thickness, that's zero point eight times two times fifteen, divided by zero point zero zero four, which is twenty-four divided by zero point zero zero four, six thousand watts, a large rate, which is why single-glazed windows lose so much heat and why double glazing, adding a low-conductivity air gap, helps so much. Second, the inverse-square law: if a lamp gives an intensity of thirty-six units at two metres, then at six metres, three times the distance, the intensity falls by a factor of three squared, nine, to four units. Two different parts of the module, conduction rate and radiation intensity, each just one relationship applied carefully.

NARRATOR: Let me do one more weak-versus-strong, this time on the thermal side, "compare the three methods of heat transfer", because comparison questions reward structure. The weak answer: "conduction is in solids, convection is in liquids, and radiation is from the Sun." It gestures at the right idea but gives no mechanism and is partly wrong, convection is in any fluid, gases too. The strong, band-six answer states a mechanism and a medium for each: conduction transfers heat by particle-to-particle collisions through a material with no bulk movement, requiring a medium and occurring mainly in solids; convection transfers heat by the bulk movement of a heated fluid, warm less-dense fluid rising and cool denser fluid sinking, requiring a fluid; and radiation transfers heat by electromagnetic waves, requiring no medium and so able to cross a vacuum. The marks live in the mechanism and the medium for each, especially the point that radiation alone needs no medium. When a question says "compare", give the axis of comparison, here mechanism and medium, for every item; that structure is what lifts the band.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the single deepest thread of the whole module, the one that connects it back to Dynamics and forward to next year: energy. In Module Two you learned energy is conserved and transformed, never destroyed. Every topic in this module is that law in a new costume. A wave is energy transported without matter; superposition and resonance are about where wave energy concentrates; sound intensity and light intensity both dilute as energy spreads over an expanding sphere, the inverse-square law; temperature is the average kinetic energy of particles; heat is energy flowing from hot to cold; specific heat and latent heat are energy bookkeeping for warming and for changing state; and conduction, convection and radiation are three routes for that same energy to travel. QUESTION: Question one. A wave on a string has a frequency of twenty-five hertz and a wavelength of zero point zero eight metres. Calculate its speed and its period. Then state whether a wave on a string is transverse or longitudinal. Three marks. Pause the player, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. The speed is frequency times wavelength, twenty-five times zero point zero eight, two metres per second. The period is one over the frequency, one over twenty-five, zero point zero four seconds. A wave on a string is transverse, because the particles of the string oscillate perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. Three marks: the speed of two metres per second from the wave equation; the period of zero point zero four seconds as the reciprocal of frequency; and transverse with the perpendicular-motion reason. The trap is mixing up period and frequency; the period is the reciprocal of frequency, in seconds.

QUESTION: Question two. Explain the difference between heat and temperature, then explain why a coastal town has a milder climate than an inland desert at the same latitude. Four marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance, while heat is energy transferred from a hotter object to a colder one because of the temperature difference between them, energy in transit. The coastal town's milder climate is due to water's very high specific heat capacity: the ocean can absorb and release a large amount of thermal energy with only a small change in its own temperature, so it warms slowly by day and in summer and cools slowly by night and in winter, moderating the air temperatures of nearby land. The inland desert, lacking this large body of water, heats and cools rapidly, giving temperature extremes. Four marks: temperature as average particle kinetic energy; heat as transferred energy; water's high specific heat lets the ocean buffer temperature; hence the coast is milder. This links the heat-versus-temperature distinction to the specific-heat application.

QUESTION: Question three. A ray of light passes from air, refractive index one, into a transparent plastic at an angle of incidence of fifty degrees, where the sine of fifty degrees is zero point seven seven. The angle of refraction is thirty degrees, where the sine of thirty is zero point five. Calculate the refractive index of the plastic, and state the speed of light in it, taking the speed of light in vacuum as three times ten to the eight metres per second. Four marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By Snell's law, n one sine i equals n two sine r, so one times zero point seven seven equals n two times zero point five. Therefore n two equals zero point seven seven over zero point five, which is one point five four, the refractive index of the plastic. The speed of light in the plastic is the vacuum speed over the refractive index, three times ten to the eight divided by one point five four, about one point nine five times ten to the eight metres per second. Four marks: correct Snell's-law setup; refractive index of one point five four; using n equals c over v rearranged; and the speed of about one point nine five times ten to the eight metres per second. The trap is forgetting that a higher refractive index means a lower speed.

QUESTION: Question four. Two waves of equal amplitude travel towards each other along a spring and overlap. Explain, using the principle of superposition, what is observed at a point where they are always in phase, and at a point where they are always out of phase. Then name the phenomenon this produces. Four marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing three. By the principle of superposition, when the waves overlap the resultant displacement at any point is the sum of their individual displacements. At a point where the two waves are always in phase, crest meeting crest, their displacements always reinforce, giving maximum amplitude, this is constructive interference, and the point is an antinode. At a point where the two waves are always out of phase, crest meeting trough, their displacements always cancel, giving zero displacement, this is destructive interference, and the point is a node. Because these nodes and antinodes stay in fixed positions, the phenomenon produced is a standing wave. Four marks: superposition sums displacements; in-phase reinforcement giving an antinode; out-of-phase cancellation giving a node; and the result is a standing wave. This integrates superposition, interference, and standing waves.

QUESTION: Question five, the hardest, fully integrated. A two hundred gram aluminium block at one hundred and fifty degrees Celsius is dropped into four hundred grams of water at twenty-five degrees Celsius in an insulated cup. The specific heat of aluminium is nine hundred joules per kilogram per kelvin and of water four thousand two hundred. Explain, using conservation of energy, how the system reaches a final temperature, state which substance changes temperature more and why, and explain what would be different if the block were hot enough to make some of the water boil. Five marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Because the cup is insulated, energy is conserved: the heat lost by the hot aluminium as it cools equals the heat gained by the cooler water as it warms, until both reach a common final temperature, thermal equilibrium. You set the heat lost by the aluminium, its mass times its specific heat times its temperature drop, equal to the heat gained by the water, its mass times its specific heat times its temperature rise, and solve for the final temperature. The aluminium changes temperature more, because it has both a smaller mass and a much smaller specific heat capacity than the water, nine hundred against four thousand two hundred, so for the same energy transferred its temperature changes far more, while the water, with its high specific heat and larger mass, changes only a little. If the block were hot enough to make some water reach one hundred degrees and boil, then in addition to the m c delta T terms you would need a latent-heat term, Q equals m L with the latent heat of vaporisation, for the mass of water that turns to steam, and during that boiling the water's temperature would stay constant at one hundred degrees while it changed state. Five marks: energy conservation, heat lost equals heat gained; setting m c delta T terms equal to find the final temperature; aluminium changes temperature more due to smaller mass and specific heat; a latent-heat m L term needed if water boils; and the temperature staying constant during boiling. This pulls together specific heat, conservation of energy, and latent heat, the whole thermal half of the module in one question.
