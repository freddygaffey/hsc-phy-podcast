We've done motion, forces and energy, waves, and heat; now we turn to one of the most important ideas in all of physics, electric charge, and the forces and fields it creates. This module runs from the charge on a single electron all the way to electric circuits and magnetism, and it's the direct foundation for the whole of Module Six next year, electromagnetism. Today we start at the beginning: what electric charge is, how objects become charged, the force between charges given by Coulomb's law, and the electric field that carries that force through space. By the end you'll be able to describe charging processes, apply Coulomb's law, and define and use the electric field. But first, a recap, because two ideas from earlier modules carry straight in.

NARRATOR: Two threads feed into today. From the Dynamics module, a force is a push or pull, a vector, with a size and a direction, and forces can act at a distance without contact. From the Waves module, just last week, you met the inverse-square law twice, sound intensity and light intensity both fall off as one over the distance squared because the energy spreads over an expanding sphere. Hold both, because today the force between two charges is exactly that kind of force, acting at a distance, a vector, and following an inverse-square law in the distance, just like gravity. So electrostatics isn't a brand-new world; it's the force-and-field ideas you already have, applied to a new property of matter called charge.

QUESTION: Quick check from earlier modules. What two things specify a force, since it's a vector? And in an inverse-square law, if you triple the distance, by what factor does the quantity fall? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. A force, being a vector, is specified by its magnitude, its size, and its direction. And in an inverse-square law, tripling the distance makes the quantity fall by a factor of three squared, that is nine. Magnitude and direction for the vector, and a factor of nine for tripling the distance. Lock the inverse-square idea especially, because Coulomb's law, the force between charges, is an inverse-square law, and the factor-of-nine reasoning will come straight back. Now let's meet charge itself.

NARRATOR: Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter, carried by the particles inside atoms. An atom has a central nucleus containing positively charged protons and uncharged neutrons, surrounded by negatively charged electrons. There are exactly two kinds of charge, positive and negative, and the fundamental rule, the one everything else rests on, is this: like charges repel, and unlike charges attract. Two positives push apart, two negatives push apart, but a positive and a negative pull together. Normally an atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons, so it's electrically neutral, the charges balance. An object becomes charged when this balance is upset, when it gains or loses electrons. Gain extra electrons and it becomes negatively charged; lose electrons and it becomes positively charged. Notice it's the electrons that move, the light, outer particles, not the protons locked in the nucleus. That rule, like repels, unlike attracts, and the fact that charging means transferring electrons, are in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Two deep principles govern charge, and the syllabus names both. First, charge is conserved: it can't be created or destroyed, only transferred from one object to another. When you charge one object negative by giving it electrons, another object is left equally positive, having lost them; the total charge is unchanged. This is exactly like conservation of energy and momentum from the Dynamics module, another great conservation law. Second, charge is quantised: it comes only in whole-number multiples of a basic unit, the charge on a single electron or proton, called the elementary charge, about one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. The coulomb is the unit of charge, and it's a large unit, one coulomb is the combined charge of roughly six times ten to the eighteen electrons, so everyday static charges are tiny fractions of a coulomb, often measured in microcoulombs, millionths. You can't have half an elementary charge; any charged object carries a whole number of these basic units. Conservation and quantisation of charge are in Listing one, and both are favourite short-answer points.

NARRATOR: Now, how do objects actually become charged? The syllabus wants three methods, and they're in Listing two. First, charging by friction: when two different insulating materials are rubbed together, electrons are transferred from one to the other, so one becomes positive and the other negative. Rub a balloon on your hair and electrons move to the balloon, leaving the balloon negative and your hair positive, which is why your hair stands up and is attracted to the balloon. This is the oldest known way to make charge, and it's why you get a shock after walking across carpet. Second, charging by conduction, also called charging by contact: when a charged object touches a neutral conductor, charge flows between them until it's shared, leaving both with the same sign of charge. Touch a neutral metal sphere with a negatively charged rod and electrons flow onto the sphere, leaving it negative too. The key feature of conduction is direct contact, and the object ends up with the same sign as the charger.

NARRATOR: The third method is the subtle one, charging by induction, and it's worth getting exactly right because it produces the opposite sign and needs no contact with the charging object. Bring a charged rod near a neutral conductor, without touching. The rod's field pushes the conductor's electrons: if the rod is negative, it repels the conductor's electrons to the far side, leaving the near side positive and the far side negative, the charges have separated, though the conductor is still neutral overall. Now, while the rod is still held near, you earth the conductor, connect it to the ground, briefly: electrons flee down to earth, or come up from it, to get further from the rod. Remove the earth, then remove the rod, and the conductor is left with a net charge of the opposite sign to the rod. So induction charges an object by bringing a charge near, separating the charges, earthing, then removing, and crucially the object ends up oppositely charged to the inducing charge, without ever touching it. That contrast, conduction gives the same sign by contact, induction gives the opposite sign without contact, is a classic exam discriminator, and it's in Listing two.

QUESTION: A retrieval one worth stopping for. A positively charged rod is used to charge a neutral metal sphere. If you charge the sphere by conduction, what sign does it end up with? If you charge it by induction, what sign does it end up with? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. By conduction, contact, the sphere ends up positive, the same sign as the rod, because positive charge is effectively shared on contact, electrons flow from the sphere to the rod. By induction, no contact, the sphere ends up negative, the opposite sign to the rod: the positive rod attracts the sphere's electrons to the near side, and when you earth the sphere, electrons flow up from the earth onto it, so after removing the earth and the rod the sphere is left negative. Conduction gives the same sign, induction gives the opposite sign. If you mixed those up, that's the single most common electrostatics error, fix it now.

NARRATOR: Now the quantitative law of the force between charges: Coulomb's law. It states that the electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the two charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. In symbols, the force F equals a constant k times the first charge times the second charge, divided by the distance squared. The constant k is about nine times ten to the nine newton metres squared per coulomb squared. Look at the structure: bigger charges mean a bigger force, in direct proportion, and greater distance means a much smaller force, falling off as the inverse square, exactly like gravity. If both charges are the same sign the force is repulsive, pushing apart; if opposite, attractive, pulling together. Coulomb's law is the workhorse calculation of this episode, and it's in Listing three with a worked example.

QUESTION: Let's reason with Coulomb's law before we compute. Two charges sit a certain distance apart with some force between them. If you double one of the charges and also double the distance, what happens to the force? Pause the player, work it through, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Take the two changes one at a time. Doubling one charge doubles the force, because force is proportional to the product of the charges, so that's a factor of two up. Doubling the distance divides the force by two squared, that's four, because of the inverse square, so a factor of four down. Combine them: two divided by four is one half. So the force becomes half of what it was. The trap is to think the two doublings cancel; they don't, because charge is a direct factor but distance is squared, so the distance change wins. That inverse-square dominance is the heart of Coulomb's law, and it's why distance matters so much. The worked numbers are in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Let me work an actual Coulomb's-law number so you've seen the substitution, it's in Listing three. Suppose two charges, each of two times ten to the minus five coulombs, sit zero point three metres apart. The force is the constant k times the product of the charges over the distance squared. So that's nine times ten to the nine, times two times ten to the minus five, times two times ten to the minus five, all divided by zero point three squared. The top is nine times ten to the nine times four times ten to the minus ten, which is three point six; the bottom is zero point zero nine; so the force is three point six over zero point zero nine, about forty newtons. Both charges are positive, the same sign, so the force is repulsive, pushing them apart. The recipe never changes: substitute the two charges and the distance, square the distance, and check the signs to decide attract or repel.

NARRATOR: It's worth pausing on how closely Coulomb's law mirrors Newton's law of gravitation, because the parallel is deep and examinable. Both are inverse-square laws acting at a distance through a field: gravity's force is proportional to the product of two masses over the distance squared, Coulomb's is proportional to the product of two charges over the distance squared, the same mathematical shape. But there are two big differences. Gravity is always attractive, because there's only one kind of mass, whereas the electric force can be attractive or repulsive, because there are two kinds of charge. And the electric force is vastly stronger than gravity for everyday particles, the electrical repulsion between two protons is something like ten to the thirty-six times their gravitational attraction. So the same inverse-square form governs both the falling of an apple and the spark from a doorknob, a beautiful unity you'll see again next year.

NARRATOR: Now the central concept of the module: the electric field. A charge creates a field, a region of space around it where another charge would feel a force, and it's the field that mediates the force, reaching across empty space. We make this precise. The electric field strength at a point, given the symbol E, is defined as the force per unit charge that a small positive test charge would experience there: E equals F over q, force divided by charge, measured in newtons per coulomb. So if you know the field at a point, the force on any charge placed there is just F equals E times q. The field is a vector, it has a direction, and by convention that direction is the direction of the force on a positive test charge: so the field points away from a positive charge, which would push a positive test charge away, and towards a negative charge, which would pull it in. The distinction to keep sharp is this: the field E is a property of the location, it's there whether or not a charge sits in it; the force F is what a particular charge actually experiences when placed in the field. Field is force per unit charge, force is field times charge. That definition is in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "distinguish between electric field strength and electric force". The band-six version: the electric field strength at a point is the force per unit positive charge that would be experienced at that point, E equals F over q, measured in newtons per coulomb; it is a property of the location, created by other charges, and exists whether or not a charge is placed there. The electric force is the actual push or pull, in newtons, experienced by a specific charge q when it is placed in the field, given by F equals E times q. So the field describes the location; the force describes what happens to a particular charge in it. The marks are for E equals F over q as force per unit charge, for it being a property of the location, and for the force being what a specific charge experiences. The weak answer, "the field is where the charge pushes things", earns little because it gives no definition and no equation. Name E equals F over q and the location-versus-charge distinction.

NARRATOR: We picture the field with field lines, and the syllabus names the patterns, they're sketched in Listing four. Field lines show the direction and strength of the field by these rules: the lines point in the direction of the field, so they start on positive charges and end on negative charges, out of positive, into negative; the closer together the lines, the stronger the field; and field lines never cross, because the field has only one direction at each point. Around a single positive point charge the lines radiate straight outward in all directions, like the spikes of a sea urchin; around a negative charge they point straight inward. Between two opposite charges, a dipole, the lines curve from the positive to the negative. And between two parallel oppositely charged plates, the lines are straight, parallel, and evenly spaced, pointing from the positive plate to the negative plate, this is a uniform field, the same strength and direction everywhere between the plates. Those patterns, especially the radial point-charge field and the uniform parallel-plate field, are guaranteed exam items.

NARRATOR: The uniform field between parallel plates deserves its own equation, because it sets up next year's work directly. For two parallel plates separated by a distance d with a voltage V between them, the field strength is simply the voltage divided by the separation: E equals V over d. This gives a second unit for the electric field, volts per metre, which is exactly equivalent to newtons per coulomb. So a uniform field can be described either as the force per unit charge, E equals F over q, or as the voltage gradient between the plates, E equals V over d. This parallel-plate setup, a uniform field of known strength, is precisely how charged particles are accelerated and deflected in the devices you'll study in Module Six next year, so the E equals V over d relationship is a bridge straight into Year Twelve. Both field equations are in Listing four, with a worked example in Listing three.

QUESTION: Let's use the uniform-field equation. Two parallel plates are separated by zero point zero two metres, with a voltage of one hundred volts between them. What is the electric field strength between them? Pause the player, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. For parallel plates the field is the voltage over the separation, E equals V over d. Substitute: one hundred volts divided by zero point zero two metres, which is five thousand. So the field strength is five thousand volts per metre, equivalently five thousand newtons per coulomb. The field is uniform, so it's that same value everywhere between the plates. The method is just dividing the voltage by the plate separation, and note the answer carries either unit, volts per metre or newtons per coulomb, they're equivalent.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can demonstrate charging by friction with rods and cloths, and test the like-repels-unlike-attracts rule with a charged rod and a suspended pith ball or a thin stream of water bending towards a charged comb. You can show induction with an electroscope, whose leaves diverge when a charged rod is brought near, even without contact. And you can map field-line patterns by suspending small seeds or grass in oil and watching them line up along the field around charged electrodes. A key examinable point is that humidity ruins electrostatics experiments, because moist air conducts charge away, so they work best on dry days, that's why you get more static shocks in winter. Describing such an investigation and what it shows is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: thinking protons move during charging, it's the electrons that transfer; the nucleus stays put. Trap two: mixing up conduction and induction, conduction is contact giving the same sign, induction is no contact giving the opposite sign. Trap three: dropping the inverse square in Coulomb's law, doubling the distance quarters the force, not halves it. Trap four: confusing the field E with the force F, the field is force per unit charge, a property of the location; the force is what a specific charge feels. Trap five: drawing field lines that cross, or that point into a positive charge, lines go out of positive, into negative, and never cross. Avoid those five and electrostatics is secure.
