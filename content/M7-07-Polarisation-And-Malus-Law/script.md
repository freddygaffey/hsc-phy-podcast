NARRATOR: Two episodes back, in the diffraction and interference episode, the key idea was that when light passes through two narrow, closely spaced slits, each slit acts as a coherent source of waves, and those waves overlap. Where they arrive in step, crest on crest, you get constructive interference and a bright fringe. Where they arrive exactly out of step, crest on trough, you get destructive interference and a dark fringe. The whole pattern only makes sense if light is a wave, because the dark fringes are the giveaway: two streams of particles can only ever add up to more, but two waves can cancel to nothing. We also said the slits have to be tiny and close together because the wavelength of visible light is minuscule, a few hundred nanometres, so you need a tiny scale to see the effect. Hold onto the words coherent, monochromatic, path difference, constructive, destructive.

NARRATOR: Then last episode we turned that into a calculation with the diffraction grating. That whole number m is the order of the maximum. We said smaller spacing or longer wavelength pushes the spots out to larger angles, and that the spacing d is just one divided by the number of lines per metre. The memory hook there was that the grating equation is simply path difference equals a whole number of wavelengths, dressed up with geometry. Same physics as the double slit, just sharpened.

NARRATOR: Start with what polarisation actually is. Picture a beam of light coming straight towards you, out of the screen. The electric field is oscillating in a plane perpendicular to that direction of travel, so it's wiggling in the plane of the screen, side to side, up and down, or at any angle in between. Now, in ordinary light, the kind from the Sun, from a lamp, from a glowing filament, the electric field isn't confined to any one direction in that plane. It oscillates in every possible direction perpendicular to the beam, randomly, and over time equally in all of them. Vertical, horizontal, diagonal, every plane is represented. That is unpolarised light. Every plane of oscillation is present.

NARRATOR: Linearly polarised light, also called plane-polarised light, and those two terms are exact synonyms, is the opposite. The electric field is confined to a single plane. It only oscillates in one direction, perpendicular to the beam. If unpolarised light is a rope being shaken in every direction at once, polarised light is a rope being shaken only up and down.

NARRATOR: Now here is the comparison the dot-point literally asks for, and I want you to learn the wording, because "compare" is a NESA verb and the marker is looking for an explicit contrast using the right vocabulary. Notice the structure: same framework, electric field, perpendicular to propagation, and then the contrast turns on one word, "all planes" versus "one plane". That word "whereas" is doing work; the marker rewards the explicit comparison.

NARRATOR: And there is a trap buried right there that I need to flag now, because it's the cheapest mark to lose. It is the electric field whose plane defines the polarisation. Not the magnetic field. Not vaguely "the wave oscillates in one plane". If you write "the magnetic field is confined to one plane", or you just say "the light oscillates in one direction" without naming the field, you've thrown away the precision the marker wants. QUESTION: Hang on, why the electric field specifically? If both fields are oscillating and both are transverse, why does only the electric one count?

NARRATOR: Fair question. Partly it's convention, but there's a physical reason behind the convention. When light interacts with matter, with the electrons in a filter, in your eye, in a detector, it's overwhelmingly the electric field that pushes those charges around. The electric force on a charge is enormous compared with the magnetic force at these speeds. So the electric field is the part that actually does things, the part filters act on, and the plane of polarisation is defined by it. The magnetic field is still there, still perpendicular, still ninety degrees across from the electric field. But when the syllabus and the marker say "the plane of the electric field", they mean it literally. Name the electric field.

NARRATOR: Right, that's the comparison done. Now the second dot-point: how does a filter actually polarise light? This is a "explain" question, which means the marker wants the mechanism, the cause and effect, not just a statement.

NARRATOR: A polarising filter has what's called a transmission axis, sometimes called the axis of polarisation. Think of it as a single allowed direction. The filter transmits only the component of the electric field that is aligned with, that is parallel to, its transmission axis. Every component of the field in any other direction is absorbed. So the filter is a gatekeeper that asks, of the incoming electric field, "how much of you points along my axis?" and lets that much through, and absorbs the rest.

NARRATOR: Now follow what happens when unpolarised light hits the first filter. The incoming light has its electric field oscillating in all directions equally. The filter only passes the part aligned with its axis. So whatever way you turn the filter, it picks out one direction and passes the component along it, absorbing everything else. The light that emerges is now polarised, oscillating in a single plane, the plane of the filter's transmission axis. That's the mechanism: it transmits the component of the electric field parallel to its transmission axis and absorbs the components in all other planes, so the emerging light oscillates in a single plane. Learn that sentence. That is the model answer to "explain how a polarising filter produces polarised light".

NARRATOR: But there's a second thing that happens at the first filter, and it's a favourite of examiners. The intensity drops to exactly half. Whatever the intensity of the unpolarised light going in, the polarised light coming out has half that intensity. And here's the crucial qualifier: this halving happens regardless of how the filter is oriented. Turn the filter any way you like, the unpolarised light still comes out at half intensity, because unpolarised light has its energy spread equally over all directions, so on average half of it is aligned closely enough with the axis to get through and half is in the blocked directions. In words: the intensity emerging from the first polariser equals one half the intensity of the unpolarised light falling on it.

NARRATOR: This is so important I'm going to say it a second way, because at four or five times speed a single mention slides past. The first filter, acting on unpolarised light, always halves the intensity, and it does this no matter which way you rotate it. There is no angle in this step. The "half" is fixed. We give the first filter a name: the polariser. Its job is to take unpolarised light and produce polarised light at half intensity.

NARRATOR: Now bring in a second filter. This one acts on light that is already polarised, and we call it the analyser. And this is where the angle finally enters, and where Malus's law lives. The polarised light arriving at the analyser is oscillating in one plane. The analyser has its own transmission axis. Call the angle between the polarised light's plane and the analyser's axis "theta". The analyser passes only the component of the already-polarised field that lies along its axis.

NARRATOR: Here is the heart of the whole episode, the single most important conceptual point, so lean in. The transmitted electric field amplitude is the component of the incoming field along the new axis. If the incoming field has amplitude E-nought, then the component along an axis at angle theta is E-nought times the cosine of theta. That's just resolving a vector; you've done it a hundred times in mechanics. But, and this is the whole game, intensity is not proportional to the field amplitude. Intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude. So when you square that transmitted amplitude to get the transmitted intensity, the cosine gets squared too. The transmitted intensity equals the maximum intensity times the cosine of theta, squared.

NARRATOR: That is Malus's law. In the syllabus form, in words: the transmitted intensity equals the maximum intensity, I-max, multiplied by the square of the cosine of the angle theta between the polarised light's plane and the analyser's transmission axis. I-max here is the intensity of the polarised light arriving at the analyser, the value you'd get at zero degrees when everything is aligned. Theta is the angle between the two axes. And the answer is a cosine squared, never a bare cosine.

QUESTION: This is the bit I always get wrong. How do I make sure I never drop that square?

NARRATOR: Good, because dropping the square is the single most common error on this entire topic, and it will cost you the mark every time. So here's your memory hook, and I want you to lock it in: Malus loves to square. M-L-S. Malus, loves, square. Étienne-Louis Malus, the Frenchman the law is named after, loves to square. Whenever you write down Malus's law, the cosine wears a little exponent two. If you've written a bare cosine, you've forgotten that Malus loves to square. The physical reason behind the hook, the thing that earns you the explanation mark, is: the field component is the cosine, but intensity goes as amplitude squared, so the intensity carries cosine squared. Say that to yourself: the field is the cosine, the intensity is the cosine squared. Malus loves to square.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the three benchmark values, because they come up constantly and knowing them by heart lets you sanity-check any answer. At zero degrees, the analyser's axis is aligned with the polarised light. Cosine of zero is one, one squared is one, so all the light passes. Maximum transmission. At ninety degrees, the analyser is crossed, perpendicular to the light's plane. Cosine of ninety is zero, zero squared is zero, so no light passes at all. Total extinction. And the one in the middle, the useful one: at forty-five degrees, cosine of forty-five is one over root two, and one over root two squared is one half. So at forty-five degrees, exactly half the polarised light gets through. Zero degrees all, ninety degrees nothing, forty-five degrees half. Those three are your anchors.

NARRATOR: There's also sixty degrees, which examiners love because the number is clean: cosine of sixty is one half, and one half squared is one quarter. So at sixty degrees, a quarter of the polarised light gets through. And thirty degrees: cosine of thirty is root three over two, squared that's three quarters. So thirty degrees passes three quarters. Notice the nice symmetry, thirty degrees passes three quarters, sixty degrees passes one quarter. No angle, no cosine. The half. The second filter, the analyser, acting on already-polarised light, gives you Malus's law, the cosine squared. Never apply the cosine-squared to the unpolarised step, and never forget the half on the first step. Unpolarised into the polariser is always the half. Polarised through the analyser is always cosine squared. Two filters, two rules.

NARRATOR: Now to the deepest point of the episode, the reason polarisation matters far beyond a calculation. Why does the simple fact that light can be polarised prove that light is a transverse wave? This is conceptual, it's high-value, and it's the part that separates a band-six answer from a band-four one.

NARRATOR: Think about what polarising actually requires. To polarise a wave, you have to be able to restrict its oscillation to one plane, and block the others. That's only possible if the wave has more than one possible plane of oscillation to begin with, planes that are perpendicular to the direction of travel. A transverse wave has exactly that: it oscillates sideways, across the direction of travel, and "sideways" includes a whole fan of directions, vertical, horizontal, every angle between. You can pick one and block the rest.

NARRATOR: Now consider a longitudinal wave, like sound. In a longitudinal wave the oscillation is along the direction of travel, the medium compresses and rarefies back and forth in the same line the wave moves. There is no sideways. There's only the one direction, forwards and backwards along the beam, and a filter has nothing to choose between, nothing to restrict. A longitudinal wave cannot be polarised. It's not that we haven't found a way; it's that there's physically no transverse plane to filter. So the test is decisive: if a wave can be polarised, it must be transverse.

NARRATOR: Light can be polarised. We've just spent twenty minutes on exactly how. Therefore light is a transverse wave. That single observation rules out the longitudinal model completely. And here's the history that makes it a great exam line, because the syllabus loves naming the contest. Christiaan Huygens, the great wave theorist, the man whose wave model we'll defend in the next episode, actually got this wrong. Huygens proposed that light was a longitudinal wave, like sound. The discovery in the early nineteenth century that light can be polarised proved Huygens wrong on that specific point: light is a wave, yes, just as he said, but a transverse one, not a longitudinal one. Do not credit Huygens with the transverse model. He thought it was longitudinal. Polarisation corrected him.

NARRATOR: And this is exactly why polarisation is the third pillar of wave-model evidence, and why it's special. Diffraction proves light is a wave; particles can't bend around a corner. Interference proves light is a wave; particles can't cancel to make darkness. But both of those only prove "wave", they don't tell you transverse or longitudinal. Sound diffracts and sound interferes, and sound is longitudinal. So diffraction and interference can't settle the transverse question. Only polarisation can. Polarisation is the unique clincher for the transverse nature of light. That's the line that earns the top mark: diffraction and interference establish that light is a wave, but polarisation is the only one of the three that establishes light is specifically a transverse wave.
