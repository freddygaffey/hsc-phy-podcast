NARRATOR: Three episodes back, in Charged Particles in Electric Fields, the headline was this. A uniform electric field exerts a constant force on a charge — the charge q multiplied by the field strength E — and that force points along the field for a positive charge, and opposite to the field for a negative charge. Constant force means constant acceleration, and a charge fired sideways across the field traces a parabola, exactly like a projectile under gravity. We also did the energy bookkeeping: when a charge falls through a potential difference, the field does work on it equal to the charge times the voltage, and that work becomes kinetic energy. So the charge q times the voltage V equals one half m v squared. Hold onto that energy equation. It is the heart of the first half of today's lesson.

NARRATOR: Two episodes back, in Charged Particles in Magnetic Fields, the headline was almost the opposite. The magnetic force on a moving charge — the charge q, times its speed v, times the magnetic field B — is always perpendicular to the velocity. And because it's always perpendicular to the velocity, it does no work. The speed never changes. Only the direction changes. That gives you uniform circular motion, with a radius equal to m v over q B. Remember what each letter does in that radius equation: heavier particles, bigger m, curve in a wider circle; faster particles, bigger v, wider circle; stronger field, bigger B, tighter circle. That radius equation is the heart of the second half of today's lesson.

NARRATOR: And one episode back, in The Great Comparison, we put those two side by side and drilled the contrast for the extended-response marks. The electric field and the gravitational field both give a constant force and parabolic paths, and they can do work even on a particle that starts at rest. The magnetic force is the odd one out: it needs the charge to already be moving, it sits permanently at right angles to the motion, it does no work, and it bends things into circles. We practised structuring that answer with the word "whereas". Keep that contrast warm, because today you'll watch a single ion meet all three behaviours, one after another, inside one machine.

NARRATOR: Let's start with the simpler device: the electron gun. One field, one job. The function of an electron gun is to produce a narrow beam of electrons travelling at a known, controllable speed. That's it. Known and controllable — those two words are the whole point, and the electric field is what delivers them.

NARRATOR: It happens in stages. Stage one is getting the electrons loose in the first place. You take a metal filament — this is the cathode, the negative electrode — and you run a current through it until it glows red hot, just like the element in a toaster. Heat that metal enough and electrons near the surface gain enough thermal energy to escape the metal altogether. They literally boil off the surface. The proper term, the one the marker wants, is thermionic emission. "Thermionic" — thermal, because it's driven by heat; "emission" because electrons are emitted. Say "thermionic emission" in your answer, not "the electrons come off when it's hot".

NARRATOR: Stage two is the part that matters: the electric field accelerates them. A short distance away from the cathode sits a second plate, the anode — the positive electrode. You apply a direct-current voltage V between them. That sets up a uniform electric field in the gap between the plates, with strength equal to the voltage divided by the plate separation. Now, an electron sitting in that field feels a force equal to its charge times the field strength. Here's the bit students fumble: the electron is negative, so the force on it points opposite to the field lines. The field lines run from the positive anode to the negative cathode, but the force on the electron runs the other way — from the cathode toward the anode. So the electron is pulled across the gap from the negative plate toward the positive plate, speeding up the whole way. That's an accelerating force.

NARRATOR: And here is stage three, the core of the device — the energy conversion. As the field drags the electron across that potential difference, it does work on it. The work done equals the charge times the voltage. Where does that energy go? Entirely into kinetic energy, because the electron started essentially at rest and there's nothing in the vacuum to slow it down. So electric potential energy converts into kinetic energy, and we can write the energy balance: the charge q times the voltage V equals one half m v squared. Rearrange that for the speed and you get: v equals the square root of two q V over m.

NARRATOR: Look at what that gives you. The speed depends only on the charge, the mass, and the voltage you chose. The charge and mass of the electron are fixed constants of nature. So the only thing you control is V, the accelerating voltage — and by choosing V, you choose the exit speed. Turn up the voltage, the electrons come out faster. That is the function of the electron gun stated as physics: the electric field does work q V, which becomes kinetic energy, giving electrons of a known, tunable speed. The anode has a small hole drilled in line with the filament, so the accelerated electrons shoot straight through it as a narrow beam, ready for whatever comes next.

NARRATOR: Now notice what is not here. There is no magnetic field doing the accelerating. Only an electric field can speed a particle up, because only an electric field does work on it. This is worth saying twice because it's the single most-penalised error in this whole topic, and we'll meet it again. A magnetic field can steer or focus a beam later on, but it can never be the thing that gives the electrons their speed. In the basic electron gun, acceleration is purely the electric field's job.

NARRATOR: Second, the exit speed. All of that energy is kinetic, so one half m v squared equals one point six times ten to the minus seventeen joules. So when that electron enters the magnetic field, it bends, but it keeps moving at five point nine times ten to the six metres per second. That's the no-work insight from two episodes ago, and it's load-bearing for everything that follows. If you got that, you've got the conceptual core of the module.

The electron gun is the bridge between Module 6 and Module 8. Markers love a student who sees that.

NARRATOR: So now the main event: the mass spectrometer. Its function is to separate ions by mass — to measure the mass-to-charge ratio of particles and, crucially, to measure the relative abundance of different isotopes of an element. You feed in a sample, and out the far end you get a readout that says, in effect, "this much neon-twenty, this much neon-twenty-two." It's how we know isotopic abundances in the first place. And it does this in three stages, in a strict order, each stage built from physics you already own.

NARRATOR: Here's your memory hook for the three stages, and we'll reuse it every time. The mnemonic is A-S-D — Accelerate, Select, Deflect. Picture pressing those three keys in order on a keyboard, A then S then D, your fingers walking left to right, the same direction the ion travels through the machine. Accelerate, Select, Deflect. Say it now. A-S-D. If you can dump those three letters on your exam paper and then write a force statement under each, you've structured the whole extended response before you've thought about anything.

NARRATOR: Stage A — Accelerate. This is the electron gun all over again, just applied to ions instead of electrons. The sample is vaporised by a heater and then ionised — typically a beam of electrons is fired at it to knock electrons off the atoms, leaving positive ions, each with a charge q. Those ions are then accelerated by an electric field through a potential difference V, exactly the physics from the front of this episode. The field does work q V on each ion, which becomes kinetic energy, so q V equals one half m v squared. This launches the ions into the next region moving fast. Effect of the electric field here: it accelerates — it adds kinetic energy and gives the ions speed. Same equation, same story as the gun.

NARRATOR: Stage S — Select. This is the velocity selector, and it's the elegant heart of the machine. The fast ions enter a region with two fields at once, crossed at right angles to each other: an electric field and a magnetic field. Now think about the two forces on a moving ion. The electric force is the charge times the electric field — and notice it does not depend on how fast the ion is going. It's the same size whatever the speed. The magnetic force is the charge times the speed times the magnetic field — and that one does depend on speed. The faster the ion, the bigger the magnetic force. The two fields are arranged so these two forces point in opposite directions, fighting each other.

NARRATOR: Now here's the trick. For an ion travelling at exactly the right speed, the electric force and the magnetic force are equal and opposite. They cancel. Net force zero. That ion sails straight through the exit slit, undeflected. But an ion going faster has a bigger magnetic force than electric force, so it gets pushed off to one side and hits the wall. An ion going slower has a bigger electric force, so it gets pushed off the other way and is blocked. Only one speed survives. Set the magnetic force equal to the electric force — the charge cancels from both sides — and you find the pass-through speed equals the electric field divided by the magnetic field. v equals E over B. The full balance is in Listing 3.

QUESTION: But hang on — if it sorts by speed, isn't it sorting by mass too? Doesn't that already separate the isotopes?

NARRATOR: No — and this is a classic trap, so let me kill it now. The velocity selector selects by speed, full stop. Not by mass. Look at the pass condition: speed equals E over B. The mass doesn't appear in it at all, and neither does the charge. A heavy ion and a light ion, if they're both moving at E over B, both pass straight through. So the selector doesn't separate your isotopes. What it does is guarantee that every single ion entering the next stage has the same, identical, known speed. And that's the whole reason it exists: it removes speed as a variable. Once every ion is going at the same speed, any difference in how they curve later must be due to mass alone — not to one ion being faster than another. The selector sets up the fair test. The mass separation happens next.

NARRATOR: There's a deeper law underneath the velocity selector worth naming for the top marks. When a charge sits in both an electric and a magnetic field at once, the total force on it is described by the Lorentz force law: the electric part, charge times electric field, plus the magnetic part, charge times velocity crossed with the magnetic field. The velocity selector is just that law with the two parts set equal and opposite so they cancel. You don't need to manipulate the cross product in the HSC, but if you can name the Lorentz force law as the underlying physics, that's the kind of precision that separates a top-band answer.

NARRATOR: Stage D — Deflect. Now the ions are mono-speed, all travelling at E over B, and they enter a final region: a uniform magnetic field, on its own this time, pointing perpendicular to their velocity. And you already know exactly what happens, because we did it two episodes ago and we just did it with the electron. The magnetic force is perpendicular to the velocity, does no work, keeps the speed constant, and bends each ion into a circular arc — usually a semicircle that carries it round to a detector. The magnetic force is supplying the centripetal force for that circle. Set the magnetic force equal to the centripetal force requirement — charge times speed times field equals mass times speed squared over radius — cancel one speed, rearrange, and you get the master equation of the whole machine: the radius equals m v over q B.

NARRATOR: Now look at that radius equation with the velocity selector in mind, because this is where it all pays off. Every ion has the same speed v — the selector guaranteed it. Every ion has the same charge q — they're all singly ionised. The field B is the same for all of them. So in r equals m v over q B, the only thing that varies from ion to ion is the mass m. The radius is directly proportional to the mass. A heavier isotope, bigger m, swings round on a bigger circle and lands further out on the detector. A lighter isotope, smaller m, curls tighter and lands closer in. Different masses, different radii, different landing positions. The position on the detector tells you the mass. And the number of hits stacking up at each position tells you the relative abundance of each isotope. That's the readout.

NARRATOR: So there's your three-stage story in force language: the electric field accelerates and gives kinetic energy, the crossed fields balance to fix a single speed, and the magnetic field alone provides the centripetal force that sorts by mass. Accelerate, Select, Deflect. A-S-D. Electric, both, magnetic.

That's two, times one point six times ten to the minus nineteen, times one thousand, divided by three point three two times ten to the minus twenty-six. Square root that and you get about nine point eight times ten to the four metres per second. Slower than the electron — about a hundred thousand metres per second — because a neon ion is tens of thousands of times heavier.

NARRATOR: Stage D, the radius for neon-twenty. NARRATOR: And there's your separation. Neon-twenty lands at four point one centimetres, neon-twenty-two lands about four millimetres further out at four point five centimetres. Same charge, same speed, same field — the radius separated them purely by mass. The detector position identifies which isotope it is, and the relative number of hits at each spot gives you their abundance in the sample. That's a mass spectrometer working, narrated from first principles. One caveat to keep your reasoning honest, and it's in Listing 4: because we're using a velocity selector, every ion exits the selector at the same speed E over B regardless of mass, so the radius is proportional to mass. If a machine had no velocity selector and only accelerated the ions through the same voltage, then the lighter ions would come out faster and the radius would instead be proportional to the square root of mass over charge. The standard HSC velocity-selector spectrometer is the first case: radius proportional to mass. Decide which model the question describes and stay consistent.

NARRATOR: The weak answer, the band-four version, says something like: "The ions enter the machine and are deflected by a magnetic field, and heavier ones are deflected differently, so they get separated." NARRATOR: Here's the strong answer, structured on A-S-D, three stages, a force statement in each. "First, the ions are ionised and accelerated through a potential difference by an electric field. The field does work equal to the charge times the voltage on each ion, converting electric potential energy to kinetic energy, so they gain a high speed. Second, the ions pass through a velocity selector — a region of crossed electric and magnetic fields. The electric force is independent of speed, whereas the magnetic force depends on speed, and the two are arranged to oppose. Only ions whose speed equals the electric field divided by the magnetic field experience zero net force and pass straight through; all others are deflected and blocked. This ensures every ion entering the next stage has the same speed. Third, these single-speed ions enter a uniform magnetic field, where the magnetic force acts perpendicular to their velocity and provides the centripetal force for circular motion, giving a radius equal to mass times speed over charge times field. Because all ions have the same charge, speed, and field, the radius depends only on mass: heavier isotopes follow a larger-radius path and strike the detector further out, separating the isotopes by mass. The position gives the mass and the number of hits gives the relative abundance." That's a full-mark answer. Notice it's the ASD story, with a cause-and-effect force statement at every stage, and it uses "whereas" to draw the electric-versus-magnetic contrast — markers reward that contrast language explicitly.

NARRATOR: Let me drill the traps, because each one is a specific way students throw marks away on this topic. There are a few and they're worth naming.

NARRATOR: Trap one, the big one: do not say the magnetic field speeds the ions up. It cannot. The magnetic force is perpendicular to velocity, does zero work, and changes only direction, never speed. Only the electric field changes speed. If you write "the magnetic field accelerates the ions to high speed", you've made the single most-penalised error in the module. The magnetic field bends; the electric field speeds up.

NARRATOR: Trap two: don't label instead of explain. "Ions are deflected by a magnetic field" is a label. "The magnetic force provides the centripetal force, so the radius equals m v over q B, and heavier ions have a larger radius" is an explanation. The verb is "outline the functions and effects" — give the force, give the consequence.

NARRATOR: Trap three: watch the sign of the charge when you state a direction. The right-hand rules give you the direction of the force on a positive charge. For an electron, or any negative charge, the force points the opposite way — so either flip it or use your left hand, and always state the sign of the charge explicitly in your answer. And a related point markers care about: a hand rule is a memory aid, not a law. If a question asks why the force points a certain way, cite the force interaction — the Lorentz force, the charge moving through the field — not "because of the right-hand rule". The rule finds the direction; the law explains it.

NARRATOR: Trap four: the velocity selector selects by speed, not by mass — we covered it, but it's worth the repetition because it's the one students get backwards. Mass separation happens in the deflection chamber, not the selector.

NARRATOR: Trap five, the quiet one: units. The field B must be in tesla, so convert millitesla to tesla. Distances in metres, so convert millimetres and centimetres. Voltage in volts. A mismatched unit in r equals m v over q B gives a wrong answer with no warning — it's a silent killer in calculations. And one assumption to state out loud in energy questions: all the work q V becomes kinetic energy only if the particle starts from rest and there are no losses, which holds in the vacuum of these machines. Say "starting from rest" when you write the energy balance.
