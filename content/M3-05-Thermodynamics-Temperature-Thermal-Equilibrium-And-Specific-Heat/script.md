NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Kinetic energy is the energy a body has because of its motion, equal to one half its mass times its speed squared. And the great rule is the conservation of energy: energy is transformed from one form to another but is never created or destroyed, the total stays constant. Kinetic energy is the energy of motion, and energy is always conserved. Hold both, because temperature is going to turn out to be about the kinetic energy of particles, and heating is going to be a transfer of energy that's carefully conserved. Let's build the particle picture.

NARRATOR: Everything is made of particles, atoms and molecules, and those particles are always moving, that's the kinetic particle model of matter. In a solid they vibrate about fixed positions; in a liquid they move around while staying close; in a gas they fly about freely at high speed. Because they're moving, they have kinetic energy. Now here's the central definition of the whole topic: the temperature of a substance is a measure of the average kinetic energy of its particles. When a substance is hotter, its particles are moving faster on average, more kinetic energy each; when it's colder, they move more slowly. So temperature is not a substance and not an amount of energy, it's an average, the average kinetic energy per particle. That definition, temperature equals average particle kinetic energy, is the most important sentence in the episode, and it's in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now the distinction that earns the most marks in this topic, and the one students most often blur: the difference between temperature and heat. Temperature, we just said, is the average kinetic energy of the particles. Heat is different. Heat, also called thermal energy transfer, is energy that flows from one object to another because of a temperature difference between them. The crucial subtlety: an object does not contain heat. It contains internal energy, the total kinetic and potential energy of all its particles. Heat is the name we give to internal energy while it is in transit, flowing from the hotter object to the colder one. So heat is energy on the move, driven by a temperature difference, whereas temperature is a property an object has at an instant, its average particle kinetic energy. Don't say "this object has a lot of heat"; say it has a lot of internal energy, or it's at a high temperature. The heat-versus-temperature distinction is in Listing one, and a clear statement of it is worth easy marks.

NARRATOR: A consequence worth pausing on: temperature and internal energy are not the same, and a small hot thing can carry less energy than a large warm thing. A spark from a sparkler is at a far higher temperature than a bath of warm water, its particles move faster, but the spark has tiny mass and very few particles, so its total internal energy is minuscule, which is why it doesn't burn you, while the bath, at a much lower temperature, holds enormously more internal energy because it has so many more particles. Temperature is the average energy per particle; internal energy is the total over all the particles, which depends on how many there are. So a high temperature does not automatically mean a lot of energy. That separation between average and total is exactly why we need both ideas, and it's in Listing one.

NARRATOR: One refinement of the particle picture, because the word "average" in the definition of temperature is doing real work, and it returns next year. At any instant the particles of a substance are not all moving at the same speed; there's a whole spread, a distribution, some moving slowly, some very fast, most somewhere in the middle. Temperature measures the average of their kinetic energies, but the spread around that average is always there. When you heat a substance, the whole distribution shifts to higher speeds and the average rises, but there are still slow particles and fast ones. This matters: it's why some water can evaporate from a puddle that's well below boiling, the fastest few particles at the high end of the distribution escape even though the average isn't high enough to boil. And it's the direct foundation of black-body radiation in Module Seven, where the distribution of particle kinetic energies sets the spectrum of light a hot object emits. So keep the picture sharp: temperature is the average of a distribution of particle energies, not a single shared speed.

QUESTION: A retrieval one worth stopping for. In one sentence each, define temperature and define heat, making the difference clear. Pause the player, write both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. Heat is energy that is transferred from a hotter object to a colder object because of the temperature difference between them, energy in transit. The difference is that temperature is a property the object has, its average particle kinetic energy, while heat is energy flowing because of a temperature difference, not something the object contains. If your answer used "heat" and "temperature" as if they meant the same thing, that's the classic error, fix it: temperature is average particle energy, heat is transferred energy.

NARRATOR: Now, which way does heat flow, and when does it stop? Heat always flows spontaneously from the hotter object to the colder object, never the other way on its own. Put a hot cup of coffee in a cool room and energy flows from the coffee to the room until they're at the same temperature; the coffee never spontaneously gets hotter by drawing energy from the cooler room. When two objects in contact reach the same temperature, there is no longer any net flow of energy between them, and we say they have reached thermal equilibrium. At thermal equilibrium the average particle kinetic energies are equal, so neither object gains energy at the other's expense; energy may still pass both ways at the particle level, but the flows balance, so there's no net transfer. This is the basis of how a thermometer works: you let it come to thermal equilibrium with whatever you're measuring, and then its temperature equals the object's. There's a quiet but important principle hidden here, sometimes called the zeroth law of thermodynamics: if object A is in thermal equilibrium with object B, and B is in equilibrium with C, then A and C are at the same temperature too. That's what lets a thermometer be meaningful at all, the thermometer agrees with the object, so any two objects that agree with the thermometer must agree with each other. It sounds obvious, but it's the logical foundation that makes "temperature" a consistent, measurable quantity. Thermal equilibrium, equal temperatures and no net heat flow, is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "explain, in terms of energy, what happens when a hot object and a cold object are placed in contact, until they reach thermal equilibrium". The band-six version: the particles of the hot object have a higher average kinetic energy than those of the cold object. Where the objects touch, faster particles collide with slower ones and transfer kinetic energy to them, so energy flows as heat from the hot object to the cold object. This continues, the hot object cooling and the cold object warming, until the average kinetic energies of the particles in both objects are equal, that is, until they are at the same temperature. At that point there is no net transfer of energy, and the objects are in thermal equilibrium. The marks are for: heat flowing hot to cold via particle collisions, the transfer continuing until equal temperatures, and equilibrium meaning equal average kinetic energy with no net flow. The weak answer, "the hot one cools the cold one until they're the same", earns less because it never mentions particle kinetic energy or the direction and stopping condition in energy terms.

NARRATOR: Now the temperature scales, because thermodynamics needs the right one. The everyday Celsius scale sets zero at the freezing point of water and one hundred at its boiling point, useful, but its zero is arbitrary, just a convenient point, not the true bottom of temperature. Physics uses the Kelvin scale, the absolute scale, whose zero, called absolute zero, is the coldest possible temperature, the point at which particle motion is at its absolute minimum and you can extract no more kinetic energy. Absolute zero is about minus two hundred and seventy-three degrees Celsius, which is zero kelvin. The degrees are the same size on both scales, a change of one kelvin equals a change of one degree Celsius, so to convert you just add or subtract two hundred and seventy-three: kelvin equals Celsius plus two hundred and seventy-three. So water freezes at two hundred and seventy-three kelvin and boils at three hundred and seventy-three kelvin. The reason physics prefers Kelvin is that temperature in kelvin is directly proportional to the average kinetic energy of the particles, double the kelvin temperature and you double the average particle kinetic energy, which is not true of Celsius because its zero is in the wrong place. The scales and the conversion are in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Now the big quantitative tool of the episode: specific heat capacity. Experience tells you different substances need different amounts of energy to heat up: a metal spoon warms almost instantly in hot water while the water itself takes ages to boil. The specific heat capacity of a substance, given the symbol c, is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of one kilogram of it by one kelvin, or equivalently by one degree Celsius, since the degree sizes match. It's measured in joules per kilogram per kelvin. A high specific heat means a substance needs a lot of energy to change its temperature; a low specific heat means it heats up and cools down easily. The relationship that ties it all together is: the heat energy transferred equals the mass times the specific heat capacity times the change in temperature. In symbols, the change in heat Q equals m c delta T, where delta T is the temperature change. That equation is the workhorse of every thermal calculation in this topic, and it's in Listing three with worked examples.

NARRATOR: Water deserves a special mention because its specific heat capacity is unusually high, about four thousand two hundred joules per kilogram per kelvin, far higher than most materials, metals are typically only a few hundred. This single fact has huge consequences. It's why water is used as a coolant in car engines and power stations, it can soak up a lot of energy with only a modest temperature rise. It's why the ocean moderates climate: coastal areas have milder temperatures than inland deserts because the sea absorbs and releases vast amounts of energy with little temperature change, smoothing out the swings. And it's why it takes a long time to boil a pot of water but the metal pot heats up almost instantly. Whenever you see water behaving as a thermal buffer, its high specific heat is the reason. It even shapes biology, your body is mostly water, so it can absorb the heat generated by exercise or a fever with only a small rise in core temperature, a built-in thermal stabiliser. That high value, and these consequences, are noted in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. The heat energy is mass times specific heat capacity times temperature change, Q equals m c delta T. Substitute: two kilograms times four thousand two hundred joules per kilogram per kelvin times thirty kelvin. Two times four thousand two hundred is eight thousand four hundred, times thirty is two hundred and fifty-two thousand joules, that's two hundred and fifty-two kilojoules. So you need about two hundred and fifty-two thousand joules. The method is always the same: identify the mass, the specific heat, and the temperature change, multiply the three together. And note delta T is the change, the rise of thirty kelvin, not an absolute temperature, that's a common slip.

NARRATOR: One more use of the equation, worth a mark: it works in reverse and it's the basis of mixing problems. If you know how much energy was transferred and the mass and specific heat, you can find the temperature change, delta T equals Q over m c. And when a hot object is placed in a cold liquid, energy is conserved: the heat lost by the hot object equals the heat gained by the cold one, assuming none escapes to the surroundings. Setting the heat lost equal to the heat gained, each written as m c delta T for its own substance, lets you solve for a final equilibrium temperature. That's just conservation of energy from Module Two applied to heat, the energy doesn't vanish, it moves from the hot body to the cold one until they reach thermal equilibrium. This lost-equals-gained method is in Listing three. Let me show the reverse calculation quickly, because it's just as examinable as the forward one. Suppose you transfer sixty-three thousand joules of energy into a half-kilogram block of copper, specific heat about three hundred and ninety joules per kilogram per kelvin, and you want the temperature rise. Rearrange to delta T equals Q over m c, sixty-three thousand divided by the product of zero point five and three hundred and ninety, that's sixty-three thousand over one hundred and ninety-five, about three hundred and twenty-three kelvin. Notice how large that rise is, the same energy that warmed two kilograms of water by just thirty kelvin earlier sends a small block of copper soaring, precisely because copper's specific heat is roughly ten times smaller than water's. The single equation runs in every direction; you just isolate whichever quantity you're missing.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can measure a substance's specific heat capacity with a calorimeter: supply a measured amount of electrical energy to a known mass with an immersion heater, measure the temperature rise, and rearrange Q equals m c delta T to find c. You can demonstrate thermal equilibrium by placing objects at different temperatures in contact and recording how their temperatures converge to a common value. And you can show water's high specific heat by heating equal masses of water and of a metal, or of cooking oil, with the same heater and seeing the water's temperature rise far more slowly. A key point for the calorimeter experiment, examinable, is that some energy is always lost to the surroundings, so measured values are slightly off, and good method, insulation and lids, reduces that error. Describing such an investigation and its main source of error is examinable.
