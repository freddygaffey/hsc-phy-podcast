# Supplementary Materials

These materials are NOT spoken in the episode. They collect the key equations, numbers, and a worked example that the narration refers to in plain English.

### Listing 1 — Binding energy per nucleon (the master concept)

$$
\text{binding energy per nucleon} = \frac{\text{total binding energy}}{\text{number of nucleons}}
$$

$$
\text{total binding energy} = \text{mass defect} \times c^{2}
$$

$$
\text{mass defect} = (\text{sum of masses of separate protons and neutrons}) - (\text{mass of nucleus})
$$

Exam shortcut (from teacher slides / NESA data sheet): \(1\ \mathrm{u} = 931.5\ \mathrm{MeV}/c^{2}\), so

$$
\text{binding energy (MeV)} = \text{mass defect (in u)} \times 931.5
$$

(do NOT also divide by \(c^{2}\) in SI — the \(931.5\) already carries the \(/c^{2}\) unit).

Key feature of the curve \(B/A\) versus mass number \(A\): it rises steeply for light nuclei, PEAKS near iron-56 / nickel-62, and falls slowly for heavy nuclei.

- Iron-56: \(B/A = 8.791\ \mathrm{MeV}\) per nucleon (conventional "peak")
- Nickel-62: \(B/A = 8.795\ \mathrm{MeV}\) per nucleon (true maximum, technicality)

Consequence:

- fusion of nuclei LIGHTER than iron \(\to\) product higher on curve \(\to\) EXOTHERMIC (releases energy)
- fusion of nuclei HEAVIER than iron \(\to\) product lower on curve \(\to\) ENDOTHERMIC (absorbs energy)

This is why stellar fusion stops at iron and why a catastrophe is required to build everything heavier.

### Listing 2 — The Chandrasekhar limit and degeneracy pressure

Electron degeneracy pressure arises from the Pauli exclusion principle (no two electrons in the same quantum state).

- Non-relativistic electrons: \(P \propto \text{density}^{5/3}\)
- Relativistic electrons: \(P \propto \text{density}^{4/3}\) — softer, cannot resist gravity

Chandrasekhar limit (maximum mass supportable by electron degeneracy pressure):

$$
M_{\text{Ch}} \approx 1.44\ \text{solar masses} \quad (\text{commonly quoted as } \approx 1.4)
$$

Above \(M_{\text{Ch}}\) the core cannot be held up and MUST collapse.

Derived by S. Chandrasekhar, 1930-1935. Nobel Prize in Physics 1983.

### Listing 3 — The core-collapse sequence (timeline and numbers)

1. Silicon shell burning dumps iron onto the core (final \(\approx 1\) day of silicon burning).
2. Iron core mass crosses \(\approx 1.44\) solar masses (Chandrasekhar limit).
3. Electron capture (neutronisation): \(e^{-} + p \to n + \text{neutrino}\) — removes the electrons providing degeneracy pressure.
4. Photodisintegration: \(\gamma + {}^{56}\mathrm{Fe} \to 13\,{}^{4}\mathrm{He} + 4n\) (absorbs \(\approx 124\ \mathrm{MeV}\) per iron nucleus) — removes thermal support; both effects accelerate collapse.
5. Collapse speed of outer core: up to \(70{,}000\ \mathrm{km/s}\) (\(\approx 23\%\) of \(c\)).
6. Core shrinks: \(\approx 8{,}000\ \mathrm{km}\) diameter \(\to \approx 19\ \mathrm{km}\) diameter in \(< 1\) second.
7. Reaches nuclear saturation density \(\approx 2.4\times10^{14}\ \mathrm{g/cm^{3}}\); temperature \(\sim 10^{11}\ \mathrm{K}\).
8. Strong force turns repulsive; equation of state stiffens; core BOUNCES.
9. Shock wave launched outward, then STALLS at \(\approx 100\text{-}200\ \mathrm{km}\).
10. Neutrino heating (\(\approx 1\%\) of neutrino flux absorbed) REVIVES the shock \(\to\) explosion.

Energy budget:

- Total released (gravitational): \(\sim 10^{46}\ \mathrm{J}\) (more than the Sun's whole-life output)
- Carried by neutrinos: \(\approx 99\%\)
- Kinetic energy of ejecta: \(\sim 10^{44}\ \mathrm{J}\) (\(\approx 1\%\))
- Radiated as visible light: \(\sim 10^{43}\ \mathrm{J}\) (\(\approx 0.01\%\))

Remnant: neutron star (core \(< \approx 3\) solar masses) or black hole (core \(> \approx 3\) solar masses). NOTE: the iron core IMPLODES; the visible supernova is the outer envelope being blasted off.

Progenitor mass range (Jacaranda Ch12): stars of \(\approx 8\) to \(50\) solar masses end as core-collapse (Type II) supernovae. Lifetimes of millions of years (vs billions for a Sun-like star). A neutron star has the density of an atomic nucleus and a diameter of little more than \(\approx 20\ \mathrm{km}\), with a mass greater than the Sun's.

Type II vs Type I (spectral distinction, Jacaranda Ch12):

- Type II: hydrogen lines PRESENT \(\to\) core collapse of a massive star (this episode)
- Type I: hydrogen lines ABSENT \(\to\) e.g. white dwarf exceeding \(\approx 1.4\) solar masses in a binary (accretion of hydrogen, runaway fusion)

### Listing 4 — The r-process (rapid neutron capture)

Required conditions:

- free neutron density: \(\sim 10^{24}\ \text{neutrons/cm}^{3}\) (\(\approx 1\) gram of free neutrons per cm³)
- temperature: \(\sim 10^{9}\ \mathrm{K}\) (1 gigakelvin)
- timescale: seconds (free neutrons decay in \(\approx 10\) minutes)

Mechanism (two competing clocks):

- neutron capture: \((Z, A) + n \to (Z, A+1)\) — no Coulomb barrier: neutron is neutral
- beta decay: \(n \to p + e^{-} + \text{antineutrino}\) — raises \(Z\) by 1: climbs the periodic table

RAPID process: capture is much faster than beta decay, so nuclei become extremely neutron-rich before decaying, then cascade up to thorium and uranium.

SLOW process (s-process, for contrast): capture much slower than beta decay; stays near the valley of stability; tops out near lead/bismuth; occurs in aging AGB stars.

Outcomes: the r-process makes \(\approx\) half of all nuclei heavier than iron, and essentially ALL of the heaviest (thorium, uranium, and beyond). Abundance peaks at mass numbers \(A \approx 80, 130, 195\) (nuclei stalling at the neutron magic numbers \(N = 50, 82, 126\)).

Confirmed sites: neutron star mergers (dominant for heaviest elements) and rare supernovae.

### Listing 6 — Worked example: energy released forming a helium-4 nucleus

Question: How much energy is released when two protons and two neutrons fuse into one helium-4 nucleus? (Illustrates WHY fusion below iron is exothermic.)

Data (atomic mass unit \(\mathrm{u} = 1.6605\times10^{-27}\ \mathrm{kg}\); \(c = 3.0\times10^{8}\ \mathrm{m/s}\)):

- mass of proton \(= 1.00728\ \mathrm{u}\)
- mass of neutron \(= 1.00867\ \mathrm{u}\)
- mass of He-4 nucleus \(= 4.00151\ \mathrm{u}\)

Step 1 — mass of separate nucleons:

$$
\begin{aligned}
2\ \text{protons} &= 2\times1.00728 = 2.01456\ \mathrm{u} \\
2\ \text{neutrons} &= 2\times1.00867 = 2.01734\ \mathrm{u} \\
\text{total} &= 4.03190\ \mathrm{u}
\end{aligned}
$$

Step 2 — mass defect:

$$
\text{mass defect} = 4.03190 - 4.00151 = 0.03039\ \mathrm{u}
$$

Step 3 — convert to kilograms:

$$
0.03039\ \mathrm{u}\times1.6605\times10^{-27}\ \mathrm{kg/u} = 5.046\times10^{-29}\ \mathrm{kg}
$$

Step 4 — energy released (\(E = mc^{2}\)):

$$
E = (5.046\times10^{-29})(3.0\times10^{8})^{2} = 4.54\times10^{-12}\ \mathrm{J} = 28.3\ \mathrm{MeV}
$$

(about \(7.1\ \mathrm{MeV}\) per nucleon).

Faster route using the \(931.5\ \mathrm{MeV}/c^{2}\) shortcut (matches teacher slides exactly):

$$
E = \text{mass defect (u)}\times931.5 = 0.030376\times931.5 = 28.30\ \mathrm{MeV\ total}
$$

$$
\frac{28.30}{4\ \text{nucleons}} = 7.07\ \mathrm{MeV}\ \text{per nucleon}
$$

(Compare with iron-56 at \(8.79\ \mathrm{MeV}\) per nucleon: He-4 is still climbing toward the iron peak, which is exactly why further fusion below iron keeps releasing energy.)

Interpretation: energy is RELEASED because He-4 sits higher on the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve than free nucleons. The same logic, run in reverse, shows that fusing iron into heavier nuclei would REQUIRE energy input — which is why no star can fuse past iron, and why the heavy elements need a supernova or neutron star merger.
