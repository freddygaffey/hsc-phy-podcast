NARRATOR: Look at the ring on your finger. The gold in it is older than the Sun. It is older than the Earth, older than the solar system itself. And it was not made gently. Every single atom of gold that has ever existed was forged in a handful of seconds, inside a catastrophe so violent that for a few weeks it could outshine an entire galaxy of a hundred billion stars. The same is true of the silver in a coin, the platinum in a catalytic converter, the uranium in a reactor, the iodine in your thyroid. None of it was made by a star quietly burning. All of it was made by a star dying. And the strange, almost unbelievable truth of modern physics is this. There is a hard ceiling on what fusion inside a star can build. That ceiling is iron. To make anything heavier than iron, the universe has to break the star.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how that happens. It is a story that runs from a young Indian physicist doing arithmetic on a steamship in nineteen thirty, to a research assistant in the Chilean Andes developing a photographic plate by hand in nineteen eighty-seven, to a gravitational wave that rippled across the Earth on a single afternoon in twenty seventeen and finally told us where the gold comes from. Let us start at the ceiling. Let us start at iron.

QUESTION: Why iron? Why should fusion stop there, of all the elements?

NARRATOR: To answer that, we need one idea, and it is the central idea of this whole episode. It is called binding energy per nucleon. A nucleus is made of protons and neutrons, and we call those, collectively, nucleons. The binding energy is the energy that holds those nucleons together. If you wanted to pull a nucleus completely apart, into separate free protons and neutrons, the binding energy is the energy you would have to put in to do it. Now divide that total binding energy by the number of nucleons in the nucleus. That gives you the binding energy per nucleon. And you can think of it as a measure of how tightly bound, how stable, how comfortable each particle in the nucleus is. The higher the binding energy per nucleon, the more stable the nucleus, and the lower its energy.

NARRATOR: Here is the crucial fact. If you plot binding energy per nucleon against the mass of the nucleus, you do not get a straight line. You get a curve that rises steeply for the light elements, climbs up through carbon and oxygen and silicon, reaches a peak, and then slowly falls away again for the very heavy elements. And the peak of that curve, the most tightly bound, most stable place to be in the entire periodic table, sits right around iron. Iron-fifty-six has a binding energy per nucleon of about eight point seven nine million electron volts. It is, to a very good approximation, the bottom of the well. The most stable matter in the universe.

QUESTION: And what does sitting at the bottom of the well have to do with fusion releasing or absorbing energy?

NARRATOR: Everything. When you fuse two light nuclei together, say two hydrogen nuclei, the product is more tightly bound than the things you started with. It has climbed up the curve toward the peak. And because it is more tightly bound, it sits at a lower energy, and that leftover energy comes out, as heat, as light, as the radiation that makes a star shine. Fusion of light elements releases energy. That is why the Sun burns. But once you reach iron, you are at the top of the curve. There is nowhere higher to climb. If you try to fuse iron into something heavier, the product is on the downhill side of the curve. It is less tightly bound, it sits at a higher energy, and so the reaction does not release energy. It consumes it. Fusing anything heavier than iron is endothermic. It takes energy in rather than giving it out.

NARRATOR: Think about what that means for a massive star. We are talking here about a genuine heavyweight, a star born with more than about eight times the mass of the Sun, the kind that swells in its old age into a red supergiant. For most of its life, a big star is a battle. Gravity is forever trying to crush it inward, and the outward pressure created by the energy pouring out of fusion in the core pushes back and holds it up. The whole story of how a star builds heavier and heavier elements is the story of that balance shifting. As long as fusion releases energy, the star has a defence against gravity. The star burns hydrogen into helium for millions of years. Then, when the hydrogen core runs out, gravity wins for a moment, the core contracts, heats up, and the rising temperature ignites the next fuel, fusing helium into carbon and oxygen. Then carbon into neon and magnesium. Each time a fuel runs out, gravity squeezes the core hotter, and a heavier element catches fire. Each stage is hotter, faster, and more desperate than the last. Carbon burning in a massive star lasts maybe a thousand years. Neon burning lasts about a year. Oxygen burning, a few months. And silicon burning, the very last stage, the stage that finally produces iron, lasts roughly one single day. So a red supergiant ends up layered like an onion, shells of progressively heavier elements wrapped around an iron heart, and iron is exactly where the building has to stop, because iron sits at the peak of the binding energy curve.

QUESTION: One day. So when the core finally becomes iron, what happens?

NARRATOR: This is the moment the whole episode turns on. When the core of a massive star becomes iron, fusion in that core produces no more energy. The furnace goes out. The outward push that has held the star up for millions of years vanishes, in the centre, almost instantly. And gravity, which never rests, finally has nothing standing in its way. But before we watch that collapse, we need to understand the one thing that was holding the iron core up in those final moments, because the failure of that one thing is what triggers the most violent event in the universe. And to understand it, we have to go back to a steamship, and to a nineteen-year-old.

NARRATOR: The year is nineteen thirty. A young man named Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar boards a ship in India, bound for England, where he is to study physics at Cambridge. He is nineteen years old. The voyage takes about eighteen days, and to fill the time, he does some calculations. He is thinking about white dwarf stars. A white dwarf is what is left when a star like our Sun dies. It has stopped fusing. It is just a dense, hot cinder, slowly cooling. And the question is, what holds a white dwarf up? It is not fusion anymore. Fusion has stopped.

QUESTION: If fusion has stopped, what is left to fight gravity?

NARRATOR: The answer comes from quantum mechanics, from a rule discovered by Wolfgang Pauli, called the Pauli exclusion principle. The exclusion principle says that two electrons can never occupy the same quantum state. They cannot be squeezed into exactly the same condition of position and motion. Now, in a white dwarf, the matter is crushed so tightly that the electrons are packed almost shoulder to shoulder. And because no two of them can share a state, when you try to compress them further, they are forced into higher and higher states of motion. They push back. This pushback is a pressure, and it has nothing to do with heat. It is purely a quantum effect, born of the exclusion principle. We call it electron degeneracy pressure. And it is what holds a white dwarf up against gravity, even when the star is stone cold.

NARRATOR: Here is what Chandrasekhar worked out on that ship. Electron degeneracy pressure is strong, but it is not unlimited. As you pile more mass onto the star, you compress it more, and the electrons are forced to move faster and faster, until they approach the speed of light. And once the electrons become relativistic, once they are moving close to light speed, the way their pressure grows with density changes. It softens. It can no longer keep pace with the relentless squeeze of gravity. And Chandrasekhar found that there is a maximum mass. Above a certain mass, electron degeneracy pressure simply cannot win, no matter how dense the star becomes. The star must collapse. That maximum is now called the Chandrasekhar limit, and its value is about one point four times the mass of our Sun. One point four four, to be more precise.

NARRATOR: This was a stunning result. It said that there is a fundamental ceiling, set by quantum mechanics and relativity together, on how massive a self-supporting dead star can be. Go over it, and there is nothing to stop total collapse. But when Chandrasekhar presented this work in England, he ran into a wall, and the wall had a name. Arthur Eddington. Eddington was the most powerful and famous astrophysicist in the English-speaking world. He had measured the bending of starlight that confirmed Einstein. And in nineteen thirty-five, at a meeting of the Royal Astronomical Society, with the young Chandrasekhar sitting right there, Eddington stood up and ridiculed the idea. He said, and I am quoting him, I think there should be a law of Nature to prevent a star from behaving in this absurd way.

QUESTION: Was Eddington right? Was there such a law?

NARRATOR: There was not. Chandrasekhar was right, and Eddington was wrong. But Eddington's prestige was so enormous that the result was held in doubt for years. Niels Bohr and Wolfgang Pauli privately told Chandrasekhar his work was sound, but few would say so publicly against Eddington. Chandrasekhar eventually left Cambridge, and built the rest of his great career in the United States, at the University of Chicago. He received the Nobel Prize in Physics in nineteen eighty-three, forty-eight years after Eddington stood up to mock him. And the limit he found on that steamship is the trigger for everything that follows.

NARRATOR: So return to our massive star, with its newborn iron core. The core is not burning. It is held up only by electron degeneracy pressure, exactly like a white dwarf. And here is the trap. The shells of silicon still burning around the core keep dumping fresh iron down onto it. The core grows heavier, and heavier, day by day, creeping toward one point four solar masses. And then it crosses the Chandrasekhar limit. Electron degeneracy pressure can no longer hold. And the most extraordinary collapse in nature begins.

NARRATOR: Let me describe what happens in the next single second, because almost nothing in the universe happens this fast. As the core crosses the limit, two things make the collapse run away. First, the pressure of those relativistic electrons starts to disappear in a brutal way. Under the crushing density, electrons are forced into the protons inside the nuclei. An electron and a proton combine to make a neutron and a neutrino. This is called electron capture, or neutronisation. And every electron that gets absorbed is an electron that was helping to hold the core up. So the very pressure supporting the core is being deleted, particle by particle. Second, the core is now so hot that high-energy gamma rays begin to smash the iron nuclei apart, back into helium and free particles. This process is called photodisintegration, and it is endothermic. It drinks energy out of the core, removing the thermal support too. The floor falls out from under the star.

NARRATOR: And so the core collapses. Not slowly. The inner core falls inward at speeds reaching seventy thousand kilometres every second. That is nearly a quarter of the speed of light. A ball of iron roughly the size of the Earth, about eight thousand kilometres across, collapses down to a sphere only about twenty kilometres across, smaller than a city, in less than one second. The density climbs past anything in ordinary experience. It reaches the density of an atomic nucleus itself, about two hundred trillion grams in every cubic centimetre. A sugar cube of this material would weigh as much as a mountain. The temperature soars to around a hundred billion degrees.

QUESTION: When the collapse reaches nuclear density, what stops it? Does anything stop it?

NARRATOR: Something does, and it is the climax of the collapse. At nuclear density, the neutrons themselves begin to resist. Just like the electrons before them, neutrons obey the Pauli exclusion principle, and they generate their own degeneracy pressure. But more than that, the strong nuclear force, which is attractive at normal nuclear distances, suddenly turns ferociously repulsive when you try to squeeze nucleons even closer together. The equation of state, the relationship between pressure and density, stiffens almost instantaneously. The infalling core slams into this incompressible wall, overshoots it slightly, and then rebounds. It bounces. The innermost core snaps back like a compressed spring, and it drives a shock wave outward, into the outer layers of the star that are still raining inward at a quarter of the speed of light. This is the core bounce. This is the seed of the explosion.

NARRATOR: But here is a subtlety that took physicists decades to sort out, and it corrects a very common misconception. The iron core does not explode. The iron core implodes. It collapses inward, and what is left of it becomes a neutron star, a city-sized ball of pure neutrons, with a density equal to the density of an atomic nucleus and a mass greater than the Sun's packed into a ball little more than twenty kilometres across, or, if the collapsing core is left with more than about three solar masses, no known force can hold it up and it collapses all the way to a black hole. This explosion, the death of a massive star, is what astronomers call a Type II supernova, the kind whose spectrum still shows the hydrogen of the star's outer layers, and it is these explosions, occurring in stars from roughly eight to fifty times the mass of the Sun, that seed the galaxy with the elements forged inside. The explosion we see, the supernova, is the outer layers of the star being blasted off into space. And the shock wave that does the blasting almost fails. It stalls. As the shock plows outward, it loses energy tearing apart the iron nuclei still falling in, and it loses energy to a flood of neutrinos pouring out of the collapsed core. Within milliseconds, the shock grinds to a halt, just a hundred or two hundred kilometres out. For a moment, the explosion hangs in the balance.

QUESTION: So what revives it? What turns a stalled shock into the explosion that lights up a galaxy?

NARRATOR: Neutrinos. Those ghostly, almost massless particles that we usually think of as passing through everything without touching it. In the heart of a collapsing star, neutrinos are made in unimaginable numbers, because every electron capture makes one. The total energy released by the collapse is around ten to the forty-six joules. Let me put that in perspective. That is more energy, in about ten seconds, than the Sun will radiate in its entire ten-billion-year lifetime. And around ninety-nine percent of that colossal energy is carried away by neutrinos. Now, neutrinos barely interact with matter. But the matter just behind the stalled shock is so incredibly dense that even neutrinos cannot all slip through. A tiny fraction of them, perhaps one percent, get absorbed. And that one percent is enough. It reheats the material behind the shock, re-energises it, and shoves it outward again. The revived shock tears through the rest of the star and blows the entire outer envelope into space. The star explodes. And only about one percent of the total energy goes into that visible explosion, and only about a hundredth of one percent comes out as the light we actually see. The supernova that can outshine a galaxy is the faint sparkle on top of a neutrino furnace.

NARRATOR: Now we have the explosion. But we still have not made gold. We are still stuck at iron. The shock wave, the bounce, the neutrinos, all of that gets the star to blow up, but blowing up an iron core does not, by itself, make uranium. To make the elements beyond iron, we need a specific and bizarre process, and its name tells you almost everything about it. It is called the r-process. The r stands for rapid. Rapid neutron capture.

QUESTION: Neutron capture. Why neutrons, and why does it have to be rapid?

NARRATOR: Neutrons are the perfect tool for building heavy elements, for one simple reason. A neutron has no electric charge. A proton is repelled by the positive charge of a nucleus, so it is hard to push a proton in. But a neutron feels no such repulsion. It can drift right up to a nucleus and be captured, with no electrical barrier at all. So if you can flood a region with free neutrons, nuclei will gobble them up. Take an iron nucleus. It captures a neutron and becomes a slightly heavier iron isotope. It captures another, and another. But here is the thing. These neutron-heavy nuclei are unstable. Given time, a neutron inside one of them will turn into a proton, by emitting an electron, in a process called beta decay. And when a neutron becomes a proton, the atomic number goes up by one. The element changes. You climb one step up the periodic table.

NARRATOR: So there are two clocks running. One clock is how fast the nucleus captures the next neutron. The other clock is how fast it beta-decays. And this is the whole game. In the slow process, the s-process, which happens in ordinary aging stars, neutron capture is rare, so a nucleus captures a neutron and then waits around, beta-decaying whenever it wants, ambling gently up the chart of stable nuclei. The slow process can build elements up to lead and bismuth, but it runs out of road there, and it takes thousands of years. But in the rapid process, the r-process, neutrons are so dense, so overwhelming, that a nucleus captures the next neutron long before it has any chance to beta-decay. It gulps down neutron after neutron after neutron, building wildly neutron-rich nuclei that could never normally exist, nuclei far out beyond the edge of the stable elements. Only when it can hold no more, or when the neutron flood finally ends, does it beta-decay in a cascade back toward stability, climbing rapidly up the periodic table to its final resting element.

NARRATOR: The conditions you need for this are extreme almost beyond belief. You need a density of free neutrons of around ten to the twenty-four neutrons per cubic centimetre. That is roughly a gram of pure free neutrons crammed into every cubic centimetre of space. You need temperatures around a billion degrees. And you need it all to happen in seconds, because free neutrons are unstable and decay in about ten minutes. In those few frenzied seconds, the r-process builds the chart of nuclei all the way up. Past lead. Past bismuth. Up to thorium, and uranium, and beyond. Every atom of uranium that has ever existed, every atom of gold, of platinum, of the iodine your body needs, was built by rapid neutron capture in conditions like these. The r-process is responsible for roughly half of all the elements heavier than iron, and for essentially all of the very heaviest. If you hold gold, you are holding the frozen output of an r-process.

QUESTION: So the theory is beautiful. But how do we actually know any of this is true? Has anyone ever watched it happen?

NARRATOR: This is where the story stops being theory and becomes observation, and it begins on a mountaintop in Chile in nineteen eighty-seven. On the night of February twenty-third, a research assistant from the University of Toronto named Ian Shelton was working at Las Campanas Observatory, high in the Andes. He was running a long photographic exposure of the Large Magellanic Cloud, a small companion galaxy to our Milky Way, about a hundred and sixty-eight thousand light-years away. High winds were battering the dome that night, and at one point the rolltop roof slammed shut on him. He took the glass photographic plate he had been exposing, an eight-by-ten-inch plate, and walked to the darkroom to develop it by hand.

NARRATOR: When the plate came up in the developing fluid, there was a bright star on it that had not been there the night before. It was so bright that Shelton's first thought was that it was a flaw, a defect in the plate. So he walked outside, and he looked up at the southern sky with his own eyes. And there it was. A star, visible to the naked eye, sitting in the Large Magellanic Cloud, that simply had not existed the previous night. At almost the same moment, the telescope operator at Las Campanas, Oscar Duhalde, saw it by eye as well. And independently, an amateur astronomer named Albert Jones, in Nelson, New Zealand, spotted it too. It was the closest supernova to Earth since Kepler's supernova in sixteen oh four, three hundred and eighty-three years earlier. It was given the name Supernova nineteen eighty-seven A. The first supernova discovered in nineteen eighty-seven.

NARRATOR: Shelton tried to telephone the central bureau in Cambridge, Massachusetts, that registers such discoveries. The call would not go through. In the end a driver had to be sent a hundred kilometres down the mountain to the town of La Serena to send the alert by telegram. But here is the part Ian Shelton could not have known as he stood there in the cold, staring up at that new star. Three hours earlier, something had already swept silently through the Earth, and through Shelton's own body, and through every detector humanity had ever built. A burst of neutrinos.

QUESTION: Neutrinos? Three hours before the light. How is that possible, if nothing travels faster than light?

NARRATOR: Nothing does travel faster than light, and that is not what happened, and this is one of the most beautiful confirmations in all of astrophysics. Remember how the explosion works. The core collapses, and in that instant of collapse and bounce, the neutrinos are created, and neutrinos barely interact with anything, so they stream straight out of the centre of the star at almost the speed of light, escaping into space immediately. But the light, the visible flash of the supernova, cannot escape from the centre. It is trapped. The photons have to wait for the shock wave to fight its way out from the core, through the entire enormous body of the star, all the way to the surface, before the surface flares up and light pours out. That journey, from the core to the surface, takes the shock a couple of hours. So the neutrinos got a head start of about two to three hours. Not because they were faster, but because they left from deep inside while the light was still bottled up. Both crossed the hundred and sixty-eight thousand light-years to Earth at essentially the same speed. The neutrinos simply had a head start out of the gate.

NARRATOR: And we caught them. On February twenty-third, nineteen eighty-seven, at thirty-five minutes past seven in the morning, Universal Time, three underground detectors registered a burst of neutrinos lasting about thirteen seconds. In Japan, a detector called Kamiokande the Second caught about twelve of them. In the United States, the Irvine-Michigan-Brookhaven detector caught eight. And in the Caucasus mountains, the Baksan detector caught about five. Roughly two dozen neutrinos in total, out of a flood of perhaps ten to the fifty-eight that passed through the planet. The Japanese detector had only recently been upgraded with new electronics for an unrelated experiment on solar neutrinos. It was, in a sense, ready by luck. A young physicist named Masayuki Nakahata was among those who found the signal buried in the data.

NARRATOR: Think about what those two dozen particles proved. The theory said a core-collapse supernova should release ninety-nine percent of its energy as a brief, intense burst of neutrinos, and that this burst should arrive a few hours before the light. And there it was. The numbers matched. The timing matched. For the first time in history, we had detected neutrinos from a star beyond the Sun, and in doing so we confirmed the entire picture of core collapse. The field of neutrino astronomy was born in those thirteen seconds. The data even let physicists put the first real limit on the mass of the neutrino, less than about twenty electron volts. And it confirmed something else nobody had expected, that the star which exploded, a blue supergiant called Sanduleak minus sixty-nine two-oh-two, could be the progenitor of such an explosion at all.

QUESTION: So nineteen eighty-seven A confirmed the collapse, the bounce, and the neutrino burst. But did it confirm the r-process? Did it prove where the gold comes from?

NARRATOR: No. And this is the honest, important twist in the story. For decades, the textbook answer was that the r-process happened inside core-collapse supernovae like nineteen eighty-seven A. It seemed natural. You have the neutrons, you have the heat, you have the explosion. But when physicists modelled it carefully, they ran into trouble. The material blasted out of a normal core-collapse supernova turns out to be very hard to make neutron-rich enough. It is difficult to get that flood of free neutrons, ten to the twenty-four per cubic centimetre, in the right place at the right time. The supernova makes plenty of iron. But making the full sweep of the heaviest r-process elements, the gold and the platinum and the uranium, seemed to need somewhere even more extreme. The site of the r-process became one of the great open questions in astrophysics. And to settle it, we needed a completely new way of seeing the universe. We needed to hear it.

NARRATOR: Bring the story forward thirty years, to the seventeenth of August, twenty seventeen. By now, humanity has built an instrument that almost defies belief. It is called LIGO, the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory, two enormous detectors in the United States, with a third, called Virgo, in Italy. These machines do not collect light at all. They listen for gravitational waves, ripples in the fabric of spacetime itself, predicted by Einstein a century earlier. When something violent and massive moves out in the universe, it sends a wave through spacetime, stretching and squeezing space as it passes. LIGO can detect a stretch smaller than a thousandth the width of a proton. Two years earlier, in twenty fifteen, it had detected its first gravitational waves, from two black holes merging. But black holes merging make no light. They are silent and dark to telescopes.

NARRATOR: On that August afternoon, at forty-one minutes past noon Universal Time, LIGO and Virgo detected something different. A gravitational wave signal that was not a brief chirp but a long, rising song, lasting about a hundred seconds, sweeping upward in pitch from twenty-four cycles per second toward hundreds. The shape of the signal told the physicists exactly what they were hearing. Not two black holes, but two neutron stars, the dense remnants of two previous supernovae, locked in a death spiral, whirling around each other faster and faster as they spiralled inward, until they touched and merged. The event was named GW one-seven-oh-eight-one-seven, for the date.

QUESTION: Two neutron stars merging. What does that have to do with the gold in my ring?

NARRATOR: Everything, as it turned out. Think about what a neutron star is. It is the collapsed core left behind by a supernova, a city-sized ball of almost pure neutrons. So if you take two of them and smash them together, you have, for a fraction of a second, the most neutron-rich environment imaginable anywhere in the universe. When they merged, the collision flung off a cloud of neutron-rich debris, about sixteen thousand Earth-masses of it, at twenty to thirty percent of the speed of light. And in that debris, the r-process ran. Rapid neutron capture, on a scale that dwarfs anything a single supernova could manage, building gold and platinum and the heaviest elements in a few seconds.

NARRATOR: And this time, unlike with the black holes, there was light to see. One point seven four seconds after the merger, a gamma-ray burst arrived. And then, when the gravitational-wave alert went out, more than seventy observatories on seven continents and in space swung toward the source, a galaxy called NGC forty-nine ninety-three, about a hundred and forty million light-years away. Within about eleven hours, telescopes found a glowing point of light that had not been there before. They called it a kilonova. And a kilonova is exactly what the theory of the r-process predicts you should see. When all those neutron-rich nuclei are freshly forged, they are radioactive. As they beta-decay back toward stability over the following hours and days, they release energy, and that energy heats the expanding cloud of debris and makes it glow. The kilonova is, quite literally, the afterglow of newly minted gold and platinum cooling down. Nearly four thousand astronomers from over nine hundred institutions ended up working on this single event.

NARRATOR: And then came the proof, the thing that closed the case. Two years later, in twenty nineteen, a team led by Darach Watson at the Niels Bohr Institute in Copenhagen went back and re-analysed the spectrum of light from that kilonova. Buried in it, at a wavelength of about eight hundred and ten nanometres, they identified the fingerprint of a specific element. Strontium. A heavy element, an r-process element, that had been manufactured in the merger. It was the first time a single, identifiable heavy element had been caught spectroscopically being born in a neutron star collision. The merger had thrown out roughly ten Earth-masses of gold and platinum combined, and about five Earth-masses of strontium. We were no longer guessing. We had seen the r-process happen, heard the collision that drove it, and identified one of the elements it made.

NARRATOR: So today the picture is this. Neutron star mergers like GW one-seven-oh-eight-one-seven are a major site, very likely the dominant site, for forging the heaviest r-process elements. Rare and special kinds of supernovae probably contribute too. But the gold is, above all, the wreckage of colliding neutron stars, which are themselves the corpses of earlier exploded stars. The chain is staggering. A massive star lives and dies in a supernova, leaving a neutron star. Another does the same. The two neutron stars, perhaps over billions of years, slowly spiral together and collide, and in that collision the r-process makes gold, and scatters it across space, where it eventually finds its way into a cloud that collapses to form a new star, with planets, with an Earth, with a jeweller, with a ring.

NARRATOR: It is worth pausing on how recent all of this certainty is. The theoretical foundation was laid in a single legendary paper in nineteen fifty-seven, titled Synthesis of the Elements in Stars, written by Margaret Burbidge, Geoffrey Burbidge, William Fowler, and Fred Hoyle, a paper so famous it is known simply by their initials. It named the r-process and the s-process and mapped out how stars build the elements. But the actual site of the r-process, the answer to where the gold comes from, was not nailed down until that afternoon in twenty seventeen, well within living memory. Some of you listening are older than the answer to this question.

NARRATOR: And the legacy of these two events runs deep. Nineteen eighty-seven A launched neutrino astronomy and led directly to the construction of ever larger detectors, Super-Kamiokande and beyond, built and waiting to catch the neutrinos from the next nearby supernova. GW one-seven-oh-eight-one-seven launched the era of multi-messenger astronomy, where we observe the same event in gravitational waves and in light at the same time, and it even gave us a brand-new way to measure the expansion rate of the universe. We have gone, in a single human lifetime, from Chandrasekhar doing arithmetic on a steamship, to standing under the southern sky watching a star explode, to feeling the ground ripple as two dead stars collide and make gold.

NARRATOR: So the next time you see something heavier than iron, gold, silver, the platinum in a wedding band, the uranium that powers a reactor, the very iodine in your own body, remember what it cost to make it. Iron is where a star's furnace gives up. Everything beyond iron required a catastrophe. It required a core to exceed the Chandrasekhar limit and collapse at a quarter of the speed of light, or two neutron stars to fall together and shatter. Every atom of it carries the fingerprint of a dying star, and now, at last, we can read that fingerprint in the light.

NARRATOR: For the New South Wales Higher School Certificate Physics course, this story is the evidence behind Module Eight, From the Universe to the Atom. It is the case study for how elements up to and including iron are synthesised by stars, and how atoms larger than iron are synthesised, by the rapid neutron capture process, the r-process, occurring in supernovae and in neutron star mergers. It rests on the concept of binding energy per nucleon, the curve that peaks at iron and explains why fusion releases energy below iron but cannot release energy above it. That single fact is the answer to the syllabus question of why, in terms of binding energy, iron is the heaviest element that can be synthesised in a red supergiant. And it is the dramatic final chapter of stellar evolution past the main sequence, the way the shifting balance between the inward force of gravity and the outward pressure created by nuclear fusion drives the synthesis of successively heavier elements, all the way up to the death of a massive star, the failure of degeneracy pressure at the Chandrasekhar limit, the core collapse, and the Type II supernova. When the syllabus asks you to account for the origin of the heavy elements, this is the answer. They were made in the violent deaths of stars, and we have the neutrinos and the gravitational waves to prove it.
