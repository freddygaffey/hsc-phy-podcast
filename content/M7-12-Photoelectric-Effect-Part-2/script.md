NARRATOR: Second, the threshold frequency. Each metal grips its surface electrons with a characteristic minimum binding energy. The syllabus name for that is the work function, written as the Greek letter phi, and you'll also see it written as a capital W, particularly on the data sheet and in some textbooks. Phi and W are the same quantity, the work function, the minimum energy needed to liberate an electron from that metal's surface. Now, if a single photon's energy, Planck's constant times frequency, is less than the work function, that photon simply does not have enough energy to free the electron. And since the electron can only take energy from one photon at a time, it doesn't matter how many of these too-weak photons arrive, or how long they keep coming. None of them, individually, can do the job. So below a threshold frequency, nothing happens. Different metals have different work functions, so they have different threshold frequencies. NARRATOR: Now let's turn that fourth point into the equation, because this is where conservation of energy enters, and the dot-point literally demands you name it. Here's the energy bookkeeping. A photon arrives carrying energy equal to Planck's constant times frequency. That energy is spent on two things and only two things. First, a fixed amount, the work function, is spent freeing the electron from the metal's grip. Second, whatever is left over becomes the kinetic energy the electron flies away with. Energy in equals energy to escape plus energy left as motion. That's just the law of conservation of energy applied to a single collision. Rearrange it and the kinetic energy equals the photon energy minus the work function.

NARRATOR: One subtlety that earns the top mark: why maximum kinetic energy, why the word maximum? Because not every electron is bound by exactly the work function. Electrons deeper in, or held a little tighter, lose more energy escaping, so they come out slower. The electrons that started most weakly bound lose the least and emerge fastest. They carry the maximum kinetic energy. So the equation is the maximum kinetic energy equals the photon energy minus the work function. Written in symbols it's K-max equals Planck's constant times frequency, minus phi. You'll also see it as E-k-max equals h f minus W. NARRATOR: Let me give you the strong extended-response answer now, in full, because this exact wording shows up year after year: "use the law of conservation of energy to account for the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons."
