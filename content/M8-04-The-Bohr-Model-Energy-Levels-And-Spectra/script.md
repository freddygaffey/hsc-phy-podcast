NARRATOR: Let's start where we've been. Over the last three episodes we've watched the atom get built, piece by piece, out of pure experimental evidence — and we've watched two whole models get knocked over. Keep that demolition in your head, because today we put up the model that finally stands.

NARRATOR: Three episodes back, we did cathode rays. The birth of the electron. Remember the evidence: the Maltese cross casting a sharp shadow, telling us cathode rays travel in straight lines. The little paddle wheel turning, telling us they carry momentum, so they have mass. The beam bending towards the positive plate in an electric field, telling us they're negatively charged. And the beam deflecting in a magnetic field, telling us they're charged particles and not light. Put that together and you have a stream of negatively charged particles with mass — the electron.

NARRATOR: Then two episodes back, Thomson and Millikan. Thomson's crossed-fields trick — balance the electric and magnetic forces so the beam goes straight through, which tells you the speed is the electric field strength divided by the magnetic field strength. Then switch the electric field off, let the magnetic field bend the beam alone, and you can solve for the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron. He got about one point seven six times ten to the eleven coulombs per kilogram — and crucially, the same value no matter what gas or what metal he used. The electron is universal. Then Millikan's oil drop experiment pinned down the charge by itself — one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs — and showed charge comes only in whole-number multiples of that. Charge is quantised. And out of all that, Thomson proposed the plum pudding model: electrons dotted through a diffuse sphere of positive charge, like plums in a pudding.

NARRATOR: And then last episode, the plum pudding got destroyed. Geiger and Marsden, the gold foil experiment. Fire alpha particles at a wafer-thin sheet of gold. Most go straight through — so the atom is mostly empty space. Some deflect at large angles. And about one in eight thousand bounces straight back. Rutherford's line: it was as if you'd fired a shell at tissue paper and it came back and hit you. The only way to bounce an alpha particle backwards is to hit something tiny, dense, massive and positively charged — the nucleus. So out goes the pudding, and in comes the nuclear model — a planetary atom, a tiny positive nucleus with electrons orbiting it like planets round the sun.

NARRATOR: But here's the cliffhanger we ended on, and it's the exact problem we're solving today. The nuclear model is broken by classical physics itself. Remember the chain. An electron orbiting the nucleus is moving in a circle, so it's continuously accelerating — it has centripetal acceleration, directed inwards, exactly like the circular motion you did back in Module 5, Advanced Mechanics. Now bring in Maxwell, from Module 6, Electromagnetism — and remember the Hertz story, where Hertz actually generated and detected the electromagnetic waves Maxwell predicted, by accelerating charges back and forth in a spark gap. Maxwell's result is that an accelerating charge radiates electromagnetic energy. So the orbiting electron must be radiating constantly. And if it's radiating, it's losing energy, so it must spiral inwards and crash into the nucleus — in about a hundred-millionth of a second. The nuclear model predicts that every atom in the universe should collapse instantly. They don't. Atoms are stable. The model is broken.

NARRATOR: And there's a second crack. As the electron spirals in, its orbit shrinks continuously, so it would radiate a continuous smear of every frequency on the way down. But that's not what we see. Excited atoms emit a sharp, discrete set of coloured lines — and a different set for every element. A line spectrum, not a smear. So the nuclear model fails twice: it predicts collapse when atoms are stable, and it predicts a continuous spectrum when we observe discrete lines.

NARRATOR: That is the cliff Niels Bohr is standing on in nineteen thirteen. A Danish physicist, trained briefly under Thomson and then under Rutherford himself. And the model he builds to climb off that cliff is today's entire episode: the Bohr model — stationary energy levels, the photon, and the line spectrum. By the end of this episode you'll be able to do five things in an exam. State Bohr's postulates. Explain how his model is a deliberate mixture of classical and quantum physics. Explain, in the full mark-earning chain, why every element has its own unique line spectrum. Read an energy-level diagram and tell absorption from emission without hesitating. And solve the transition problems with the energy equals Planck's constant times frequency relationship and the Rydberg equation. Let's go.

NARRATOR: Bohr's move is audacious, and you need to be able to state it cleanly, because "state Bohr's postulates" is a question that turns up almost verbatim. There are three postulates. Here's a hook to hold them: think of the three postulates as Stations, Steps, and Spin. Stations — stationary states. Steps — the photon steps between them. Spin — the quantised angular momentum, which is the electron's spin around the orbit. Stations, Steps, Spin.

NARRATOR: Postulate one. Stations. The electron can exist only in certain allowed orbits, which Bohr called stationary states — what we now call energy levels. And here's the audacious part: while the electron sits in a stationary state, it orbits the nucleus without radiating any electromagnetic energy. Read that again. Bohr is flatly contradicting Maxwell. Classical physics says an accelerating charge must radiate; Bohr simply decrees that in these special orbits, it doesn't. He has no justification. He just forbids the spiral, by decree, because that's what's needed to make atoms stable. That single assertion fixes the stability crisis — the electron in the ground state has nowhere lower to fall to, so it can't spiral in.

NARRATOR: Postulate two. Steps. The electron can move between two stationary states only by emitting or absorbing a single photon — and the photon's energy is exactly equal to the difference in energy between the two states. In words: the photon energy, which is Planck's constant times the photon's frequency, equals the energy of the higher level minus the energy of the lower level. This is the bridge from Module 7. Remember the photoelectric effect — remember the photoelectric story, where Einstein said light comes in discrete packets, photons, each carrying energy equal to Planck's constant times frequency. That photon, energy equals h f, walks straight out of Module 7 and into the atom. Bohr is borrowing Einstein's photon to carry energy in and out of the atom on every jump.

NARRATOR: Postulate three. Spin. The angular momentum of the orbiting electron is quantised — it can only take whole-number multiples of Planck's constant divided by two pi. The mass times the speed times the orbit radius equals some integer n, times h over two pi, where n is one, two, three, and so on. That integer n is the principal quantum number — it labels the energy levels. You rarely have to calculate with postulate three at this level, but you must know it exists and that it's what makes the orbits discrete rather than continuous.

QUESTION: Hang on — if Bohr just contradicts Maxwell with no reason, isn't that cheating? Why did anyone take it seriously?

NARRATOR: Fair challenge, and the honest answer is: because it worked, spectacularly, for hydrogen. Bohr's model predicted the exact wavelengths of hydrogen's spectral lines that experimenters had measured for decades and couldn't explain. When a model with an outrageous assumption nails the numbers to four significant figures, you take the assumption seriously and you go looking for why it's true. The "why" doesn't arrive until next episode, with de Broglie's standing waves. For now, Bohr's defence is the data.

NARRATOR: Now, that contradiction with Maxwell points us straight at a named dot-point you must be ready for: explain how the stationary energy level model uses a mixture of classical and quantum physics. This is worth saying carefully, because students lose marks by calling Bohr "a quantum model." It is not. It is a hybrid — a deliberate bolting-together of two incompatible pictures.

NARRATOR: Here's the mixture. The classical half: Bohr keeps the planetary picture. The electron really is a little particle going round a circular orbit, and the thing holding it in that circle is the electrostatic attraction between the negative electron and the positive nucleus — the Coulomb force. Bohr sets the Coulomb force equal to the centripetal force needed for the circular orbit, exactly the way you'd analyse a satellite in Module 5. That's pure classical mechanics. The quantum half: on top of that classical orbit he bolts three quantum rules — only certain orbits are allowed, no radiation while you're in an orbit, and energy moves only in photon-sized lumps on a jump. So one model, two worlds. The orbit is Newton and Coulomb; the rules are Planck and Einstein.

NARRATOR: And here's the exam-critical twist: that mixture is itself listed as a limitation of the model. It's conceptually unsatisfying — you can't just glue classical and quantum physics together and call it finished. So when a question asks for the model's limitations, "it is an awkward mixture of classical and quantum physics" is one of the marks. Hold that thought; we'll come back to the full limitations list at the end.

NARRATOR: Right — the payoff. Why does this give us a line spectrum, and why is that spectrum unique to each element? This is the heart of the episode and the most heavily examined part, so let's build the answer the way a marker wants it, link by link.

NARRATOR: Start with the levels. Because the electron can only sit in certain stationary states, each atom has a discrete set of allowed energies — a ladder of energy levels, not a ramp. And that ladder is set by the atom's nuclear charge and its electron arrangement, both of which are unique to each element. Hydrogen has one ladder, helium has a different ladder, sodium another. So step one: each element has its own unique set of energy levels.

NARRATOR: Now the consequence. If the energy levels are unique, then the gaps between them — the energy differences — are also unique to that element. And by postulate two, every photon the atom emits or absorbs has an energy equal to one of those gaps. Energy equals Planck's constant times frequency, so a fixed set of energy gaps means a fixed set of frequencies, which means a fixed set of wavelengths. So each element emits and absorbs only its own unique set of wavelengths — its own fingerprint of spectral lines. That's why a sodium streetlight is that exact orange, and why you can identify an element you've never touched purely from the colours of light it gives off. We'll cash this in hard in Module 8, episode thirteen, when we read the composition of stars from their spectra — Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin worked out what stars are made of using exactly these lines.

NARRATOR: Now split it into the two cases, because the HSC asks for both. Emission. You energise the atom — heat it, run a current through the gas — and an electron gets bumped up to a higher stationary state. It doesn't stay there. It drops back down to a lower level, and as it drops, the atom emits a single photon carrying exactly the energy lost. That photon is a specific wavelength, a specific colour — a bright line. Do that for all the possible drops and you get a pattern of bright coloured lines on a dark background. That's an emission spectrum.

NARRATOR: Absorption. Now shine white light — a continuous spectrum, all wavelengths — through a cool gas of the same element. An electron sitting in a lower level can absorb a passing photon, but only if that photon's energy is exactly the right size to lift it to an allowed higher level. It grabs that one wavelength out of the beam and jumps up. So that wavelength gets removed from the continuous spectrum, leaving a dark line where it used to be. Do that for every allowed jump and you get a continuous rainbow with dark lines cut into it. That's an absorption spectrum.

NARRATOR: And here's the subtlety markers love to test: the dark absorption lines of an element sit at exactly the same wavelengths as its bright emission lines. Same element, same energy gaps, so the same photon energies — whether the atom is grabbing them or throwing them. If you can say that sentence, that's a mark most students miss.

QUESTION: So how should I actually write the answer to "explain how the Bohr model accounts for the line emission spectrum of hydrogen"? What separates a weak answer from a full-mark one?

NARRATOR: This is the question, so let me show you both. The weak answer — the band-four version — is: "electrons jump down and give off light." It's not wrong. It's just worth almost nothing, because it states an observation and stops. There's no mechanism, no quantisation, no conservation of energy. A marker can't give you the marks because you haven't shown the chain.

NARRATOR: Here's the strong answer, and listen for the links clicking into place. "In the Bohr model the electron in a hydrogen atom can occupy only certain discrete stationary states, each with a fixed, quantised energy. When the atom is excited, the electron is raised to a higher stationary state. It then transitions back to a lower state, and in doing so the atom emits a single photon. By the law of conservation of energy, the photon's energy is exactly equal to the difference in energy between the two states — that is, Planck's constant times the photon's frequency equals the higher energy minus the lower energy. Because the energy levels are discrete and unique to hydrogen, only photons of specific frequencies, and therefore specific wavelengths, can be emitted. These appear as a discrete set of bright lines — a line emission spectrum — rather than a continuous spectrum." That answer is in Listing five if you want to read it back later.

NARRATOR: Hear the difference? The weak answer gives the marker one fact. The strong answer gives them the whole causal chain: discrete levels, then a transition, then a photon, then conservation of energy fixing the photon's energy to the gap, then h f fixing the frequency, then only specific frequencies, therefore discrete lines. Every link in that chain is a place the marker can award a mark. The rule, and write this down, is: never state an observation without its mechanism. "Electrons give off light" is an observation. The Bohr chain is the mechanism.

NARRATOR: Notice I leaned on conservation of energy in there, and that's a named dot-point in its own right — explain how the law of conservation of energy applies to electrons transitioning between energy levels. The statement is simple and the marks are easy: the photon carries away exactly the energy the electron loses, no more and no less — on absorption, the photon supplies exactly the energy the electron gains. There's no energy left over and none borrowed from nowhere. That exact balance is precisely why spectral lines are sharp and sit at razor-precise wavelengths, instead of being fuzzy bands. Sharp lines are conservation of energy made visible.

NARRATOR: Now let's get you fluent at reading an energy-level diagram, because "interpret energy level diagrams" is a guaranteed question and there are easy marks in it if you don't trip on the conventions. Picture the diagram: a set of horizontal lines stacked up the page, each labelled with an energy in electron volts. Three rules to lock in.

NARRATOR: Rule one — the energies are negative, and the most negative one is at the bottom. Why negative? Because the electron is bound to the nucleus; you'd have to put energy in to rip it free. Zero energy is defined as the electron being completely free of the atom. So the ground state, the lowest, most tightly bound level — that's n equals one for hydrogen, sitting at minus thirteen point six electron volts — is the most negative, at the bottom. As you climb the ladder the levels get less negative, closer to zero. The top of the ladder, n approaching infinity, is zero electron volts — that's ionisation, the electron just barely free. Students who draw the ground state at the top, or treat the energies as positive, throw away marks. Most negative, most bound, bottom of the diagram. That's the convention.

NARRATOR: Rule two — and here's the classic trap — absorption is an arrow pointing up, emission is an arrow pointing down. Up means the electron gains energy by absorbing a photon. Down means the electron loses energy by emitting a photon. Students reverse these under pressure constantly. Here's the hook: "Up you Absorb, Down you Emit." Up-Absorb, Down-Emit. The electron climbing the ladder has to eat a photon to do it; the electron falling down throws a photon off. Up-Absorb, Down-Emit — burn it in.

NARRATOR: Rule three — the size of the gap sets the photon. A bigger energy gap means a bigger photon energy, which by energy equals h f means a higher frequency, and by the wave equation, speed equals frequency times wavelength, a higher frequency means a shorter wavelength. So big jumps give high-frequency, short-wavelength photons; small jumps give low-frequency, long-wavelength photons. Don't mix the direction up. The largest jumps — electrons falling all the way down to the ground state — give the highest-energy ultraviolet photons. Small jumps between the upper levels give low-energy infrared photons.

NARRATOR: That third rule organises the whole hydrogen spectrum into named series, and this is a memorisable list, so here's your hook. The series are named by which level the electron falls to. Falls to n equals one — the Lyman series, ultraviolet. Falls to n equals two — the Balmer series, visible. Falls to n equals three — the Paschen series, infrared. Hook: "Little Birds Pause" — Lyman, Balmer, Paschen — for landing on levels one, two, three. And the energy ordering rides along with it: Lyman lands on the lowest level so it's the biggest jumps, so ultraviolet; Paschen lands higher up on smaller gaps, so infrared; Balmer sits in between, in the visible. Little Birds Pause: one, two, three; ultraviolet, visible, infrared.

NARRATOR: The Balmer series is the star, because it's the only one in visible light — those are the hydrogen lines you can actually see with a spectroscope, and "investigate the Balmer series" is a named dot-point. The most famous Balmer line is the red one, called H-alpha, at six hundred and fifty-six nanometres, produced when an electron falls from n equals three down to n equals two. We'll calculate that exact wavelength in a minute.

QUESTION: Before we do the maths — pause the player here. Without looking anything up: which series is in the visible region, which level do those electrons fall to, and is a bigger energy gap a longer or a shorter wavelength? Say all three out loud, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The Balmer series is the visible one, and those electrons fall to n equals two. And a bigger energy gap is a shorter wavelength — bigger gap, bigger photon energy, higher frequency, shorter wavelength. If you got "longer," go back to rule three and the wave equation: frequency and wavelength are inversely related, so more energy means less wavelength. Lock it in.

NARRATOR: Now the calculations, because "solve problems" is an explicit dot-point and these come up every year. The main tool is the Rydberg equation, and it's on your data sheet, so you don't memorise it — you memorise how to use it. In words: one over the wavelength equals the Rydberg constant times the quantity one over n-final squared minus one over n-initial squared. The Rydberg constant is one point zero nine seven times ten to the seven per metre. N-final is the level the electron ends on; n-initial is the level it starts on. For emission, the electron drops, so n-final is the smaller number.

NARRATOR: Let's do the H-alpha line — worked example A, the red Balmer line. The question: find the longest wavelength in the visible hydrogen spectrum. Longest wavelength means smallest photon energy, which means the smallest gap. In the Balmer series the electron lands on n equals two, and the smallest gap is the jump from just above it — n equals three down to n equals two. So n-final is two, n-initial is three. Plug in: one over the wavelength equals one point zero nine seven times ten to the seven, times the quantity one over two squared, which is one over four, minus one over three squared, which is one over nine. One over four is zero point two five; one over nine is zero point one one one one. The difference is zero point one three eight nine. Multiply by the Rydberg constant and you get one point five two four times ten to the six per metre. That's one over the wavelength — so flip it. The wavelength is one divided by that, which is six point five six times ten to the minus seven metres. Six hundred and fifty-six nanometres. Red light. That's H-alpha, and the full working is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Notice the exam discipline there. The Rydberg constant is in units of per metre, so the equation hands you one over the wavelength in per metre, and you must remember to take the reciprocal at the end to get the wavelength itself. Forgetting to flip it is the single most common error on this calculation. The number one point five times ten to the six is not a wavelength — wavelengths of visible light are around ten to the minus seven metres, not ten to the plus six. If your answer comes out enormous, you forgot to invert.

NARRATOR: Now a second style of problem — worked example B — which uses the energy levels directly and ties together three of your data-sheet equations. Hydrogen's levels follow a neat rule: the energy of level n is the ground-state energy divided by n squared, where the ground state is minus thirteen point six electron volts. The question: an electron falls from n equals four to n equals one. Find the energy, frequency and wavelength of the emitted photon.

NARRATOR: Step one, the n equals four level. Minus thirteen point six divided by four squared — four squared is sixteen — gives minus zero point eight five electron volts. Step two, the photon energy is the difference between the levels. And here is the trap the lesson plan flags explicitly: take the magnitude of the difference. The electron goes from minus zero point eight five up at the top down to minus thirteen point six at the bottom. The energy released is thirteen point six minus zero point eight five, which is twelve point seven five electron volts. If you subtract the other way and get minus twelve point seven five, and then try to find a frequency, you'll get a negative frequency — which is physically meaningless. A photon's energy is always positive. Always take the magnitude of the energy difference. Twelve point seven five electron volts.

NARRATOR: Step three, convert to joules to use energy equals h f, because Planck's constant is in joule-seconds. One electron volt is one point six oh two times ten to the minus nineteen joules, so twelve point seven five electron volts is two point oh four times ten to the minus eighteen joules. Step four, the frequency is the energy divided by Planck's constant: two point oh four times ten to the minus eighteen, divided by six point six three times ten to the minus thirty-four, which gives three point oh eight times ten to the fifteen hertz. Step five, the wavelength, from speed equals frequency times wavelength, so wavelength is the speed of light divided by the frequency: three times ten to the eight, divided by three point oh eight times ten to the fifteen, which is nine point seven times ten to the minus eight metres — about ninety-seven nanometres. That's ultraviolet, and it should be: the electron landed on n equals one, so this is a Lyman-series line, and Lyman is ultraviolet. QUESTION: Quick one — in that problem, why did we have to convert the electron volts into joules before dividing by Planck's constant? Couldn't we just leave it in electron volts?

NARRATOR: No, and this is a unit-discipline mark. Planck's constant on your data sheet is six point six three times ten to the minus thirty-four joule-seconds — it's expressed in joules. So if you divide an energy in electron volts by a constant in joule-seconds, the electron volts don't cancel and your frequency is wrong by a factor of about ten to the nineteen. NARRATOR: Now let's close the loop on Bohr by being honest about what his model can't do — the limitations. This is a named dot-point, "identify the limitations of the stationary energy level model," and it does double duty: it's exam marks now, and it sets up the entire next episode. There are five limitations, and they're a memorisable list, so here's your hook: "Hydrogen Is My Sole Why." Five fingers.

NARRATOR: H — Hydrogen only. The model works beautifully for hydrogen and for other one-electron systems, but it cannot correctly predict the spectral wavelengths of any multi-electron atom. The moment you add a second electron, with the electrons repelling each other, the simple model falls apart.

NARRATOR: I — Intensities. The model can't explain the relative intensities of the spectral lines. It tells you which lines exist, but not why some are bright and some are faint — why some transitions are favoured over others.

NARRATOR: M — Magnetic splitting. It can't account for the splitting of spectral lines. When you look closely, single lines turn out to be several fine lines — that's fine and hyperfine structure — and when you put the atom in a magnetic field, lines split further still. That magnetic-field splitting is called the Zeeman effect, and Bohr can't touch it.

NARRATOR: S — it's a Sticky mixture, the classical-quantum hybrid we discussed. Gluing Newton's orbit to Planck's quanta is theoretically unsatisfying — it's an inelegant patch, not a unified theory.

NARRATOR: And the big one. W — Why. Bohr never explains why the orbits are stable and non-radiating, or why only certain orbits are allowed. He just asserts it. There's a famous moment where Rutherford reads Bohr's paper and writes back asking, in effect: how does an electron decide what frequency it's going to vibrate at when it jumps from one stationary state to another? Bohr had no answer. He'd written down the rules without the reason. Hydrogen Is My Sole Why — Hydrogen-only, Intensities, Magnetic splitting, Sticky mixture, Why.

NARRATOR: And that unanswered "Why" is the perfect bridge to next episode. Next time we meet Louis de Broglie and the idea that matter behaves as a wave — that an electron has a wavelength equal to Planck's constant divided by its momentum. And here's the beautiful payoff you'll get: if you picture the electron as a wave wrapped around its orbit, only orbits whose circumference fits a whole number of electron wavelengths let the wave join smoothly onto itself and form a standing wave. Every other orbit makes the wave cancel itself out. So the allowed orbits — Bohr's quantised stationary states — are exactly the standing-wave orbits. The "why" Bohr couldn't supply turns out to be standing waves, just like the way only certain notes stand on a guitar string. We'll prove the electron really is a wave with the electron diffraction story, the Davisson-Germer experiment — that's case_electron_diffraction, coming up. Today Bohr asserts the levels; next time de Broglie explains them.

QUESTION: Question five, the big one. Explain how the Bohr model accounts for the line emission spectrum of hydrogen, and explain how the law of conservation of energy applies. This is a five-or-six-mark extended response. Pause, write a full paragraph, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. In the Bohr model the electron in a hydrogen atom can occupy only certain discrete stationary states, each with a fixed, quantised energy. When the atom is excited, the electron is raised to a higher stationary state. It then transitions back down to a lower state, and in doing so the atom emits a single photon. By the law of conservation of energy, the photon carries away exactly the energy lost by the electron — no more and no less — so the photon's energy equals the difference in energy between the two states: Planck's constant times the photon's frequency equals the higher energy minus the lower energy. Because the hydrogen energy levels are discrete and unique to hydrogen, only specific energy differences are possible, so only photons of specific frequencies and wavelengths are emitted. These appear as a discrete set of bright lines — a line emission spectrum — rather than a continuous spectrum. The sharpness of each line is itself a consequence of conservation of energy: the photon energy is fixed precisely to the gap, so the wavelength is precise. That answer chains discrete levels, transition, single photon, conservation of energy fixing the energy, h f fixing the frequency, and discrete lines as the result — every link earns its mark.
