NARRATOR: Start with the core idea. Picture a magnetic field as a set of field lines, arrows running through space, and remember that where the lines are packed close together the field is strong, and where they're spread out the field is weak. Now hold up a loop of wire, a flat ring, somewhere in that field. Some of those field lines pass through the hole in the ring. Magnetic flux is, very simply, a measure of the total magnetic field passing through that loop. If you like, picture it as the total number of field lines threading through the hole. That's it. NARRATOR: Now here is the distinction that earns and loses more marks than anything else in this topic, so I'm going to be emphatic. Magnetic flux is not the same thing as magnetic field strength. They are two different quantities with two different units, and the syllabus expects you to keep them apart. Field strength, the quantity B, measured in tesla, tells you how strong the field is at a point, how closely packed the lines are right there. Magnetic flux, on the other hand, is the total amount of field passing through a whole area. Field strength is a property of a point in space. Flux is a property of a surface, a loop, an area. You cannot have flux without specifying an area, in the same way you cannot have a total rainfall without specifying a bucket to catch it in.

NARRATOR: And here's the precise relationship between them, which is also the cleanest way to remember the difference. The magnetic field strength B is the flux density. That word, density, is doing real work. Density means "amount per unit area." So the field strength is the amount of flux passing through each unit of area, and the flux is the field strength multiplied by the total area it threads through. Flux equals field strength times area, when the field goes straight through. In symbols, when the field is perpendicular to the loop, flux equals B times A. Field strength is flux per square metre; flux is field strength times the square metres. Say that to yourself: field strength is the flux density, flux is the field strength spread over the whole area.

NARRATOR: Now the second piece, and this is the one the syllabus specifically calls out: the angle. So far I've been careful to say "when the field goes straight through the loop." But the field doesn't always go straight through. It can hit the loop at an angle, and that changes how much of it actually gets through. To handle this properly you need one concept that students love to skip, and skipping it is what wrecks their answers. The concept is the area vector, also called the normal.

NARRATOR: Here's the idea. Take any flat surface, like the flat face of our loop. There is one direction that points straight out of that surface, perpendicular to it, like a flagpole standing up out of flat ground. That perpendicular direction is called the normal to the surface, and we represent it with an arrow called the area vector. The area vector points straight out of the face of the loop, at ninety degrees to the plane of the loop. Every flat loop has one. If the loop is lying flat on a table, its area vector points straight up at the ceiling. Get that picture solid, because the angle in the flux equation is measured from this area vector, and almost every flux mistake in the HSC comes from measuring the angle from the wrong reference.

NARRATOR: So here's the full equation, the one that's printed on the HSC data sheet. Magnetic flux equals the field strength B, times the area A, times the cosine of theta, where theta, and this is the crucial bit, theta is the angle between the magnetic field and the area vector. The angle between the field and the normal to the loop. Not the angle between the field and the plane of the loop. The angle between the field and the perpendicular to the loop. I'm going to say that several more times before we're done, because it is the whole ballgame. Flux equals B A cosine theta, theta measured from the normal.

QUESTION: So why cosine, and why measured from the normal rather than from the surface itself? It feels arbitrary.

NARRATOR: It's not arbitrary, it falls straight out of what flux means. Remember, flux is the amount of field getting through the loop, and only the part of the field that's actually pointing through the loop counts. The part of the field that runs sideways, along the surface, doesn't go through at all; it just skims across. So we need the component of the field that points along the area vector, straight through the loop. When you take the component of the field along the area vector, the trigonometry gives you the field strength times the cosine of the angle between the field and that vector. That perpendicular component, B times cosine theta, is the part that threads the loop. Multiply it by the area and you have the flux. So you can write flux two equivalent ways: flux equals B A cosine theta, or flux equals the perpendicular component of the field times the area. Same equation. The cosine is just extracting the through-the-loop part of the field. And it's measured from the normal because the through-the-loop direction is the normal direction. When the field lies along the normal, all of it gets through; when the field is perpendicular to the normal, none of it does. Cosine does exactly that: cosine of zero is one, cosine of ninety is zero.

NARRATOR: Let's make that concrete with the two extremes, because these two cases are worth memorising as a pair. Case one: the field points straight through the loop, along the area vector. Then the angle theta between the field and the area vector is zero. Cosine of zero is one. So the flux is B times A times one, which is just B A. That is the maximum possible flux. Every field line threads the loop. And notice the geometry: when the field is along the area vector, the field is perpendicular to the plane of the loop. The face of the loop is presented square-on to the field. Maximum flux happens when the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field. Say it both ways so neither catches you out: maximum flux when the field is parallel to the area vector, which is the same as when the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field.

NARRATOR: Case two: the field lies flat in the plane of the loop, skimming across the face rather than going through it. Now the field is perpendicular to the area vector, so the angle theta between the field and the area vector is ninety degrees. Cosine of ninety is zero. So the flux is B times A times zero, which is zero. No field lines pass through the loop at all; they all graze past in the plane of the wire. Zero flux happens when the plane of the coil is parallel to the field. Again, both ways: zero flux when the field is perpendicular to the area vector, which is the same as when the plane of the coil is parallel to the field.

NARRATOR: Now the units, quickly, because they come up and they're cheap marks. Flux is measured in webers, spelled w-e-b-e-r, named after Wilhelm Weber. Don't mix them up: an answer for flux that comes out "in tesla" is a flag that you've actually calculated a field strength, and an answer in webers when the question asked for flux density means you've calculated flux by mistake.

QUESTION: Fourth question, the big one, worth five or six marks. "Describe magnetic flux, and explain how and why the flux through a coil changes as the coil rotates in a uniform magnetic field." Pause, plan a structured answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Magnetic flux is the measure of the total magnetic field passing through a given area, and for a flat coil it equals the field strength B times the area A times the cosine of theta, where theta is the angle between the field and the area vector, the normal to the coil. It is measured in webers, and it is distinct from the field strength, or flux density, which is measured in tesla. When the coil rotates in a uniform field, B and A stay constant, but the angle theta between the field and the coil's area vector changes continuously, so by the equation the flux changes continuously too. The flux is maximum, equal to B A, when the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field, the area vector aligned with the field and theta zero, so all field lines thread the coil. The flux is zero when the plane of the coil is parallel to the field, the area vector perpendicular to the field and theta ninety degrees, so the field skims across and no lines pass through. The cause of the change is purely the changing angle, not any change in the field or the area. And this continuously changing flux is significant because, by Faraday's law, a changing flux induces an EMF, which is why a rotating coil acts as a generator.
