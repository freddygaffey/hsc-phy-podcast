NARRATOR: Last episode was nuclear fission and the reactor. The core idea: a uranium-235 nucleus captures a slow-moving neutron, becomes wildly unstable, and splits into two roughly mid-mass fragments, plus two or three spare neutrons, plus about two hundred mega-electron-volts of energy. Those spare neutrons can trigger further fissions — a chain reaction. We sorted the reactor components with the mnemonic the Fuel's Cooled, Moderately Controlled — fuel, coolant, moderator, control rods. The control rods absorb neutrons to set the rate; the moderator slows neutrons down so they're captured more readily — and don't ever swap those two, because the exam loves to catch you on it. Controlled means each fission triggers exactly one more, for steady power; uncontrolled means more than one each time, which runs away into a bomb. And the headline comparison: a single fission event releases something like twenty million times the energy of burning a single atom of fuel. Hold that comparison — we extend it today.

NARRATOR: Two episodes back was the episode that today completely cashes in: mass defect, binding energy, and energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. Three things to have firmly in hand. First, mass defect: a bound nucleus weighs less than its separate protons and neutrons, and that missing mass was converted into energy when the nucleus formed. Second, binding energy: the energy equivalent of that mass defect, equal to the mass defect times the speed of light squared — and it measures how tightly bound, how stable, the nucleus is. Third, and this is the spine of today's whole lesson, the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve: binding energy per nucleon plotted against mass number. It rises steeply from the light nuclei on the left, peaks at iron-56 at about eight point eight mega-electron-volts per nucleon — the most stable nucleus there is — and then declines gently across the heavy nuclei on the right. Everything moves toward that iron peak to release energy. Fission, on the right of the peak, releases energy by coming down toward iron from above. Today we look at the steep left-hand slope, where fusion lives.

NARRATOR: And remember the data-sheet shortcut and the trap from that episode: one atomic mass unit is equivalent to nine hundred and thirty-one point five mega-electron-volts. To get an energy in mega-electron-volts, you multiply the mass defect in atomic mass units by nine hundred and thirty-one point five — and you do NOT also divide by the speed of light squared, because the "per c-squared" is already baked into that number. We're going to use that exact shortcut again today, and the exact same trap is waiting, so it's lodged in your head before we get there.

NARRATOR: Three episodes back was half-life — the time for half the nuclei in a sample to decay — with the decay being exponential because the rate is proportional to how many nuclei are left, and the decay constant lambda tied to half-life by lambda equals the natural log of two divided by the half-life. And before that, radioactivity and the strong force: that tug-of-war between the long-range Coulomb repulsion pushing protons apart and the short-range strong nuclear force gripping nucleons together — gripping hard, but only between near neighbours, over a range of about ten to the minus fifteen metres. Keep that tug-of-war loaded, because the strong force versus the Coulomb force is the entire reason fusion needs a star's worth of heat to get going. That contrast is the heart of today's central "explain why."

NARRATOR: So here's the thread. We did fission as the splitting of heavy nuclei moving down the right side of the binding-energy curve toward iron. Today is the mirror image: fusion, the joining of light nuclei moving up the left side of the curve toward iron. Same graph, same engine — mass defect converted to energy by Einstein's relationship — but coming at the iron peak from the opposite direction. And this is the process that lights the stars. Let's go.

NARRATOR: Here's what you'll be able to do by the end. One: define nuclear fusion and use the binding-energy curve to account for the energy it releases — a near-guaranteed extended response. Two: explain why fusion demands extreme temperatures and pressures, with the strong, mark-earning version that names the Coulomb barrier — this is the single most examined "explain why" in the topic. Three: write and balance fusion equations, conserving mass number and atomic number. Four: calculate the energy released in a fusion reaction, with full working. And five: compare fusion with fission and with fossil fuels, and explain the iron limit that caps how far a star can build. Five clean skills. Let's build them.

NARRATOR: Start with the definition, said the way a marker wants it. Nuclear fusion is the process of joining two light nuclei — light meaning small mass number, the ones on the left of the curve — to form a single, heavier nucleus that is more tightly bound, releasing energy in the process. Notice the three load-bearing words in that sentence: light nuclei, heavier product, more tightly bound. Get those three in and you've defined fusion properly. The classic example is the lightest fusion of all: hydrogen fusing into helium, which is what the Sun does, turning about six hundred million tonnes of hydrogen into helium every second.

NARRATOR: Now the central question — why does joining them release energy? And the answer the marker wants is not "because mass turns into energy." That's true but it's the band-four answer. The full answer runs through the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve, so let's walk the curve. The light nuclei — hydrogen, its heavy isotopes, helium — sit low and on the far left of the curve, low binding energy per nucleon. They are loosely bound. When you fuse two of them, the product sits higher up the curve, further toward the iron peak, with a greater binding energy per nucleon. The product is more tightly bound, more stable. And here's the consequence: because the product is more tightly bound, its total mass is less than the combined mass of the reactants. There's the mass defect. That missing mass is converted to energy by Einstein's relationship — energy equals the mass defect times the speed of light squared — and it leaves the reaction as kinetic energy of the products and as gamma radiation.

NARRATOR: So the full chain, the one you write for full marks: light nuclei have low binding energy per nucleon; the fused product is nearer the iron peak with higher binding energy per nucleon; the product is therefore more tightly bound and has less mass than the reactants; that mass defect is released as energy by energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. Four links — low on the curve, higher product, less mass, energy out. Always tie it to the curve. Always name which side of the iron peak you're on.

QUESTION: You keep saying "always tie it to the curve." What does a weak answer leave out, exactly?

NARRATOR: Here's the marking boundary, and seeing it is how you climb bands. The weak answer says: "Fusion releases energy because mass is destroyed and turned into energy." Two problems. First — and we hammered this last episode — mass is not destroyed, it is converted; "destroyed" breaks conservation and quietly costs you. Second, and more importantly, it never mentions the binding-energy curve at all. It doesn't say the product is more tightly bound; it doesn't say it's moved toward the iron peak. The strong answer adds exactly those two things: the product sits higher on the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve, nearer iron, so it's more tightly bound, and that greater binding corresponds to the lost mass that's released as energy. Source of the energy named — the increase in binding energy. Direction on the curve named — toward iron. Put those two in and a one-mark answer becomes a full-mark answer.

NARRATOR: And here's a beautiful detail that earns a bonus mark and sets up the comparison later. Per nucleon, fusing the very lightest elements releases more energy than fission does — because the curve is steepest right at the light end. Going from hydrogen up to helium climbs a huge vertical distance on that graph; coming down from uranium toward the mid-mass fragments is a much gentler slope. So per nucleon, the steep left-hand climb of fusion beats the gentle right-hand descent of fission. We'll quantify that comparison shortly.

NARRATOR: Now the second skill, and the most heavily examined idea in the whole topic: why does fusion need such extreme temperature and pressure? If hydrogen turning to helium releases all this energy, why isn't a bucket of hydrogen exploding on its own right now? This is the central "explain why," and it has a weak version and a strong version that are worlds apart on the marking scheme. Let me build the strong one piece by piece.

NARRATOR: The problem is that both nuclei are positively charged. Two protons, two deuterons — whatever you're fusing — both carry positive charge, so they experience a long-range electrostatic repulsion that pushes them apart. That repulsion has a name you should use: the Coulomb barrier, or just the Coulomb repulsion. It's the same long-range Coulomb force from the strong-force tug-of-war we did in the radioactivity episode — the one trying to blow heavy nuclei apart. As two nuclei approach, that repulsion gets fiercer and fiercer, a wall they have to climb.

NARRATOR: Now, why would they ever stick if they're repelling? Because of the other force in that tug-of-war: the strong nuclear force. The strong force is powerfully attractive — strong enough to overwhelm the Coulomb repulsion — but, crucially, it acts only over an extremely short range, about ten to the minus fifteen metres, the size of a nucleus itself. It's the Velcro-covered-magnet force: grips ferociously, but only when the surfaces are practically touching. So the two nuclei have to be pushed close enough — within that ten-to-the-minus-fifteen-metre range — for the strong force to grab hold and snap them together. Outside that range, all they feel is the Coulomb wall pushing them apart.

NARRATOR: So the whole challenge is: get the nuclei close enough, against the Coulomb repulsion, for the short-range strong force to take over. How? You fire them at each other hard — very hard. They need enormous kinetic energy to overcome the Coulomb barrier and approach within strong-force range. And here's the link that earns the mark: temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles. So to give every nucleus that much kinetic energy, you need an extraordinarily high temperature. In the core of the Sun, that's about fifteen million kelvin. That is the temperature requirement, and it follows directly from the Coulomb barrier — not from some vague "they need to be hot to join."

NARRATOR: Then the pressure. High temperature gives each collision enough energy, but you also need the collisions to happen often enough to sustain the reaction. That's what high pressure and high density buy you: they pack the nuclei close together so collisions are frequent. High temperature for energetic-enough collisions; high pressure and density for frequent-enough collisions. Both, for the same reason — to keep the fusion going. And in a star, where does that crushing pressure come from? Gravity. A star is so massive that its own gravity crushes the core to fifteen million kelvin and the density of lead many times over, igniting fusion — and once it's lit, the energy pouring out pushes back against gravity and holds the star up. That balance — gravity in, radiation pressure out — is what a stable star is, and it's exactly the idea we'll build the whole of stellar life on in two episodes' time.

QUESTION: Before I move on — pause the player here and have a real go at this one, because it's the exam question. "Explain why nuclear fusion requires extremely high temperatures and pressures." Write the strong answer in your head, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the full-mark answer. Both nuclei are positively charged, so they repel each other through the long-range electrostatic, or Coulomb, force — the Coulomb barrier. To fuse, they must be brought close enough, within about ten to the minus fifteen metres, for the short-range strong nuclear force to overcome that repulsion and bind them. Overcoming the Coulomb barrier requires the nuclei to collide with very high kinetic energy, and since temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy, this demands an extremely high temperature — around fifteen million kelvin in the Sun's core. High pressure and density are also required so that collisions are frequent enough to sustain the reaction. Notice the four mark-earning moves: name the Coulomb repulsion; name the short-range strong force taking over; link high temperature to the kinetic energy needed to beat the barrier; and use pressure for collision frequency. Now hear the weak answer next to it: "Fusion needs high temperature to make the nuclei join." That sentence is true and it is worth almost nothing — it names no force, no barrier, no link to kinetic energy. It's the difference between one mark and four.

NARRATOR: And a trap to kill on sight, because it's a favourite. Do not confuse your forces. The force that binds the nucleons and overcomes the Coulomb repulsion is the strong nuclear force — not the weak force. The weak force mediates beta decay; it changes a quark's flavour, turning a proton into a neutron. It does not bind nucleons. And do not say gravity binds the nucleus either — gravity is fantastically too feeble at the nuclear scale. Gravity only does macroscopic work, supplying the crushing pressure across the whole mass of a star. So at the nuclear scale: strong force binds, Coulomb force repels, weak force does beta decay, and gravity is irrelevant. Get those four straight and you won't trip.

NARRATOR: Third skill: writing fusion equations. Good news — there's nothing new here. You conserve mass number on the top and atomic number on the bottom, exactly as you did for alpha and beta decay back in the radioactivity episode and for fission last episode. The totals of the top numbers must match on both sides; the totals of the bottom numbers must match on both sides. Let me give you the two equations you must know cold.

NARRATOR: First, the Earth reaction — the one reactors like ITER are chasing. Deuterium plus tritium. Deuterium is heavy hydrogen: one proton, one neutron, mass number two, atomic number one. Tritium is even heavier hydrogen: one proton, two neutrons, mass number three, atomic number one. Fuse them and you get helium-4 — mass number four, atomic number two — plus a single free neutron, mass number one, atomic number zero. Check the conservation: top numbers, two plus three is five, and four plus one is five — balanced. Bottom numbers, one plus one is two, and two plus zero is two — balanced. That reaction releases about seventeen point six mega-electron-volts, and we'll work it out in full in a moment. One thing the exam likes to test: that free neutron carries away most of the energy, and because neutrons are uncharged and hard to contain, that's part of what makes the reaction tricky to harness.

NARRATOR: Second, the stellar prototype — the first step of how the Sun does it. Two protons — two hydrogen-1 nuclei — fuse, and one of them converts into a neutron in the process, emitting a positron and a neutrino. The product is deuterium, mass number two, atomic number one, plus that positron and neutrino. So two hydrogen-1 nuclei go to one deuterium nucleus, plus a positron, plus a neutrino. That proton-turning-into-a-neutron is a beta-plus process — the weak force again — and the positron it spits out is a piece of antimatter we'll meet properly next episode, when it annihilates with an electron inside a PET scanner. This first step is the opening move of the proton-proton chain, the full sequence that powers the Sun, which we cash in completely in the stellar nucleosynthesis episode. For now, just know it as the prototype: hydrogen fusing toward helium, conserving mass number and atomic number like everything else.

QUESTION: Why does a positron and a neutrino come out of that first step at all? Couldn't two protons just stick together?

NARRATOR: They can't, and the reason is charge and stability. Two protons stuck together would be helium-2 — two protons, no neutrons — which is not bound; it flies straight back apart. To make a stable light nucleus you need a neutron in there, so one of the two protons has to convert into a neutron on the spot. That conversion is beta-plus decay: a proton becomes a neutron, and to conserve charge it emits a positron, the positive antiparticle of the electron, and to conserve energy and momentum and lepton number it emits a neutrino. So the positron and neutrino aren't optional extras — they're forced on you by the conservation laws, exactly the same conservation-law bookkeeping that predicted the neutrino in the first place, back in the Chadwick and beta-decay story. Conservation laws don't bend for convenience.

NARRATOR: Right — let's compute. This is the fourth skill, and the deuterium-tritium reaction is the canonical worked example, so I'll narrate every step. The full working is in Listing 1, and the masses I'm using are in Listing 2. We want the energy released when deuterium fuses with tritium to give helium-4 plus a neutron.

NARRATOR: Step one: the mass defect, which is the total mass of the reactants minus the total mass of the products. Working in atomic mass units. Deuterium is two point zero one four one zero atomic mass units. Tritium is three point zero one six zero five. Add them: the reactants come to five point zero three zero one five atomic mass units. Now the products. Helium-4 is four point zero zero two six zero three, and the neutron is one point zero zero eight six six five. Add them: the products come to five point zero one one two seven atomic mass units. The mass defect is reactants minus products: five point zero three zero one five minus five point zero one one two seven, which is zero point zero one eight eight four atomic mass units. That's the missing mass — and notice it's tiny, under two-hundredths of an atomic mass unit. Watch what it's worth.

NARRATOR: Step two: convert that mass defect to energy. Use the data-sheet shortcut from the binding-energy episode — multiply the mass defect in atomic mass units by nine hundred and thirty-one point five to get the energy in mega-electron-volts. Zero point zero one eight eight four times nine hundred and thirty-one point five is about seventeen point six mega-electron-volts. There's your headline figure: about seventeen point six mega-electron-volts released per deuterium-tritium fusion event. Some textbooks quote seventeen point six nine using slightly more precise intermediate masses — either is fine; the number to remember is about seventeen point six.

NARRATOR: And here it is again — the trap, said loudly because it's the most common error on this calculation. When you multiply by nine hundred and thirty-one point five, you do NOT also divide by the speed of light squared. The nine-thirty-one-point-five already has units of mega-electron-volts per c-squared; the c-squared is done for you. Multiply by nine hundred and thirty-one point five and stop. If you also divide by the speed of light squared, you'll be wrong by about ten to the seventeen and your units will be nonsense. Multiply, full stop.

NARRATOR: If you'd rather go the long way — and the exam sometimes makes you — you convert the mass defect to kilograms first, then use energy equals mass times the speed of light squared in full. Zero point zero one eight eight four atomic mass units is about three point one four times ten to the minus twenty-nine kilograms. Multiply by the speed of light squared, which is nine times ten to the sixteen, and you get about two point nine times ten to the minus twelve joules — which, divided by the energy of one mega-electron-volt, comes back to the same seventeen point six mega-electron-volts. Both routes, same answer. That cross-check is in Listing 1. Pick the shortcut in the exam unless they force joules on you; it's faster and the trap is the only thing to watch.

NARRATOR: Now feel how big seventeen point six mega-electron-volts is per event, and that leads us straight to the fifth skill — comparing fusion, fission, and fossil fuels. Let's stack them up, because "compare the energy released by nuclear reactions with fossil fuels" is a named syllabus comparison and they want orders of magnitude. Burning fossil fuel is a chemical reaction — it rearranges electrons. Burning a single carbon atom in coal releases only about ten electron-volts. A single fission event releases about two hundred mega-electron-volts — that's two hundred million electron-volts, roughly twenty million times more than burning one atom. That's the comparison we made last episode. Now fusion. A single deuterium-tritium event releases about seventeen point six mega-electron-volts — less per event than one fission of uranium, which is two hundred. So per event, fission wins.

NARRATOR: But — and this is the subtlety the exam rewards — the syllabus comparison is per unit mass, not per event. And per unit mass, or per nucleon, fusion releases more energy than fission. Why? Because fusion involves only a handful of nucleons each time, sitting on the steep part of the curve, whereas a fission event involves over two hundred nucleons on the gentle part. Divide the energy by the number of nucleons and fusion comes out ahead. So state your basis of comparison clearly. Per single event, fission's two hundred mega-electron-volts beats fusion's seventeen. But per kilogram of fuel, the order is: fusion is greater than fission, and both are vastly greater than chemical burning. Remember it as the mnemonic Fusion Feeds the Cosmos — Fusion, then Fission, then Chemical, in descending energy per unit mass. Fusion feeds the cosmos; fission feeds our reactors; chemical feeds your campfire.

QUESTION: If fusion gives more energy per kilogram and its waste is cleaner, why on Earth aren't we running on fusion already?

NARRATOR: That's exactly the right question, and it's a real exam point about viability. The problem is everything we built in the second skill: confinement. To fuse, you need matter at tens or hundreds of millions of kelvin, dense and held together long enough for collisions to sustain the reaction. A star does this for free — gravity supplies the crushing pressure across an enormous mass, and the star confines itself. On Earth we have no easy way to confine matter that hot and that dense. There is no material container — anything that hot vaporises instantly — so reactors like ITER use powerful magnetic fields to hold a plasma, a gas so hot its electrons have been stripped off, and they need temperatures of hundreds of millions of kelvin, even higher than the Sun's core, to make up for the lower pressure we can achieve. So far we've only managed brief net energy gains in the lab, or uncontrolled fusion in a thermonuclear bomb, which uses a fission bomb as the match to reach fusion temperatures. Sustained, controlled, net-positive fusion power is still not viable. So if a question asks whether fusion is a viable energy source today, the answer is no — promising, cleaner waste, abundant fuel, but the confinement problem isn't solved. Stars manage it only because gravity does the confining for free.

NARRATOR: Now the last big idea, and it closes the loop with the binding-energy curve we opened on: the iron limit. Fusion releases energy only as long as you're climbing toward the iron peak — only up to iron-56. Once you reach iron, you're at the very top of the curve, the most tightly bound nucleus there is. Fuse two nuclei past iron and the product sits lower on the curve, less tightly bound — which means it would have more mass than the reactants, so you'd have to put energy in rather than get it out. Fusion past iron costs energy; it doesn't release it. So iron is the heaviest element a star can build by ordinary energy-releasing fusion. The same curve that enables fusion on the way up also forbids it past the peak. That's the iron limit, and it's the reason stars eventually die: a massive star builds an iron core, fusion can no longer hold it up, and it collapses. That collapse is the supernova story — and it's where every element heavier than iron actually comes from, forged in the violence of the explosion, not by ordinary fusion. We pay all of that off in the stellar nucleosynthesis and supernovae episodes. For now, the exam line is simply: fusion releases energy up to iron-56; beyond iron it consumes energy; therefore iron is the heaviest element built by fusion in a star.

QUESTION: So the same graph explains fission, fusion, and the iron limit?

NARRATOR: One graph, three results, and if you can say this in one breath you've mastered the binding-energy curve. Everything wants to move toward the iron peak, because that's where binding energy per nucleon is highest and nuclei are most stable. Heavy nuclei on the right of the peak release energy by splitting and coming down toward iron — that's fission. Light nuclei on the left release energy by joining and climbing up toward iron — that's fusion. And the peak itself is a wall: nothing past iron can fuse and release energy, because there's nowhere higher to go — that's the iron limit. Fission down from the right, fusion up from the left, both arriving at iron, and iron the dead end. That single sentence is worth a small fortune of marks across this module, and you'll meet it a third time when we explain why a supergiant star stops at iron.

NARRATOR: Let me also give you the names, quickly, because a "describe the development of our understanding" question can ask for them. Arthur Eddington proposed, around nineteen twenty-six, that fusion of hydrogen is what powers the Sun — before anyone could prove it. The Australian physicist Mark Oliphant, working with Rutherford in nineteen thirty-two, first observed fusion in the laboratory and discovered tritium and helium-3 by firing heavy-hydrogen nuclei at a heavy-hydrogen target. And Hans Bethe worked out the detailed proton-proton chain and the CNO cycle in nineteen thirty-eight, which earned him a Nobel Prize — that's the physics we'll lay out fully in the stellar episode.
