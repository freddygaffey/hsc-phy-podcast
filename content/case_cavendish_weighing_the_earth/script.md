NARRATOR: In 1687, Isaac Newton published the most important book in the history of physics. The Principia. In it, he wrote down the law of universal gravitation, and with it, he explained the motion of everything. The fall of an apple. The arc of a cannonball. The orbit of the Moon. The path of a comet that wouldn't return for another seventy-five years. Give Newton the position and speed of a planet today, and he could tell you where it would be in a thousand years. The whole clockwork solar system, laid out in a single equation.

NARRATOR: And yet there was one number Newton could not give you. One simple, physical, almost childish question that his beautiful law could not answer. How heavy is the Earth? How much does the ground under your feet actually weigh? Newton could tell you the trajectory of a comet to the day. He could not tell you the mass of the planet he was standing on. And that answer would have to wait. Not ten years, not fifty. It would wait one hundred and eleven years, until a strange, silent, fabulously wealthy man in a garden shed in south London, working with two lead balls and a thin twisted wire, finally weighed the world.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how he did it.

NARRATOR: Let me start with why Newton was stuck, because it's more subtle than it sounds. Newton's law of universal gravitation says that any two masses attract each other with a force. That force is proportional to the first mass, multiplied by the second mass, divided by the square of the distance between their centres. Double one of the masses, you double the force. Move them twice as far apart, the force drops to a quarter. It's the famous inverse-square law. But there's one more piece. To turn that proportionality into an actual number of newtons, you have to multiply by a constant. A single number that sets the overall strength of gravity in the universe. Today we call it big G, the universal gravitational constant. Newton's law, in full, says the gravitational force equals big G, times the first mass, times the second mass, divided by the distance squared.

NARRATOR: And here is the trap that Newton could not escape. He knew the law was correct. He had spectacular evidence. He could watch the Moon orbit the Earth, and from the size and timing of that orbit, he could work out one thing with great precision. He could work out the product. He could work out big G multiplied by the mass of the Earth. That combination, big G times the Earth's mass, controls how fast things orbit, how hard you're pulled to the ground, how the Moon swings around us. The whole solar system runs on that product. But a product is two numbers tangled together. Newton could find big G times the mass of the Earth. He could never separate them. He could not say, this much of it is big G, and this much of it is the Earth.

NARRATOR: Think about what that means. Every gravitational measurement you could make in Newton's day involved the Earth. Drop a ball, weigh yourself, watch the Moon. The Earth was always one of the two masses. So the Earth's mass and big G were always multiplied together, always inseparable, like two unknowns in a single equation. One equation, two unknowns. It can't be solved.

QUESTION: So how do you ever break them apart?

NARRATOR: You need a second, completely independent equation. And the only way to get one is to measure gravity in a situation where the Earth is not involved at all. You have to measure the gravitational pull between two ordinary objects. Two objects whose masses you already know, sitting on a laboratory bench. If you can measure the tiny force of attraction between, say, two lead balls, and you know exactly how heavy those balls are and how far apart they sit, then Newton's law gives you big G directly. The Earth never enters into it. And once you have big G on its own, you go back to that old tangled product, big G times the mass of the Earth, divide out the G you just measured, and out falls the mass of the Earth. The whole planet, weighed.

NARRATOR: It sounds straightforward when you say it like that. It is one of the hardest measurements anyone had ever attempted. Because gravity, the force that holds galaxies together and crushes stars, is on a human scale almost unbelievably feeble. Hold a fridge magnet over a paperclip. That little magnet lifts the paperclip off the table, beating the gravitational pull of the entire Earth, six thousand billion billion tonnes of planet, with a magnet you got free on a cereal box. Gravity is, by a vast margin, the weakest of the fundamental forces. And the gravitational attraction between two objects you could fit in a room is so small it is almost not there at all. To measure it, you would need an instrument of almost supernatural sensitivity. And you would need a person patient enough, obsessive enough, and rich enough to spend years chasing a force the weight of a grain of sand.

NARRATOR: Enter Henry Cavendish.

NARRATOR: Henry Cavendish was born on the tenth of October, seventeen thirty-one, in Nice, on the Mediterranean coast. His mother had travelled there for her health. She died when he was not yet two years old. He was English aristocracy of the very highest rank, connected through both parents to dukes, to the Dukes of Devonshire and the Dukes of Kent. He would, in time, inherit a fortune so enormous that he became the single largest depositor in the entire Bank of England. He was, quite possibly, the richest man of science who has ever lived.

NARRATOR: And he cared about none of it. Cavendish wore the same style of faded, old-fashioned coat for decades, a coat that had gone out of fashion in his youth and which he simply never updated. He had no interest in money except as something that accumulated in his bank account while he ignored it. His one consuming passion was measurement. Precise, careful, relentless measurement of the physical world.

NARRATOR: But to call him merely eccentric undersells it. Henry Cavendish was one of the most profoundly, painfully shy human beings ever recorded. He found human contact almost unbearable. He could speak, with effort, to one person at a time, and only about science. Put him in a group and he would fall silent, or flee. He communicated with his female servants exclusively in writing, leaving notes on the hall table, because he could not stand to meet them in person. There is a story, possibly polished by retelling but rooted in truth, that when he once encountered a housemaid on the stairs, he was so distressed that he had a second staircase built at the back of his house, purely so he would never risk bumping into a servant again. He dined alone. At Royal Society dinners, fellow scientists learned that the way to get a response from Cavendish was never to address him directly. You spoke into the air, as if to no one, in his general vicinity, and if you were lucky, an answer might come back. Modern writers, including the neurologist Oliver Sacks, have suggested that Cavendish showed many of the traits of what we would now call autism. Whatever the label, the result was a mind of extraordinary, undistracted focus, locked onto the physical world and almost nothing else.

NARRATOR: The historian Russell McCormmach captured him beautifully. He wrote that we might think of Cavendish as the man who weighed superlatives. Over his career, Cavendish measured the most valuable thing, gold. The vilest, alcohol. The lightest, hydrogen, a gas he was the first to properly characterise. And the heaviest of all, the Earth itself. His reputation for accuracy was so total that the British government actually employed him to weigh and measure spirits, alcohol, for the purposes of taxation. When Henry Cavendish told you the density of something, that was simply the end of the discussion.

NARRATOR: So this was the man. But the experiment that made him immortal did not begin with him. It began with his friend, a country clergyman, and one of the most criminally underrated geniuses in the whole history of science. His name was the Reverend John Michell.

NARRATOR: John Michell was the rector of a parish in Yorkshire, a clergyman in a small town called Thornhill. And in his spare time, in between his church duties, he thought thoughts that were a century or two ahead of everyone else. It was Michell who, in seventeen eighty-three, first conceived of what we now call a black hole. He reasoned that if you made a star massive enough, its gravity would be so strong that light itself could not escape from its surface, and the star would become invisible, a dark star. He worked this out in the seventeen eighties. We did not take the idea seriously again until the twentieth century. Michell was also the first person to suggest using binary stars, two stars orbiting each other, as a way to weigh stars. He more or less invented the science of seismology after the Lisbon earthquake. The man was a fountain of ideas a hundred years early.

NARRATOR: And John Michell designed an instrument. An instrument so delicate it could, in principle, measure the gravitational attraction between two lead balls on a tabletop. He called it a torsion balance. Let me tell you how it works, because the cleverness of it is the heart of this entire story.

NARRATOR: Imagine a thin wire, hanging vertically, fixed at the top. From the bottom of that wire, you hang a horizontal rod, attached at its middle point, so the rod hangs level, like the beam of a tiny see-saw, free to rotate. Now, on each end of that rod, you fix a small lead ball. The whole thing dangles there, perfectly balanced, twisting freely on the wire.

NARRATOR: Here is the beautiful part. A wire like this resists being twisted. If you rotate the rod a little, the wire fights back, trying to untwist itself and return the rod to where it started. And the key fact, the thing that makes the whole experiment possible, is that a thin wire resists twisting only very, very weakly. The restoring force of a thin wire under torsion is fantastically gentle. Which means that even a minuscule sideways push on those little lead balls, a push far too small to register on any ordinary scale, will twist the wire by a measurable amount. The torsion balance is, in effect, the most sensitive force-measuring device of its age. It converts an almost non-existent force into a visible rotation. It's a way of seeing the invisible.

NARRATOR: So Michell's plan was this. Take the hanging rod with its two small lead balls. Then bring two enormous, heavy lead spheres up close, one near each small ball, positioned so that their gravitational pull tugs the small balls sideways, twisting the rod around. Measure how far it twists. Because you know how feebly the wire resists, you can work backwards from the twist to the force. And that force is gravity, pure gravity, between masses you can hold in your hands. No Earth involved.

NARRATOR: It was a magnificent idea. But John Michell was a busy parish priest, and he was getting old, and the instrument was monstrously difficult to build and to use. He worked on it, on and off, for years. He never finished his measurements. On the twenty-first of April, seventeen ninety-three, John Michell died, sixty-eight years old, his great apparatus built but its central question unanswered. He had constructed a machine to weigh the world, and he never got to use it.

NARRATOR: After his death, the apparatus passed first to a Cambridge professor named Francis Wollaston, who had no particular use for it. And from Wollaston, it came at last to the one person in England with the wealth, the patience, the obsessive precision, and the sheer bloody-minded focus to finish the job. It came to Henry Cavendish.

NARRATOR: By the time Cavendish received Michell's apparatus, he was already an old man, in his mid-sixties. He could easily have left it in a corner. Instead, he took it apart and rebuilt it almost entirely, keeping Michell's basic design but reconstructing it to his own exacting standards. And as he did, he confronted the central, maddening problem of the whole enterprise. The signal he was trying to measure was so small that almost anything could swamp it.

NARRATOR: Let me give you a sense of the scale. The two large lead spheres Cavendish used weighed about a hundred and fifty-eight kilograms each. Picture a lead ball thirty centimetres across, about the size of a basketball but made of solid lead, weighing as much as two grown adults. The small balls on the rotating rod weighed about seven hundred and thirty grams each, a bit lighter than a bag of sugar. And when he brought a giant sphere up close to one of the little ones, the gravitational force pulling them together was around one hundred and seventy nanonewtons. A nanonewton is a billionth of a newton. One hundred and seventy nanonewtons is roughly the weight of a single grain of fine sand. That is the force Cavendish set out to measure, with eighteenth-century technology, by hand.

QUESTION: How do you even begin to measure something that faint without ruining it just by being in the room?

NARRATOR: That question is exactly the one Cavendish obsessed over, and his answer is what separates a genius experimenter from everyone else. He realised that he himself was the biggest threat to the measurement. The warmth of his own body would create tiny air currents in the room. Those currents, gentle as a sleeping baby's breath, would push the rod around far harder than gravity ever could. Even standing near the apparatus, his own body had a gravitational pull. A draught, a temperature difference between two walls, a faint static charge on the metal, any one of these could completely drown the signal he was hunting.

NARRATOR: So Cavendish did something remarkable. He built the entire apparatus inside a sealed wooden case, a mahogany box almost two metres wide, to keep out air currents. And he put that case inside a closed, purpose-built shed in the garden of his estate at Clapham Common, in south London. And then, crucially, he never went inside the shed while measuring. He operated the whole experiment from outside. He moved the huge hundred-and-fifty-eight-kilogram lead spheres into position using a system of ropes and pulleys that he could work from beyond the shed wall, without setting foot in the room and without breathing on his instrument. And to read the result, the tiny twist of the rod, he mounted telescopes in the walls of the shed and peered through them from outside, lit by lamplight, reading a finely divided scale to catch a movement smaller than the width of your finger.

NARRATOR: It is one of the most careful, disciplined experiments ever performed. Cavendish removed himself from his own experiment. He became a pair of distant eyes at a telescope, watching a sealed box in a locked shed, where two lead balls hung from a wire and whispered to each other in the language of gravity.

NARRATOR: He began in earnest in seventeen ninety-seven, into seventeen ninety-eight. He was sixty-seven years old. And he ran the experiment again, and again, and again. Seventeen separate trials in all. Each one took enormous patience, because the rod did not simply swing to a new position and stop. When the gravitational pull tugged it, it would swing past the balance point, then back, then forward again, oscillating slowly like the most languid pendulum imaginable. In his first trials, a single full swing took about fifteen minutes. He had to time these oscillations precisely, because, as we'll see, the rhythm of that swing was itself part of the calculation. Later he stiffened the wire to speed things up, bringing the swing down to around seven and a half minutes, and ran fourteen more trials.

NARRATOR: His neighbours, watching this odd, silent old man tending his mysterious shed at all hours, are said to have told their children that it was the place where the world was weighed.

NARRATOR: Now let me show you how he turned that slow, almost imperceptible twisting of a rod into the mass of a planet. The physics is genuinely lovely, and it comes together in two steps.

NARRATOR: The first problem is the wire. To get a force out of the twist, Cavendish needed to know exactly how stiff the wire was, how much it resisted being turned. How do you measure the stiffness of a wire that resists so feebly you can barely detect it at all? And here is the trick that makes the whole experiment work. You let the rod swing. You give it a little push and you watch it oscillate back and forth, and you time how long each swing takes. Because the period of that oscillation, the time for one full swing, depends directly on the stiffness of the wire. A stiffer wire snaps the rod back faster, giving a quick swing. A floppier wire lets it wander lazily, giving a long, slow swing. So by timing the oscillation, those fifteen-minute swings, Cavendish could calculate the exact stiffness of his wire without ever needing to apply a known force to it. The swinging rod measured its own suspension. That is the masterstroke buried inside this experiment.

NARRATOR: Once he knew the stiffness of the wire, the rest follows. When the great lead spheres were brought close and the rod settled at its new twisted position, he was watching a balance of two torques, two turning effects. On one side, gravity, pulling the small balls toward the big ones, trying to twist the rod. On the other side, the wire, resisting, trying to twist it back. At the point where the rod comes to rest, those two are exactly equal. The twisting effect of gravity equals the twisting effect of the wire. And because he now knew the wire's stiffness, and he could see how far it had twisted, he could calculate the gravitational force precisely. About one hundred and seventy nanonewtons, the weight of a grain of sand, measured in a garden shed in seventeen ninety-eight.

NARRATOR: And from that force, big G falls straight out of Newton's law. He knew the force. He knew the mass of each ball, large and small. He knew the distance between their centres, about twenty-two and a half centimetres. Newton's law says the force equals big G times one mass times the other, divided by the distance squared. Every quantity in that equation was now known except big G. So he rearranged it and solved for the one unknown. For the first time in human history, someone had measured the strength of gravity itself, the constant big G, in an experiment that did not involve the Earth at all. The full calculation, the way the oscillation period gives the stiffness and the stiffness gives the force and the force gives big G, is laid out step by step in Listing one.

QUESTION: And then, from big G, you finally get the mass of the Earth?

NARRATOR: Exactly. This is the payoff that had waited a hundred and eleven years. We can run it ourselves, with the modern numbers, and it's a calculation any HSC student can do. You stand on the surface of the Earth, and you feel a gravitational field strength of about nine point eight newtons per kilogram. That is just the force of gravity on every kilogram of you, and it is exactly the same number as the acceleration due to gravity, nine point eight metres per second squared. The two are the same quantity wearing two different sets of units. And that field strength, by Newton's law, equals big G times the mass of the Earth, divided by the Earth's radius squared. This is the second great equation of the topic, the gravitational field strength of a planet, big G times the central mass over the radius squared. Notice that it does not depend on the mass of the object you drop. A feather and a cannonball feel the very same field. Notice too that it falls away as the inverse square of distance, so the field is strongest at the surface and weakens as you climb. Close to the ground, over the scale of a room, it is so nearly uniform that we treat it as constant and simply call it nine point eight. Now look at that equation at the surface. The field strength, nine point eight, we measure every time we drop something. The Earth's radius, about six point three seven million metres, had been known since the ancient Greeks. And now, thanks to Cavendish, we finally have big G as well. So there is exactly one unknown left in the whole equation, the mass of the Earth. Rearrange it, and the mass of the Earth equals the gravitational field strength times the radius squared, divided by big G. Put the numbers in, and out comes a staggering figure. The Earth weighs about five point nine seven times ten to the twenty-four kilograms, which your data sheet rounds to six times ten to the twenty-four. Roughly six thousand billion billion tonnes. The full working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: That is the number Newton could never reach. For a hundred and eleven years it sat just out of his grasp, hidden inside that tangled product of big G and the Earth's mass. Cavendish, with his lead balls and his twisted wire, finally prised the two apart, and the mass of the entire planet dropped out of the equation.

NARRATOR: But here is something that almost everyone gets wrong about this experiment, and it's worth getting right, because the truth is more interesting than the legend.

NARRATOR: Henry Cavendish did not set out to measure big G. He never calculated big G. The symbol big G, the universal gravitational constant, does not appear anywhere in his work. In fact, the symbol big G for the gravitational constant wouldn't be commonly used by physicists until around eighteen seventy-three, three quarters of a century after Cavendish was finished. Cavendish was after something more concrete, something he could picture. He wanted to know the density of the Earth. How tightly packed is the stuff our planet is made of, compared to water? His paper, read to the Royal Society on the twenty-first of June, seventeen ninety-eight, is titled, plainly, Experiments to Determine the Density of the Earth.

NARRATOR: And his answer was beautiful. He found that the Earth, on average, is about five and a half times denser than water. Five point four eight times, in his published figure. Now think about what that tells you. The rocks at the surface, the granite under our feet, are only about two and a half to three times denser than water. Liquid water itself is, by definition, one. So if the whole planet, averaged out, is five and a half times denser than water, far denser than the rock at its surface, then the inside of the Earth must be something much, much heavier than rock. Something like iron. Cavendish's single measurement, made with two lead balls in a shed, was the first real evidence that the Earth has a dense metallic core. He couldn't see inside the planet. But he could weigh it, and from its weight he could deduce that its heart is made of metal. We did not confirm that directly, with the seismic waves of earthquakes, until well into the twentieth century. Cavendish saw it first, in seventeen ninety-eight, on a torsion balance.

NARRATOR: There's a lovely footnote to all this precision. In eighteen twenty-one, eleven years after Cavendish had died, an astronomer named Francis Baily went back through the old paper, rechecking the arithmetic. And he found a small mistake. Cavendish had made a slip in his calculations, and his published density of five point four eight was very slightly off. Corrected, his own data actually gave five point four four eight times the density of water. Here is the remarkable thing. The modern, accepted value for the density of the Earth is five point five one four. Cavendish, working alone, by hand, in a garden, in seventeen ninety-eight, with a wooden rod and a twisted wire, landed within about one percent of the right answer. One percent. With no electronics, no computers, no modern materials, measuring a force the weight of a grain of sand. It is one of the great triumphs of experimental physics, and it stood essentially unimproved for the better part of a century.

NARRATOR: So how did it come to be called the experiment that measured big G, when Cavendish never measured big G? That reframing happened later. In eighteen eighty-nine, a British physicist named C.V. Boys gave a presentation that recast Cavendish's work in modern terms, describing it as an experiment to find the constant of gravitation, from which the density of the Earth then follows. Boys went on, in eighteen ninety-five, to perform his own improved version, using finer fibres and smaller balls. And his framing stuck. For more than a century afterwards, textbooks taught generations of students that Cavendish set out to measure big G. He didn't. He set out to weigh the Earth, and he did. The big G was always sitting there inside his numbers, waiting for someone to pull it out and give it a name.

NARRATOR: Now, here is the part of the story that should genuinely surprise you. You might assume that big G, having been measured back in seventeen ninety-eight, and being one of the most fundamental constants in all of physics, must by now be known to fantastic precision. We've had two hundred and twenty years and laser interferometers and atomic clocks. Surely we've nailed it down.

NARRATOR: We have not. Big G is, to this day, the most poorly known of all the fundamental constants of nature. Let me give you the comparison, because it's almost embarrassing. The speed of light is known exactly, by definition. Planck's constant, the cornerstone of quantum mechanics, is known to better than one part in a billion. The fine structure constant, to a fraction of a part per million. And big G? The best modern value is six point six seven four three times ten to the minus eleven, and it is uncertain at the level of about twenty-two parts per million. That makes it tens of thousands of times less precisely known than the other great constants. Newton's gravitational constant, the very first one anyone tried to measure, the one Cavendish bagged to within a whisker over two centuries ago, remains the worst-measured number in fundamental physics.

QUESTION: How is that possible? Why is gravity, of all things, so hard to pin down precisely?

NARRATOR: Because of the same brutal fact that made Cavendish's experiment so hard in the first place. Gravity is staggeringly weak. Every other fundamental constant can be measured in experiments where you can shield out the rest of the universe. You can put your apparatus in a metal box and block out stray electric and magnetic fields. But there is no such thing as a gravity shield. You cannot block out gravity. Which means that when you try to measure the tiny gravitational pull between two balls in your lab, the gravitational pull of everything else is also tugging on your apparatus. The walls of the building. The experimenter standing nearby. The water table rising and falling under the laboratory after it rains. The mountains in the distance. All of it pulls, and none of it can be switched off.

NARRATOR: And it gets stranger. Since around nineteen ninety, teams around the world have measured big G using beautiful, modern, state-of-the-art equipment, the descendants of Cavendish's torsion balance. And their results disagree with each other. Not by a little. They disagree by far more than their own stated error bars say they should. One careful team gets one value, another equally careful team gets a value that's different by several times their combined uncertainties. Somebody, somewhere, has a systematic error they cannot find, and it has happened to almost everyone. Two hundred and twenty years after Cavendish, the precise value of big G is still, genuinely, an open and unsolved problem in physics. There are experiments running right now, today, trying to settle it.

NARRATOR: Which means that the strange, shy aristocrat in his Clapham shed didn't just answer Newton's old question. He opened one that we still haven't fully closed.

NARRATOR: Henry Cavendish died on the twenty-fourth of February, eighteen ten, alone, as he had largely lived. He is said to have arranged even his death to be private, asking to be left undisturbed in his final hours. He published only a fraction of the work he actually did. Decades after his death, the physicist James Clerk Maxwell went through Cavendish's unpublished papers and discovered, to his astonishment, that Cavendish had quietly anticipated huge swathes of electrical science, Ohm's law, the relationship between charge and force, work that others had later been credited for, all sitting unpublished in the drawers of a man too indifferent to fame to tell anyone. In his honour, the great physics laboratory at Cambridge, where the electron and the structure of DNA would later be discovered, was named the Cavendish Laboratory.

NARRATOR: And Michell's torsion balance, the instrument a country priest dreamed up and never got to finish? A modern gravitational physicist once observed that Michell's torsion balance has been the basis of essentially every important experiment on gravity ever since. Every refinement, every modern measurement of big G, every test of Newton's law, traces back to that hanging rod and that twisted wire. The design that weighed the Earth is still, two centuries later, how we weigh the universe.

NARRATOR: Newton gave us the law. He told us that gravity reaches across space with a strength proportional to mass and falling off as the square of distance. But a law with an unknown constant in it is a sentence with a blank in it. It took a reclusive genius, a clergyman's forgotten machine, two lead balls, a twisted wire, and a sealed shed in south London to fill in that blank. To turn Newton's elegant proportionality into a real number you could weigh planets with. To take the abstract and make it concrete. That, in the end, is what the Cavendish experiment really did. It didn't just weigh the Earth. It put a number on gravity itself.

NARRATOR: In the New South Wales HSC Physics course, this story is the human evidence behind a single, central thread in Module five, Advanced Mechanics, in the focus area called Motion in gravitational fields. The syllabus asks you to analyse the factors that affect the force of gravity, with reference to Newton's law of universal gravitation, force equals big G times the product of the two masses divided by the distance squared. It asks you to analyse the factors that affect a planet's gravitational field strength, g equals big G times the central mass divided by the radius squared, and to solve problems for the field strength at any point in a gravitational field, including at the surface of a planet. It even asks you to explain why a planet's radial gravitational field looks like a uniform field close to its surface, which is exactly why we can call g a constant nine point eight in the lab. And running underneath every one of those dot points is the constant big G. It is not a number that fell from the sky. It is a measured quantity. It is the number Henry Cavendish pulled out of a twisting wire in seventeen ninety-eight. On your data sheet it is rounded to six point six seven times ten to the minus eleven newton metres squared per kilogram squared, the mass of the Earth to six times ten to the twenty-four kilograms, the radius to six point three seven one million metres, and gravity at the surface to nine point eight. Every time you substitute big G into a gravitation calculation, every time you compute the mass of a planet from its gravitational field strength, you are standing on the result of that experiment in the garden shed. Remember the shed when you write the number.
