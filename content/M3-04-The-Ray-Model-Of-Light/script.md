NARRATOR: Welcome back to Module Three, Waves and Thermodynamics. We've treated waves in general and then sound in detail; today we turn to light. Now, light is a wave, an electromagnetic wave, as we said in the first episode and as you'll prove next year. But for a huge range of everyday optics, mirrors, lenses, prisms, optical fibres, we don't need the full wave picture. We can treat light as travelling in straight lines called rays, and that simpler ray model is today's tool. By the end you'll be able to apply the law of reflection, calculate refraction with the refractive index and Snell's law, find the critical angle for total internal reflection, explain dispersion, and use the inverse-square law for light intensity. This is the physics of how we see, of cameras and glasses and the internet's fibre backbone. But first, a recap, because today's reflection and refraction are last episode's wave behaviours, now applied to light.

NARRATOR: Two episodes ago we met reflection, refraction and diffraction as behaviours of any wave. Reflection bounces a wave off a boundary with the angle of incidence equal to the angle of reflection. Refraction bends a wave when it crosses into a new medium because its speed changes, with the frequency staying constant and the wavelength changing. Today we apply exactly those two behaviours to light, using rays, and we make refraction quantitative with a number called the refractive index. So hold the refraction logic from before: a wave changes speed at a boundary, and if it hits at an angle, it bends. For light, slowing down means bending towards the normal. That single sentence is the heart of today.

QUESTION: Quick check from the wave-behaviour episode. When a wave refracts into a new medium, which quantity stays the same, and what is the immediate cause of the bending? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The frequency stays the same when a wave crosses a boundary, it's fixed by the source. And the bending is caused by the change in the wave's speed in the new medium, the speed change is what makes the direction change when the wave arrives at an angle. Frequency constant, speed change causes the bending. Good. Now let's set up the ray model and build reflection and refraction for light.

NARRATOR: The ray model treats light as travelling in straight lines, rays, that point in the direction the light travels. It's a model, a simplification, it ignores the wave nature, but it's extremely powerful for predicting how light reflects and refracts, how mirrors and lenses form images. We measure all angles in optics from the normal, the line drawn perpendicular to the surface at the point where the ray hits, not from the surface itself. Get that habit now, angles from the normal, because every law today is written that way. The ray model and these conventions are summarised in Listing one.

NARRATOR: First, reflection. When a ray of light hits a surface, it reflects, and the law of reflection says the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, both measured from the normal. A smooth, polished surface like a mirror reflects all the rays in a parallel beam the same way, giving a clear image, that's specular reflection; a rough surface scatters the rays in all directions, that's diffuse reflection, which is why you can see this page from any angle but it doesn't act as a mirror. A plane, flat, mirror forms an image that is upright, the same size as the object, and located as far behind the mirror as the object is in front, and it's a virtual image, meaning the light doesn't actually pass through where the image appears to be; your brain just traces the reflected rays back to an apparent source behind the glass. Curved mirrors, concave and convex, focus or spread the rays to form magnified or reduced images, which is how a shaving mirror enlarges your face and a shop security mirror gives a wide view. The reflection rules are in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now refraction, and this is where the numbers come in. When light passes from one medium into another it changes speed, and if it meets the boundary at an angle it bends. The quantity that captures how much a medium slows light is the refractive index, given the symbol n, defined as the speed of light in a vacuum divided by the speed of light in that medium. So n equals c over v. Because light always travels slower in a medium than in a vacuum, the refractive index is always greater than one: it's about one for air, one point three three for water, around one point five for glass. A higher refractive index means light travels slower in that medium, and we loosely call such a medium optically denser. The key rule for the direction of bending: when light enters a denser, higher-index medium it slows down and bends towards the normal; when it enters a less dense, lower-index medium it speeds up and bends away from the normal. Towards the normal going in to denser, away going out to less dense. That rule, and the definition n equals c over v, are in Listing two.

QUESTION: A retrieval check. Light travels from air into glass. Does it speed up or slow down, and does it bend towards or away from the normal? And if the refractive index of the glass is one point five, how does the speed of light in the glass compare with its speed in a vacuum? Pause the player, answer all three, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Going from air into glass, light enters a denser, higher-index medium, so it slows down and bends towards the normal. And with a refractive index of one point five, the speed of light in the glass is the vacuum speed divided by one point five, so light travels one point five times slower in the glass than in a vacuum, about two times ten to the eight metres per second. Slows down, bends towards the normal, travels one point five times slower. That direction rule, towards the normal entering a denser medium, is worth a guaranteed mark.

NARRATOR: Now the quantitative law of refraction, Snell's law, the workhorse of this whole episode. Snell's law states that the refractive index of the first medium times the sine of the angle of incidence equals the refractive index of the second medium times the sine of the angle of refraction. In symbols, n one sine i equals n two sine r. The angle of incidence i is in the first medium, the angle of refraction r is in the second, both measured from the normal. You can rearrange it to find any one quantity given the others, and that's the standard calculation: given the two indices and the incident angle, find the refracted angle, or given the bending, find an unknown index. There's a worked Snell's-law example in Listing three. The law encodes the bending rule automatically: if n two is bigger than n one, denser second medium, then sine r is smaller than sine i, so r is less than i, the ray bent towards the normal, exactly as the rule says.

QUESTION: Let's use Snell's law. A ray of light in air, refractive index one, strikes a glass block of refractive index one point five at an angle of incidence of thirty degrees. The sine of thirty degrees is zero point five. Find the sine of the angle of refraction. Pause the player, set up Snell's law and solve, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. Snell's law: n one sine i equals n two sine r. Substitute: one times sine thirty equals one point five times sine r, so one times zero point five equals one point five times sine r. Then sine r equals zero point five divided by one point five, which is zero point three three. So the sine of the angle of refraction is zero point three three, and the angle itself is about nineteen degrees. Notice the refracted angle, nineteen degrees, is smaller than the incident angle, thirty degrees: the ray bent towards the normal, because it entered a denser medium, just as the rule predicted. The method is always the same, write n one sine i equals n two sine r, substitute, and solve for the unknown.

NARRATOR: Refraction is also how lenses work, and lenses are where the ray model earns its keep. A lens is a shaped piece of glass that refracts the rays passing through it, at the front surface and again at the back, to bend them to a focus. A convex, or converging, lens is thicker in the middle and bends parallel rays inward to meet at a focal point; it's the lens in a magnifying glass, a camera, and the eye itself, and depending on how far the object sits from it, it forms either a magnified upright image, as in a magnifying glass, or a real inverted image, as on a camera sensor or the retina. A concave, or diverging, lens is thinner in the middle and spreads parallel rays apart, forming a reduced upright image, used to correct short-sightedness. The same towards-the-normal and away-from-the-normal refraction rules govern every ray; a lens just applies them twice on a curved surface to steer light to where you want it. You trace images by drawing a few standard rays and finding where they cross. Lenses and mirrors are both noted in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now a spectacular consequence of refraction: total internal reflection. Consider light travelling the other way, from a denser medium into a less dense one, say from water or glass out into air. Going from dense to less dense, the ray bends away from the normal, so the refracted angle is larger than the incident angle. As you increase the incident angle, the refracted ray bends further and further from the normal, until at a particular incident angle, the critical angle, the refracted ray would lie right along the surface, at ninety degrees from the normal. Beyond that critical angle, the light cannot refract out at all, and instead it is entirely reflected back into the denser medium. That's total internal reflection, and it's total, a hundred percent of the light reflects, no ordinary mirror manages that. The two conditions you must state are: the light must be going from a denser to a less dense medium, and the angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle. The critical angle is found from Snell's law by setting the refracted angle to ninety degrees, which gives the sine of the critical angle equal to n two over n one, or simply one over n when the second medium is air. The critical-angle rule is in Listing two, with a worked value in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "state and explain the conditions required for total internal reflection". The band-six version: total internal reflection occurs when, first, light travels from an optically denser medium, higher refractive index, towards an optically less dense medium, lower refractive index; and second, the angle of incidence at the boundary exceeds the critical angle for those two media. Below the critical angle the light refracts out, bending away from the normal; at the critical angle the refracted ray runs along the boundary; beyond it, no refraction is possible and all the light reflects back into the denser medium. The critical angle is found from Snell's law with the refracted angle set to ninety degrees, giving the sine of the critical angle equal to the ratio of the two indices. The marks are for both conditions, dense-to-less-dense and angle greater than critical, plus the explanation via the critical angle. The weak answer, "the light bounces back inside the glass", earns little because it omits the two conditions. State both conditions, then explain with the critical angle.

NARRATOR: Total internal reflection isn't just a curiosity, it runs the modern world through optical fibres. An optical fibre is a thin glass thread; light entering one end hits the walls at angles greater than the critical angle, so it totally internally reflects again and again, thousands of times, racing down the fibre with almost no loss, even around bends. This carries internet and phone data as pulses of light across oceans, and carries images out of the body in medical endoscopes. The same physics makes a diamond sparkle: diamond's very high refractive index, about two point four, gives it a small critical angle of around twenty-four degrees, so light entering it is very easily trapped by total internal reflection, bouncing around inside before finally escaping, which gives the brilliant flashing you see. Total internal reflection in fibres and gemstones is the headline application, and it's in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. NARRATOR: Now dispersion, which explains rainbows and the prism, and bridges to next year. The refractive index of a medium isn't quite the same for every colour, it's slightly higher for shorter wavelengths, the blue and violet end, than for longer wavelengths, the red end. So when white light, a mixture of all colours, enters a prism, each colour refracts by a slightly different amount, violet bending most, red bending least, and the colours fan out into a spectrum. That spreading of white light into its colours by refraction is called dispersion. A rainbow is dispersion on a grand scale: sunlight enters each raindrop, disperses into colours, reflects off the back of the drop, and disperses again on the way out, sending the spectrum to your eye. Dispersion is direct evidence that white light is a mixture of colours and that colour is tied to wavelength, an idea you'll develop fully in Module Seven when you study the electromagnetic spectrum and the wave model of light. Dispersion is noted in Listing one.

NARRATOR: The last idea ties back to last episode: the inverse-square law, now for light intensity. A point source of light, like a small bulb or a distant star, radiates its energy outward over the surface of an expanding sphere, so the intensity, the power per unit area, falls off as one over the distance squared, exactly as sound intensity did. Double your distance from a lamp and its light is a quarter as intense; move ten times further and it's a hundredth. We write it as a comparison: the intensity at one distance times that distance squared equals the intensity at another distance times its distance squared, I one r one squared equals I two r two squared. This is why distant stars look faint and why a camera flash lights a near subject far more brightly than the background. The inverse-square law for light is in Listing two, and there's a worked example in Listing three. Notice it's the same law you used for sound, any energy spreading from a point follows it.

QUESTION: An inverse-square check. A light meter reads two hundred lux at one metre from a small lamp. What will it read at four metres from the lamp? Pause the player, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. Use I one r one squared equals I two r two squared. So two hundred times one squared equals I two times four squared, that's two hundred times one equals I two times sixteen. So I two equals two hundred over sixteen, twelve point five lux. Four times the distance gives one over four squared, one sixteenth, of the intensity, so two hundred lux drops to twelve point five lux. The trap, as always with inverse-square, is forgetting to square the distance ratio, it's sixteen, not four. This same law is how astronomers gauge distance: a star of known true brightness looks fainter the further away it is, by the inverse square, so comparing how bright it truly is with how bright it appears gives its distance, a tool you'll meet again in the astrophysics of later study.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can verify the law of reflection and measure refraction with a ray box and a glass block on paper: trace the incident and refracted rays, measure the angles from the normal with a protractor, and confirm Snell's law, even calculate the block's refractive index from the ratio of the sines. You can find a material's critical angle by shining light out through a semicircular block and increasing the angle until the refracted ray vanishes and total internal reflection takes over. You can show dispersion by sending a narrow beam of white light through a prism onto a screen and seeing the spectrum. And you can check the inverse-square law with a light meter at increasing distances from a small bulb. Each is one of today's laws made measurable, and describing one, with the variable changed and the result, is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: measuring angles from the surface instead of from the normal, always from the normal. Trap two: getting the bending direction backwards, entering a denser, higher-index medium bends light towards the normal, not away. Trap three: mixing up i and r, or n one and n two, in Snell's law, keep incidence with medium one, refraction with medium two. Trap four: forgetting that total internal reflection needs both conditions, dense to less dense and angle above the critical angle. Trap five: dropping the square in the inverse-square law, four times the distance is a sixteenth of the intensity, not a quarter. Avoid those five and ray optics is secure.
