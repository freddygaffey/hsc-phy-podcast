Now let's define current.

NARRATOR: Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge. When charges, in a metal wire these are electrons, flow past a point, the current is how much charge passes per second. In symbols, current I equals the charge q divided by the time t, I equals q over t. The unit is the ampere, often just "amp", and one ampere is one coulomb of charge passing per second, which, since a coulomb is a huge number of electrons, means an ordinary one-amp current is something like six times ten to the eighteen electrons streaming past every second. So a current of two amps means two coulombs of charge flow past each second. Current needs a complete, unbroken loop, a circuit, to flow, and it needs a source, a battery or power supply, to drive it. The battery's push comes from the electric field it sets up around the circuit, the field from last episode, now doing a job. Current as charge per time, I equals q over t, is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: A quick word on the particle picture, because it clears up a common puzzle. In a metal, there's a sea of free electrons drifting along when a voltage is applied, but they drift surprisingly slowly, often less than a millimetre per second. So how does a light come on the instant you flick the switch? The answer is that the electric field, which travels through the wire at close to the speed of light, pushes on all the electrons all around the circuit almost simultaneously, so they all start drifting at once, like a tube full of marbles where pushing one end makes a marble pop out the far end immediately, even though no single marble travelled the length of the tube. The current, the flow, starts everywhere at once even though each electron moves slowly. And because charge is conserved, the current is the same all the way round a single loop, the electrons aren't used up or piling up; the same number per second pass every point. That "current is the same around a single loop" idea is the seed of the series-circuit rules next episode.

NARRATOR: Now a point of convention that confuses students every year, so let's nail it. We define conventional current as flowing from the positive terminal of the battery, around the circuit, to the negative terminal. But in a metal wire, the things actually moving are electrons, which are negative, and they flow the other way, from negative to positive. So conventional current and electron flow point in opposite directions. This is purely a historical accident, current was defined before anyone knew electrons existed, and we've kept the convention. For this course, unless told otherwise, you work with conventional current, positive to negative, but you should know that the real carriers in a metal, the electrons, drift the opposite way. Conventional current positive to negative, electron flow negative to positive, that distinction is in Listing one and it's a common exam check. The good news is that for almost every calculation it makes no difference which you picture, the physics of the circuit, the currents and voltages, comes out the same; the direction convention only matters when a question asks you explicitly about the carriers or about magnetic effects later on.

QUESTION: A retrieval one. A current of three amperes flows in a wire. How much charge passes a point in five seconds? And in which direction does conventional current flow, relative to electron flow? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Rearrange I equals q over t to get charge q equals current times time, I times t, three amperes times five seconds, fifteen coulombs. So fifteen coulombs pass in five seconds. And conventional current flows in the opposite direction to electron flow: conventional current goes from positive to negative, while the electrons in the metal drift from negative to positive. Fifteen coulombs, and conventional current opposes electron flow. The calculation is just rearranging I equals q over t, and the direction point is the one to keep crisp.

NARRATOR: Now the second quantity, and the one most often confused with current: voltage, properly called potential difference. Voltage is the energy transferred per unit charge as charge moves between two points. In symbols, voltage V equals the energy, or work, W, divided by the charge q, V equals W over q, measured in volts, where one volt is one joule per coulomb. So a voltage of twelve volts across a component means twelve joules of energy are transferred for every coulomb of charge that passes through it. When the source is a battery, the energy it supplies per unit charge to push current around is called its electromotive force, or EMF, also measured in volts, despite the misleading name it's an energy-per-charge, not a force. Here's the distinction you must hold, and it's worth real marks: current is the flow, how much charge moves per second; voltage is the energy per charge, how much energy each coulomb carries or delivers. They are completely different quantities. Voltage as energy per charge, V equals W over q, is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "distinguish between electric current and voltage", because this is a guaranteed exam item and the weak answer loses easy marks. The band-six version: electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit, defined by I equals q over t and measured in amperes; it describes how much charge is moving per second. Voltage, or potential difference, is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points, defined by V equals W over q and measured in volts; it describes how much energy each unit of charge carries or delivers. So current is about flow and voltage is about energy per charge; they are distinct quantities, and a circuit needs both, a voltage to drive the charge and a resulting current of charge. The marks are for I equals q over t as rate of flow of charge, V equals W over q as energy per charge, and a clear statement that they are different. The weak answer, "voltage pushes the current along", earns little because it gives no definitions and no equations. Name both equations and the flow-versus-energy distinction.

NARRATOR: Now the third quantity, resistance, and the law at the centre of the episode. As charge flows through a component, it collides with the atoms of the material and is impeded; resistance is the measure of how much a component opposes the flow of current. Resistance R is defined as the voltage across a component divided by the current through it, R equals V over I, measured in ohms. Rearranged, this gives the famous Ohm's law: the voltage across a conductor equals the current through it times its resistance, V equals I R. A high resistance means that for a given voltage, only a small current flows; a low resistance lets a large current through. The three forms, R equals V over I, V equals I R, and I equals V over R, are the same relationship rearranged, and they're the workhorse of circuit calculations. The Ohm's-law trio is in Listing two, with worked examples in Listing three.

NARRATOR: It's worth knowing what actually determines a component's resistance, because it explains a lot of circuit design. Four things set the resistance of a wire. First, length: a longer wire has more resistance, because the charges collide with more atoms on a longer journey, resistance is proportional to length. Second, cross-sectional area: a thicker wire has less resistance, because there's more room for charge to flow, like a wider pipe, resistance is inversely proportional to area. Third, the material: some materials, like copper and silver, are intrinsically good conductors with low resistance, while others, like nichrome, resist more, this property is called resistivity. Fourth, temperature: for a metal, higher temperature means higher resistance, because the hotter atoms vibrate more and impede the electrons more, which is exactly the effect behind the non-ohmic filament lamp we'll meet in a moment. So long, thin, high-resistivity, hot conductors resist most; short, thick, low-resistivity, cool ones resist least. That's why connecting wires are short, thick copper, low resistance, while a heater element is long, thin, high-resistivity nichrome, deliberately high resistance to get hot.

QUESTION: Let's use Ohm's law. A resistor has a voltage of twelve volts across it and a current of three amperes flowing through it. What is its resistance? And if you doubled the voltage to twenty-four volts, with the resistance unchanged, what current would flow? Pause the player, work both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. Resistance is voltage over current, R equals V over I, twelve volts divided by three amperes, four ohms. For the second part, with the resistance fixed at four ohms and the voltage doubled to twenty-four volts, the current is voltage over resistance, I equals V over R, twenty-four over four, six amperes. So the resistance is four ohms, and doubling the voltage doubles the current to six amperes. Notice that last point: for a fixed resistance, current is proportional to voltage, double the voltage and you double the current. That proportionality is the signature of an ohmic conductor, which is exactly where we go next.

NARRATOR: Ohm's law looks universal, but it has an important limitation the syllabus wants you to understand, and it splits components into two kinds. A component is ohmic if its resistance stays constant as the voltage changes, so the current is directly proportional to the voltage. If you plot current against voltage for an ohmic conductor, like a metal resistor at constant temperature, you get a straight line through the origin, the constant gradient reflecting the constant resistance. Ohm's law, in the strict sense, applies only to these ohmic conductors at constant temperature. A component is non-ohmic if its resistance changes with conditions, so the current-voltage graph is not a straight line, it curves. The classic example is a filament lamp: as more current flows, the filament heats up, and a hotter metal filament has a higher resistance, so the graph bends over, the current rises less and less steeply as the voltage increases. A diode is another non-ohmic component, it lets current through easily one way and barely at all the other. So the key idea: ohmic means constant resistance and a straight I-V line; non-ohmic means changing resistance and a curved line. That distinction, with the filament-lamp explanation, is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong answer for "explain, using a current-voltage graph, why a filament lamp is non-ohmic". The band-six version: for a filament lamp, as the voltage and hence the current increase, the filament gets hotter; in a metal, a higher temperature means a higher resistance, because the more vigorously vibrating atoms impede the flow of electrons more. So as the voltage rises, the resistance rises too, and the current increases by less and less for each extra volt. On a current-versus-voltage graph this shows as a line that curves, flattening out, rather than the straight line of an ohmic conductor. Because the resistance is not constant, Ohm's law does not apply to the filament lamp. The marks are for: increasing current heats the filament, higher temperature gives higher resistance, so the I-V graph curves, and resistance is not constant so it's non-ohmic. The weak answer, "the lamp doesn't obey Ohm's law", just restates the question. Name the heating, the rising resistance, and the curved graph.

QUESTION: A retrieval check. Sketch in your mind, or on paper, the current-versus-voltage graph for, first, an ohmic resistor, and second, a filament lamp. Describe the shape of each, and say what the shape tells you about the resistance. Pause the player, describe both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and the shapes are in Listing two. For an ohmic resistor, the current-versus-voltage graph is a straight line through the origin: equal increases in voltage give equal increases in current, so the gradient is constant, which means the resistance is constant. For a filament lamp, the graph is a curve that starts steep near the origin and then bends over, flattening as the voltage increases: each extra volt produces less and less extra current, which means the resistance is increasing as the lamp heats up. Straight line equals constant resistance, ohmic; curve flattening off equals increasing resistance, non-ohmic. Reading resistance as the steepness of an I-V graph, and seeing it change along a curve, is a classic exam skill.

NARRATOR: Now the fourth quantity, power, where energy comes back in. Electrical power is the rate at which a component converts electrical energy into other forms, heat in a resistor, light in a lamp, motion in a motor. The basic formula is power equals voltage times current, P equals V I, measured in watts; this makes sense from the definitions, voltage is energy per charge and current is charge per time, so their product is energy per time, which is power. Using Ohm's law you can write power in two other handy forms: substituting V equals I R gives P equals I squared R, and substituting I equals V over R gives P equals V squared over R. Which form you use depends on what you're given, but all three give the same power. And the total energy converted is just power times time, energy W equals P times t, in joules. These power relationships are in Listing four, with worked examples in Listing three.

QUESTION: Let's use them. A heater operates at two hundred and forty volts and draws a current of five amperes. What is its power? And how much energy does it convert in sixty seconds? Pause the player, work both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, in Listing three. Power is voltage times current, P equals V I, two hundred and forty volts times five amperes, one thousand two hundred watts, that's one point two kilowatts. The energy converted in sixty seconds is power times time, W equals P t, one thousand two hundred watts times sixty seconds, seventy-two thousand joules, that's seventy-two kilojoules. So the heater's power is twelve hundred watts and it converts seventy-two thousand joules in a minute. The method is always the same: power is voltage times current, and energy is power times time. And note the everyday unit on your electricity bill, the kilowatt-hour, is exactly energy as power times time, a kilowatt for an hour. That's why a high-power appliance run for a long time costs the most: a two-kilowatt heater on for three hours uses six kilowatt-hours, while a ten-watt phone charger would have to run for hundreds of hours to use the same energy. Power tells you the rate; multiply by the time to get the energy you actually pay for.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. The central experiment is to measure the current-voltage relationship of a component: connect it in a circuit with a variable power supply, put an ammeter in series to read the current and a voltmeter in parallel to read the voltage across it, vary the voltage, record matched pairs, and plot current against voltage. A straight line tells you it's ohmic and its gradient gives one over the resistance; a curve, as for a filament lamp, tells you it's non-ohmic. A key examinable detail is meter placement: the ammeter goes in series, because it must have the same current flowing through it as the component, while the voltmeter goes in parallel, because it must read the potential difference across the component. Getting that placement right, and explaining why, is a frequent exam point. Describing this investigation and interpreting its graph is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: confusing current and voltage, current is the flow of charge per time, voltage is the energy per charge, they're different quantities. Trap two: forgetting conventional current and electron flow are opposite, conventional is positive to negative, electrons go negative to positive. Trap three: assuming every component is ohmic, only ohmic conductors at constant temperature obey Ohm's law with a straight I-V line; lamps and diodes don't. Trap four: in power problems, picking the wrong formula for what you're given, remember P equals V I, and the Ohm's-law variants P equals I squared R and P equals V squared over R. Trap five: swapping the ammeter and voltmeter, ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel. Avoid those five and circuits are secure.
