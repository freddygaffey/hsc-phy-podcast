NARRATOR: In the early days of nineteen twenty-seven, in a research laboratory in lower Manhattan, two American physicists were firing a beam of electrons at a small lump of nickel. They had been doing versions of this experiment, on and off, for six years. They expected the electrons to behave the way everybody knew electrons behaved — like tiny billiard balls, like grains of shot from a shotgun, bouncing off the metal and scattering in every direction. That is what a particle does when it hits a wall. It ricochets. And for years, that is more or less what they had seen.

NARRATOR: But on this particular run, the electrons did something that no particle has any business doing. They came off the nickel not in a smooth, uniform spray, but in sharp, bright peaks at very specific angles — and dead zones in between, where almost no electrons arrived at all. If you have ever shone a laser through a fine diffraction grating and seen it split into a row of evenly spaced dots, you already know what this pattern looks like. It is the unmistakable signature of waves. Waves that overlap, reinforce each other in some directions, and cancel each other out in others. The electrons were interfering. As waves.

NARRATOR: And here is the part that should stop you cold. An electron has mass. An electron has charge. You can weigh it, you can deflect it with a magnet, you can count them one at a time as they strike a detector. By every reasonable definition, an electron is a particle — a tiny, discrete piece of matter. And yet here it was, behaving exactly like a ripple on a pond. This is the story of how we discovered that the most fundamental building blocks of matter are not little balls at all. They are something far stranger. And the strangest part is that the man who first dreamed it up was a French aristocrat who had spent his university years studying medieval history.

QUESTION: So how does a particle become a wave? And how did anyone ever come to suspect such a ridiculous thing in the first place?

NARRATOR: To answer that, we have to go back a few years, to Paris, and to a man with one of the longest names in physics. Louis Victor Pierre Raymond de Broglie. He was born in eighteen ninety-two into one of the oldest and grandest noble families in France — a family that had produced dukes and field marshals and government ministers for centuries. Louis was, by birthright, a prince, and would later inherit the title of Duke. His path was supposed to lead to the diplomatic service. So at the Sorbonne, the young de Broglie studied history, and in nineteen ten, at the age of eighteen, he took a degree in it.

NARRATOR: But there was a complication. Louis had an older brother, Maurice, who was seventeen years his senior and who had broken with family tradition to become a physicist — and not just any physicist, but a serious experimental researcher who had built a private laboratory in the family mansion and was doing cutting-edge work on X-rays. Through Maurice, Louis was pulled, slowly, into the world of physics. He switched fields, took a science degree in nineteen thirteen, and then the First World War interrupted everything. Louis spent the war years posted to the Eiffel Tower, of all places, working on military radio communications. And it was there, surrounded by the crackle of wireless waves, that he started thinking seriously about the deepest puzzle in physics.

QUESTION: And what was that puzzle?

NARRATOR: The puzzle was light. For two hundred years, physicists had argued about what light is. Isaac Newton thought it was a stream of tiny particles. Then, in the early eighteen hundreds, Thomas Young shone light through a pair of narrow slits and got an interference pattern — bright and dark bands — which is something only waves can produce. So for most of the nineteenth century, the verdict was settled: light is a wave. James Clerk Maxwell even worked out exactly what kind of wave — a ripple of electric and magnetic fields, travelling through space at three hundred million metres per second. The case looked closed.

NARRATOR: And then it un-closed. In nineteen oh five, Albert Einstein explained a phenomenon called the photoelectric effect — where light shining on a metal knocks electrons out of it — and the only way he could make the numbers work was to say that light comes in discrete little packets of energy. Lumps. Particles. We now call them photons. Each photon carries a definite amount of energy, and a definite amount of momentum, even though it has no mass at all. So light, which Young and Maxwell had proven was a wave, was now also, somehow, a particle. Depending on the experiment you did, you saw one face or the other. This is the famous wave-particle duality of light, and by the nineteen twenties physicists had grudgingly learned to live with it, even if nobody really understood it.

NARRATOR: Now here is the leap that made de Broglie immortal. Everyone was wrestling with the question: how can a wave, like light, also be a particle? De Broglie turned the question inside out. He asked: if a wave can be a particle — then why can't a particle be a wave? If light, which we thought was purely a wave, turns out to also have a particle nature, then perhaps matter, which we thought was purely particles, has a hidden wave nature too. Perhaps the duality runs both ways. Perhaps it is universal.

NARRATOR: It was a beautifully symmetric idea, and de Broglie made it precise. For light, Einstein had shown that a photon's momentum is equal to Planck's constant divided by the wavelength of the light. Planck's constant is that tiny fundamental number, six point six three times ten to the minus thirty-four, that sets the scale of all quantum effects. De Broglie simply flipped the relationship around. He said: take any particle of matter — an electron, a grain of sand, a planet — and it has a wavelength equal to Planck's constant divided by its momentum. And since momentum is just mass times velocity, that means the wavelength of any moving object equals Planck's constant divided by its mass times its velocity. This is the de Broglie equation, and it is one of the most important single lines in the entire HSC Physics course. The faster and heavier the object, the shorter its wave. The lighter and slower it is, the longer its wave. The full statement is in Listing one of the supplementary notes.

QUESTION: That is a very tidy equation. But did de Broglie have any evidence? Or did he just have a pretty piece of mathematics?

NARRATOR: At the time he proposed it, he had essentially no direct evidence. What he had was a feeling for symmetry and a few powerful arguments from Einstein's theory of relativity, which showed that the idea was at least internally consistent. He wrote it all up and submitted it as his doctoral thesis to the University of Paris in November of nineteen twenty-four. The title was Researches on the Theory of the Quanta. And his examination committee did not quite know what to make of it.

NARRATOR: Picture the scene. Four of the most distinguished scientists in France sat in judgement. The chairman was Jean Perrin, a Nobel laureate. There was a crystallographer named Mauguin, a mathematician named Élie Cartan, and de Broglie's own supervisor, Paul Langevin. And they were genuinely puzzled. The idea that an electron had a wavelength struck them as bizarre, almost mystical. Mauguin later admitted he thought the matter waves were, in his words, very interesting imaginary objects. Perrin pressed de Broglie: could any experiment actually verify this? De Broglie, to his enormous credit, said yes — fire a beam of electrons at a crystal, and you should see them diffract, just as X-rays diffract. And this was not an idle comparison. Back in nineteen twelve, the German physicist Max von Laue had shown that the regularly spaced rows of atoms in a crystal act as a natural diffraction grating for X-rays, because the spacing between the atoms is about the same as an X-ray's wavelength. The Braggs, father and son, had then turned that insight into a precise tool for reading the atomic structure of crystals. So de Broglie was pointing to a ready-made apparatus: if an electron really had a wavelength comparable to the atomic spacing, the very same crystal that diffracts X-rays should diffract electrons too. He had already named the test that would, three years later, prove him right.

NARRATOR: But the committee was nervous. The supervisor, Langevin, was personally intrigued but intellectually honest — he simply did not know whether these waves were physically real or just a mathematical trick. So Langevin did something that changed the history of physics. He took a copy of de Broglie's thesis and mailed it to the one person on Earth whose opinion would settle the matter: Albert Einstein, in Berlin.

NARRATOR: Einstein read it. And on the sixteenth of December, nineteen twenty-four, he wrote back to Langevin with a verdict that de Broglie would treasure for the rest of his life. Einstein said that de Broglie had, quote, lifted a corner of the great veil. From the most famous physicist alive, there could be no higher praise. The thesis passed. De Broglie got his doctorate. And — far more importantly — Einstein did not just praise the idea privately. He championed it. He wrote about de Broglie's matter waves in his own papers in early nineteen twenty-five, and that is how the idea escaped from a single French thesis and spread across Europe, landing eventually on the desk of an Austrian physicist named Erwin Schrödinger, who would build the whole of modern quantum mechanics on top of it. But that is getting ahead of the story.

QUESTION: So de Broglie had the idea, and Einstein blessed it. But somebody still had to actually fire those electrons at a crystal. Who did it?

NARRATOR: This is where the story takes a wonderful turn, because the people who proved de Broglie right were not trying to prove de Broglie right. They had never heard of him. They were not even doing fundamental physics. They were doing industrial research for the telephone company.

NARRATOR: Meet Clinton Joseph Davisson, born in eighteen eighty-one, a small, precise, careful American physicist who worked at the research laboratories of the Western Electric Company in New York City — the outfit that, in nineteen twenty-five, would be renamed Bell Telephone Laboratories. Bell Labs would go on to become the most productive industrial research lab in history, the birthplace of the transistor and the laser and information theory. But in the early nineteen twenties, Davisson had a very practical problem. The telephone network ran on vacuum tubes, and vacuum tubes contained metal surfaces that were constantly being bombarded by electrons. Davisson wanted to understand exactly what happened when a stream of electrons hit a metal surface — how they bounced, how they scattered. It was, in the most literal sense, applied research. Better understanding of electrons hitting metal meant better, longer-lasting tubes, which meant better telephones.

NARRATOR: Working alongside him was a younger man named Lester Halbert Germer, born in eighteen ninety-six, who had joined the experiment as Davisson's assistant in October of nineteen twenty-four. Remember Germer's name, because there is a quiet injustice waiting for him at the end of this story. The two of them set up an apparatus inside a sealed vacuum tube: an electron gun that fired a beam of electrons at a target made of nickel, and a detector that could swing around on an arc to measure how many electrons came off at each angle. They would fire the beam, sweep the detector around, and plot the intensity of the scattered electrons against the angle. Simple. Methodical. And for a while, profoundly boring. The electrons scattered off the polycrystalline nickel in a smooth, featureless way — exactly as you would expect from particles bouncing off a rough surface.

QUESTION: So what changed? What turned a boring industrial experiment into one of the most important discoveries of the twentieth century?

NARRATOR: An accident. A genuine laboratory accident, in April of nineteen twenty-five. And the details matter, because this is one of the great examples in all of science of luck favouring the prepared mind. While the experiment was running, a bottle of liquid air sitting near the apparatus exploded. The blast cracked the vacuum tube, and air rushed in onto the hot nickel target. At those temperatures, nickel exposed to air oxidises badly — it gets a thick crust of rust. The target was ruined.

NARRATOR: Now, Davisson and Germer did not want to throw away their carefully made nickel target. So they did the standard metallurgical thing to clean it up: they heated it for a long time at high temperature, in hydrogen and in vacuum, to chemically strip away the oxide layer and restore a clean metal surface. This worked. The oxide came off. But the prolonged heating did something they had not anticipated and did not even notice at the time. Up to that point, the nickel had been what is called polycrystalline — a jumble of countless tiny crystal grains, all pointing in random directions, like a heap of gravel. The intense heating annealed the metal. It made those tiny grains merge and grow. By the time it cooled, the target was no longer a heap of gravel. It had become a small number of large single crystals, with neat, orderly rows of atoms marching in straight lines across the whole width of the electron beam.

NARRATOR: Without meaning to, and without knowing it, they had manufactured a near-perfect diffraction grating made of atoms. And when they put the repaired target back in and resumed the experiment, the scattering pattern had completely changed. Instead of the old smooth, boring spray, sharp peaks now appeared at particular angles. Electrons were piling up at certain directions and avoiding others. Something was going on. But — and this is crucial — Davisson and Germer had no idea what. They had never heard of de Broglie. They did not connect their strange peaks to anything about waves. They just had a weird result that they could not explain, and they kept investigating it, slowly, for another year.

QUESTION: How does a researcher with a mysterious result finally figure out what it means?

NARRATOR: Sometimes, by getting on a boat. In the summer of nineteen twenty-six, Davisson travelled to England — by some accounts while on his second honeymoon — and he attended a meeting of the British Association for the Advancement of Science, held at Oxford. And at that meeting, the air was electric with talk of the brand-new wave mechanics. The German physicist Max Born gave a lecture, and in it, Born actually used some of Davisson's own earlier electron-scattering data — published results from the Bell Labs work — and argued that the strange features in it were evidence for the wave nature of the electron, exactly as predicted by de Broglie and now described by Schrödinger's new equation.

NARRATOR: Imagine sitting in that audience. Davisson had crossed the Atlantic and discovered that the most exciting theorists in Europe were discussing his own data — and that they thought it pointed to something he had never even considered. He suddenly understood. The peaks in his apparatus were not some metallurgical quirk. They were diffraction. His electrons were behaving like waves. He had been staring at the confirmation of de Broglie's hypothesis for over a year without recognising it. He sailed home a changed man, and this time he knew exactly what he was looking for.

NARRATOR: Back in New York, Davisson and Germer rebuilt and refined the experiment with deliberate purpose, hunting for the diffraction peaks de Broglie's theory predicted. And in January of nineteen twenty-seven, they found them, clean and clear. The headline result is beautifully simple. They accelerated their electrons through a potential difference of fifty-four volts. They found a strong, sharp peak in the scattered electrons at an angle of fifty degrees. And then they did the calculation that turns this from a curiosity into a triumph.

QUESTION: Walk me through that calculation. How do you go from a voltage and an angle to proving de Broglie was right?

NARRATOR: It comes in two halves, and the magic is that the two halves agree. Here is the first half — the theory. When you accelerate an electron through fifty-four volts, it gains a precise amount of kinetic energy. From that energy you can work out its momentum, because kinetic energy and momentum are linked through the electron's mass. And once you know the momentum, de Broglie's equation hands you the wavelength directly — Planck's constant divided by the momentum. Run those numbers for a fifty-four volt electron, and de Broglie's formula predicts a wavelength of about zero point one six seven nanometres. A nanometre is a billionth of a metre, so this is a fantastically tiny wavelength — but, and this is the whole point, it is almost exactly the spacing between the atoms in the nickel crystal. That is no accident. That is precisely why the crystal can diffract the electrons: a diffraction grating only works when its slit spacing is comparable to the wavelength passing through it. Atomic spacings are around a tenth of a nanometre, and a fifty-four volt electron's de Broglie wavelength is right in that range. The full theoretical calculation is laid out step by step in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Now the second half — the experiment. Forget de Broglie for a moment. Take the angle they measured, fifty degrees, and the known spacing of the nickel atoms, which physicists already knew precisely from years of earlier X-ray crystallography. Feed those into the standard law of diffraction — the same Bragg condition used for X-rays, where constructive interference happens when the extra path travelled by waves bouncing off neighbouring rows of atoms equals a whole number of wavelengths. Solve that for the wavelength, using nothing but the geometry of the experiment, and you get about zero point one six five nanometres.

NARRATOR: Look at those two numbers. De Broglie's theory, knowing nothing about the experiment, predicted zero point one six seven nanometres. The experiment, knowing nothing about de Broglie, measured zero point one six five nanometres. They agree to better than two parts in a hundred. Two completely independent roads — one starting from a bold theoretical guess about matter waves, the other from the cold geometry of a crystal — arrive at the same number. That is what scientific proof looks like. The electron has a wavelength, and it is exactly the wavelength de Broglie said it would be.

QUESTION: That is one experiment, in one lab, in New York. Is that enough to overturn our entire picture of matter?

NARRATOR: Ordinarily, no. One result, however beautiful, always leaves room for doubt. Maybe there was something peculiar about that particular nickel crystal, or that particular apparatus. But here is the extraordinary thing: at almost exactly the same time, on the other side of the Atlantic, a completely different physicist using a completely different method got the very same answer. And the identity of that physicist is the most poetic twist in the whole story.

NARRATOR: His name was George Paget Thomson. And his father was Sir Joseph John Thomson — J.J. Thomson — the man who, in eighteen ninety-seven, had discovered the electron in the first place and proved it was a particle, a discrete charged corpuscle, for which he won the Nobel Prize in nineteen oh six. The father had shown the electron was a particle. Now the son was about to show the electron was a wave. Let that sink in. One family, two generations, two Nobel Prizes — one for proving the electron is a particle, the other for proving the very same electron is a wave. It is almost too neat to be true, and yet it is exactly true.

NARRATOR: George Paget Thomson — usually called G.P. to distinguish him from his father — was, in nineteen twenty-seven, a professor at the University of Aberdeen in Scotland. Working with a student named Alexander Reid, he took an approach entirely opposite to Davisson and Germer's. Where the Americans used slow electrons — only fifty-four volts — and bounced them off the surface of a single large crystal, Thomson used very fast electrons, accelerated through thousands of volts, and fired them straight through extremely thin films of metal — gold, aluminium, platinum — and even celluloid, films only about thirty billionths of a metre thick. The electrons punched right through and landed on a photographic plate on the other side.

NARRATOR: And what appeared on that plate was the clincher. Not a row of dots, not a few peaks — but a set of perfect concentric rings. Bright rings on a dark background, like a target, or the ripples from a stone dropped in a pond, frozen in place. The reason it was rings rather than dots is itself a lovely piece of physics. The thin metal film is polycrystalline — it is made of millions of tiny crystals pointing in every possible direction. For any given direction the electron wave wants to diffract, there are always some crystals oriented just right to send electrons off at that angle, and because those crystals point every which way around the beam, the diffracted electrons fan out into a complete cone, which lands on the plate as a ring. It is precisely the same pattern you get when you diffract X-rays through a powder. Thomson measured the radii of his rings, applied exactly the same mathematics used for X-ray diffraction, and recovered the de Broglie wavelength of his electrons — agreeing with the prediction to within about five percent.

NARRATOR: And here is the beautiful thing about having two confirmations. Davisson and Germer's electrons reflected off a crystal surface; Thomson's electrons transmitted clean through a film. Slow electrons versus fast electrons. Peaks versus rings. Reflection versus transmission. Two utterly different geometries, two different countries, two physicists who did not even know of each other's work when they published — and both landed on de Broglie's wavelength. When two independent experiments that share almost nothing in common point to the same conclusion, that conclusion is no longer in doubt. The electron is a wave. Matter has a wavelength. De Broglie was right.

QUESTION: So if every electron has a wave nature, what was waving back when de Broglie first imagined his atom? Did his idea actually fix anything that was broken?

NARRATOR: It fixed something that had been troubling physicists for over a decade — the deepest unanswered question about the structure of the atom. To see it, we have to back up to Niels Bohr. In nineteen thirteen, Bohr had built a model of the hydrogen atom that worked astonishingly well. He pictured the single electron orbiting the nucleus like a planet around the sun, but with a radical rule bolted on: the electron was only allowed to occupy certain special orbits, certain fixed energy levels, and no others. In those special orbits it would, mysteriously, not radiate away its energy and spiral into the nucleus, which is what ordinary physics said it should do. When the electron jumped from a high orbit to a low one, it spat out a photon of a precise frequency — and this explained, with gorgeous accuracy, the exact colours of light that hydrogen gas emits.

NARRATOR: Bohr's model worked. But it rested on an assumption that nobody could justify. Bohr simply decreed that the electron's angular momentum — its quantity of spin around the nucleus — could only come in whole-number multiples of Planck's constant divided by two pi. Why? Bohr had no answer. He just found that if he assumed it, the numbers came out right. Even his own mentor, Ernest Rutherford, needled him about it. Rutherford asked, in effect: how does the electron know which orbit to be in? How does it decide what frequency to emit when it jumps? It seemed the electron would have to know in advance where it was going to end up. The whole rule had the air of a magic trick — it produced the right answer, but for no reason anyone could state.

NARRATOR: De Broglie's matter waves dissolved the mystery completely. Picture the electron not as a tiny ball whizzing around the nucleus, but as a wave smeared around the circular orbit. Now ask: what happens to a wave that loops back on itself? Think of a vibrating guitar string, or a wave running around a circular loop of wire. For the wave to survive, it has to join up smoothly with itself after going all the way around. The crest has to meet a crest, the trough a trough. If a whole number of wavelengths fits exactly around the loop, the wave reinforces itself, over and over, and forms a stable, standing wave that persists. But if the circumference is not a whole number of wavelengths, then after one trip around, the wave arrives slightly out of step with where it started — crest landing on trough — and it destroys itself by destructive interference. It cannot exist.

NARRATOR: So de Broglie's condition for a stable electron orbit was simply this: the circumference of the orbit must be a whole number of de Broglie wavelengths. And when you write that condition down mathematically and substitute in the de Broglie wavelength — Planck's constant over the momentum — out falls Bohr's rule, exactly. The angular momentum comes out quantised, in precisely the whole-number multiples of Planck's constant over two pi that Bohr had simply assumed. What Bohr had to postulate out of nowhere, de Broglie derived from a single physical picture. The electron is not in those special orbits because of an arbitrary decree. It is in those orbits because they are the only ones where its own wave fits. The allowed orbits are the resonances, the harmonics, of the electron-wave — exactly like the allowed notes of a vibrating string. The full derivation is in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Suddenly Rutherford's awkward question had an answer. The electron does not need to know where it is going. It simply cannot exist in any orbit except the ones where its wave closes up on itself. The discrete energy levels of the atom — the very thing that makes each element glow with its own unique set of spectral colours — are nothing more than the standing-wave patterns available to a confined electron-wave. The atom is, in a real sense, a musical instrument, and the spectral lines are the notes it is allowed to play.

QUESTION: This is clearly a turning point. What happened after the two Thomson generations and the two confirmations in nineteen twenty-seven?

NARRATOR: Everything happened. The confirmation of matter waves was one of the foundation stones on which the entire edifice of modern quantum mechanics was built. Schrödinger had already, in nineteen twenty-six, taken de Broglie's wave idea and turned it into a full mathematical theory — the Schrödinger equation — which to this day is the master equation governing the behaviour of electrons in every atom, every molecule, every solid. When Davisson and Thomson confirmed electron diffraction in nineteen twenty-seven, they were not just vindicating de Broglie; they were providing the experimental ground truth that Schrödinger's strange new wave mechanics actually described reality. That same year, the greatest physicists alive gathered at the Solvay Conference in Brussels to thrash out what it all meant — Einstein, Bohr, Schrödinger, Heisenberg, de Broglie himself, Born, Dirac — in what is often called the most extraordinary meeting of minds in the history of science.

NARRATOR: The Nobel Prizes followed swiftly. De Broglie received the nineteen twenty-nine Nobel Prize in Physics, awarded for his discovery of the wave nature of electrons — a Nobel for a doctoral thesis, which is almost unheard of. And in nineteen thirty-seven, Clinton Davisson and George Paget Thomson shared the Nobel Prize, for their experimental discovery of the diffraction of electrons by crystals.

NARRATOR: But there is a shadow over that nineteen thirty-seven prize, and honesty demands we name it. Lester Germer — the man who had stood beside Davisson at the apparatus through every step of the work, through the accident, through the year of confusion, through the triumphant peaks of January nineteen twenty-seven — received no share of the Nobel Prize. None. The prize went to Davisson alone from the Bell Labs side. To this day, Germer's exclusion is remembered as one of the more glaring omissions in the history of the award. The experiment is rightly called Davisson-Germer; the medal carried only one of those names.

NARRATOR: And the consequences for technology were staggering. Once you understand that electrons are waves with wavelengths thousands of times shorter than visible light, an astonishing possibility opens up. The reason an ordinary light microscope cannot see anything smaller than about the wavelength of light — a few hundred nanometres — is a fundamental limit set by diffraction. But electrons have wavelengths a thousand times smaller. So if you could build a microscope that used electron waves instead of light waves, you could see a thousand times finer detail. In nineteen thirty-one, just four years after the diffraction was confirmed, Ernst Ruska built the first electron microscope, a direct child of de Broglie's idea. Today, electron microscopes let us see individual atoms. Ruska won his own Nobel Prize for it in nineteen eighty-six.

NARRATOR: And electron diffraction itself became a workhorse of science. Techniques with names like low-energy electron diffraction and reflection high-energy electron diffraction — direct descendants of the Davisson-Germer and Thomson experiments — are used every day to map the atomic structure of crystal surfaces and to monitor, atom layer by atom layer, the growth of the semiconductor chips inside the very device you are listening on right now. The same de Broglie principle was later confirmed for neutrons, for whole atoms, even for large molecules. Matter waves are not a quirk of the electron. They are a universal property of all matter.

QUESTION: There is one thing that still bothers me. When those electrons interfere, are they interfering with each other? Like a crowd of waves bumping together?

NARRATOR: This is the single most important misconception about the whole phenomenon, and clearing it up takes us to the very heart of quantum mechanics. The intuitive picture is that you fire a great swarm of electrons at the crystal, and the waves of all those electrons slosh together and interfere, the way waves from many boats criss-cross a harbour. That picture is wrong. And we know it is wrong because of one of the most beautiful experiments ever performed.

NARRATOR: Decades after Davisson and Germer, physicists learned to fire electrons one at a time, with such long gaps between them that there is never more than a single electron inside the apparatus at any given moment. One electron goes through, lands, and makes a single dot on the detector — a single point, exactly where a particle would strike. Then a long pause. Then the next electron, another single dot, somewhere else, apparently random. If you watch the first few, it looks like pure particle behaviour, scattered dots with no pattern at all. But keep going. Let thousands of electrons pass through, one lonely electron at a time, each completely isolated from all the others, and slowly, dot by dot, an interference pattern builds up on the screen. The bright bands and dark bands assemble themselves out of thousands of individual particle impacts.

NARRATOR: But each electron went through entirely alone. There were no other electrons for it to interfere with. So what was it interfering with? The answer — the staggering answer — is that each electron interferes with itself. The wave associated with a single electron passes through the crystal, explores all the available paths at once, and interferes with itself to decide where that one electron is likely to land. The wave is not a crowd effect. It is a property of every individual electron, on its own. The electron does not become a wave inside the crystal and a particle at the detector. It carries both natures with it the whole time. It travels like a wave and arrives like a particle. And what that wave actually represents — this was Max Born's great insight — is not a physical ripple of any substance, but a wave of probability. Its height at each point tells you the likelihood of finding the electron there. That is as close as anyone has come to answering the question de Broglie's examiners asked back in nineteen twenty-four: what, exactly, is waving? The answer is probability itself.

NARRATOR: So when those two physicists in Manhattan saw their sharp peaks in nineteen twenty-seven, they were not watching a billiard ball bounce off a wall. They were watching the universe reveal that the line we draw between particles and waves — between solid matter and ghostly ripples — is a line that nature does not recognise. Every electron in every atom in your body is a standing wave, humming a quiet harmonic around its nucleus. The chair you are sitting on is solid because the electron-waves of its atoms cannot occupy the same notes. The aristocrat who studied history had, with one symmetric leap of imagination, seen further into the nature of matter than anyone before him. Einstein was right. He had lifted a corner of the great veil.

NARRATOR: For the New South Wales HSC Physics course, this story sits in the matter, energy and the cosmos module, in the section on modelling the atom. The syllabus asks you to explain why the production of electron diffraction patterns provides evidence for electrons behaving as waves — and that is exactly what the Davisson-Germer experiment and G.P. Thomson's thin-film experiment give you. The relationship you must be able to use is de Broglie's — that the wavelength of a particle equals Planck's constant divided by its momentum, that is, divided by its mass times its velocity — and the syllabus expects you to use it to relate the wave and particle properties of matter. You also need to be able to explain how standing waves can be used to represent electrons in stationary energy levels: the standing-wave electron, with a whole number of de Broglie wavelengths fitting around a Bohr orbit, is the picture that finally explains why the energy levels of an atom are quantised. And here is the precise way the syllabus wants you to phrase the punchline — the proposal of electrons as waves in atoms addresses a key limitation of the stationary energy level model. Remember, that earlier model was an uneasy mixture of classical physics and quantum physics, and it could not say why only certain orbits were allowed. The matter-wave picture explains that, and it is the bridge to Schrödinger, whose full wave mechanics — and whose contribution you are also asked to analyse — would soon replace the simple orbit picture altogether, recasting Bohr's neat circular paths as three-dimensional clouds of probability. When the exam asks you for the evidence that particles behave as waves, the answer is two physicists, two crystals, and one French duke who dared to ask the question the other way around.
