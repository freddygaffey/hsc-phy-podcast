NARRATOR: It was just before five in the morning in Louisiana. September the fourteenth, twenty fifteen. In a flat green corner of the American South, inside a long L-shaped building that almost nobody had ever heard of, two laser beams were quietly racing back and forth down two perfectly straight tunnels, each one four kilometres long. The building was empty. Most of the scientists who ran it were asleep. And then, for one fifth of one second, the two tunnels changed length. Not by a millimetre. Not by the width of a hair. One of the tunnels grew longer, and the other grew shorter, by a distance about one thousand times smaller than the width of a single proton. The smallest measurement human beings have ever made. And it was caused by something that had happened more than a billion years earlier, more than a billion light-years away, when two black holes — each one heavier than thirty of our Suns — spiralled into each other and merged into one.

NARRATOR: In the final instant before they joined, those black holes were whirling around each other hundreds of times every second, at more than half the speed of light. And as they did, they shook the universe. They took three entire Suns' worth of mass and converted it, directly, into ripples in spacetime itself. For that fraction of a second, the power pouring out of that collision was greater than the light from every star in the entire observable universe combined. That power crossed a billion light-years of empty space, getting fainter and fainter, until it washed over the Earth as the gentlest of trembles — and two machines, sitting three thousand kilometres apart, felt it.

NARRATOR: Albert Einstein had predicted those ripples in nineteen sixteen. He called them gravitational waves. And for the rest of his life, and for decades after his death, almost everyone — including Einstein himself, at times — believed they would be far too faint to ever, ever measure. This is the story of how we proved them wrong.

QUESTION: So what actually is a gravitational wave? Where does it come from?

NARRATOR: To answer that, we have to throw out the picture of gravity that most of us carry around. In Newton's picture — the one you learn first — gravity is a force. The Earth pulls on the Moon, the Sun pulls on the Earth, two masses reach across empty space and tug on each other, and the strength of that pull is given by the gravitational constant times the two masses, divided by the distance between them squared. That picture is beautiful, and for almost everything we do, it works perfectly. It put satellites in orbit. It got us to the Moon. But Newton himself was deeply uneasy about it, because it never explained how the pull actually crossed the empty space in between. How does the Sun reach out and grab the Earth across one hundred and fifty million kilometres of nothing?

NARRATOR: Einstein answered that question, and the answer changed everything. In his theory of general relativity, gravity is not a force at all. Gravity is the shape of space and time. Mass and energy bend the four-dimensional fabric of spacetime around them, and other objects simply roll along the curves. The Earth doesn't orbit the Sun because the Sun is pulling on it with a rope. The Earth orbits the Sun because the Sun has carved a valley into spacetime, and the Earth is rolling around the inside of that valley. Matter tells spacetime how to curve. Curved spacetime tells matter how to move.

NARRATOR: And here is the crucial part. If spacetime can be bent, then spacetime can also wobble. If you take a heavy object and you shake it — really violently, really asymmetrically — you send out a disturbance in the curvature, a ripple, that travels outward in every direction at the speed of light. Those ripples are gravitational waves. They are literally waves in the shape of space. As one passes through you, it stretches space in one direction while squeezing it in the perpendicular direction, then it reverses, squeezing where it stretched and stretching where it squeezed. Distances themselves get longer and shorter. Not the objects — the space between them.

QUESTION: If gravitational waves are produced by shaking heavy objects, why don't we make them in a lab? Why do we need black holes?

NARRATOR: Because gravity is, by a staggering margin, the weakest force in nature. We saw why in the equation. The gravitational constant is tiny — about six point six seven times ten to the minus eleven. That is the reason you can pick up a paperclip with a five-cent magnet and overpower the gravitational pull of the entire Earth. To make a gravitational wave strong enough to detect, you don't need a heavy object. You need an unimaginably heavy object, moving at an unimaginable speed, in an unimaginably violent way. You need two black holes, each thirty times the mass of the Sun, orbiting each other hundreds of times a second. Nothing we can build in a laboratory comes within a trillion trillion times of that. The universe is the only laboratory violent enough. And even then, by the time the wave reaches us, it is almost nothing at all.

NARRATOR: To understand just how almost-nothing, we have to talk about the most extraordinary measuring machine ever constructed. Its name is LIGO. The Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory.

NARRATOR: The idea behind LIGO is, on the face of it, simple. You take a single laser beam, and you split it in two. You send one half down a long tunnel pointing, say, north, and the other half down an identical tunnel pointing west. At the far end of each tunnel is a mirror. The light bounces off the mirror and comes racing back. And when the two beams meet again, they interfere with each other — that's the inter-ferometer part of the name. Here is the trick. The machine is set up so that, normally, when the two beams recombine, the crest of one wave lines up exactly with the trough of the other, and they cancel each other out completely. Destructive interference. The detector at the output sees darkness. Nothing.

NARRATOR: But now suppose a gravitational wave passes through. It stretches one arm and squeezes the other. Suddenly the north beam has a slightly longer journey, and the west beam has a slightly shorter one. The two beams no longer line up perfectly when they recombine. The perfect cancellation breaks. A tiny flicker of light leaks out to the detector. And that flicker — that ghost of light appearing where there should be darkness — is the signature of a passing ripple in spacetime.

QUESTION: Hang on. If the gravitational wave stretches the arm, doesn't it also stretch the wavelength of the laser light inside the arm? And if the ruler stretches by the same amount as the thing you're measuring, how can you possibly detect anything? The stretching should cancel out.

NARRATOR: This is the single most common objection to how LIGO works, and it is a brilliant question, because it sounds completely watertight. And the answer reveals what LIGO is really measuring. LIGO is not comparing the wavelength of light to the length of the arm, like laying a ruler against a table. What LIGO measures is time. Specifically, it measures how long it takes light to travel down the arm and back. When the arm gets longer, the light takes longer to make the round trip. That extra travel time shows up as a shift in the phase of the wave when it returns. And — this is the key — the two arms are changing in opposite directions at the same moment. One arm is getting longer while the other is getting shorter. So you are always comparing one arm against the other. Even if you worried about the light wavelength stretching in one arm, it is being squeezed in the other. The difference between the two arms is real, and it is measurable. That differential — the contrast between the two perpendicular directions — is exactly the fingerprint a gravitational wave leaves behind.

NARRATOR: Now. How small is the change LIGO has to detect? The arms are four kilometres long. The gravitational wave from those two black holes changed each arm's length by about four times ten to the minus eighteen metres. Let me try to give that number a shape. The diameter of a single proton is a little under two times ten to the minus fifteen metres. So the change LIGO measured in each arm was a few thousandths of the width of a proton — and measured against the tiny dense core of the proton, the famous comparison the LIGO team used, it comes out at roughly one thousandth of a proton. If you scaled the four-kilometre arm up to the distance from the Earth to the nearest star — about four light-years — the change you would be looking for would be smaller than the width of a human hair. That is the precision. That is what they had to build.

QUESTION: That seems impossible. The whole world is shaking all the time. Trucks, earthquakes, ocean waves, people walking around. How do you measure something a thousand times smaller than a proton when the ground itself is vibrating a hundred billion times more than that?

NARRATOR: You have just named the central nightmare of the entire project. The ground at any spot on Earth is constantly trembling — from passing trucks, from distant earthquakes, from waves crashing on coastlines hundreds of kilometres away, from the wind, even from lightning. That natural trembling moves the ground by something like ten to the minus seven metres — a tenth of a millionth of a metre. Compare that to the signal they wanted to detect, ten to the minus eighteen metres. The noise is about a hundred billion times bigger than the thing you're trying to find. It's like trying to hear a single whispered word during the loudest rock concert in history.

NARRATOR: So the engineers waged war on noise. Every single mirror — and these are not ordinary mirrors, they are forty-kilogram cylinders of the purest fused silica glass ever made — every mirror hangs from a four-stage pendulum, a pendulum hanging from a pendulum hanging from a pendulum hanging from a pendulum. The final stage dangles from four glass fibres thinner than a human hair, welded directly to the glass. Each stage filters out vibration, so that by the time you get to the mirror, the shaking of the Earth has been smoothed away almost entirely. The mirrors are not bolted down. They float. They are, as nearly as possible, freely suspended test masses, hanging in space, feeling nothing but the geometry of spacetime around them. And that matters enormously, because a gravitational wave does not push the mirrors like a sound wave pushes a microphone. The mirrors barely feel a force at all. What changes is the distance between them — the space itself.

NARRATOR: Then there's the laser. Inside each arm, the light doesn't just go down and back once. It is trapped between mirrors and bounces back and forth around three hundred times before it's released, so that the effective path length becomes not four kilometres but more like twelve hundred kilometres of travel — which makes the timing measurement that much more sensitive. To beat the random statistical noise of counting individual photons, the laser power circulating inside the arms is cranked up to about seven hundred and fifty kilowatts — close to a megawatt of light, trapped in a tube. And the whole apparatus sits inside one of the largest sustained vacuums ever created, so that not even a stray air molecule drifting across the beam can spoil the measurement.

NARRATOR: But here is the masterstroke, the idea that makes the whole thing actually work. You build two of them. One in Livingston, Louisiana. The other nearly two thousand miles away in Hanford, Washington State. A truck driving past Livingston shakes Livingston. It does not shake Hanford. A wave crashing on the coast of Washington rattles Hanford, not Louisiana. Local noise is, by definition, local — it never appears in both detectors at once. But a gravitational wave, sweeping across the entire planet at the speed of light, must pass through both. So the rule is brutally simple: ignore anything that only one detector sees. Only believe a signal if both detectors see the same thing, within the few milliseconds it takes light to travel between them. Two witnesses, three thousand kilometres apart, agreeing on the same impossible event.

NARRATOR: The road to building this was long, and it was paved with doubt. The idea of using laser interferometry to catch gravitational waves came from Rainer Weiss at MIT, who worked it out in the early nineteen seventies — he even wrote it up in a technical report in nineteen seventy-two. In nineteen seventy-five, Weiss met the brilliant theoretical physicist Kip Thorne of Caltech, and the two of them, joined later by the experimentalist Ronald Drever, formed the core of what would become LIGO. Thorne was so confident gravitational waves could be found that he was famous for betting on it. For years, the National Science Foundation poured in money — at one point it was the single largest grant the agency had ever awarded — into a project that many established scientists thought was a fool's errand, chasing a signal that might be forever out of reach.

NARRATOR: The first version of LIGO ran from two thousand and two to twenty ten. It detected nothing. And that was not a failure — it was exactly what the physics predicted. The instrument simply wasn't sensitive enough yet. So they tore much of it out and rebuilt it. Advanced LIGO, ten times more sensitive than the original, came online in the late summer of twenty fifteen. And here is the detail that still gives physicists chills. On September the fourteenth, twenty fifteen, the upgraded detectors were not even officially running yet. They were in engineering mode — a shakedown phase, the final checks before the first formal observing run was due to begin. They had been switched on for barely two days. The universe was not supposed to deliver anything yet. Nobody was watching closely.

NARRATOR: The first human being to see it was a thirty-three-year-old Italian postdoc named Marco Drago, sitting at the Max Planck Institute in Hanover, Germany. He was a physicist who also wrote fantasy novels in his spare time. At ten to noon, German time, an automated email landed in his inbox — the software had flagged something interesting in the data, just three minutes after the wave had passed through the detectors. Drago clicked the links. Up came two graphs, one from each detector. And there it was: a clean, swooping curve, rising in frequency and rising in pitch — a shape the LIGO team would later say looked exactly like the spectrogram of a bird's chirp. Two detectors. The same chirp. Three thousand kilometres apart, and the two signals lined up to within about seven thousandths of a second — exactly the time it takes light to cross between them.

NARRATOR: Drago's first thought was not "Eureka." His first thought was: this must be a fake. Because LIGO had a system for keeping its scientists honest — secret signals, blind injections, deliberately fed into the data to test whether the team could find and analyse a real event. The signal was so perfect, so textbook, that Drago assumed someone had planted it. He wasn't alone. As word spread and an emergency phone call convened the collaboration, the prevailing reaction was disbelief. Only four people on Earth had the authority to know whether a blind injection had been made. The collaboration held its breath and asked them. The answer came back: no injection. Nobody had planted anything. The signal was real. The universe had sent it.

QUESTION: So they had a signal. But how did they know it was two black holes? How do you read a story like that out of one fifth of a second of squiggle?

NARRATOR: This is where the physics becomes genuinely beautiful, and where everything you might have learned about gravity and orbits pays off. Think back to two objects orbiting each other — a planet around a star, a moon around a planet. They are bound together, trading kinetic and potential energy, and ordinarily they orbit forever. But general relativity adds a twist that Newton never knew about. When two heavy objects orbit each other, they radiate energy away as gravitational waves. The orbit is leaking energy into the fabric of spacetime.

NARRATOR: And here is the runaway. As the orbit loses energy, the two objects fall closer together. But when they get closer, by the laws of orbital motion, they have to orbit faster. And the faster and closer they orbit, the more gravitational wave energy they radiate. Which makes them fall closer still. Which makes them orbit faster still. It is a feedback loop that feeds on itself, accelerating without limit, right up until the two objects collide. In the final fraction of a second before two black holes merge, they are whipping around each other hundreds of times every second.

NARRATOR: Now think about what that does to the wave you'd detect. The frequency of the gravitational wave is twice the orbital frequency, so as the orbit speeds up, the wave rises in pitch. And as the objects get closer and more violent, the wave grows in amplitude. Frequency rising. Amplitude rising. Both climbing together, faster and faster, until the merger. Engineers call that signal a chirp, because if you turn it into sound, that is exactly what it sounds like — a swooping rise like a bird call, ending in a single thump as the two black holes become one. In the LIGO signal, in about two tenths of a second, the frequency swept upward through the detector's band from about thirty-five cycles per second all the way to two hundred and fifty. About eight visible wobbles took it from thirty-five up to a hundred and fifty cycles per second, the moment the amplitude reached its peak — and then, as the new black hole rang down and settled, the signal climbed on toward two hundred and fifty before fading to silence. The two had become one.

NARRATOR: And the precise shape of that chirp encodes everything. The rate at which the pitch rises, the way the amplitude grows — these depend on the masses of the two objects, on how close they got, on how fast they spun. The single cleanest quantity you can pull out is a combination of the two masses that physicists call the chirp mass, and it controls the whole sweep of the signal. The full relationship — the formula that governs how fast a binary inspirals as it bleeds energy into gravitational waves — was worked out back in the early nineteen sixties, decades before anyone could test it. It's set out in the supplementary notes in Listing one and Listing two.

NARRATOR: To decode the wave, the LIGO team did something audacious. Using general relativity, on supercomputers, they had pre-calculated a vast library of around a quarter of a million theoretical waveforms — every possible chirp, for every possible pair of masses and spins. Then they slid the real signal against that library, looking for the template that matched best. And one template matched almost perfectly. It told them: two black holes, one about thirty-six times the mass of the Sun, the other about twenty-nine. They had spiralled together and merged into a single black hole of about sixty-two solar masses.

QUESTION: Wait. Thirty-six plus twenty-nine is sixty-five. But the final black hole was sixty-two. Three solar masses are missing. Where did three entire Suns go?

NARRATOR: They didn't go anywhere. They became the wave. This is the single most staggering fact in the whole story, and it ties directly back to the most famous equation in physics — Einstein's mass-energy equivalence, energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. Three solar masses' worth of matter was converted, in a fraction of a second, directly into the energy of gravitational waves. Not into light. Not into heat. Into pure ripples in spacetime. And because the speed of light squared is such an enormous number, three solar masses of mass becomes a truly cosmic amount of energy — around five times ten to the forty-seventh joules, poured out in a heartbeat. For that brief instant, this single event was radiating more power than all the stars in all the galaxies in the entire visible universe, added together. All of it carried away invisibly, as a tremor in the shape of space.

NARRATOR: And the waveform — the actual curve LIGO recorded — matched the prediction of general relativity, computed from Einstein's equations, point for point. The inspiral, the merger, and even the final ring-down, the way the newborn black hole quivered and settled. One hundred years after Einstein wrote down the theory, on almost exactly its hundredth anniversary, the universe handed us a perfect confirmation, signed and dated.

NARRATOR: They named it GW one five zero nine one four — gravitational wave, twenty fifteen, September fourteenth. The black holes were about one point three billion light-years away, which means the collision happened one point three billion years ago, long before complex life crawled onto land on Earth. The wave had been travelling toward us, across the void, for longer than there have been animals on this planet, and it arrived in the two days after we finally switched on a machine good enough to feel it.

NARRATOR: But they couldn't tell anyone. Not yet. Because an extraordinary claim demands extraordinary proof, and a thousand scientists in the LIGO collaboration now had to spend the next five months checking everything. Re-checking the calibration. Ruling out every conceivable instrumental glitch, every gremlin, every way the signal could be an artefact rather than a wave. Writing the discovery paper. Submitting it for peer review. And — astonishingly — keeping the secret. More than a thousand people, across dozens of countries, sat on the biggest news in physics for five months, and it did not leak.

NARRATOR: Then, on February the eleventh, twenty sixteen, in a packed room in Washington D.C., the LIGO executive director David Reitze stepped up to the microphone and said the words the world had been waiting a century to hear. "We have detected gravitational waves. We did it." The signal-to-noise ratio was twenty-four — the signal stood far above the noise. The statistical significance was beyond five sigma; the odds of it being a random fluke were less than one in two hundred thousand years of running the detectors. Reitze said the observation fulfilled Einstein's legacy on the hundredth anniversary of general relativity. Kip Thorne, who had bet on this his whole life, said that humanity was now embarking on a marvellous new quest — to explore the warped side of the universe, the objects made from warped spacetime itself. And Gabriela González, the spokesperson for the collaboration, put it most simply. This detection, she said, is the beginning of a new era. The field of gravitational-wave astronomy is now a reality.

QUESTION: That sounds like the perfect ending. So why isn't it? You said there was a second event, GW one seven zero eight one seven. What made that one even bigger?

NARRATOR: Because GW one five zero nine one four, as miraculous as it was, was silent. Two black holes merging produce a gravitational wave — but no light. Black holes are black. There was nothing to see in any telescope, nothing to confirm with our eyes, only the tremor in the detectors. It was as if we had heard a sound in the dark without ever seeing what made it. The next great event, almost exactly two years later, would let us hear and see the same thing at once. And in doing so, it would tie together nearly every strand of physics you could name.

NARRATOR: August the seventeenth, twenty seventeen. By now there were three detectors listening — the two LIGOs in America, joined for the first time by Virgo, the European detector in Italy. At forty-one minutes past noon, Universal Time, a signal arrived. But this one was completely different from the black hole chirp. The black hole signal had lasted two tenths of a second. This one lasted around a hundred seconds — a long, slow, rising chirp that climbed for a minute and a half before reaching its crescendo. The reason it was so long is the reason it was so important. The objects were much lighter than black holes. They were neutron stars — the ultra-dense corpses of dead stars, each one barely larger than a city but heavier than our Sun, each one a single colossal atomic nucleus. Two neutron stars, with a combined mass of about two and three-quarter Suns, spiralling in to collide.

NARRATOR: And then something happened that had never happened before in the history of science. One point seven four seconds after the gravitational wave reached its peak — less than two seconds — gamma-ray telescopes in orbit, the Fermi and INTEGRAL satellites, registered a burst of gamma rays coming from the same patch of sky. A short gamma-ray burst, one of the most energetic flashes in the universe. We had felt the collision through gravity, and then, two seconds later, we had seen its first light.

QUESTION: Why the delay? If gravity and light both travel at the speed of light, why did the gamma rays arrive nearly two seconds after the gravitational waves?

NARRATOR: Two reasons, and both are physics in action. First, the gravitational waves are produced by the merging neutron stars directly, in the instant of collision. The gamma rays are produced by what happens next — the collision launches a jet of matter, and that jet takes a moment to organise itself, punch through the surrounding debris, and beam its radiation toward Earth. A second or two of head start for gravity is exactly what you'd expect. And second — this is the profound part — the fact that the two signals arrived within two seconds of each other, after travelling side by side for one hundred and thirty million years, told us something we had never been able to test before. It told us that gravity and light travel at the same speed, to an accuracy of better than one part in a thousand trillion. Some theories that tried to replace general relativity predicted that gravity might travel at a slightly different speed. In a single stroke, this event ruled out whole families of those theories. Einstein, again, was right.

NARRATOR: But the real prize was still to come, and it required the eyes of the entire human astronomical community. The detectors, working together, had narrowed the source down to a patch of sky about twenty-eight square degrees. Now the race was on. Observatories all over the planet swung their telescopes toward that patch, hunting for a point of light that hadn't been there the night before. Just under eleven hours after the merger, a telescope in Chile called the Swope found it — a new pinprick of light in a galaxy called NGC four nine nine three, around a hundred and thirty million light-years away. Within ninety minutes, six independent teams had confirmed it. For the first time, we were looking, with our telescopes, at the visible aftermath of an event we had first detected through ripples in spacetime.

NARRATOR: And what we saw, as we watched it over the following days and weeks, confirmed one of the oldest and deepest mysteries in all of physics. Where do the heavy elements come from? Where does gold come from? Where does platinum come from, and uranium, and all the elements heavier than iron?

NARRATOR: Here is the puzzle. The lightest elements, hydrogen and helium, were forged in the Big Bang. The elements up to iron are built inside stars and blasted out by supernova explosions — the carbon in your cells, the oxygen you breathe, the iron in your blood, all of it cooked in stellar furnaces. But iron is a wall. Iron sits right at the peak of what physicists call the binding-energy curve — its nucleus is the most tightly bound there is — and that is exactly why iron is the heaviest element a star can ever forge. Fusing anything lighter than iron releases energy, which is what keeps a star shining. But to build anything heavier than iron, you have to put energy in rather than getting it out, so even a dying star simply cannot do it. To make gold, you need something far more extreme. You need to cram so many neutrons onto a nucleus, so fast, that it climbs up past iron, past silver, all the way to gold and beyond. Physicists call this rapid neutron capture — the r-process. And for decades, the great unanswered question was: where in the universe is there an environment neutron-rich enough, and violent enough, to run the r-process at scale?

NARRATOR: The leading suspect was exactly this — the collision of two neutron stars. When they merge, they fling out a spray of neutron-rich matter, the most neutron-rich stuff in the universe, moving at something like a tenth of the speed of light. In that debris cloud, the r-process should run riot, building heavy elements by the planet-load. The freshly forged radioactive atoms would then glow — a special kind of explosion that theorists had named a kilonova. But it had never been confirmed. It was a beautiful theory waiting for evidence.

NARRATOR: And GW one seven zero eight one seven gave us the evidence, in front of our eyes. The new point of light in NGC four nine nine three was a kilonova. As the world's telescopes watched, it evolved exactly as the r-process predicted. First it glowed blue — the lighter, faster-moving debris. Then, over days, it shifted to red — the heavier, slower elements. And when astronomers spread its light into a spectrum, they found the fingerprint of strontium, a heavy r-process element, manufactured in the merger and detected directly for the first time. The models estimated that this single collision produced something like sixteen thousand Earth-masses of heavy elements — including roughly ten Earth-masses of gold and platinum, flung out into space to one day, perhaps, end up in a ring on someone's finger.

NARRATOR: Stop and feel the weight of that. The gold in your jewellery, the gold in every wedding ring on Earth, was not made on Earth, and it was not made by the Sun. It was forged in the unimaginable violence of two neutron stars colliding, somewhere in the galaxy, billions of years before the Earth even formed. We had suspected it for half a century. On the night of August the seventeenth, twenty seventeen, we watched it happen.

QUESTION: Why does it matter that we saw it in gravitational waves and gamma rays and visible light all at once? What does combining them actually give you?

NARRATOR: It gives you a whole new way of doing astronomy. For all of human history, every single thing we knew about the cosmos beyond our solar system came from light — from collecting photons. Gravitational waves are a completely different messenger, made not of light but of warped spacetime, and they tell us things light never could. Astronomers call this multi-messenger astronomy — using gravitational waves and light together to study one event, the way you might learn more from both watching a film and hearing its soundtrack than from either alone. GW one seven zero eight one seven was the first time in history we did it. From the gravitational wave alone, we could read off the precise distance to the source — it acts as what's called a standard siren — and from the light, we could measure how fast that galaxy is receding. Put the two together and you get an entirely independent measurement of how fast the universe is expanding, the Hubble constant, derived in a way nobody had ever managed before.

NARRATOR: The recognition came fast. In October twenty seventeen, just six weeks after the neutron star merger, the Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Rainer Weiss, Barry Barish — who had taken over the project in nineteen ninety-four and built it into a thousand-person collaboration — and Kip Thorne, for the detector and for the first observation of gravitational waves. Ronald Drever, one of the founding trio, had died just months earlier, and so could not share it. The journal Science named the neutron star merger its Breakthrough of the Year. Forty years of work, a machine that could measure a thousandth the width of a proton, a signal from the edge of time — and it had all paid off.

NARRATOR: And it keeps paying off. Since those first detections, LIGO and Virgo, now joined by a detector in Japan called KAGRA, have found gravitational waves from more than ninety cosmic collisions — black holes merging, neutron stars merging, the universe ringing like a bell over and over again. A new detector is being built in India. Even larger and more sensitive observatories are being designed for the future, with names like the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer. Before nineteen fifteen, every astronomer who had ever lived had studied the universe with light. Now we have a second sense. We can listen to the fabric of spacetime itself. And it turns out the universe has a great deal to say.

NARRATOR: Think about what had to come together for that one fifth of a second to mean anything. You had to understand gravity well enough to know that two masses orbiting each other radiate energy and spiral inward — that's the physics of gravitational fields and orbital energy. You had to understand special relativity well enough to know that three solar masses of matter can become pure energy through energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. You had to understand nuclear physics and the death of stars well enough to know what a neutron star is, and how the r-process forges gold in the wreckage of one. Every one of those threads — gravity, relativity, nuclear physics, the life and death of stars — runs straight through the heart of the New South Wales H-S-C Physics course, through Module five, Module seven, and Module eight. LIGO is the capstone where they all converge. It is the evidence that the physics you are learning is not a set of separate topics in separate chapters. It is one connected description of one connected universe — and on September the fourteenth, twenty fifteen, that universe sent us a billion-year-old message, smaller than a proton, to prove it.
