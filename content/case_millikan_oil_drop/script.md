NARRATOR: Imagine standing in a darkened laboratory in Chicago, your eye pressed to the eyepiece of a small telescope. In the field of view, against a black background, hangs a single point of light. It is a droplet of oil, smaller than the eye could ever see unaided, lit from the side so that it glitters like a tiny star. And it is doing something that oil droplets are not supposed to do. It is not falling. It hangs there, perfectly motionless, suspended in mid-air, defying gravity. You hold your breath because the slightest current of air could disturb it. And in that frozen, hovering droplet is hidden the answer to a question that physicists had been asking for decades. How much electric charge does a single electron carry?

NARRATOR: Robert Andrews Millikan spent the better part of eleven years chasing droplets like this one. He was hunting for one number. Not a big number, not a grand cosmic constant written across the sky, but a tiny, almost unimaginably small quantity of electric charge. The charge on one electron. The smallest piece of electricity that exists in the everyday world. He found it by making oil float in mid-air. And the value he found, one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, would turn out to be one of the most important numbers in all of physics.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how a man watched falling droplets for over a decade to weigh a single electron's charge. It is a story of patience, of a brilliant graduate student who got written out of history, of a bitter feud with an Austrian rival who refused to back down, and of a measurement so famous that it became a cautionary tale about how scientists fool themselves. Let us begin at the beginning, with a problem that nobody could solve.

QUESTION: So before Millikan, did anyone actually know what an electron was?

NARRATOR: They knew it existed. In eighteen ninety-seven, the English physicist J.J. Thomson, working at the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge, had done something extraordinary. He took those mysterious glowing beams inside vacuum tubes, the cathode rays, and he proved they were streams of tiny negatively charged particles. He measured the ratio of their charge to their mass, and he found that this particle, the electron, was almost two thousand times lighter than the lightest atom. It was the first subatomic particle ever discovered. Thomson had found a piece of the atom itself. So yes, the electron was real.

QUESTION: But if Thomson measured the charge-to-mass ratio, didn't he already know the charge?

NARRATOR: No. And this is the crucial gap. Thomson measured a ratio. He measured charge divided by mass. But a ratio is two unknowns tangled together. If I tell you that some number divided by another number equals ten, you still do not know either number. It could be one hundred divided by ten, or fifty divided by five, or a million divided by a hundred thousand. Thomson knew the ratio, but he did not know the charge by itself, and he did not know the mass by itself. To untangle them, you needed to measure just one of them directly. And the easier one to go after was the charge. So the race was on to measure the charge of a single electron, and whoever did it first would unlock the absolute mass of the electron as a bonus.

NARRATOR: Thomson and his students tried. They had a method they called the cloud method. They would take a cloud of water droplets formed inside an ionised gas, where each droplet carried some electric charge, and they would watch the whole cloud settle under gravity while also pushing on it with an electric field. The problem was that this measured only the average charge spread across millions and millions of droplets at once. It was like trying to find out how much one person weighs by weighing an entire crowd and dividing. The clouds evaporated. They were fuzzy. The numbers that came out were unreliable, scattered, and frankly disappointing. Nobody trusted them. The cloud method was a dead end, and everyone knew it.

QUESTION: So why did Millikan think he could do better?

NARRATOR: Robert Millikan was a professor at the University of Chicago, working in the Ryerson Physical Laboratory. He was not a young man making his name. By the time he seriously took up this problem around nineteen oh-six, he was nearly forty years old and somewhat anxious that he had not yet made a great discovery. He took Thomson's cloud method and asked a deceptively simple question. What if, instead of watching a whole cloud of millions of droplets, I could isolate just one? What if I could watch a single droplet, all by itself, and measure the charge on that one droplet directly?

NARRATOR: His first attempts used water droplets, just as Thomson had. And here he ran into a wall. Water evaporates. A tiny water droplet, only a few millionths of a metre across, would shrink and vanish within minutes, sometimes within seconds. By the time you had set up your measurement, your droplet was gone. The whole experiment kept dissolving in front of his eyes. It was maddening. He needed a substance that would not evaporate, something that could hang in the air long enough to be studied carefully. And the solution to that problem came not from Millikan himself, but from a twenty-five-year-old graduate student from Springville, Utah, named Harvey Fletcher.

QUESTION: Who was Harvey Fletcher?

NARRATOR: Harvey Fletcher was Millikan's doctoral student, and his role in this story has been quietly buried for most of a century. Fletcher was the one who suggested they stop using water and switch to oil. Watch oil, the kind used to lubricate clocks and watches, which evaporates so slowly that a droplet could be observed for hours instead of seconds. It was a brilliant, practical fix. And according to Fletcher's own account, written decades later, he did something even more important. He was the one who first saw individual droplets hovering.

NARRATOR: The story, as Fletcher told it, goes like this. He was watching a fine mist of oil that had been sprayed into the apparatus and given an electric charge. As the cloud thinned out and dispersed, he noticed that some individual droplets did not fall. They hung there, balanced, held in place by the electric field pushing up against gravity pulling down. That was the breakthrough. Forget the cloud entirely. Watch one droplet. Balance it in mid-air. This balanced-drop observation is the heart of the entire experiment, and a graduate student from Utah was at the eyepiece when it happened.

QUESTION: So how did the apparatus actually work? What were they looking at?

NARRATOR: Let me paint the picture for you, because the apparatus is beautifully simple in concept. At the top sat an atomiser, essentially a perfume spray, which puffed a fine mist of oil droplets into the air. As the oil was forced through the nozzle, the droplets picked up a small static electric charge from friction, just as you pick up charge rubbing a balloon on your hair. Some of these droplets drifted down through a tiny hole into the main chamber.

NARRATOR: The main chamber was the clever part. It was the space between two horizontal metal plates, one above the other, separated by about sixteen millimetres, a little more than a centimetre. These were connected to a battery that could put a high voltage across them, around five thousand volts. When you charge two parallel plates like that, you create a uniform electric field in the gap between them, an invisible region of force pointing straight up or straight down. A charged droplet sitting in that gap feels a steady electric force.

NARRATOR: Off to the side was a beam of X-rays. Millikan could fire these into the chamber, and the X-rays would knock electrons off the air molecules, ionising the gas. The droplets could then pick up or lose electrons, changing their charge on command. This was a way to deliberately alter the charge on a droplet in the middle of an experiment, and as we will see, it turned out to be the key to proving the deepest result of all.

NARRATOR: And finally, there was a short telescope, a microscope really, pointed sideways into the gap between the plates, with the droplets lit from the side so they showed up as bright sparks against darkness. Millikan would sit there, eye to the eyepiece, watching a single droplet, and turning a knob to adjust the voltage on the plates. That was the whole instrument. An atomiser, two charged plates, an X-ray gun, and a telescope. With this he was going to weigh the charge of an electron.

QUESTION: All right, so a charged droplet hangs between two plates. How does that give you the charge?

NARRATOR: This is where the physics becomes genuinely elegant, so let me take it slowly, in two stages. The first thing Millikan needed to know was the mass of the droplet. And here is the difficulty. These droplets are so small, a few millionths of a metre across, weighing on the order of a hundred-trillionth of a kilogram, that you cannot possibly put one on a scale. So how do you weigh something you can barely see?

NARRATOR: The trick is to turn the electric field off completely and just let the droplet fall under gravity. Now, you might think it would accelerate, falling faster and faster like a dropped stone. But it does not. A tiny droplet falling through air is fighting against air resistance, the drag of the air molecules. And because the droplet is so small and so light, the air resistance balances its weight almost instantly. The droplet reaches a steady, constant speed, called the terminal velocity, and falls at that unchanging speed. The same reason a feather or a speck of dust drifts down gently rather than plummeting.

NARRATOR: Now there is a law of physics, called Stokes's law, that tells you exactly how much drag force a small sphere feels as it moves slowly through a fluid like air. The drag depends on the size of the sphere, the speed it is moving, and the thickness, or viscosity, of the air. When the droplet falls at its steady terminal velocity, the upward drag force exactly equals the downward pull of gravity. And since Millikan could measure the terminal velocity by timing how long the droplet took to fall a fixed distance, and since he knew the viscosity of the air and the density of the oil, he could run Stokes's law backwards to calculate the radius of the droplet. Once you know the radius and the density of the oil, you know the volume, and therefore you know the mass. He had weighed the droplet, not with a scale, but by watching how fast it fell. That is the first stage.

QUESTION: And the second stage is the charge?

NARRATOR: Exactly. Now Millikan switched the electric field back on. He turned up the voltage until the upward electric force on the charged droplet exactly balanced the downward force of gravity, and the droplet froze in mid-air, hovering motionless. At that perfect balance point, the physics is breathtakingly simple. The electric force pushing up equals the weight pulling down. Now, the electric force on a charged object equals the charge on the object multiplied by the strength of the electric field. And the field strength is just the voltage across the plates divided by the distance between them, both of which Millikan knew precisely. The weight is the mass, which he had already worked out, multiplied by gravity.

NARRATOR: So at the moment the droplet hangs perfectly still, the upward electric force, the field strength times the charge, exactly equals the downward weight, the mass times gravity. Everything in that balance is known except the charge. So you simply rearrange it. The charge equals the mass times gravity, divided by the field strength. Or, putting in the field as voltage over distance, the charge equals the mass times gravity times the plate separation, divided by the voltage. Every single quantity on the right-hand side is a number Millikan could measure. And out pops the charge on that one droplet. He had done it. He had measured the electric charge on a single, isolated oil droplet. Something nobody had ever done before.

NARRATOR: And here is a subtle point worth knowing, because it is the version your textbook leans on. Millikan did not always have to bring the droplet to a dead stop. He could also turn the voltage so the droplet rose or fell at a steady, uniform velocity rather than hanging frozen, and the same balance of forces let him extract the charge. Stationary or moving at constant speed, the principle is identical. The forces on the droplet are balanced, the net force is zero, and the charge falls straight out of the equation. The textbook even gives the rule of thumb you should memorise. The weight pulling down, mass times gravity, equals the electric force pushing up, the field times the charge. Set those equal and solve for the charge. That single line is what an examiner wants to see.

QUESTION: But hold on. The charge on a droplet isn't the same as the charge on an electron, is it? A droplet could have lots of electrons stuck to it.

NARRATOR: That is the most important question in the entire story, and you have put your finger on exactly the thing that makes this experiment immortal. You are completely right. When Millikan measured the charge on a droplet, he was not measuring the charge of one electron. A droplet might be carrying five extra electrons, or twelve, or eighteen, or thirty. The charge he measured was the total charge of however many electrons happened to be clinging to that particular droplet. So measuring one droplet tells you almost nothing by itself.

NARRATOR: But Millikan did not measure one droplet. He measured hundreds of them, one after another, day after day, year after year. And then he looked at all the numbers together. And here is where the magic happened. The charges were not random. They did not come out as any old value. When he lined up all his measurements, every single charge he ever measured turned out to be a whole-number multiple of one particular tiny value. He would find a droplet with a charge of, say, two electrons' worth. The next might have eight. The next eighteen. But never two and a half. Never seven point three. Never any fraction in between. Always a clean whole-number multiple of one fundamental amount.

QUESTION: So the charges came in lumps?

NARRATOR: In lumps. In packets. Think of it this way. Imagine you collected hundreds of jars of coins from strangers, and you weighed each jar, and you found that every single jar weighed an exact whole-number multiple of, say, eleven point three grams. One jar weighs eleven point three grams, another weighs twenty-two point six, another weighs ninety point four grams which is exactly eight times eleven point three. You would conclude, correctly, that every jar contains only one type of coin, and that one coin weighs eleven point three grams. You never see a coin directly, but you deduce its weight from the fact that all the totals are multiples of it. That is precisely what Millikan did with electric charge. The charges on his droplets were always multiples of one indivisible unit, and that unit was the charge on a single electron. The smallest building block of electricity. He had found the fundamental charge.

NARRATOR: This is what physicists mean when they say charge is quantised. It does not come in any amount you like. It comes only in whole-number multiples of one tiny indivisible lump. You can have one electron's worth of charge, or two, or a billion, but you can never have half an electron's worth. Charge is grainy. It is lumpy at the smallest scale. Your textbook gives that smallest lump a name. It calls it the quantum of charge, or the elementary charge, the single indivisible packet of which all charge is built, and its value is one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. And Millikan's oil droplets proved it directly, with a measurement you could almost watch with your own eye.

QUESTION: How sure could he be, though? Couldn't the lumpiness just be a coincidence in his numbers?

NARRATOR: This is where the X-ray gun earned its keep, and where Millikan's experiment goes from convincing to absolutely decisive. Because Millikan could do something no one before him could. He could take a single droplet, measure its charge, and then, without losing it, fire X-rays into the chamber to change its charge while he watched. The droplet would suddenly pick up an extra electron, or lose one, and its hovering balance would shift. He would readjust the voltage, measure the new charge, and compare.

NARRATOR: And every single time the charge changed, it changed by a whole-number multiple of that same fundamental unit. The droplet might jump from eight electrons to nine, or from twelve to ten. But the jump was always a clean whole number of those fundamental lumps. Never a fraction. He was watching, on a single droplet, the charge change one electron at a time. It was like watching a bucket fill drop by drop and finding that every drop was exactly identical in size. There was no escaping the conclusion. Electric charge exists only in discrete, indivisible units, and Millikan had measured the size of that unit.

QUESTION: So what number did he actually get?

NARRATOR: In his definitive paper, published in nineteen thirteen in the journal Physical Review, titled On the Elementary Electrical Charge and the Avogadro Constant, Millikan reported a value of one point five nine two times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. Now, a coulomb is the everyday unit of electric charge, the kind that flows through your phone charger. And ten to the minus nineteen of one is an almost absurdly tiny fraction. To give you a sense of it, it takes about six billion billion electrons flowing past every second to make up one amp of current in a household wire. The charge on a single electron is staggeringly small, and Millikan had pinned it down.

NARRATOR: The modern, internationally accepted value, fixed exactly by definition since two thousand and nineteen, is one point six oh two times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. Compare that to Millikan's one point five nine two. He was off by only about six-tenths of one percent. For a measurement made in nineteen thirteen, with a hand-built apparatus, timing droplets by eye through a telescope, that is breathtaking accuracy.

QUESTION: Where did that small error come from?

NARRATOR: Almost the entire error came from one source. Remember that to calculate the size of the droplet, Millikan had to use the viscosity of air, the measure of how thick and resistant the air is. He used the best published value available to him at the time, but that value was slightly wrong. The accepted figure for the viscosity of air was off by a fraction, and because that number fed directly into his calculations, his final answer for the charge inherited that small error. It was not a mistake in his method, nor a flaw in his reasoning. He simply trusted a value from the scientific literature that later turned out to be slightly inaccurate. Fix that one number, and his result snaps almost exactly onto the modern value. It is a reminder that a measurement is only ever as good as the constants you feed into it.

NARRATOR: For this achievement, along with his later experimental work confirming Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect, Robert Millikan was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in nineteen twenty-three. He had become, in the eyes of the world, the most precise experimental physicist of his era. But the road to that prize was not smooth, and the story has two shadows over it that we cannot ignore. The first is a feud. The second is a graduate student named Harvey Fletcher.

QUESTION: Let's start with the feud. Who disagreed with Millikan?

NARRATOR: His name was Felix Ehrenhaft, an Austrian physicist at the University of Vienna. And Ehrenhaft was not a crank. He was a serious, respected scientist, and he was doing experiments much like Millikan's, but instead of oil droplets he used tiny particles of metal, gold and silver and bismuth, that he made by vaporising metal wires in electric arcs. He measured the charges on these particles. And Ehrenhaft announced, starting around nineteen ten, the same year Millikan published his early results, that he was finding charges smaller than the electron. Fractions of the fundamental charge. A half. A fifth. A tenth. He even claimed to see charges as small as one one-hundredth of an electron's charge. He called these things subelectrons.

QUESTION: Subelectrons? Charges smaller than an electron?

NARRATOR: That is exactly what he claimed. And if he was right, it would have been catastrophic for Millikan's entire conclusion. Because the whole point of Millikan's result was that the electron's charge is the smallest indivisible unit, that charge is quantised, that you can never go below one electron. If Ehrenhaft could measure a charge of one-fifth of an electron, then charge was not quantised at all. It would mean charge could come in any amount, and the electron would not be a fundamental building block. The two men were making directly contradictory claims about the deepest nature of electricity. They could not both be right.

NARRATOR: And so began one of the most bitter and public disputes in early twentieth-century physics. It was argued at the great scientific conferences of the age, in rooms that contained the towering figures of physics, Max Planck, Albert Einstein, Jean Perrin, Arnold Sommerfeld, Max Born, Erwin Schrodinger. This was not a quiet disagreement in the back pages of a journal. This was a confrontation at the very front line of physics, watched by the people who were building the quantum revolution.

QUESTION: So how was it resolved? Was there some experiment that proved Ehrenhaft wrong?

NARRATOR: And here is the uncomfortable, fascinating truth. There never was a single decisive experiment that settled it. No one stood up and produced one clean result that demolished the subelectron. Ehrenhaft's measurements were genuinely puzzling. His metal particles were messier than oil droplets, probably contaminated, possibly with complicated charge distributions on their surfaces, possibly affected by the way light pushed on them. His subelectron values were almost certainly artifacts, errors created by his experimental method rather than real fractional charges. But nobody at the time could prove that conclusively.

NARRATOR: Einstein himself, as late as nineteen forty, thirty years into the dispute, said, and I quote, I believe that nobody has a clear idea about the causes producing the apparent sub-electronic charges he found. Think about that. The greatest physicist of the age, three decades later, admitting that the puzzle of Ehrenhaft's results was still not cleanly explained. What happened instead was that the physics community slowly, collectively, decided that Millikan was right. Not through one knockout blow, but through the steady accumulation of consistent evidence. Millikan's method was cleaner. His results were more reproducible. Other experiments, using entirely different techniques, kept confirming the quantised charge. And one by one, physicists came over to Millikan's side.

NARRATOR: Felix Ehrenhaft never gave up. He never published a retraction. He kept defending the subelectron into the nineteen forties, long after the rest of physics had moved on. Eventually he drifted to other unorthodox claims, including a search for magnetic monopoles, isolated north or south magnetic poles, which have never been found. He became a marginal figure, and the controversy faded not with a bang but with a slow, quiet ebbing away. It is a striking lesson in how science actually works. Sometimes the truth wins not by a single decisive experiment, but by the slow weight of consistency, by which side keeps being confirmed and which side keeps standing alone.

QUESTION: And the controversy delayed Millikan's Nobel Prize?

NARRATOR: It did. Millikan was considered for the Nobel Prize as early as nineteen twenty, but he was passed over, and one of the reasons noted at the time was precisely that the dispute with Ehrenhaft had not been resolved. The Swedish Academy was cautious. They did not want to award the highest honour in physics for a result that another respectable scientist was still publicly contesting. It was only in nineteen twenty-three, once the tide had clearly turned in Millikan's favour, that the prize was finally his. The feud cost him three years of recognition.

QUESTION: Now what about Harvey Fletcher? You said he got written out of the story.

NARRATOR: This is the part that, for me, gives the whole tale a melancholy human weight. Remember that Harvey Fletcher, the graduate student from Utah, suggested using oil instead of water, and was at the eyepiece for the crucial observation of balanced droplets. He did a great deal of the actual hands-on experimental work. He and Millikan together produced the foundational research. And when it came time to publish, the work was split into papers.

NARRATOR: Millikan proposed an arrangement. He, Millikan, would be the sole author on the most important paper, the one measuring the electron's charge, the paper that would become famous and win the Nobel Prize. And Fletcher would be sole author on a companion paper, about Brownian motion, the random jiggling of the droplets, which Fletcher could use as his doctoral thesis. The justification Millikan offered was a University of Chicago rule that a doctoral thesis had to be the sole work of the student, single-authored. The charge-measurement paper had two authors and so, Millikan argued, could not serve as Fletcher's thesis. But the Brownian motion paper, single-authored by Fletcher, could.

QUESTION: Did Fletcher agree to that?

NARRATOR: He agreed. But according to Fletcher's own account, written near the end of his life, he felt he had little choice. Millikan held enormous power over Fletcher's future, over whether he would get his doctorate at all. Fletcher took the deal. And so his name does not appear on the famous oil-drop paper. The arrangement was kept secret. Fletcher told no one. He went on to a brilliant career of his own at Bell Laboratories, where he became a pioneer of acoustics and is independently credited with helping to invent stereophonic sound. He lived a long and distinguished life. But he never received public credit, during his lifetime, for his role in one of the most important experiments of the century.

NARRATOR: The truth came out only after Fletcher died, in nineteen eighty-one. He had written a memoir, and he left instructions that it be published after his death. It appeared in the magazine Physics Today, and in it he told the whole story of the deal, the oil suggestion, the balanced droplets, the secret arrangement. It forced a reappraisal, among historians, of how credit is shared, and how it is sometimes quietly taken, in collaborative science. Some historians note that Millikan did support Fletcher's career afterward, helping him land at Bell Labs, and see that as a kind of implicit compensation. But the fact remains. The graduate student who suggested the oil, and who watched the first droplets hover, was written out of the headline.

QUESTION: There's one more shadow you mentioned. Something about Millikan's data?

NARRATOR: Yes. And this is the story that turned Millikan's experiment into a legend in a completely different way, a cautionary tale taught in philosophy of science courses around the world. It began in nineteen seventy-eight, decades after Millikan's death, when a physicist and historian named Gerald Holton got access to Millikan's original laboratory notebooks. The handwritten records, the raw observations, droplet by droplet, scribbled down at the eyepiece.

NARRATOR: And what Holton found was uncomfortable. Millikan's published nineteen thirteen paper presented data from fifty-eight oil droplets. But the notebooks contained records for around one hundred and seventy-five droplets. In other words, Millikan reported only about a third of the droplets he had actually measured. He had left out roughly two-thirds of his data. And in the margins of his notebooks, next to individual droplets, he had written little comments. Beautiful, he wrote beside some. And beside others, things like, this will not work, or, no good, throw out.

QUESTION: So he was cherry-picking the data that looked good?

NARRATOR: That is the accusation, and it is more pointed than just selecting data. Because in the published paper itself, Millikan wrote that his result was based on, quote, all of the drops experimented upon during a particular period. All of the drops. But Holton's analysis of the notebooks suggested that this was not true. He had not used all the drops. He had used the ones he chose to use. And that statement in the paper, claiming he had used all of them, appears to have been false.

QUESTION: So did Millikan commit fraud?

NARRATOR: This is fiercely debated, and the answer is genuinely not simple, so let me be careful and fair. The defence, argued most prominently by the physicist and historian Allan Franklin, is that the droplets Millikan excluded had legitimate technical problems. Some were measured when the apparatus was disturbed, or the oil was contaminated, or the conditions were not properly controlled. An experienced experimenter develops a feel for which measurements are trustworthy and which are corrupted by some glitch. And crucially, Franklin showed that if you put all one hundred and seventy-five droplets back in, including the excluded ones, the final answer barely changes. The value of the electron's charge comes out essentially the same. The excluded droplets would only have made his error bars slightly larger. They would not have changed the result.

NARRATOR: So this was not a case of inventing fake data, or of throwing out good droplets that gave an inconvenient answer in order to fake a result. The answer Millikan published was correct. But the prosecution's point still stands. He selected his data, and then he told the world he had not. The statement that he used all the drops was misleading at best. The honest consensus among historians today is somewhere in the middle. This was not outright fabrication, but it was, at minimum, questionable scientific ethics, a polishing of the record, a presentation that was cleaner and more certain than the messy reality behind it.

QUESTION: You said it became a cautionary tale. How?

NARRATOR: It became one through the words of another great physicist, Richard Feynman, in a famous nineteen seventy-four address at Caltech that he called Cargo Cult Science. Feynman used Millikan's experiment as his central example, but not for the data selection. He pointed at something even more subtle and unsettling, something that happened after Millikan published.

NARRATOR: Feynman noticed that when other scientists measured the electron's charge in the years after nineteen thirteen, their values crept upward over time. The first new measurement after Millikan was a little higher than his. The next was a little higher still. And the next. Over the decades, the measured value slowly climbed until it settled at the modern, correct figure, which is noticeably higher than Millikan's. Now ask yourself, why would the measurements climb gradually like that, instead of just scattering randomly around the true value from the start?

QUESTION: Yes, why would they?

NARRATOR: Because of human psychology. Here is how Feynman put it. When a scientist got a value that was much higher than Millikan's, they thought, something must be wrong with my apparatus, and they hunted for the error until they found a reason to bring the number down closer to Millikan's. But when they got a value close to Millikan's, they thought, good, this looks right, and they did not look so hard for mistakes. So the errors that pushed the number too far from Millikan got caught and corrected, while the errors that kept it near Millikan got left alone. The whole field was unconsciously dragged toward Millikan's slightly wrong answer, and it took decades to crawl free of it.

NARRATOR: It is one of the most important warnings in all of science. Not about lying, not about fraud, but about the much more dangerous and invisible enemy, the unconscious bias of the experimenter who already knows what answer they expect to get. The number you are looking for can quietly bend the number you find. Feynman's lesson, drawn directly from Millikan's electron, is that the hardest person to keep honest in science is yourself.

QUESTION: So with all these shadows, where does that leave Millikan's experiment? Was it a triumph or a scandal?

NARRATOR: It was both, and that is what makes it such a rich and human story. Strip away the controversies, and what remains is a genuine triumph. Millikan measured the fundamental charge to better than one percent. He proved, directly and visibly, that electric charge is quantised, that it comes in indivisible lumps. And that single number, the charge on the electron, unlocked a cascade of other knowledge.

NARRATOR: Once you know the charge on one electron, you can finally untangle Thomson's ratio and calculate the actual mass of the electron, about nine times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms, almost unimaginably light. You can combine the electron's charge with the known facts of electrochemistry to calculate Avogadro's number, the count of atoms in a mole, which confirmed the entire atomic theory of matter and let chemists count atoms. The charge of the electron defines the electronvolt, the natural unit of energy in atomic and particle physics that scientists still use every single day. Millikan's number is woven into the foundations of modern physics.

NARRATOR: And the proof of quantisation was a pillar of the emerging quantum revolution. Just as Max Planck had shown that energy comes in discrete packets, and Niels Bohr was showing in that same year, nineteen thirteen, that electrons in atoms occupy only discrete orbits, Millikan showed that charge too comes in discrete units. The picture that was forming was profound and strange. At the smallest scales, nature is not smooth and continuous. It is grainy. It comes in countable lumps. Energy in lumps, charge in lumps. Reality, at its foundation, is quantised. Millikan's hovering oil droplet was one of the first direct, almost tangible demonstrations of that astonishing truth.

QUESTION: Does Millikan's result still hold up today? Modern physics has quarks, doesn't it, with fractional charges?

NARRATOR: A wonderful question, and the answer is subtle. Yes, in the nineteen sixties and seventies, physicists discovered quarks, the particles that make up protons and neutrons. And quarks do carry fractional charges, one-third or two-thirds of the electron's charge. At first glance, this sounds like Ehrenhaft was right all along, that there are charges smaller than the electron.

NARRATOR: But here is the resolution. Quarks are never found alone. They are permanently locked up inside protons, neutrons, and other particles, bound together so tightly that you can never pull one out by itself. This is called confinement. And the charges of those bound combinations always add up to a whole-number multiple of the electron's charge. A proton, made of quarks with charges of two-thirds, two-thirds, and minus one-third, adds up to exactly one electron's worth of positive charge. So for any free particle, anything you can actually isolate and measure on its own, the charge is still always a whole-number multiple of the fundamental charge. Millikan's law holds perfectly. You simply cannot isolate a free third of a charge. Ehrenhaft's subelectrons were not quarks. They were experimental errors. And the deepest modern measurements, from the quantum Hall effect to the Josephson effect, confirm the value of the fundamental charge with a precision Millikan could only have dreamed of.

NARRATOR: In fact, in two thousand and nineteen, the international community redefined the entire system of measurement units around fundamental constants of nature.
