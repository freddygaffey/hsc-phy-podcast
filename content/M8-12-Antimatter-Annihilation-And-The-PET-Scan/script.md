NARRATOR: Last episode was nuclear fusion — powering the stars. The core idea: light nuclei fuse into a more tightly bound nucleus and release energy, climbing up the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve toward iron. To get them to fuse you need a colossal temperature, to slam the nuclei together hard enough to beat their Coulomb repulsion and bring them inside the short range of the strong nuclear force, and high pressure and density so the collisions happen often enough. The deuterium-tritium reaction, the one we're chasing on Earth in reactors like ITER, releases about seventeen point six mega-electron-volts. And the iron limit caps stellar fusion: once a star builds iron in its core, fusion stops releasing energy, because iron sits at the very peak of the curve. Hold onto that curve — it's the spine of this whole nuclear block.

NARRATOR: Before that was mass defect, binding energy, and the equation that runs today's episode: energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. Mass and energy are two forms of the same thing, fully interconvertible, and the conversion rate is the speed of light squared — about nine times ten to the sixteen, a gigantic number — so a tiny scrap of mass is worth an enormous amount of energy. We met the mass defect: a bound nucleus weighs less than its separate parts, and that missing mass was released as the binding energy when the nucleus formed. And the exam phrase to never break: mass is not destroyed, it is converted into energy; only the combined quantity, mass-energy, is conserved. Today we push that idea to its absolute limit.

NARRATOR: Two episodes back was half-life and the mathematics of decay. The exam point that matters most today: you match a radioisotope to a job by its half-life and its radiation type. A medical isotope wants a half-life long enough to do the imaging but short enough to clear the patient's body fast and keep the radiation dose down. File that suitability logic away — it comes back in about twenty minutes when we choose a tracer for a PET scan.

NARRATOR: Start with the particle itself. The positron is the antiparticle of the electron. Get this definition exactly right, because it's a pure recall mark and students throw it away with vagueness. The positron has exactly the same mass as the electron — nine point one one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms — and exactly the same magnitude of charge, but the charge is positive, plus one elementary charge, where the electron's is negative one. Same mass, opposite charge. That's the whole definition. Its symbol, when you write a nuclear equation, is an "e" with a mass number of zero on the top left and a charge of plus one on the bottom left — chemically written, the same way you'd write a beta particle but with the charge flipped to positive.

NARRATOR: And that's the general pattern for all antimatter: a particle and its antiparticle share the same mass and the same spin, and differ only in the sign of their charge and a few other quantum labels. Antimatter isn't science fiction — it's matter with the charges reversed. The positron is just the simplest, most exam-relevant example.

QUESTION: Where did the idea of antimatter even come from? Is this a "name the scientist" mark?

NARRATOR: It can be, so know the two names and what each one did, because the exam likes to test whether you can separate prediction from discovery. The positron was predicted by Paul Dirac, around nineteen twenty-eight to nineteen thirty-one, out of pure mathematics. Dirac wrote down a relativistic quantum equation for the electron, and the equation had extra solutions — solutions with negative energy — that only made sense if there existed a particle just like the electron but with the opposite charge. He predicted antimatter from an equation, before anyone had seen it. Then, in nineteen thirty-two to thirty-three, Carl Anderson discovered it for real, in a cloud chamber, watching cosmic-ray tracks. He saw a track that curved in a magnetic field exactly like an electron's, but in the opposite direction — same mass, opposite charge. There was Dirac's particle. Memory hook for the pair: Dirac Dreamt it, Anderson Anchored it. Dreamt from the maths, anchored in the lab.

NARRATOR: Now, where do positrons actually come from? The exam wants two production routes, so here's a clean hook for the pair — call it the two B's: Beta-plus, and Burst-from-a-photon. Let's take them one at a time.

NARRATOR: First, beta-plus decay. This is the link straight back to our radioactivity episode. Inside an unstable nucleus that has too many protons — a proton-rich nucleus — a proton converts into a neutron, and in doing so it emits a positron and a neutrino. In words, the nuclear equation reads: a proton becomes a neutron, plus a positron, plus a neutrino. The neutron stays in the nucleus; the positron and neutrino fly out. Notice what happens to the daughter nucleus: the mass number is unchanged, because a proton became a neutron and they're both nucleons, but the atomic number drops by one, because we lost a proton.

NARRATOR: Now here's the trap, and it's worth a mark every time. In beta-plus decay the atomic number goes down by one. Students constantly reverse it, because ordinary beta-minus decay does the opposite — there a neutron becomes a proton, and the atomic number goes up by one. So lock in the contrast: beta-minus, neutron-to-proton, atomic number rises; beta-plus, proton-to-neutron, atomic number falls. And the second trap, which is a conceptual one: the nucleus does not contain a positron sitting there waiting to leave. The positron is created at the instant of decay, when the proton converts. Nuclei do not store electrons or positrons. Say "created," never "released from inside."

QUESTION: If I had to write a real example of beta-plus decay, what would earn the mark?

NARRATOR: Use a real isotope and balance it. Carbon-eleven is perfect, because it's an actual isotope used in PET scans, so it's a two-for-one. Carbon-eleven — six protons — decays to boron-eleven — five protons — plus a positron, plus a neutrino. Check the bookkeeping out loud, because that's what the marker checks: mass number, eleven equals eleven on both sides, good. Atomic number, six on the left equals five for boron plus one for the positron, equals six on the right, balanced. The textbook's other standard example is sodium-twenty-two decaying to neon-twenty-two plus a positron plus a neutrino — same pattern, atomic number eleven dropping to ten. Either one is a model answer.

NARRATOR: That's the first B, Beta-plus. The second B is Burst-from-a-photon — properly called pair production, and it's the reverse of the process we'll spend most of today on, so it's worth getting straight now to avoid mixing them up later. In pair production, a single high-energy gamma photon passes near a nucleus and converts its energy into mass, creating an electron and a positron together — a particle and its antiparticle, born as a pair. In words: a gamma photon becomes an electron plus a positron.

NARRATOR: Two things the exam wants here. First, why a pair — why does it make both, not just one? Because charge must be conserved. The photon has zero charge; to make matter out of it and keep the total charge at zero, you must make one negative particle and one positive particle, which cancel. So an electron and a positron together. Second, there's a minimum energy: the photon has to carry at least enough energy to pay for the rest mass of both particles. Each particle's rest energy is about zero point five one one mega-electron-volts, so the photon needs at least twice that, one point zero two two mega-electron-volts. Below that threshold, no pair. That number, one point zero two two mega-electron-volts, is a value to memorise — it's exactly two electron rest-energies, and it'll show up again in a moment.

NARRATOR: Right. Now the heart of the episode: positron-electron annihilation. This is a named syllabus dot-point and the purest example of energy equals mass times the speed of light squared in the whole course, so we're going to do it slowly and we're going to nail the mark-earning phrasing.

NARRATOR: Here's the chain of events. A positron is emitted — say from a beta-plus decay. It travels a short distance through matter, slowing down. Now, matter is absolutely full of electrons — every atom has them — so the positron very quickly meets an electron. And when a particle meets its antiparticle, they annihilate. That word is exact and you should use it: they annihilate. Their entire combined mass is converted into energy. There is no matter left over at all — not a smaller particle, not a fragment — the electron and the positron both cease to exist as matter, and all of their rest mass becomes electromagnetic energy.

NARRATOR: And that energy comes out as light — specifically, as two gamma-ray photons, flying off in almost exactly opposite directions, about a hundred and eighty degrees apart. Two photons. Opposite directions. Those two facts are the entire difference between a band-six answer and a band-three answer, so let me tell you why each one is true, because the exam will ask you to explain, not just state.

NARRATOR: First, why two photons in opposite directions, rather than one? This is the single most penalised error in the topic, so I'm going to labour it. The answer is conservation of momentum. Think about the moment of annihilation. The positron has slowed almost to rest, and the electron it meets is essentially at rest too, so the total momentum of the pair, just before they annihilate, is very nearly zero. Conservation of momentum says the total momentum after must also be very nearly zero. Now, here's the key physics: a photon always carries momentum. It can never have zero momentum — a photon at rest doesn't exist. So a single photon flying off on its own would carry momentum in some direction, and the total momentum after would not be zero. That violates conservation of momentum. The only way to make light with zero total momentum is to produce two equal photons travelling in opposite directions, so their momenta cancel. Two photons, back to back, momenta cancelling to zero — that's what momentum conservation forces. One photon is impossible.

QUESTION: Hold on — let me make sure I've actually got this. Pause the player here and answer it yourself before I do: why exactly must annihilation produce two photons in opposite directions, and not one? Pause now, say the full reason out loud, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the full-mark version. Just before annihilation the electron and positron are essentially at rest, so the system's total momentum is zero. Momentum must be conserved, so the products must also have zero total momentum. A single photon always carries momentum and cannot have zero, so one photon is forbidden; two photons emitted in opposite directions have equal and opposite momenta that cancel to zero, satisfying conservation of momentum. If you said "momentum has to be conserved, and two opposite photons cancel but one can't" — that's the mark. If you only said "two gamma rays come out," you've stated the what but not the why, and you've left marks on the table.

NARRATOR: Now the energy side, which is where the mass-energy conservation dot-point lives. The total energy carried away by the two photons equals the total rest mass of the electron and positron times the speed of light squared. Since each particle's rest energy is zero point five one one mega-electron-volts, the two together are one point zero two two mega-electron-volts — and that energy is shared equally between the two photons, so each photon carries exactly zero point five one one mega-electron-volts. Get those two numbers the right way round, because it's a classic slip: each photon is zero point five one one; the total released is one point zero two two. Don't say each photon is one point zero two two.

NARRATOR: And this is conservation of mass-energy made visible — literally visible, as gamma light. Mass goes in: two particles, with definite rest mass. Energy comes out: two photons, carrying exactly the energy equivalent of that mass. Nothing is lost, nothing is destroyed in the forbidden sense — the mass is converted, completely, into energy, and the books balance to the last electron-volt. In fusion, less than one percent of the mass converts. Here, one hundred percent of the matter's rest mass becomes energy. That's why I called it the purest case of energy equals mass times the speed of light squared in the syllabus. Remember when we did mass defect and I kept hammering "converted, not destroyed"? This is the extreme version of the same law.

NARRATOR: Let me hand you the model extended-response answer now, because "describe positron-electron annihilation and explain how it conserves mass-energy" is about as predictable as exam questions get. Here's the strong answer. A positron is the antiparticle of the electron — identical mass, opposite, positive charge. When a positron meets an electron, the two annihilate: their entire combined rest mass is converted into energy, in accordance with energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. The energy is released as two gamma-ray photons travelling in opposite directions, each carrying zero point five one one mega-electron-volts — the rest energy of one particle — for a total of one point zero two two mega-electron-volts. Mass-energy is conserved because the energy of the photons exactly equals the rest energy of the two particles destroyed. Two photons in opposite directions are required to conserve momentum: the pair annihilates essentially at rest, so the products must have zero total momentum, which a single photon — always carrying momentum — cannot provide, but two equal back-to-back photons can.

NARRATOR: Now hear the weak answer, because seeing the boundary is how you climb bands. A weak answer says: "The positron and electron destroy each other and make gamma rays." That earns you maybe one mark out of three or four. Look at everything it's missing. It doesn't say how many photons — and "two" is load-bearing. It doesn't say opposite directions — and PET depends entirely on that. It doesn't give the energy — zero point five one one each, one point zero two two total. And critically, it never explains why: no mention of momentum conservation, no mention of mass-energy conservation by name. The strong answer says two, opposite, zero-point-five-one-one each, and explains both conservation laws. Add those and a one-mark answer becomes a full-mark answer. And one word to ban on sight: don't write that the particles are "destroyed" and leave it there — say the mass is "converted into energy." Same trap as the binding-energy episode.

NARRATOR: Quick pause to keep pair production and annihilation from blurring together, because the exam loves to test exactly this confusion. They are reverses of each other. Pair production: a gamma photon becomes an electron and a positron — energy into mass — it happens near a nucleus, it needs at least one point zero two two mega-electron-volts, and it makes two particles to conserve charge. Annihilation: an electron and a positron become two gamma photons — mass into energy — and it makes two photons to conserve momentum. Notice the elegant symmetry, and notice the different reasons for "two": pair production makes two particles for charge; annihilation makes two photons for momentum. Memory hook: Production makes Particles for charge; annihilation makes Photons for momentum. Two P's, two reasons. Don't swap them.

NARRATOR: Okay — now we take this physics into a hospital. Positron emission tomography, PET. This is the second named dot-point: explain how PET uses positron-electron annihilation. And the beautiful thing is that PET is just the annihilation chain you already understand, wrapped in clever engineering. Let me give you the seven-step story, and I'll give you a memory hook so you can reproduce the whole sequence in order under exam pressure.

NARRATOR: The hook is the word TRACER, spelled T-R-A-C-E-R, and it walks you through the steps. T — Tracer injected. R — Reaches active tissue. A — Annihilation, with an electron. C — Coincidence detection. E — Establish the line. R — Reconstruct the image. Hold "TRACER" in your head and the seven beats fall out. Let's walk them.

NARRATOR: Step one, the Tracer. The patient is injected with a radioactive tracer — a chemical containing a short-lived positron-emitting radioisotope, a beta-plus emitter. The classic one is fluorine-eighteen, built into a molecule called FDG, which is a glucose analogue — a molecule the body treats like sugar. Carbon-eleven, the isotope we balanced earlier, is another real PET tracer, along with nitrogen-thirteen and oxygen-fifteen. And here's where our half-life suitability logic comes straight back from two episodes ago: the isotope must be short-lived. The half-life has to be long enough to inject the tracer, let it spread, and run the scan — but short enough that it clears the body quickly and the patient's radiation dose stays low. Fluorine-eighteen's half-life is about a hundred and ten minutes, which threads that needle. This is exactly the M8-08 suitability argument, and a question can absolutely ask you to justify the half-life choice — so if it does, do not say "a longer half-life is better." Longer is worse: more dose, slower clearance.

NARRATOR: Step two, it Reaches the active tissue. Because FDG behaves like glucose, it concentrates wherever the body is consuming a lot of glucose — that is, in metabolically active tissue. Tumours are hungry, fast-metabolising tissue, so they soak up the tracer. Active regions of the brain do too. So the tracer naturally accumulates in exactly the places a doctor wants to see. Hold onto this point, because it's the answer to a particular exam question I'll flag at the end: PET images function, not just anatomy.

NARRATOR: Step three, Annihilation. Inside the body, each emitted positron travels only a tiny distance — a few millimetres at most — before it meets one of the countless electrons in the surrounding tissue, and the two annihilate. And now you know exactly what that produces: two gamma photons, each zero point five one one mega-electron-volts, flying off in opposite directions, about a hundred and eighty degrees apart. The whole PET machine is built to catch those two opposite photons.

NARRATOR: Step four, Coincidence detection. The patient lies inside a ring of gamma detectors that completely surrounds them. When an annihilation happens, the two opposite photons race outwards and strike the detector ring on opposite sides at essentially the same instant. The electronics watch for exactly that: two detections arriving simultaneously, on opposite sides of the ring. That's called coincidence detection — two hits at the same time count as one real event, and anything that arrives alone is ignored as noise. The word "coincidence" is the technical term and worth using.

NARRATOR: Step five, Establish the line. Here's the clever geometry. Because the two photons came from a single annihilation and flew off in exactly opposite directions, the annihilation must have happened somewhere on the straight line joining the two detectors that fired. That straight line is called the line of response, and the annihilation point lies on it. A single coincidence doesn't tell you where on the line — but it does tell you the annihilation was on that line, which is a huge amount of information.

NARRATOR: Step six, Reconstruct. Now scale it up. The scanner records not one but millions of these coincidence events, each giving its own line of response, crisscrossing the body from every angle. Where many lines intersect, that's where lots of annihilations happened, which is where lots of tracer collected, which is where the tissue is most metabolically active. A computer takes all those millions of lines and reconstructs a three-dimensional image of tracer concentration throughout the body. Bright spots are high uptake — a tumour, or an active brain region lighting up.

NARRATOR: And that's PET. The directionality you might have been tempted to drop from the annihilation answer turns out to be the entire basis of the imaging — without the two photons being exactly opposite, there's no line of response, and the whole technique collapses. So when you describe annihilation, never omit "opposite directions"; PET is the reason it matters.

QUESTION: Can you give me the full model answer for "explain how a PET scan produces an image"? That feels like a guaranteed six-marker.

NARRATOR: It is, and here's the structured version that hits every mark. A positron-emitting radioisotope, carried in a tracer such as FDG, is injected and concentrates in metabolically active tissue, because FDG behaves like glucose. Each emitted positron annihilates with a nearby electron, producing two gamma photons of zero point five one one mega-electron-volts travelling in opposite directions. A ring of detectors around the patient registers the two photons in coincidence — simultaneously, on opposite sides — and the straight line joining those two detectors, the line of response, passes through the annihilation point. By combining millions of these lines of response, a computer reconstructs a three-dimensional image of tracer concentration, which maps the metabolic activity of the tissue and reveals features such as tumours or active brain regions. Notice the spine of that answer: tracer concentrates, positron annihilates into two opposite photons, coincidence detection defines a line, many lines build the image. Four beats, all in the TRACER hook.

NARRATOR: Worked example one: the energy of an annihilation photon. The question: calculate the energy, in joules and in mega-electron-volts, of each gamma photon produced when an electron and a positron at rest annihilate. NARRATOR: Step one: the total energy released equals the rest energy of both particles, because all their mass converts. QUESTION: Quick one — why don't we just detect the gamma photons that come straight out of the radioactive decay, like in the half-life episode? Why bother with the positron and the annihilation at all?

NARRATOR: Good instinct, and the contrast sharpens both topics. NARRATOR: Model answer. The fluorine-eighteen, carried in FDG, a glucose analogue, is injected and concentrates in metabolically active tissue because the body treats FDG like glucose. Fluorine-eighteen is a positron emitter: each emitted positron travels a few millimetres, meets an electron, and annihilates, producing two gamma photons of zero point five one one mega-electron-volts in opposite directions. A surrounding ring of detectors registers the two photons in coincidence — simultaneously, on opposite sides — and the line joining those detectors, the line of response, passes through the annihilation site. A computer combines millions of such lines to reconstruct a three-dimensional image of tracer concentration, mapping metabolic activity and revealing, for example, a tumour. A short-lived isotope is used because the half-life must be long enough to image the patient but short enough that the tracer decays and clears quickly, minimising the radiation dose — fluorine-eighteen's half-life of about a hundred and ten minutes achieves this.
