NARRATOR: On a December morning in nineteen thirty-eight, in a laboratory in the Berlin suburb of Dahlem, two chemists stared at a result they could not believe. They had been bombarding uranium with neutrons for months, expecting to build heavier elements, elements beyond uranium, the heaviest thing in nature. Instead, sitting in their test tubes was barium. Barium is not a heavy element. Barium is small. Its nucleus has fifty-six protons. Uranium has ninety-two. Nothing in the physics of the time could explain how firing a neutron at a uranium atom could leave you with something only a little more than half its size.

NARRATOR: Otto Hahn was the chemist in charge. He was so unsettled that he sat down late at night, nearly eleven o'clock on the nineteenth of December, and wrote a letter to a former colleague who had fled the country five months earlier. She was the physicist he had worked beside for thirty years. Her name was Lise Meitner, and she was now an exile in Sweden. In the letter, Hahn admitted he and his junior partner Fritz Strassmann simply could not make sense of it. He wrote, in effect, perhaps you can come up with some sort of fantastic explanation. We know ourselves that it can't actually burst apart into barium.

NARRATOR: But it had. And over the Christmas holidays, sitting on a log in the snowy woods of a small Swedish coastal town, Lise Meitner did the calculation that explained it. On scraps of paper, she worked out that when a uranium nucleus splits, the energy released is around two hundred million electron volts. That is roughly twenty million times more energy, atom for atom, than you get from burning coal. She had just understood nuclear fission. And within seven years, that understanding would end a world war, level two cities, and change the relationship between physics and power forever.

NARRATOR: This is the story of nuclear fission. The chain reaction. The reactor. The bomb. And the brilliant exiled physicist who did the maths that started it all, and was then written out of the history books.

QUESTION: So before we go any further, what actually is fission? What does it mean to split an atom?

NARRATOR: Let's build it carefully, because the whole story turns on getting the physics right. Every atom has a nucleus at its centre, a tiny dense ball of protons and neutrons, what we call nucleons. Two forces are fighting inside that nucleus. The first is the electrostatic force, the repulsion between the positively charged protons. Like charges repel, and protons are all crammed together, so they push each other apart, hard. The second force is the strong nuclear force. It is enormously powerful, but it only works over an incredibly short range, about one femtometre, which is one times ten to the minus fifteen metres. It is a nearest-neighbour force. Each nucleon only feels the strong force from the nucleons right next to it.

NARRATOR: In a small nucleus, the strong force wins easily. Every nucleon is close to every other nucleon, so the binding holds them tight. But as you add more and more protons, the electrostatic repulsion grows, because every proton repels every other proton across the whole nucleus, not just its neighbours. By the time you reach uranium, with ninety-two protons, the nucleus is enormous and the repulsion is almost strong enough to tear it apart on its own. Uranium is sitting right at the edge of stability. It is a coiled spring waiting for a nudge.

QUESTION: And the neutron is the nudge?

NARRATOR: The neutron is the nudge. And here is a beautiful piece of physics that people get backwards all the time. You might think you need a fast neutron, a hard punch, to break the nucleus. The opposite is true. A slow neutron, a thermal neutron, ambling along at the speed of a gas molecule at room temperature, is far more effective. The reason is that the neutron has no charge. It is not repelled by the nucleus, so it does not need speed to get in. In fact, if it comes in too fast, it tends to glance off or recoil away. A slow neutron lingers near the nucleus long enough to be captured. Physicists call this a larger reaction cross-section. The slower the neutron, the bigger the target appears.

NARRATOR: When uranium-two-thirty-five captures a slow neutron, it briefly becomes uranium-two-thirty-six, but in a wildly excited, unstable state. And now we get to the insight that Meitner had on that log in the woods. To understand what happens next, you have to picture the nucleus not as a solid ball, but as a drop of liquid.

QUESTION: A liquid drop? An atomic nucleus?

NARRATOR: A liquid drop. This was a model that the Danish physicist Niels Bohr had been developing. Think about a droplet of water. It is held together by surface tension, which pulls it into a nice round sphere. Now imagine you give that droplet some energy. You make it wobble. It stretches into an oval, then back to a sphere, then stretches the other way. The surface tension keeps trying to pull it back into a ball. The nucleus behaves the same way. The strong nuclear force acts like surface tension, holding it together. The electrostatic repulsion between the protons acts to push it apart.

NARRATOR: Now here is the critical thing for uranium. When that excited uranium nucleus starts to wobble and stretch into an elongated shape, the two ends move further apart. And because the electrostatic force pushes apart even at long range, as the nucleus stretches, the repulsion between the two ends starts to win against the surface tension trying to pull it back. Past a certain point, there is no going back. The droplet necks in the middle, pinches off, and splits into two smaller droplets, two new nuclei, which then fly apart violently, driven by their mutual electrical repulsion. Plus, two or three free neutrons are flung out in the process. That is fission. The word, by the way, was borrowed from biology, from the way a living cell divides in two.

QUESTION: And the energy? Where does two hundred million electron volts come from?

NARRATOR: This is the heart of it, and this is what Meitner calculated. The energy comes from mass. Specifically, from a tiny amount of mass that simply disappears in the reaction. Here is the key fact that underpins all of nuclear physics. The mass of any nucleus is slightly less than the sum of the masses of the protons and neutrons that make it up. That missing mass is called the mass defect. When nucleons bind together, they release energy, and by Einstein's relation, energy and mass are the same thing, so the bound nucleus weighs a little less than its loose parts. The energy equivalent of that missing mass is what physicists call the binding energy. It is the energy you would have to pour back in to pull the nucleus apart into separate, free protons and neutrons. A bigger mass defect means a bigger binding energy means a more tightly bound, more stable nucleus.

NARRATOR: Now there is a single graph that explains why fission releases energy at all, and it is worth carrying in your head for the exam. If you plot the binding energy per nucleon, the binding energy shared out among all the protons and neutrons, against the size of the nucleus, you get a curve that rises steeply for light nuclei, reaches a peak, and then slopes gently downward for the heaviest ones. The peak of that curve, the most tightly bound, most stable nucleus in all of nature, sits at iron, specifically iron-fifty-six, at almost nine million electron volts per nucleon. Everything in the universe is, in a sense, trying to get to iron. And here is the crucial point for fission. Uranium sits far out on the right-hand slope, only about seven and a half million electron volts per nucleon. When it splits into two middling fragments like barium and krypton, those fragments sit higher up the curve, closer to the iron peak, around eight and a half million electron volts per nucleon. The products are more tightly bound than the original. They have a larger total binding energy. And that surplus binding energy, that extra mass defect, is exactly what gets released. This is also why fusion of light nuclei releases energy, by climbing the steep left-hand side of the same curve toward iron from below. Fission slides down the right side, fusion climbs up the left, and both pour out energy as they head for iron-fifty-six.

NARRATOR: Now follow the bookkeeping. Take a uranium-two-thirty-five nucleus and a neutron. Add up their masses. Then look at the products, say a barium-one-forty-one nucleus, a krypton-ninety-two nucleus, and three neutrons. Add up their masses too. And here is the astonishing thing. The products weigh less than the reactants. Some mass has vanished. For this particular reaction, the missing mass is about zero point one eight six atomic mass units. That does not sound like much. But you convert it to energy using Einstein's equation, energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. And because the speed of light is three times ten to the eighth metres per second, and you square it, that conversion factor is gigantic. One atomic mass unit of vanished mass becomes nine hundred and thirty-one point five million electron volts of energy.

NARRATOR: So zero point one eight six atomic mass units of missing mass turns into roughly one hundred and seventy-three million electron volts of energy, carried mostly as the kinetic energy of the two fission fragments hurtling apart. Add in the energy from the gamma rays and the radioactive decay of the fragments afterward, and the total comes to about two hundred million electron volts per single fission event. Meitner, sitting on that log, estimated the two product nuclei would be lighter than the original by about one fifth the mass of a proton, applied Einstein's equation, and got two hundred million electron volts. Her nephew Otto Frisch, who was standing there listening, later said it was an energy large enough to make a visible grain of sand jump.

QUESTION: Two hundred million electron volts. Is that actually a lot? It is hard to picture an electron volt.

NARRATOR: On its own, two hundred million electron volts is a minuscule amount of energy in human terms, about three times ten to the minus eleven joules. But remember, that is from a single atom. Compare it to a chemical reaction. When you burn a single atom of carbon in coal, you get about ten electron volts. Ten. Fission gives you two hundred million. That is twenty million times more energy from a single atom. This is why nuclear is in a different universe from chemical energy. One kilogram of uranium-two-thirty-five, if it fissioned completely, would release about eight times ten to the thirteenth joules, equivalent to roughly twenty thousand tonnes of TNT. From one kilogram. That is the secret the universe had been keeping, and in December nineteen thirty-eight it was finally out.

NARRATOR: But to understand why this discovery landed in Berlin, and why the woman who explained it was sitting in exile in Sweden, we have to go back. Because this is not just a story about physics. It is a story about people, about a thirty-year partnership, and about a regime that drove the brightest minds out of Germany and into the arms of its enemies.

NARRATOR: Lise Meitner was born in Vienna in eighteen seventy-eight, the third of eight children in a Jewish family. She was brilliant, and she fought for every inch of her education at a time when universities barely admitted women. In nineteen oh-seven she moved to Berlin and began working with the chemist Otto Hahn. They made an extraordinary team. He was the master of radiochemistry, the painstaking art of separating and identifying radioactive substances. She was the physicist, the one who understood what the chemistry meant. But the institute would not let a woman work in the main building. For years, Meitner had to work in a converted carpenter's workshop in the basement, with a separate entrance, and she was not even paid at first. She and Hahn discovered the element protactinium together in nineteen seventeen. For three decades they were partners and close friends.

NARRATOR: And then came the Nazis. When Hitler took power in nineteen thirty-three, Jewish scientists across Germany were stripped of their posts. Meitner, an Austrian citizen, was protected for a while by her foreign nationality. But on the twelfth of March nineteen thirty-eight, Germany annexed Austria in the event known as the Anschluss. Overnight, Meitner became a German citizen, and that meant she was now subject to the full force of the Nazi racial laws. She was fifty-nine years old. She was one of the most distinguished physicists in Europe. And she was suddenly trapped, because the regime had also begun forbidding its prominent scientists from leaving the country.

QUESTION: So how did she get out?

NARRATOR: It was a rescue, organised in secret by colleagues abroad. Dutch physicists, chief among them Dirk Coster, spent weeks lobbying their government to let her cross the border without a valid passport. On the eleventh of July nineteen thirty-eight, Coster travelled to Berlin. On the thirteenth and fourteenth of July, Meitner boarded a train toward the Dutch frontier. She carried two small suitcases with summer clothes and ten Reichsmarks, the maximum the regime allowed anyone to take out. Hahn had given her a diamond ring he had inherited from his mother, to be used as a bribe at the border if things went wrong. As the train approached the crossing, a Nazi military patrol moved through the carriages checking documents. Meitner had no valid papers. She later said she was paralysed with fear. But Coster had done his work, and the officers let her pass. She crossed into the Netherlands, then went on to Sweden, and started her life over again at fifty-nine, in a country whose language she did not speak, in a junior post under a man who gave her almost no support.

NARRATOR: And it was there, in exile, that Hahn's desperate letter reached her. He and Strassmann had found barium and could not explain it. They needed their physicist. Even in exile, even cut off, she was still the one who could see what the chemistry meant.

NARRATOR: The timing was almost cinematic. Meitner was spending Christmas in the small town of Kungälv, on the Swedish coast north of Gothenburg, staying with a friend. Her nephew, the young physicist Otto Frisch, came to visit her for the holidays. Frisch was himself a refugee, working with Bohr in Copenhagen. Meitner showed him the letter. Frisch was skeptical. He wanted to go skiing. But Meitner insisted they think it through, and so they went for a walk in the snow, Meitner on foot, Frisch on cross-country skis beside her.

NARRATOR: As they walked, they reasoned it out. Could the liquid drop model explain it? They pictured the uranium nucleus as a wobbling, unstable drop. Frisch later wrote that the uranium nucleus might indeed resemble a very wobbly, unstable drop, ready to divide itself at the slightest provocation. They realised that for a nucleus as large as uranium, the electrostatic repulsion almost exactly cancels the surface tension that holds it together. The nucleus is balanced on a knife edge. A single captured neutron is enough to tip it over, to set it wobbling until it splits in two.

NARRATOR: Then Meitner did the energy calculation. She knew the masses. She worked out that the two fragments together would weigh less than the original uranium, by about one fifth of a proton's mass. She applied Einstein's mass-energy equivalence. And out came two hundred million electron volts. They sat down on a log, and on a scrap of paper, the explanation of nuclear fission was written for the first time. Meitner and Frisch published it in the journal Nature in February nineteen thirty-nine. It was Frisch who gave the process its name, fission, after asking a biologist what they called cell division.

QUESTION: So Hahn and Strassmann did the experiment, but Meitner and Frisch explained it. Who actually discovered nuclear fission?

NARRATOR: This is one of the great injustices in the history of science, and it is worth pausing on. Hahn and Strassmann made the experimental observation. That was real and important work, world-class radiochemistry. But they did not understand what they had found. They literally could not believe it. It was Meitner and Frisch who provided the theory, who applied the liquid drop model, who calculated the energy, who predicted that neutrons would be released, and who gave the phenomenon its name. By any fair reckoning, the discovery was a shared achievement. And yet, in nineteen forty-four, the Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to Otto Hahn alone. Meitner was not included. She had been nominated for a Nobel Prize forty-nine times over her career, in physics and in chemistry, and she never received one. Many historians now regard her exclusion as one of the clearest cases of the Nobel committee getting it wrong, a mixture of disciplinary boundaries, wartime politics, and the simple fact that she was a woman and an exile.

NARRATOR: But in early nineteen thirty-nine, the immediate consequence of the discovery was not a prize. It was an explosion of activity. Niels Bohr was about to sail to America. Frisch told him the news just before he left, and Bohr reportedly struck his forehead and exclaimed, oh, what idiots we have all been. We could have seen this years ago. He carried the news across the Atlantic. When he arrived in mid-January nineteen thirty-nine and told the physicists at Princeton, the result spread faster than any scientific finding before it. Within weeks, dozens of laboratories across America had repeated the experiment and confirmed it. Over a hundred papers on fission were published that single year.

QUESTION: Why such a frenzy? It is one new reaction. Why did the whole world of physics drop everything?

NARRATOR: Because of one number that came out of the experiments. Each fission of uranium does not just release energy. It releases two or three free neutrons. In nineteen thirty-nine, the French physicist Frederic Joliot and his team confirmed it experimentally, showing that two or three fast neutrons come flying out with every fission. And the moment you know that, a terrifying and exhilarating possibility opens up. If one neutron causes a fission, and that fission releases, say, two or three neutrons, then each of those neutrons could cause another fission, each of which releases more neutrons, and so on. One becomes two, two becomes four, four becomes eight, doubling and doubling. A self-sustaining chain reaction.

NARRATOR: And here is the haunting part. One man had already imagined this, years before fission was discovered. His name was Leo Szilard, a Hungarian physicist who had fled the Nazis. In September nineteen thirty-three, Szilard was standing at a traffic light on Southampton Row in London. He had just been irritated by a newspaper report in which Ernest Rutherford, one of the greatest physicists alive, had dismissed the idea of getting useful energy from the atom as, quote, moonshine. As the light turned green and Szilard stepped off the kerb, the idea struck him fully formed. If you could find an element that, when hit by one neutron, emitted two neutrons, then you could sustain a chain reaction and liberate enormous energy. He even patented the concept in nineteen thirty-four, and assigned the patent to the British Admiralty to keep it secret, because he was already afraid of who might build it first. At the time, he did not know which element would work. Fission, discovered five years later, was the missing piece. Uranium was the element. Szilard's nightmare had a fuel.

QUESTION: So the chain reaction is the dangerous part. What is the difference between a reactor and a bomb? They both use the same chain reaction.

NARRATOR: They do use the same physics, but the difference is everything, and it comes down to a single quantity that physicists call the multiplication factor, written as k. The multiplication factor is the average number of neutrons from each fission that go on to cause another fission. Think of it as the survival rate of the chain. If k is less than one, the chain dies out. Each generation has fewer fissions than the last, and the reaction fizzles. That is called subcritical. If k equals exactly one, the chain sustains itself at a steady level. Each fission triggers exactly one more. That is called critical, and that is a power reactor running smoothly. But if k is greater than one, the reaction grows. Each generation is bigger than the last, and it grows exponentially, faster and faster. That is supercritical, and that, taken to its extreme, is a bomb.

NARRATOR: The difference between a reactor and a bomb is therefore the difference between a controlled chain reaction and an uncontrolled one. In a reactor, you carefully hold the multiplication factor at exactly one. You harvest the heat gently and steadily to boil water and spin a turbine. In a bomb, you deliberately let the multiplication factor climb far above one, so that the entire energy release happens in a fraction of a second. And to make that work, you need to understand a few more pieces of physics. Let me walk you through how you actually build a controlled reactor, because the very first one is a remarkable story in its own right.

QUESTION: What do you actually need to keep a chain reaction controlled?

NARRATOR: You need several things, and each one is solving a specific problem. First, the fuel. Natural uranium is almost entirely uranium-two-thirty-eight, which does not readily fission. Only about zero point seven two percent of natural uranium is the useful isotope, uranium-two-thirty-five. So you either enrich the uranium to increase that fraction, or you find a clever way to work with natural uranium.

NARRATOR: Second, you need a moderator. Remember, slow neutrons cause fission far more readily than fast ones. But the neutrons that come flying out of a fission are fast, carrying a lot of energy. So you need something to slow them down to thermal speeds. That something is the moderator. It is made of light atoms, like carbon in graphite, or hydrogen in water, that the neutrons bounce off, losing a bit of energy with each collision, like a billiard ball bouncing off other billiard balls until it slows down. This is one of the great counterintuitive points. Adding a moderator, which slows the neutrons down, actually speeds up the chain reaction, because slow neutrons are so much better at causing fission.

NARRATOR: Third, you need control rods. These are made of materials that are greedy for neutrons, that soak them up, like cadmium, boron, or hafnium. By sliding the control rods into the core, you absorb spare neutrons and lower the multiplication factor. By pulling them out, you let the reaction build. The control rods are the throttle, and the brake.

NARRATOR: Fourth, you need a coolant to carry the heat away, often the same water that serves as the moderator. And around it all, a reflector to bounce escaping neutrons back into the core, and thick shielding of concrete and lead to absorb the deadly gamma rays and stray neutrons. Those are the essential components of every fission reactor in the world, and they all trace back to one cold afternoon in Chicago in nineteen forty-two.

NARRATOR: The man who built that first reactor was Enrico Fermi, an Italian physicist who had himself fled fascism. His wife was Jewish, and after collecting his Nobel Prize in Stockholm in nineteen thirty-eight, Fermi simply did not return to Mussolini's Italy. He sailed to America instead. By nineteen forty-two, the United States had entered the war, and Fermi was leading a team at the University of Chicago with a single goal, to prove that a self-sustaining chain reaction was actually possible. The world had calculated that it should work. Nobody had ever made it happen.

NARRATOR: They built their reactor in a squash court underneath the abandoned west viewing stands of the university's football field, Stagg Field. They called it Chicago Pile-One, because that is literally what it was, a pile. They stacked it by hand from forty-five thousand blocks of ultra-pure graphite, the moderator, with lumps of uranium metal and uranium oxide embedded throughout. Fifty-seven layers in all. When finished, it stood about six metres high and seven and a half metres wide, weighing hundreds of tonnes. Control rods of cadmium ran through it, ready to absorb neutrons. There was no shielding, no cooling system. If it ran away, there was no real plan beyond a man standing by with an axe to cut a rope and let a safety rod drop, and another team on a platform above holding buckets of cadmium salt solution to throw on the pile.

QUESTION: That sounds terrifyingly improvised. Did it work?

NARRATOR: On the morning of the second of December nineteen forty-two, with about forty people crowded into the freezing squash court, Fermi gave the order to slowly withdraw the control rods, a few centimetres at a time. With each withdrawal, the neutron counters clicked faster. Fermi, calm as ever, checked his slide rule against the readings. Around three twenty-five in the afternoon, he ordered the last rod pulled out another foot. The counters did not level off. They climbed, and kept climbing, and the rising clatter showed that the neutron population was now doubling on its own, generation after generation. The multiplication factor had reached one. The chain reaction was self-sustaining. For the first time in human history, humanity had lit, and was controlling, a nuclear fire. Fermi let it run for about four and a half minutes, producing about half a watt of power, barely enough to light a small bulb, and then ordered the rods back in to shut it down.

NARRATOR: The power was trivial. The significance was world-changing. To report it, the physicist Arthur Compton telephoned a colleague in Washington and, over an unsecured line, spoke in code. He said, the Italian navigator has landed in the New World. The reply came back, how were the natives? Compton answered, very friendly. The age of nuclear energy had begun. And the project that contained it was already the largest secret scientific effort the world had ever seen. It was called the Manhattan Project.

NARRATOR: The Manhattan Project had been set in motion partly by a letter. Back in nineteen thirty-nine, Leo Szilard, the man who had dreamed up the chain reaction, was terrified that Nazi Germany, where fission had been discovered, would build an atomic bomb first. He drafted a warning to President Roosevelt and got the most famous scientist on Earth to sign it. On the second of August nineteen thirty-nine, Albert Einstein signed the letter urging the United States to begin nuclear weapons research. The bomb that fission made possible would, in a sense, be built because the world's exiled physicists feared Hitler would build it first.

NARRATOR: By nineteen forty-five, the Manhattan Project had grown to a hundred and thirty thousand people and cost around two billion dollars, a staggering sum then. It ran on three giant secret sites. At Oak Ridge in Tennessee, vast plants laboured to enrich uranium, to separate the useful uranium-two-thirty-five from the abundant uranium-two-thirty-eight, an immensely difficult task because the two isotopes are chemically identical and differ only slightly in mass. At Hanford in Washington state, huge reactors, the industrial descendants of Fermi's pile, bred a brand new element, plutonium, which is also fissile. And at Los Alamos, in the New Mexico desert, under the direction of Robert Oppenheimer, the physicists designed the actual weapons.

QUESTION: Why was enrichment such a big deal? If reactor uranium and bomb uranium are the same element, what is the difference?

NARRATOR: This is one of the most important points to understand, and it dispels a common fear. A power reactor and a bomb need very different fuel. Reactor fuel is typically enriched to only about three to five percent uranium-two-thirty-five. A bomb needs uranium enriched to over ninety percent. The reason connects back to the chain reaction. To make a bomb explode, you need the chain reaction to grow explosively in a millionth of a second, which requires the neutrons to find a fissile nucleus almost instantly. With only three percent enrichment, the chain simply cannot grow fast enough, the neutrons mostly get absorbed by the non-fissile uranium-two-thirty-eight before they can sustain a runaway. This is why it is physically impossible for a commercial power reactor to detonate like an atomic bomb. A reactor can suffer a meltdown, or a steam explosion, as happened at Chernobyl and Fukushima, but it cannot produce a nuclear detonation. The fuel is simply the wrong stuff in the wrong configuration.

NARRATOR: For a bomb, you also need a critical mass, the minimum amount of fissile material packed closely enough that, on average, at least one neutron from each fission survives to cause the next. Too little material, and too many neutrons escape from the surface before they hit anything, so the chain dies. For a bare sphere of uranium-two-thirty-five, the critical mass is about fifty-two kilograms. Surround it with a reflector to bounce neutrons back in, and you can drop that to around fifteen kilograms. It was Otto Frisch, the same Frisch from the snowy walk, together with Rudolf Peierls in Birmingham, who in nineteen forty calculated that only about a kilogram of pure uranium-two-thirty-five might be enough. That two-page memorandum stunned the British government, because it meant a bomb was not a distant fantasy. It was a deliverable weapon.

NARRATOR: The first test came on the sixteenth of July nineteen forty-five, in the desert of New Mexico, at a site Oppenheimer named Trinity. At twenty-nine minutes past five in the morning, a plutonium implosion device detonated with a force equivalent to about twenty-one thousand tonnes of TNT. The flash was brighter than the midday sun. Oppenheimer later said a line came into his mind from the Hindu scripture, the Bhagavad Gita, now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds.

NARRATOR: Three weeks later, it was used in war. On the sixth of August nineteen forty-five, a uranium bomb called Little Boy was dropped on Hiroshima, with a yield of around fifteen thousand tonnes of TNT. The bomb contained sixty-four kilograms of highly enriched uranium, and yet less than one kilogram of it actually underwent fission. Less than one kilogram. From that, tens of thousands of people died almost instantly. On the ninth of August, a plutonium bomb called Fat Man was dropped on Nagasaki. Within days, the war was over. The energy that Lise Meitner had calculated on a scrap of paper in the woods, the energy that she said could make a grain of sand jump, had been multiplied into the destruction of cities.

QUESTION: Did Meitner have anything to do with the bomb? People sometimes call her the mother of the bomb.

NARRATOR: She had nothing to do with it, and that label tormented her. When the Manhattan Project was being assembled, Lise Meitner was invited to join. She refused, flatly. I will have nothing to do with a bomb, she said. She stayed in Sweden throughout the war. When the news of Hiroshima reached her, she was horrified. The popular press, hungry for a human angle, dubbed her the mother of the atomic bomb, a phrase she rejected with everything she had. She had explained fission as a piece of pure physics, a beautiful and astonishing fact about nature. She had not built a weapon, and she would not. The discovery and the bomb were, to her, entirely different things, and history has too often blurred them together.

NARRATOR: So what did this discovery leave behind? On the destructive side, the world entered the nuclear age, with all the dread of the arms race that followed. But the same physics that powered the bombs also powered something else. Fermi's pile in the Chicago squash court was the direct ancestor of the nuclear power plant. The first electricity from nuclear fission was generated in Idaho in nineteen fifty-one. The first commercial plant opened in the Soviet Union in nineteen fifty-four. Today, around four hundred and forty reactors operate worldwide, supplying roughly a tenth of the world's electricity, all of them running on the controlled version of the very reaction Meitner first understood.

NARRATOR: And the science itself flowered. The liquid drop model that Meitner and Frisch used on that walk grew into a precise mathematical description of nuclear masses. The original goal of Hahn's experiments, to create elements heavier than uranium, was finally achieved, and we have now made elements all the way up to number one hundred and eighteen. As for Meitner, recognition came late and incomplete. In nineteen sixty-six, she shared the prestigious Enrico Fermi Award with Hahn and Strassmann, a partial setting of the record. She died in Cambridge, England, in nineteen sixty-eight, just short of her ninetieth birthday. Her epitaph, written by her nephew Otto Frisch, reads, a physicist who never lost her humanity. And in nineteen ninety-seven, element number one hundred and nine was named meitnerium in her honour, one of the very few elements named after a woman.

NARRATOR: There is something almost unbearably fitting about how this story began. Two chemists in Berlin who could not believe their own instruments. A regime that had driven away the one person who could explain the result. And that person, an exile, fifty-nine years old, starting over in a foreign country, sitting on a log in the snow with a scrap of paper, doing the calculation that explained how the universe stores energy in the heart of an atom. The energy was always there, waiting inside uranium, balanced on a knife edge between the strong force and the electric repulsion. It took a neutron to tip it over. And it took Lise Meitner to understand why.

NARRATOR: For your New South Wales HSC Physics course, this story is the evidence behind Module Eight, the properties of the nucleus. When the syllabus asks you to model and explain the process of nuclear fission, including controlled and uncontrolled chain reactions, this is that process, the slow neutron, the wobbling liquid drop, the splitting nucleus, the multiplication factor that separates a steady reactor from a runaway bomb. When it asks you to account for the release of energy using mass defect, binding energy, and Einstein's mass-energy equivalence, this is the calculation Meitner did in the woods, the missing mass converted into two hundred million electron volts. And when it asks you to assess the contribution of selected scientists to our understanding of the nucleus, remember Hahn and Strassmann who found the barium, Meitner and Frisch who explained it, Szilard who imagined the chain, and Fermi who lit the first nuclear fire under the stands of a football field. The physics is on the data sheet. The story is why it matters.
