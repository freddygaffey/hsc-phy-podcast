NARRATOR: Three episodes ago, in the motor effect lesson, we established the single force that everything in this module hangs off. A current-carrying conductor sitting in an external magnetic field feels a force. The size of it is the field strength B, times the current I, times the length of conductor L in the field, times the sine of the angle between the current and the field. That's the motor effect: F equals B I L sine theta. And the mechanism mattered just as much as the formula. The wire carries its own circular magnetic field, that field adds to the external field on one side and cancels it on the other, and the wire gets pushed from the strong side toward the weak side. Force is maximum when the wire sits perpendicular to the field, and zero when it lies along the field. Hold onto that, because today the same current is going to start fighting back.

NARRATOR: Two episodes ago we took that single wire and bent it into a loop, and we got torque. The two long sides of a current loop carry current in opposite directions, so the motor-effect forces on them point in opposite directions too. Equal and opposite forces, offset across the coil, that's a couple, and a couple produces a turning effect. The torque is the number of turns n, times the field B, times the current I, times the area A, times the cosine of the angle between the coil plane and the field. More turns, stronger field, more current, bigger area: every one of those cranks the torque up. And critically, the torque is maximum when the coil plane is parallel to the field, and drops to zero when the coil plane is perpendicular to the field, because at that point the forces are pulling straight out along the axis and there's nothing to twist.

NARRATOR: Then last episode we built the actual DC motor. Four parts: the rotor coil, the field magnets that supply the external field, the brushes that feed current in, and the star of the show, the split-ring commutator. The problem the commutator solves is that zero-torque position. A plain coil would swing up to where the plane is perpendicular to the field, overshoot, and then the force would start pushing it back the other way. It would just rock back and forth. The split-ring commutator fixes that by reversing the direction of the current through the coil exactly as the coil passes through that dead spot, so the force on each side always pushes the coil the same way round. Reverse the current at the right instant and the rocking becomes continuous rotation. And remember the one-line contrast for the exam: a DC motor uses a split-ring commutator, an AC motor uses slip rings instead.

QUESTION: Quick check before we move on. Why does the commutator have to be a split ring, with a gap, rather than one continuous ring?

NARRATOR: Because the gap is what does the reversing. As the coil spins, each brush slides from one half of the ring across the gap onto the other half, and at that moment the connection to the coil flips, so the current direction through the coil flips. A continuous ring would be a slip ring, the current would never reverse, and the coil would just oscillate. The split is the whole trick. Right, that's the recap. Now here's the twist that gives today its name.

NARRATOR: We've spent three episodes treating the motor coil as a thing that current flows through to make a force. But a coil spinning in a magnetic field is also something else, something we haven't named yet but which is about to dominate everything: it is a generator. The very same coil, doing the very same spin, is generating electricity at the same time as it's consuming it. And the electricity it generates fights the supply. That is back-EMF. A motor, running normally, is a machine quietly working against itself. By the end of this episode you'll be able to explain exactly why that happens, why it's actually a good thing and not a flaw, why a motor draws a huge surge of current the instant you switch it on, and why a motor that gets jammed will cook itself to death. Those last two are very common exam questions, and they're the same physics seen from two ends.

NARRATOR: Start with the core idea, stated as plainly as I can. When a DC motor runs, the supply pushes current through the armature coil, and that current in the external field produces a torque, by the motor effect, and the coil spins. Fine, that's last episode. But now the coil is rotating inside a magnetic field. As it rotates, the angle between the field and the coil keeps changing, which means the amount of magnetic field passing through the coil, the magnetic flux, keeps changing. We'll do flux properly next episode, but for now you only need this: flux is the field strength B, times the area A, times the cosine of the angle between the field and the line perpendicular to the coil. Spin the coil, that angle sweeps round, and the cosine rises and falls, so the flux through the coil rises and falls continuously.

NARRATOR: And here's the law that turns that into an EMF. Faraday's law of induction says that whenever the magnetic flux through a coil changes, an electromotive force is induced in that coil. The induced EMF equals the number of turns n, times the rate of change of flux, the change in flux divided by the change in time. We're previewing Faraday properly in two episodes' time, but the headline is all you need today: changing flux makes an EMF, and the faster the flux changes, the bigger the EMF. So our spinning motor coil, with its constantly changing flux, has an EMF induced in it. The motor coil is generating.

QUESTION: Okay, but if the coil is generating an EMF, which way does that EMF point? Does it help the supply push current, or fight it?

NARRATOR: It fights it. And the law that tells us so is Lenz's law, which is the minus sign sitting in front of Faraday's law. Lenz's law says the induced EMF always acts to oppose the change that produced it. The change that produced it here is the rotation driven by the supply. So the induced EMF must oppose that, which means it acts in the opposite direction to the supply voltage. An induced EMF that opposes the supply: that is back-EMF. You'll also see it called counter-EMF, same thing. Note carefully, it is an EMF, a voltage, measured in volts. It is not a current and it is not a force. The current and the force are downstream consequences of it. Getting that wrong is one of the classic mark-losers, and we'll come back to it.

NARRATOR: Now, why does it have to oppose? Why couldn't the induced EMF point the same way as the supply and help it along? Here's the deep answer, and it's worth marks on a "justify" question: because of conservation of energy. Imagine for a second the induced EMF aided the supply. Then it would push more current, the motor would speed up, faster spin means a bigger induced EMF, which pushes even more current, which speeds it up more, and you'd have a motor accelerating without limit, pouring out more and more energy, all from nothing. That's a perpetual motion machine, and it's forbidden. The only way to balance the energy books is for the induced EMF to oppose the supply, so that pushing the motor harder costs you. Lenz's law isn't an arbitrary rule about minus signs. It is conservation of energy wearing a disguise. We'll devote a whole episode to that idea later, but plant the seed now: back-EMF exists because energy is conserved.

NARRATOR: So that's the production of back-EMF, the first of our two syllabus dot-points, and I want you to hear the full causal chain in order, because a marker is looking for every link. One: the supply current in the field makes the coil spin, by the motor effect. Two: the spinning coil experiences a continuously changing flux. Three: by Faraday's law, that changing flux induces an EMF in the coil. Four: by Lenz's law, that induced EMF opposes the change driving it, so it opposes the supply voltage, and we call it back-EMF. Five: it has to oppose, because conservation of energy forbids the alternative. Motor effect, changing flux, Faraday, Lenz, energy conservation. If you can recite that chain, you've earned the production marks.

NARRATOR: Let me give you a memory hook for that chain, because five links in order is exactly the kind of thing that evaporates under exam pressure. Faraday makes it, Lenz flips it. Say that one to yourself: Faraday makes it, Lenz flips it.

NARRATOR: Now the second big idea, and it's the one that connects back-EMF to speed. The size of the back-EMF depends on how fast the motor is spinning. Why? Go back to Faraday: the induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of flux. A coil spinning slowly sweeps through its flux slowly, so the flux changes slowly, so the induced EMF is small. A coil spinning fast sweeps through the same flux in less time, so the flux changes rapidly, so the induced EMF is large. Back-EMF rises as the motor speeds up. It rises smoothly, monotonically, with rotational speed. And at the one special speed, zero, when the coil is not turning at all, the flux is not changing at all, so the back-EMF is exactly zero. Remember that: zero speed means zero back-EMF. That single fact is going to explain both the start-up surge and the stall burnout, so lock it in.

NARRATOR: With production sorted, let's get to the second dot-point, the effect of back-EMF on the current and the speed. This is where the one load-bearing equation of the whole episode lives, so let's build it carefully. The armature coil is just a coil of wire, and like any wire it has a fixed resistance, call it R. Now, what voltage actually drives current through that resistance? Not the full supply voltage, because the back-EMF is sitting inside the coil pushing the other way. The two oppose, so they partly cancel. The voltage left over to actually drive current, the net voltage, is the supply voltage minus the back-EMF.

NARRATOR: And then it's just Ohm's law. The current through the motor equals that net voltage divided by the resistance. In words: the current equals the supply voltage minus the back-EMF, all divided by the armature resistance. That is the equation you write down, and it is the equation that earns the marks, so let me say it once more, slowly. Current equals, supply voltage minus back-EMF, divided by resistance. The full statement is in Listing one if you want to read it alongside. Everything else today is a consequence of that one relationship, so make sure it's solid before we push on.

QUESTION: This is a pause-the-player one, because it's the heart of the topic and I want you to actually do the reasoning, not just hear it. Using that equation, work out what happens to the current as the motor speeds up from rest to full speed. Pause now, reason it through in words, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the reasoning. At rest, the back-EMF is zero, so the net voltage is the full supply voltage, so the current is at its absolute maximum. As the motor speeds up, the back-EMF grows, so the supply-minus-back-EMF term shrinks, so the net voltage falls, so the current falls. By the time the motor's at full speed, the back-EMF is large, often almost as big as the supply itself, the net voltage is tiny, and the current is small. So: the faster it spins, the smaller the current. That feels backwards to a lot of students, who assume fast spinning means lots of current. It's the opposite. High speed, high back-EMF, low current. Burn that in, because the reversed version is a guaranteed lost mark.

NARRATOR: Let's make this concrete with the three operating regimes, because a "discuss" or "explain" question wants you to name all three. I'll give you the picture, then we'll put real numbers on it. Regime one, start-up. You flick the switch. The coil is stationary, back-EMF is zero, the net voltage is the full supply, and the current surges to its maximum value. This start-up surge is real and it's big, big enough to dim the lights in a room when a large motor kicks in, big enough to blow a fuse or trip a circuit breaker if nothing's done about it. Regime two, normal running under load. The coil is spinning fast, the back-EMF is large and close to the supply voltage, the net voltage is small, and the current is small. The motor is running efficiently, drawing only the current it needs. Regime three, the ideal no-load limit. Imagine no mechanical load and no friction at all. The coil just keeps accelerating, and as it does the back-EMF keeps climbing, until the back-EMF equals the supply voltage exactly. At that point the net voltage is zero, so the current is zero, there's no force, no more acceleration, and the coil settles at a constant top speed. In reality, friction always demands a little torque, so a little current always flows, but the principle is exact: the motor speeds up until the back-EMF nearly cancels the supply.

NARRATOR: A memory hook for the three regimes. Notice that the back-EMF is zero in two of them, start-up and stall, and large in the middle one, normal running. So the current is a U-shape: huge at the start, drops to small while running, and would shoot up huge again if you stall it. Picture a valley. You start up on the high cliff on the left, current sky-high. You roll down into the comfortable valley floor, that's normal running, current low. And if the motor jams, you're flung straight back up the cliff on the right, current sky-high again. "Cliff, valley, cliff." Start-up cliff, running valley, stall cliff. Zero back-EMF on both cliffs, big back-EMF in the valley.

NARRATOR: Time for the numbers, because this is examined as a calculation constantly, and the method is where the marks are. Take the standard textbook motor. Its armature winding has a resistance of ten ohms. It's connected to a two hundred and forty volt supply. When it's running with a normal load, the back-EMF is two hundred and thirty-two volts. Two questions. What's the current the instant you start it? And what's the current when it's running normally?

NARRATOR: Part a, the moment of start-up. The coil isn't moving yet, so the back-EMF is zero. The net voltage driving current is therefore the full supply, two hundred and forty volts. NARRATOR: Part b, running normally. Now the back-EMF is two hundred and thirty-two volts, opposing the supply. NARRATOR: Now look at those two answers together, because the comparison is the actual insight. Twenty-four amps at start-up, zero point eight amps running. The start-up current is thirty times the running current. Thirty times. That is back-EMF in one number. The instant of switch-on, before the coil has built any back-EMF, the motor gulps thirty times its normal current. Which brings me to the named device the syllabus likes you to mention.

NARRATOR: The standard fix is a series starting resistor. You put an extra resistance in series with the armature just at start-up. So the back-EMF and the starting resistor share the job: the resistor covers the brief moment before back-EMF exists, and back-EMF takes over once the motor's moving. NARRATOR: Let me run a second worked example, solving the relationship the other way round, because exams love to give you the current and make you find the back-EMF. QUESTION: Let me flag the single most common trap right here. A student writes, for the running drill, current equals two hundred and forty divided by ten, twenty-four amps. What have they done wrong?

NARRATOR: They've used the full supply voltage instead of the net voltage. They forgot to subtract the back-EMF. NARRATOR: Now for the payoff, the part that turns this from a calculation into a "discuss" answer worth real marks: back-EMF makes the motor self-regulating. This is genuinely elegant, so follow it. Suppose the motor's humming along and you suddenly load it up, you press harder with the drill, the load on the saw increases. The extra load slows the motor down. Slower spin means less rapidly changing flux, which means the back-EMF falls. A smaller back-EMF means a bigger net voltage, supply minus a smaller number is a bigger number, so the current rises. And here's the beautiful part: a bigger current, through the motor effect, F equals B I L, means a bigger force, a bigger torque. So the motor automatically draws more current and produces more torque, exactly when the load demands it. You didn't adjust anything. The motor sensed the extra load through its own back-EMF and responded. That's why a power drill bites down harder when it hits a knot in the wood, drawing more current as it slows, and eases off again once it's through. Back-EMF is a built-in feedback loop. Name it as "self-regulating" in an answer, that's the word markers reward.

NARRATOR: And the dark side of that same mechanism is the stall, which is the other favourite exam scenario. Push the load too far, jam the rotor so it can't turn at all, and the motor is stalled.
