NARRATOR: Before we put numbers on radioactivity today, let's run back over the last few lessons, because every one of them feeds directly into what we're about to do.

NARRATOR: Last episode was radioactivity itself — the strong force, nuclear stability, and the three decays. The headline was the tug-of-war: long-range Coulomb repulsion between all the protons, fighting short-range strong-force attraction between all the nucleons. Remember the hook — the strong force is Velcro-covered magnets: short range, indiscriminate, won't be crushed. When repulsion wins, the nucleus is unstable and it decays. And when it decays, three things can come flying out — and remember the bouncer, the punter, and the ghost. Alpha is the bouncer: a helium-4 nucleus, charge plus two, highly ionising, stopped by paper — low penetration. Gamma is the ghost: a high-energy photon, no charge, weakly ionising, walks straight through metres of concrete — high penetration. Beta is the punter in between, a fast electron with charge minus one. Ionising power runs down that list, penetration runs up it. Hold all three properties in your head, because today they decide whether a radioisotope is fit for a job in a hospital.

NARRATOR: Before that, in the Standard Model, we built the catalogue of fundamental particles — leptons and quarks — and met the four forces and their carrier bosons. The one that matters for us today is the weak force, carried by the W boson, because the weak force is what actually drives beta decay: it flips a quark's flavour, a down to an up, turning a neutron into a proton and spitting out a beta particle and a neutrino. Today we ask: how fast does all that flipping happen? What's the rate?

NARRATOR: And reach back a touch further, to matter waves and the Bohr model, because there's a thread there. Gamma decay, remember, is the nuclear twin of the Bohr model — an excited nucleus dropping to a lower energy state and shedding a photon, exactly like an excited electron dropping a level. That picture of "excited, then drops, then emits" is worth keeping warm, because the radioisotopes we'll discuss today are valued precisely for emitting gamma rays you can detect from outside the body.

NARRATOR: So here's the join. Last episode told you which nuclei are unstable and what comes out when they decay. It deliberately left one enormous question hanging: when? You cannot predict when any single nucleus will let go. Uranium has been sitting in the ground for billions of years, mostly intact. A nucleus made in a lab might be gone in a thousandth of a second. So how do we ever get a useful number out of a process that's fundamentally random? That's today — half-life, and the mathematics of decay. This is the episode where radioactivity becomes quantitative.

NARRATOR: By the end you'll be able to do five exam things cleanly. One: define half-life precisely, the way a marker wants it, and read it off a decay graph. Two: explain why decay is exponential — the conceptual root that a lot of students can use the formula for but can't actually explain. Three: relate the decay constant to the half-life, and know which is on your data sheet. Four: solve the three flavours of decay problem — how much is left, how long until a target, and finding the decay constant from a drop in activity — with the exact working that earns marks. And five: justify why a particular radioisotope is suitable for medicine or industry, which is a guaranteed "evaluate" or "explain" question. Let's go.

NARRATOR: Start with the single most important idea, and it's a paradox. Radioactive decay is spontaneous and random. Spontaneous means it happens all on its own, with no trigger — and crucially, the rate is completely unaffected by external conditions. You cannot speed it up or slow it down by heating it, cooling it, squeezing it, or putting the atom into a different chemical compound. That's a favourite trap, so lock it in now: temperature, pressure, and chemistry have zero effect on radioactive decay. The half-life of an isotope is a fixed property of the nucleus itself.

NARRATOR: Random means it's impossible to predict when any individual nucleus will decay. Pick one unstable nucleus and stare at it; it might go in the next second or in the next million years, and nothing about the nucleus tells you which. It's genuinely a matter of probability — like a single radioactive atom is a loaded coin that flips itself at random moments, and one of those flips, eventually, is "decay."

NARRATOR: So how on earth do we get predictability out of that? The answer is the law of large numbers. One coin is unpredictable; ten thousand coins flipped together give you a very reliable count of heads. In a real sample you're not dealing with one nucleus, you're dealing with something like ten to the twenty-three of them. Individually random, collectively rock-solid. The statistics of a huge number of random events are extremely predictable — and that's what lets us write down an equation.

NARRATOR: Here's the key physical statement, and it's the conceptual root of everything: the rate of decay is directly proportional to the number of undecayed nuclei present. In words — the more undecayed nuclei you have, the more decays happen per second; and as the sample dwindles, the number of decays per second dwindles in exact proportion. Twice as many nuclei, twice the decays per second. Half as many nuclei, half the decays per second. The decay rate tracks the population.

QUESTION: Why does "rate proportional to amount" force the decay to be exponential, specifically? Pause the player, see if you can reason it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the chain. If the rate of decay is always proportional to how many nuclei are left, then as the nuclei get used up, the rate drops; but a lower rate means the nuclei get used up more slowly; which means the rate drops more slowly too. The decay keeps decelerating, and it decelerates in proportion to what remains. That exact condition — a quantity whose rate of decrease is proportional to the quantity itself — is the definition of exponential decay. It's the same maths you met in Module 3 with exponential relationships, and it's the same shape as a charging or discharging capacitor, or radioactive cooling. So the number of undecayed nuclei remaining equals the initial number, multiplied by the exponential function e, raised to the power of minus the decay constant times the time. That's the decay law. The full statement is in Listing 1.

NARRATOR: Let me name every piece, because the marks live in the symbols. The number remaining at time t — call it N — equals N-nought, the initial number you started with, times e to the power of minus lambda t. The letter e is the base of natural logarithms, about two point seven one eight. Lambda — that's the Greek letter lambda — is the decay constant. And t is the elapsed time. The decay constant lambda is the probability, per nucleus, per unit time, that a given nucleus decays. It's a fixed, characteristic property of each isotope — caesium has its lambda, iodine has its lambda, and they're different numbers. A big lambda means each nucleus is very likely to decay soon, so the sample empties fast. A small lambda means each nucleus is reluctant, so the sample lasts a long time.

NARRATOR: Now, that exponential is the deep truth, but the syllabus leads with a friendlier handle on it: half-life. Half-life, written t with a subscript one-half, is defined as the time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. Say it precisely in an exam — "the time for half the radioactive nuclei to decay," or equivalently "the time for the activity to fall to half its value." Not "the time for the sample to disappear." Not "the time for the half-life to halve." Half the nuclei. That precise definition is itself a mark.

NARRATOR: And here's why half-life is such a beautiful quantity: it's constant for a given isotope, no matter how much you've got. Start with a kilogram or a microgram, the half-life is the same number. So the decay marches in clean halvings. After one half-life, one half of the original remains. After two half-lives, a half of a half — a quarter. After three, an eighth. After four, a sixteenth. After n half-lives, the fraction remaining is one-half raised to the power n. It's a staircase that halves at every step and never quite reaches zero.

NARRATOR: That "never quite reaches zero" point kills a classic misconception, so flag it. After two half-lives, a quarter remains — not nothing. Students write "after two half-lives the sample is gone." Wrong. A quarter is still there. After three, an eighth. The amount keeps halving; it doesn't vanish.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the canonical graph in words, because reading a decay curve is examinable. Picture sodium-24, which has a half-life of fifteen hours. You start with a hundred grams. The curve starts high on the left and sweeps downward, steep at first, then flattening — that's the exponential shape. To read the half-life off it, you find where the amount has dropped to fifty grams — half of the original hundred — and read down to the time axis. For sodium-24 that's at fifteen hours. Here's the trap the examiners love: the half-life is the time to go from a hundred down to fifty, which is fifteen hours. It is not the time to reach twenty-five grams. Reaching twenty-five grams takes two half-lives, thirty hours. To find half-life from a graph, always halve the starting amount and read across, then down — don't accidentally use a quarter.

NARRATOR: Now the link between half-life and the decay constant, and this one is on your data sheet, so you don't memorise it — you just need to know how to use it. The decay constant lambda equals the natural logarithm of two, divided by the half-life. The natural log of two is about zero point six nine three. So lambda is roughly zero point six nine three divided by the half-life. Rearrange it the other way and the half-life is zero point six nine three divided by lambda. The relationship is in Listing 2.

NARRATOR: Read what that equation is telling you physically: lambda and the half-life are inversely related. A large decay constant — nuclei very likely to decay — gives a short half-life. A small decay constant gives a long half-life. They're two ways of saying the same thing about how fast an isotope decays. Uranium-238 has a half-life of about four and a half billion years, so its decay constant is fantastically tiny. Polonium-218 has a half-life of about a ten-thousandth of a second, so its decay constant is enormous. Same physics, opposite ends of the scale.

NARRATOR: One more quantity before we calculate: activity. The activity of a source is the number of decays per second — the rate at which nuclei are letting go. Its unit is the becquerel: one becquerel is one decay per second. You'll see megabecquerels, millions of decays per second, and gigabecquerels, billions, in medical contexts. And because activity is just lambda times the number of nuclei present, and the number of nuclei decays exponentially, the activity decays exponentially too, with the very same half-life. So the activity also halves every half-life. The becquerel is named, by the way, after Henri Becquerel — hold that name, we'll come back to him.

QUESTION: This is the single most important distinction in the whole topic, so let's voice it. What's the difference between the amount remaining and the activity, and why does it matter in an exam?

NARRATOR: They are not the same quantity, and mixing them up is the most common error on this topic. The amount remaining — N — is how many undecayed nuclei, or how many grams, or how many milligrams, are still there. The activity — often written R — is how many decays are happening per second, measured in becquerels. They follow the same exponential law and they share the same half-life, so they halve together. But they answer different questions. If a question asks "how much remains," it wants the amount, N. If it asks "what is the activity now," it wants R, in becquerels. Don't ever report a mass in milligrams when they asked for an activity, or quote a becquerel figure when they wanted the amount left. Read the verb, give the matching quantity. That habit alone saves marks every year.

NARRATOR: Right — let's calculate, because this topic is examined as a calculation and the method is where the marks sit. There are three standard problem types, and I want you to recognise them on sight.

NARRATOR: Type one: how much remains after a given time. Let me work the classic iodine-123 tracer problem, and I'll narrate every line; the full working is in Listing 3. Iodine-123 is used as a medical tracer and has a half-life of thirteen hours. We start with a twenty-milligram sample. How much is left after twenty-four hours?

NARRATOR: Step one — and this is the step students skip and lose marks for — you must find the decay constant first. You cannot substitute the half-life straight into the decay law; the formula wants lambda, not the half-life. So, lambda equals the natural log of two over the half-life: zero point six nine three divided by thirteen hours, which is zero point zero five three three per hour. Write the unit as "per hour," because the half-life was in hours — we'll come back to that unit point, it's a trap of its own.

NARRATOR: Step two — substitute into the decay law. The amount remaining equals the initial amount times e to the power of minus lambda t. That's twenty milligrams times e to the power of minus zero point zero five three three times twenty-four. The exponent is minus one point two seven nine. And e to the minus one point two seven nine is about zero point two seven eight. So we have twenty times zero point two seven eight, which is five point five seven milligrams. After twenty-four hours, about five point six milligrams of the iodine-123 remains.

NARRATOR: And here's a beautiful way to check it that also teaches the concept. Twenty-four hours, divided by the thirteen-hour half-life, is one point eight five half-lives. So the fraction remaining should be one-half raised to the power one point eight five — and that's about zero point two seven eight. Times twenty milligrams is five point five seven. Same answer. The exponential law and the "halving" picture are the same thing; the halving shortcut just uses one-half to the power of the number of half-lives. Use the exponential law for any time; use the clean halving only when you've got a whole, or easy, number of half-lives.

NARRATOR: Let me show that clean-halving shortcut on its own, because no-calculator versions come up. Same iodine-123, twenty milligrams, thirteen-hour half-life. How much after twenty-six hours? Twenty-six is exactly two half-lives. So twenty halves to ten, halves again to five. Five milligrams, no calculator needed. Or take a source with an activity of eight megabecquerels and a half-life of ten minutes — how long to fall to one megabecquerel? Count the halvings: eight to four is one, four to two is two, two to one is three. Three halvings, each ten minutes, so thirty minutes. When the numbers are clean, count halvings; it's faster and you can't slip on the exponential.

NARRATOR: Type two: find the decay constant and half-life from a measured drop in activity. This is the one where a notorious unit trap lives, so pay attention. The activity of a source is six hundred counts per minute initially, and it drops to a hundred counts per minute after six hours. Find the decay constant and the half-life. Full working in Listing 4.

NARRATOR: Activity follows the same exponential, so a hundred equals six hundred times e to the minus lambda times six. Divide both sides by six hundred: one over six equals e to the minus six lambda. Take the natural log of both sides — the natural log of one-sixth is about minus one point seven nine two. So minus one point seven nine two equals minus six lambda, which gives lambda equals one point seven nine two over six, about zero point two nine nine.

NARRATOR: Now — the trap. What is the unit of that lambda? The time was measured in hours, so lambda comes out in per hour. Zero point three zero per hour. Not per second. This is exactly where a well-known textbook slips up and labels it "per second," and it is wrong — if you blindly call it per second you're out by a factor of three thousand six hundred. The rule is iron: lambda and the time t must always carry the same time unit. If t is in hours, lambda is per hour. If you want it in proper SI per second, you convert — zero point two nine nine per hour is about eight point three times ten to the minus five per second. Either keep everything in hours or convert everything to seconds, but never mix. Then the half-life is zero point six nine three over lambda: zero point six nine three over zero point two nine nine per hour, which is about two point three two hours. So the half-life is two point three two hours. Notice we never needed the units to agree with anyone else — we just needed lambda and t to agree with each other.

NARRATOR: Type three is really type one in reverse: how long until the sample reaches a given level. You set the amount or activity to the target, divide by the initial, take the natural log, and solve for t. The algebra is the mirror image of what we just did. The method's in Listing 4 alongside the activity problem. The one thing to carry across all three types: find lambda first, keep your time units consistent, and decide up front whether the question wants amount or activity.

QUESTION: Let's lock the method in with retrieval. Pause the player. A sample has a half-life of five days. Without a calculator, what fraction remains after fifteen days, and what's the first thing you'd compute if instead they gave you an awkward time like eleven days? Work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Fifteen days is exactly three half-lives — fifteen over five. So you halve three times: a half, a quarter, an eighth. One-eighth remains. Clean number, count the halvings. But for an awkward time like eleven days, eleven over five isn't a whole number, so you can't just halve. The first thing you compute is the decay constant — the natural log of two over five days — and then you substitute into the decay law, the initial amount times e to the minus lambda times eleven. NARRATOR: Now to the other half of this episode, and it's the part most likely to come up as an extended response: what makes a radioisotope suitable for a particular job. This is the dot-point about properties of radioisotopes determining their suitability for medicine and industry, and it's a classic "evaluate" or "justify" question. The marking key is one phrase: match the property to the purpose. Everything we've built today — half-life, and the radiation type from last episode — is what you match.

NARRATOR: Take the headline case: a diagnostic medical tracer. You inject a radioisotope, it travels to a target organ, and a detector outside the body builds an image of where it concentrates. What properties does that job demand? Two of them, and a strong answer nails both.

NARRATOR: First, the half-life has to be a careful trade-off — and this is the bit weak answers get half-right. The half-life must be long enough that you can prepare the isotope, inject it, let it travel to the target organ, and complete the scan before it's all decayed away. But it must also be short enough that, once the scan is done, it decays away quickly and clears the patient's body — so the patient's total radiation dose is kept as low as possible and the isotope isn't sitting inside them causing harm for weeks. Long enough to image, short enough to clear. For a diagnostic tracer that sweet spot is typically minutes to a few days. Technetium-99m, with a six-hour half-life, is the most common medical tracer in the world precisely because six hours threads that needle perfectly.

NARRATOR: Second, the type of radiation has to be right, and here's where last episode pays off. A diagnostic tracer should be a gamma emitter. Why gamma? Two reasons, and you state both. Gamma radiation is highly penetrating — remember the ghost, walks straight through matter — so it escapes the body and reaches the external detector, the gamma camera. You can't image with radiation that never gets out. And gamma is only weakly ionising, so as it passes through the body it does minimal damage to healthy tissue. Penetrating so it can be detected, weakly ionising so it's relatively safe. That's the gamma case in one breath.

NARRATOR: Now the contrast that earns top marks — say explicitly why alpha is wrong for this job. An alpha emitter would be useless and harmful as a diagnostic tracer. Alpha is the bouncer: highly ionising and very low penetration. It cannot escape the body — it's absorbed within a few centimetres of tissue — so an external detector would never see it. And because it's so strongly ionising, all that energy would be dumped into the patient's tissue, doing serious internal damage. So alpha fails on both counts: undetectable from outside, and dangerous inside. The contrast between gamma and alpha here is exactly the inverse relationship between ionising power and penetration from last episode — cash it in.

NARRATOR: Industry uses the same logic, just with different settings on the dials. There you match half-life and penetration to the job rather than to a patient. QUESTION: Question one. Define half-life, and describe how you would determine the half-life of an isotope from its decay curve. Pause the player, write a full answer, then play.
