NARRATOR: Imagine firing the biggest gun in the British Navy. A fifteen-inch naval shell, longer than a man is tall, screaming out of the barrel at thousands of metres per second. Now imagine aiming that shell at a single sheet of tissue paper. You expect it to tear straight through, of course. It would barely notice the paper was there. But instead, every now and then, the shell hits the tissue paper, stops dead, and comes flying back at you.

NARRATOR: That is impossible. It violates everything your common sense knows about the world. And yet, in the spring of nineteen oh nine, in a smoke-stained laboratory in the north of England, that is almost exactly what a small team of physicists watched happen. Not with a shell and tissue paper, but with tiny particles of radiation and a sheet of gold so thin it was nearly invisible. Most of the radiation passed straight through. But one particle in every several thousand bounced back. And that single, baffling observation would tear apart everything humanity thought it knew about the atom, and rebuild it into the picture we still carry in our heads today.

NARRATOR: The man at the centre of this story put it best, decades later. He said it was the most incredible event that had ever happened to him in his life. This is the story of Rutherford's gold foil experiment.

QUESTION: So who was this man, and why was he firing radiation at gold in the first place?

NARRATOR: His name was Ernest Rutherford. He was born in eighteen seventy-one on a farm in New Zealand, the fourth of twelve children, in a place about as far from the great laboratories of Europe as you could get. He was big, loud, and impatient, with a booming voice that colleagues swore could disturb sensitive instruments three rooms away. He had a habit of singing "Onward, Christian Soldiers" badly and at full volume while he worked. He was not a subtle theorist. He was an experimenter, a man who trusted what he could measure with his own apparatus over any amount of elegant mathematics.

NARRATOR: By nineteen oh seven Rutherford had arrived at the University of Manchester to take up one of the most prestigious physics chairs in Britain, the Langworthy Professorship. Manchester at that time was an industrial city, grey and choked with coal smoke. Rutherford's laboratory was dirty with that same smoke, and much of his apparatus was improvised from whatever was at hand, including, by some accounts, cocoa tins and beaten gold leaf. But what the lab lacked in polish it made up for in talent. Passing through those rooms over the next few years were some of the brightest young minds in the history of physics, including Hans Geiger, Ernest Marsden, Henry Moseley, and later a young Dane named Niels Bohr.

NARRATOR: And here is a detail that makes Rutherford almost unique among the great scientists. In nineteen oh eight, while he was at Manchester, he won the Nobel Prize. But here is the twist. He won it in chemistry, for his earlier work on radioactivity, and he was slightly insulted by that, because he considered himself a physicist through and through. There is a famous line attributed to him, that all science is either physics or stamp collecting. And the deeper twist is this. Rutherford's single greatest discovery, the one we are about to describe, came after he had already won his Nobel Prize. His best work was still ahead of him.

QUESTION: What was the accepted picture of the atom before Rutherford? What did scientists actually believe an atom looked like?

NARRATOR: To understand why the gold foil result was so shocking, you have to understand the model it overturned. And for that we go back to Rutherford's own old teacher, J.J. Thomson, at Cambridge. Thomson was the man who, in eighteen ninety-seven, had discovered the electron. He had shown that those mysterious cathode rays glowing in vacuum tubes were actually streams of tiny, negatively charged particles, far smaller than any atom. This was the first subatomic particle ever found. It proved that the atom, long thought to be the smallest indivisible piece of matter, had pieces inside it.

NARRATOR: But that created a puzzle. Atoms, as a whole, are electrically neutral. They have no net charge. So if an atom contains these little negative electrons, there must be an equal amount of positive charge somewhere to balance them out. The question was, where? How is the positive charge arranged?

NARRATOR: Thomson's answer, proposed around nineteen oh four, has come down to us under a wonderfully memorable name, the plum pudding model. Picture a plum pudding, or if you prefer, a blueberry muffin. The positive charge of the atom is spread out smoothly through the entire volume of the atom, like the soft dough or sponge of the pudding. The tiny negative electrons are dotted throughout this positive sphere, like the plums or the blueberries scattered through the dough. The whole thing is one diffuse, gentle ball of mingled positive and negative charge, about a ten-billionth of a metre across.

NARRATOR: It is worth pausing on one small piece of history here. Thomson himself never actually called it the plum pudding model. That nickname was attached to it later by other people, because it was vivid and it stuck. Thomson's own description was the rather drier "uniform sphere" model. But whatever you call it, the key idea is the same, and it is crucial for our story. In Thomson's atom, the positive charge and the mass are smeared out thinly across the whole atom. There is nothing hard, nothing concentrated, nothing dense at the centre. The atom is soft and uniform all the way through.

QUESTION: If that was the picture, then why did Rutherford set up an experiment to fire particles through gold? What was he hoping to see?

NARRATOR: This is where two more people enter the story. The first is Hans Geiger, a young German physicist who had completed his doctorate at the University of Erlangen and joined Rutherford at Manchester in nineteen oh seven. Geiger was Rutherford's right hand in the laboratory, his chief experimental partner, and he had an almost superhuman patience for the most tedious work imaginable. He would later go on to co-invent the radiation detector that still bears his name, the Geiger counter. The second person is Ernest Marsden, who in nineteen oh nine was not even a graduate researcher. He was an undergraduate, just nineteen or twenty years old, in the final year of his physics degree.

NARRATOR: Now, the tool they were using was the alpha particle. Alpha particles are a form of radiation thrown out by certain radioactive substances. We now know that an alpha particle is a helium nucleus. It carries a positive charge of two, it has a mass of about four atomic units, and it comes flying out of its radioactive source at tremendous speed, roughly sixteen million metres per second, which is about five percent of the speed of light. The source Rutherford's team used was a highly radioactive substance, often referred to as radium C, which in modern terms is bismuth-two-fourteen, a decay product in the radium series.

NARRATOR: To see where an alpha particle landed, they used a screen coated with zinc sulphide. When a single alpha particle struck this screen, it produced a single, faint, momentary flash of light, called a scintillation. By sitting in a completely darkened room and watching the screen through a microscope, an observer could literally count individual atomic particles, one tiny flash at a time. This was brutal, eye-straining work. Geiger could do it for hours. Rutherford famously could not. He admitted that after about two minutes of counting scintillations in the dark he would lose patience and start swearing.

NARRATOR: Now, Rutherford had already noticed something interesting in earlier years. Alpha particles passing through thin sheets of matter were sometimes scattered, deflected a little from their straight path, by a degree or two. As early as nineteen oh six he had written that the atom must be the seat of very intense electrical forces to push these fast, heavy particles around even slightly. So when Geiger was studying this scattering at Manchester, the team was already poking at the edges of the question.

NARRATOR: And then came the moment that started it all. Rutherford, almost on a whim, turned to young Marsden and gave him a task that nearly everyone, including Rutherford himself, expected to be a complete waste of time. According to those present, he said something close to, see if you can get any effect from alpha particles directly reflected from a metal surface. In other words, do not just look at the particles that pass through the foil and come out the other side slightly bent. Look behind the foil, on the same side as the source, and see if any particles bounce backwards.

QUESTION: And why did everyone expect that to be a waste of time?

NARRATOR: Because of the plum pudding model. Remember what Thomson's atom is. A soft, diffuse cloud of positive charge with tiny electrons sprinkled in it. Now think about what happens when a heavy, fast alpha particle ploughs into that. The electrons are far too light to matter. An alpha particle is around seven thousand times more massive than an electron. Firing an alpha particle at an electron is like rolling a bowling ball through a cloud of dust. The dust does nothing. And the smeared-out positive charge of the plum pudding is so thin and so spread out that it can only give the alpha particle the tiniest of nudges as it passes through. You might get a deflection of a fraction of a degree from a single atom.

NARRATOR: To get a big deflection, say ninety degrees, you would need the alpha particle to receive a huge number of these tiny nudges, all of them happening to push in the same direction, one after another, like a long improbable streak of luck at a casino. The mathematics said the chance of that was so small as to be effectively zero. To turn an alpha particle completely around and send it back the way it came was, under the plum pudding model, simply not going to happen. So Rutherford expected nothing. He gave the task to an undergraduate precisely because he thought it was the kind of safe, negative result that would keep a student busy without risking anything important.

NARRATOR: The apparatus they built was elegant in its simplicity. A radioactive source of alpha particles, firing a narrow beam. A thin metal foil for the beam to hit. And a zinc sulphide screen with a microscope, which could be moved around to different angles to catch flashes wherever the particles ended up, even at angles behind the foil, back towards the source. And the foil itself was gold. Why gold? Because gold is the most malleable metal known. It can be beaten into a sheet thinner than any other metal, down to about a hundred nanometres, which is roughly a thousand atoms thick, thinner than a wavelength of visible light, far thinner than a human hair. A thin foil mattered, because it meant each alpha particle would pass through as few atoms as possible, making any single dramatic encounter stand out clearly instead of being buried in lots of small ones.

QUESTION: So Marsden sat down in the dark, started counting flashes behind the gold foil. What did he see?

NARRATOR: He saw flashes. Behind the foil. On the same side as the source. Alpha particles were coming back. Not many. The overwhelming majority of particles, more than ninety-nine point nine percent of them, sailed straight through the gold with barely any deflection at all, exactly as expected. But a tiny fraction did the impossible. They were deflected by more than ninety degrees, and a small number of those were knocked very nearly straight back, almost a hundred and eighty degrees, bounced right back towards where they had come from.

NARRATOR: When they counted carefully, firing alpha particles at the thin gold foil, the figure that came out was roughly one alpha particle in every eight thousand being deflected by more than ninety degrees. That one in eight thousand figure is the one you will see quoted in your textbook, and it is the number to remember. The team also tested other heavy metals, platinum among them, and the pattern held. But either way, the point is the same and it is staggering. Particles that should never, under any circumstances, have come backwards, were coming backwards.

NARRATOR: Geiger hurried to tell Rutherford. The exact words have blurred with the retelling over the decades, but the gist was, we have been able to get some of the alpha particles coming backwards. And Rutherford's reaction, in the famous account he gave many years later, captures the sheer disbelief of the moment. He said it was quite the most incredible event that had ever happened to him in his life. It was, he said, almost as incredible as if you fired a fifteen-inch shell at a piece of tissue paper, and it came back and hit you.

NARRATOR: Sit with that image for a moment, because it is exactly right. The alpha particle is the shell. It is heavy, it is fast, it carries enormous momentum. The thin gold foil is the tissue paper. And for that shell to come back, something inside the tissue paper, something tiny, has to be unbelievably hard and dense and powerful. Something that the soft, diffuse plum pudding model said could not possibly be there.

QUESTION: So at this point did Rutherford immediately announce the discovery of the nucleus and rewrite the atom? Did he see it all at once?

NARRATOR: No. And this is one of the most important and most often forgotten parts of the story. Rutherford did not understand what he was looking at right away. The backscattering was observed and the paper by Geiger and Marsden, titled "On a Diffuse Reflection of the Alpha Particles," was published in the Proceedings of the Royal Society in the middle of nineteen oh nine. But for roughly the next eighteen months, Rutherford wrestled with the result without cracking it. At first he tried to explain the bounced-back particles as the result of many small scattering events adding up, the same kind of explanation the plum pudding picture allowed, just pushed to its limit. He kept trying to make the old idea work. As late as September nineteen oh nine, giving a lecture in America, he was still attributing the effect to multiple scattering encounters. He did not yet have the answer.

NARRATOR: The breakthrough came when he stopped trying to add up many small pushes and instead asked a different question. What if it was not many small encounters, but one single, violent encounter? What if, on those rare occasions, the alpha particle came close to something so concentrated, so powerfully charged, that one single interaction was enough to fling it straight back? He sat down and did the mathematics of that single encounter through the back half of nineteen ten. By December of that year, in a letter to a chemist friend, he was deep in calculations of atomic structure for the first time. And early in nineteen eleven, Geiger remembered Rutherford coming into his room one day in the best of spirits, telling him that he now knew what the atom looked like.

QUESTION: So what did the atom look like? What was the new picture that made everything fit?

NARRATOR: The picture is the nuclear model of the atom, and it is the one you have carried in your head since school, whether you realised where it came from or not. Rutherford reasoned as follows. All of that positive charge, and very nearly all of the mass of the atom, around ninety-nine point nine percent of it, is not smeared out across the whole atom at all. Instead it is packed into an unimaginably tiny region at the very centre. He called it the nucleus. The rest of the atom, the vast volume around that central point, is essentially empty, with the light electrons somewhere out in that emptiness.

NARRATOR: Now everything fits. The reason almost every alpha particle goes straight through the gold foil is that the atom is almost entirely empty space. The particle simply sails through the void, nowhere near the tiny nucleus, and barely feels a thing. But, very rarely, an alpha particle happens to be heading almost dead-on at one of those tiny, dense, positively charged nuclei. And here is the physics. The alpha particle is positive. The nucleus is strongly positive. Like charges repel, through the electrostatic force, what we call the Coulomb force. As the alpha particle approaches that concentrated positive charge head-on, the repulsion grows enormous, because the force gets stronger and stronger the closer the two charges come. The alpha particle is pushed back, decelerated, brought to a halt, and flung back out the way it came. The shell bounces off the tissue paper, because hidden inside the tissue paper is something incredibly small and incredibly hard.

NARRATOR: The mathematics Rutherford published in nineteen eleven was beautiful. He worked out a precise formula, now called the Rutherford scattering formula, predicting exactly how many alpha particles should be deflected at each and every angle. The key feature of that formula involves a mathematical term, the cosecant of half the scattering angle, raised to the fourth power. You do not need the symbols to grasp what it means. It means that at small angles, the chance of scattering is high, which is why most particles barely bend. But the formula also predicts a small, definite, non-zero chance of scattering at very large angles, all the way back towards a hundred and eighty degrees. The full formula is set out in the supplementary materials, in Listing one.

NARRATOR: And crucially, this was not just hand-waving. Rutherford's formula made specific, testable predictions. It said the amount of scattering should depend on the thickness of the foil in a particular way, on the charge of the nucleus squared, and on the speed of the alpha particles to the fourth power. Between nineteen twelve and nineteen thirteen, Geiger and Marsden went back into the dark room and tested every one of these predictions, counting tens of thousands of scintillations, and the data matched Rutherford's nuclear model with remarkable precision. The case was made not by the dramatic bounce alone, but by the cold agreement of theory and counted flashes.

QUESTION: Just how small is this nucleus compared to the atom? How empty is empty space, really?

NARRATOR: This is where the numbers stop being abstract and start to genuinely bend your mind. From his scattering data, Rutherford could put an upper limit on the size of the nucleus. He found the nucleus had to be smaller than about ten femtometres, that is ten times ten to the minus fifteen metres, with the head-on distance of closest approach for the gold case, the point where a fast alpha is brought to a halt and flung back, working out to around thirty femtometres. The atom as a whole, by contrast, is about a tenth of a nanometre across, which is roughly one and a half times ten to the minus ten metres. Compare those two and the nucleus comes out at least ten thousand times smaller than the atom that contains it.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the analogy that physicists reach for. If you blew up an atom to the size of a giant sports stadium, an entire football stadium with seating for tens of thousands of people, the nucleus at its centre would be the size of a marble sitting on the centre spot. Everything else, all those tiers of seats, all that vast bowl of air, is empty. The electrons would be like a few grains of dust flitting somewhere up in the highest seats. That is what you are made of. That is what this desk, this chair, this rock-solid floor is made of. The reason the world feels solid at all is not that atoms are full. It is that the electric fields between those nearly-empty atoms refuse to let them pass through one another. Reckon up the volume, and an atom is about ninety-nine point nine nine nine nine and on and on percent empty space. You are, almost entirely, nothing at all.

NARRATOR: Rutherford also managed to estimate the charge of the gold nucleus from his data, and he came up with a value of about one hundred, in units of the fundamental charge. The true value for gold, we now know, is seventy-nine. He was in the right ballpark with the data he had, and the exact answer would come a couple of years later from another member of his Manchester circle. But the great qualitative leap, that the atom is mostly empty with a tiny dense charged core, that was correct, and it was revolutionary.

QUESTION: So in nineteen eleven Rutherford publishes the nuclear atom and the world celebrates. Is that what happened?

NARRATOR: Not quite. The reception was surprisingly quiet. Rutherford's nineteen eleven paper was not greeted as the earthquake we now know it to be. And there was a very good reason for the hesitation, a serious problem buried right inside the new model. If the electrons are out in the empty space around the nucleus, what are they doing? They cannot just be sitting still, because the positive nucleus would pull them straight in. So perhaps they orbit, like planets around the sun, which is why Rutherford's model is sometimes called the planetary model. But here classical physics raised its hand with a fatal objection.

NARRATOR: According to the laws of electromagnetism, worked out by James Clerk Maxwell in the previous century, any electric charge that accelerates must radiate energy as electromagnetic waves. An electron orbiting a nucleus is constantly accelerating, because it is constantly changing direction. So it must constantly radiate energy. And as it loses energy, its orbit must shrink. The electron should spiral inward and crash into the nucleus, and the calculations said it would happen almost instantly, in a tiny fraction of a second. By the rules of classical physics, the Rutherford atom should collapse the instant it formed. Every atom in the universe should already have imploded. But they had not. Matter is stable. So the nuclear model, for all its triumph in explaining the gold foil results, was, by the physics of the day, impossible.

QUESTION: So how was that paradox resolved? How does the nuclear atom survive?

NARRATOR: The answer came from that young Dane I mentioned earlier, Niels Bohr, who visited Rutherford's Manchester laboratory and took the nuclear model as his starting point. In nineteen thirteen, Bohr made a bold and radical move. He grafted the brand new ideas of quantum theory onto Rutherford's nucleus. Bohr proposed that electrons cannot orbit at just any distance. Instead they are allowed only certain specific orbits, specific energy levels, and astonishingly, while an electron sits in one of these allowed levels, it does not radiate at all. It does not spiral in. It is stable. An electron only emits or absorbs energy when it jumps from one allowed level to another, and when it does, the energy of the light it gives off is exactly equal to the difference between the two levels.

NARRATOR: This single quantum rule rescued the nuclear atom and did far more besides. It explained, with stunning precision, the specific colours of light that hydrogen gas emits, the so-called spectral lines that had baffled scientists for decades. Each line was an electron jumping between two specific energy levels, emitting a photon whose energy, and therefore whose colour, was fixed by the gap. The energy of that photon equals Planck's constant times the frequency of the light. Rutherford had given us the nucleus. Bohr gave us the energy levels around it. Together they had built the modern atom.

QUESTION: And from there, what did this one experiment in a smoky Manchester lab actually lead to?

NARRATOR: Almost everything. Consider the chain. Once you have a tiny dense nucleus, you can ask what is inside it. In nineteen thirteen, Henry Moseley, another of Rutherford's Manchester men, used X-rays to prove that the true identity of an element is set by the number of positive charges in its nucleus, what we call the atomic number. He nailed down that gold's nucleus has exactly seventy-nine of these positive units, correcting Rutherford's estimate, and he reordered the entire periodic table on this principle. Tragically, Moseley was killed at Gallipoli in nineteen fifteen, his brilliant career cut short at twenty-seven, one of the great losses of the First World War. That same war scattered the Manchester team. James Chadwick was interned in Germany as a prisoner of war, and Marsden, the undergraduate who had counted those first impossible flashes, moved to New Zealand, where he became a major figure in science, partly on Rutherford's own recommendation.

NARRATOR: But the work went on. Rutherford himself, using the very same technique of firing alpha particles at matter, achieved the first artificial transmutation of an element around nineteen nineteen, bombarding nitrogen and knocking out a proton to turn it into oxygen. He had, in a sense, fulfilled the ancient alchemist's dream of changing one element into another, not with magic but with physics. He predicted in nineteen twenty that the nucleus must also contain a neutral particle, a neutron, to account for its mass. And in nineteen thirty-two his student James Chadwick proved it, completing the picture of the nucleus as a bundle of protons and neutrons.

NARRATOR: From there the road runs straight to the modern world. Nuclear physics, nuclear energy, and yes, nuclear weapons all descend from the realisation that the atom has a dense, powerful core. The understanding of electron energy levels gave us all of chemistry as we now teach it, the logic of chemical bonds and reactions, and it underpins the spectroscopy we use to read the composition of distant stars, the lasers in your devices, and the semiconductors in every computer and phone on Earth. All of it traces back to a tiny fraction of alpha particles refusing to behave, in a darkened room, watched by a patient German and a teenage undergraduate.

NARRATOR: And perhaps the most beautiful thing about the whole story is its humility. Rutherford set Marsden that task expecting nothing. He thought it was a dead end, a way to keep a student busy. He was a Nobel laureate who had already done his prize-winning work, and he assumed his greatest days were behind him. Instead, by simply looking where no one expected anything to be, and then by having the honesty to spend eighteen difficult months admitting he did not yet understand his own result before he finally cracked it, he discovered the architecture of all matter. He found out that the solid world is, almost entirely, empty space, with everything that matters crushed into a speck ten thousand times too small to see.

NARRATOR: For your New South Wales Higher School Certificate Physics course, this is the heart of Module eight, From the Universe to the Atom. The gold foil experiment, properly called the Geiger-Marsden experiment, is the experimental evidence you are asked to use when you explain how observations led to the nuclear model of the atom. When the syllabus asks you to explain how the gold foil observations led to the proposal of a nuclear model, this is the answer in full. Most alpha particles passing straight through tells you the atom is mostly empty space. The rare large-angle deflections, and the even rarer backscattering, tell you that the positive charge and the mass must be concentrated in a tiny, dense central nucleus, because only a concentrated charge could repel a fast, heavy alpha particle so violently. The plum pudding model could not explain those bounced-back particles. The nuclear model could. That is the chain of reasoning, from a flash of light in a dark Manchester room to the structure of every atom in your body.
