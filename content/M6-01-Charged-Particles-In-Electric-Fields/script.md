NARRATOR: Welcome to Module Six, Electromagnetism. This is the first teaching episode of the module, so there's nothing earlier in this sequence to recap. Instead, let me spend a couple of minutes pulling forward the three things from Year Twelve that this whole module is going to lean on, because if those are sharp in your head, today's lesson lands almost for free.

NARRATOR: The first is projectile motion, from Advanced Mechanics, Module Five. The single most important idea there was independence of components. A ball thrown off a cliff has a horizontal motion and a vertical motion, and they don't talk to each other. Horizontally, there's no force, so the horizontal velocity is constant — it just sails along. Vertically, gravity gives a constant downward acceleration, so the vertical velocity grows steadily. You analyse the two directions completely separately, with the same time linking them, and when you put them back together, the path is a parabola. Hold onto that word, parabola, and hold onto that method of splitting motion into an unaccelerated direction and an accelerated direction. We are going to use it almost unchanged today.

NARRATOR: The second is uniform circular motion and centripetal force, also from Module Five. An object moving in a circle at constant speed is still accelerating, because its direction keeps changing, and that acceleration points toward the centre. We won't need circles today — a charge in an electric field does not go in a circle — but I'm flagging it now because next episode, when we put a charge into a magnetic field, the path suddenly becomes a circle, and the contrast between today's parabola and next time's circle is one of the most examined ideas in the entire module. So tuck that away. Electric field, parabola. Magnetic field, circle. We'll earn that distinction properly today and next time.

NARRATOR: The third prerequisite is the idea of a field itself, which you met back in Module Four with electric charge and again in Module Five with gravity. A field is a region of space where an object feels a force, and crucially, the field is there whether or not the object is. The gravitational field around the Earth exists even when nothing is falling through it; it's a property of the space, describing the force that would act on a mass if you put one there. Today we say exactly the same thing about the electric field — it's a property of the space between charged plates, describing the force that would act on a charge placed there. Same logic, different source.

NARRATOR: Good. That's the foundation. Now to today's home topic: what happens to a charged particle when you put it inside a uniform electric field, and — this is the part the exam actually rewards — how to describe that motion and those energy changes in proper extended-response language. Because here's the problem this episode exists to fix. Markers report, year after year, that most students can get the number right but cannot explain the mechanism. "The particle accelerates because of the electric field" earns almost nothing. By the end of today you'll be able to lay out the full cause-and-effect chain that earns the marks, and you'll know the one sign-convention trap that quietly destroys more answers on this topic than anything else.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the questions we're answering up front, because hearing them first makes the answers stick. By the end you'll be able to say, precisely, why the path is a parabola and not a straight line and not a circle. You'll be able to say what "falls through a potential difference" actually means, and why the work done by the field equals the gain in kinetic energy. And you'll be able to state, every single time, which way the force points — without ever falling into the trap of saying the particle "moves toward the positive plate." Let's build it.

NARRATOR: Start with the setup, because the words here are mark-earning words. Take two parallel metal plates, mounted facing each other a small distance apart, and connect them to a direct-current supply — a battery, say. The supply pushes electrons off one plate, leaving it positively charged, and piles them onto the other, making it negative. It keeps doing this until its voltage can't shift any more charge. Now you've got a positive plate and a negative plate facing each other across a gap, and in the gap between them there is an electric field.

NARRATOR: What does that field look like? Between parallel plates, the field lines run in straight lines from the positive plate across to the negative plate, and they're evenly spaced. That even spacing is the whole point. Evenly spaced field lines mean the field has the same strength and the same direction at every point in the gap. We call that a uniform electric field. Uniform is the key syllabus word — it means constant magnitude, constant direction, everywhere between the plates. At the very edges of the plates the lines bulge out a bit, what's called fringing, but at HSC level you ignore the edges and treat the field as perfectly uniform throughout. Say "uniform field" in your answer and you've signalled to the marker that you know the field is constant, which is what makes everything that follows work.

NARRATOR: Now, how strong is that field? The electric field strength of a uniform field equals the potential difference across the plates divided by the distance between them. In words: voltage divided by plate separation. If you have one hundred volts across plates five centimetres apart, the field strength is one hundred divided by zero point zero five metres, which is two thousand. And the unit there is volts per metre. Now here's a small thing that trips people up, so let me kill it now. The field strength can be written in two units that look different but are identical: volts per metre, or newtons per coulomb. They are the same unit. One volt per metre is exactly one newton per coulomb. Don't ever treat them as two different quantities, and don't write the field in plain volts — plain volts is the potential difference, not the field. The full field-strength calculation is in Listing One if you want to read the working.

QUESTION: Why are there two different-looking units for the same thing?

NARRATOR: Good — because the field can be thought of in two equally valid ways. Either as force per unit charge, which gives you newtons per coulomb, or as how rapidly the potential drops as you move across the gap, which gives you volts per metre. They're two descriptions of one physical thing, and the algebra connecting them — work equals charge times field times distance, and also work equals charge times voltage — forces the two units to be equal. We'll see that algebra in a moment. For now: same field, two names.

NARRATOR: Right. The field exists. Now drop a charge into it, and let's build the cause-and-effect chain link by link, because this chain is the extended response. Memorise the shape of it. Field, then force, then constant force, then constant acceleration, then path. Five links. Let me give you a hook for it: "Find Five Constant Accelerating Paths" — Field, Force, Constant force, constant Acceleration, Path. Five F-words and friends marching in order. Whenever you're asked to explain the motion, walk down those five links and you cannot leave out a step.

NARRATOR: Link one: the field exerts a force. A charge sitting in an electric field feels a force, and that force equals the charge multiplied by the field strength. Charge times field, that's the electrostatic force. And now the single most important sentence in this whole episode, the one the trap is built around. The direction of that force depends on the sign of the charge. For a positive charge, the force is in the same direction as the field — that is, toward the negative plate. For a negative charge, like an electron, the force is opposite to the field — toward the positive plate. The sign of the charge decides the direction, not the strength of the field. Let me say that a third way, because at five times speed it matters: positive charges are pushed along the field toward the negative plate; electrons are pushed against the field toward the positive plate. I've put the direction conventions and all the constants you'll need — charges, masses, the mass ratio, the units — in Listing Two as a reference table.

QUESTION: This is the trap, isn't it? Pause the player for a second. If I fire an electron and a proton into the same field, do they go the same way? Work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: They go opposite ways. The proton, being positive, is pushed in the direction of the field, toward the negative plate. The electron, being negative, is pushed the other way, toward the positive plate. Same field, opposite directions, entirely because of the sign of the charge. This is the trap the spec warns about: students write "the particle moves toward the positive plate" as if it were a universal rule. It is only true for negative charges. The fix is a discipline — every time you describe the force, state both the sign of the charge and the direction of the field in the same breath. "Because the charge is positive, the force acts in the direction of the field, toward the negative plate." That sentence is worth a mark on its own, and leaving out either half is where students bleed marks.

NARRATOR: Link two: a constant force gives a constant acceleration. This is just Newton's second law — force equals mass times acceleration — rearranged. The acceleration equals the force divided by the mass, which is the charge times the field, all divided by the mass. Because the field is uniform, the force is constant, so the acceleration is constant in both magnitude and direction. And notice what the mass does here. A light particle gets a huge acceleration; a heavy one barely moves. A proton is about one thousand eight hundred and thirty-seven times heavier than an electron. So in the same field, with the same size of charge, the electron's acceleration is about one thousand eight hundred times bigger than the proton's — and in the opposite direction, because of the opposite sign. The electron is flung; the proton lumbers. Keep that mass ratio, roughly eighteen hundred to one, in your back pocket — it explains why electron guns use electrons and not protons.

NARRATOR: Link three is where the projectile motion from the opener pays off, and it's the heart of why the path is a parabola. It depends on how you inject the particle. Suppose you fire the charge sideways into the field — its velocity at entry is perpendicular to the field lines, like a ball thrown horizontally off a cliff. Now split the motion into two independent directions, exactly as you did in Module Five. Across the field, perpendicular to the lines, there is no force component at all — so that velocity stays constant. The particle drifts across at a steady rate, forever, like the horizontal motion of the cliff ball. Along the field, parallel to the lines, there's the constant force, so there's constant acceleration — that velocity grows steadily from zero, just like the vertical motion under gravity.

NARRATOR: And when you combine constant velocity in one direction with constant acceleration in the perpendicular direction, the path that traces out is a parabola. That's not a coincidence; it's the same mathematics as a projectile, full stop. The electric field is playing exactly the role gravity plays for a thrown ball. The only differences are that the electric "gravity" can point in any direction you like — sideways, upward, whatever way the plates are oriented — and that it reverses for a negative charge. But the shape is identical: parabola. This is your first big cross-link, and the exam loves it. Projectile motion in Module Five and a charge in an electric field here are the same problem wearing different clothes.

NARRATOR: One caveat so you don't over-apply it. The path is a parabola only if the particle has a velocity component across the field. If instead you release the charge from rest, or fire it straight along the field lines, there's no perpendicular drift — it just accelerates in a straight line along the field, speeding up uniformly. Like a stone dropped straight down rather than thrown out. So: fired across the field, parabola; released from rest or fired along the field, straight-line acceleration. Both come from the same constant force; the difference is purely whether there's a sideways velocity to begin with.

QUESTION: So when do I get the straight line and when do I get the parabola? Can you say it in one line?

NARRATOR: One line: if the starting velocity is along the field, or zero, the path is a straight line; if the starting velocity has any component across the field, the path is a parabola. That's the whole rule. The field doesn't change — it's always a constant force in one direction. What changes is the direction you launched in.

NARRATOR: Now link four, the energy story, because the syllabus wants you to handle both the motion and the energy, and they're often two parts of the same question. As the field pushes the charge along the field direction, it does work on it. Work is force times distance, and here the work done by the field as a charge moves through a potential difference is simply the charge multiplied by that potential difference. Charge times voltage. That's the work done on the charge. And in the ideal case, with no other forces acting, all of that work becomes kinetic energy. So the work done equals the gain in kinetic energy, which is one-half the mass times the speed squared.

NARRATOR: That single equation — charge times voltage equals one-half mass times speed squared — is the workhorse of this topic. It says: a charge released from rest and accelerated through a voltage arrives with a kinetic energy exactly equal to its charge times that voltage. Rearrange it and you get the final speed: the square root of two times the charge times the voltage, divided by the mass. The derivation is in Listing Three. And here's the phrase the syllabus uses that you must be able to translate. To "fall through a potential difference" means to move from a region at one potential to a region at another, with the field doing work, charge times voltage, on the charge as it goes. It's the electrical version of a mass falling through a height in a gravity field and gaining kinetic energy. Same idea — a particle "falls" through the electrical landscape and speeds up. That's your second cross-link: gravitational potential energy converting to kinetic in Module Five, electric potential energy converting to kinetic here.

NARRATOR: And there's a neat consistency hidden in all this. We said work equals charge times voltage. We can also write work as force times distance, which is charge times field times the distance moved along the field. Set those two equal — charge times field times distance equals charge times voltage — cancel the charge, and you get field equals voltage divided by distance. Which is exactly the field-strength equation we started with. So the energy picture and the field picture are the same picture. That's also why the two units of field strength have to be equal. It all closes up. The algebra is laid out cleanly in Listing Three if you want to see it line by line.

NARRATOR: One careful word on the units, since they get muddled. The volt itself is a joule per coulomb — one volt is the energy, in joules, given to each coulomb of charge. So when you multiply charge in coulombs by voltage in volts, the coulombs cancel and you're left with joules, which is energy. The unit bookkeeping is telling you the same story as the physics: voltage times charge is energy.

NARRATOR: Also notice the watch-out the spec flags about distances. In the field equation, voltage over distance, the distance is the plate separation. But in the work equation, charge times field times distance, the distance is how far the charge actually travels along the field. Those two distances are the same number only when the charge crosses the entire gap. If it only goes halfway, you use the half-distance in the work equation but still the full separation in the field equation. Don't blindly reuse the same number — ask what each distance physically means.

NARRATOR: Let me now make the conceptual punchline explicit, because it's the bridge to the next two episodes and the exam adores it. The electric force on the charge, charge times field, is completely independent of the particle's velocity. It has the same size and the same direction whether the charge is sitting still or screaming along at a million metres per second. That's exactly why an electric field can speed a charge up, slow it down, do work on it, and bend its path into a parabola. Now contrast that with the magnetic force, which is next episode's home topic. The magnetic force depends on the velocity — no motion, no force — and it always points perpendicular to the velocity, which means it does no work at all and bends the path into a circle, not a parabola. I'm not teaching the magnetic case today; I'm planting the flag. Electric force: independent of velocity, does work, parabola. Magnetic force: needs velocity, does no work, circle. That contrast is the spine of episode three, the great comparison, and it's one of the most reliable extended-response questions in the whole HSC.

NARRATOR: Time to put it to work with the real worked examples, because the abstract chain only becomes marks when you can run the numbers. We'll do two. The first is the electron gun — energy and speed, in a straight line. The second is the proton fired sideways — the full parabolic trajectory.

NARRATOR: Worked example A, the electron gun. This is a genuine device and a syllabus application, so let me set the scene, because the story makes it memorable. In an electron gun — the heart of an old cathode ray television or an electron microscope — you have a hot filament, the cathode, the negative electrode. Heat it up and it literally boils electrons off its surface; that's called thermionic emission. Then a positive plate, the anode, sits in front of it, and the electric field between cathode and anode grabs those electrons and accelerates them. There's a small hole in the anode, and the electrons that shoot through it form a focused beam — a cathode ray, which is just a stream of electrons. Quick vocabulary lock: cathode is negative and is the electron source, anode is positive, and cathode rays are electrons, not ions. Students mix up cathode rays with ions every year. They are electrons.

NARRATOR: Now the numbers. An electron starts from rest and is accelerated through a voltage of one hundred volts. The electron's charge has magnitude one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, and its mass is nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms. First, the energy it gains. Work equals charge times voltage: one point six times ten to the minus nineteen, times one hundred, which is one point six times ten to the minus seventeen joules. That's the kinetic energy it arrives with.

NARRATOR: Second, the speed. That kinetic energy equals one-half mass times speed squared. So speed squared equals two times the kinetic energy divided by the mass: two times one point six times ten to the minus seventeen, divided by nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one. That works out to about three point five times ten to the thirteen, and taking the square root gives a speed of about five point nine times ten to the six metres per second. That's roughly five thousand nine hundred kilometres every second — about two percent of the speed of light. The full working, every substitution, is in Listing Four.

NARRATOR: Two things to bank from that. First, the order of magnitude is a sanity check you should carry into the exam: electrons pushed through around a hundred volts come out at a few times ten to the six metres per second — millions of metres per second. If your answer comes out at, say, ten thousand, you've slipped a power of ten somewhere. Second, at two percent of light speed, plain classical mechanics is completely fine. Don't drag in relativity here — relativistic effects only matter when you're at a serious fraction of the speed of light, like in a giant accelerator, and that's not this. Reaching for relativistic mass on a hundred-volt electron is a way to waste time and lose the thread.

NARRATOR: Worked example B, the trajectory — this is the parabola example, and it's the one that ties everything together. Two horizontal parallel plates, five centimetres apart, that's zero point zero five metres, with two point five six volts across them, and the upper plate is positive. A proton is fired horizontally into the gap, just below the top plate. The proton's mass is one point six seven times ten to the minus twenty-seven kilograms, its charge plus one point six oh times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. We ignore gravity — and I'll justify that in a moment.

NARRATOR: Step one, the field strength. Voltage over separation: two point five six divided by zero point zero five equals fifty-one point two volts per metre. The upper plate is positive, so the field points downward, from the positive top plate to the negative bottom plate. Step two, the force and acceleration. The proton is positive, so — say it with me — the force is in the direction of the field, downward, toward the lower plate. Force equals charge times field: one point six oh times ten to the minus nineteen, times fifty-one point two, which is about eight point one nine times ten to the minus eighteen newtons. Acceleration equals force over mass: divide by one point six seven times ten to the minus twenty-seven, and you get about four point nine times ten to the nine metres per second squared, directed downward. That's an enormous acceleration, but the particle is fantastically light, so it's fine.

NARRATOR: Now here's where the projectile method earns its keep. The vertical and horizontal motions are independent. Vertically, the proton starts with zero vertical velocity and accelerates downward, so the gap-crossing is uniformly accelerated motion: the vertical distance equals one-half the acceleration times the time squared. Set that distance to the full gap, zero point zero five metres, and solve for time — you get a crossing time of about four point five times ten to the minus six seconds, four point five microseconds. The vertical velocity it picks up by then is acceleration times time, about two point two times ten to the four metres per second downward. Meanwhile the horizontal velocity — and this is the bit students forget — never changes, because there is no horizontal force. It stays equal to the speed you fired it in at, the whole way across.

QUESTION: Why doesn't the horizontal velocity change? There's clearly a force on the proton.

NARRATOR: Because the force is purely vertical. The field points straight down, so the electrostatic force points straight down, so it only affects the vertical motion. There is no horizontal component of force anywhere in this problem, and with no horizontal force, by Newton's first law the horizontal velocity is unchanged. This is the most common error in trajectory questions: students see "force" and "acceleration" and apply them to both directions. Don't. The acceleration lives entirely in the direction of the field. The across-field velocity is constant. Write that sentence explicitly — "the horizontal velocity is constant because there is no force component in that direction" — and that's a guaranteed mark.

NARRATOR: Put the two components together and the proton's velocity is a constant horizontal part plus a steadily growing vertical part. That combination traces a parabola, curving down toward the lower plate, and the proton exits at an angle below the horizontal — the exact same shape, and the same kind of exit angle, you'd get from a ball thrown horizontally off a cliff with that same horizontal speed. The full step-by-step trajectory solution is in Listing Five.

NARRATOR: Now, why were we allowed to ignore gravity? Run the numbers. The electric force on this proton was about eight times ten to the minus eighteen newtons. The proton's weight — its mass times little g — is around ten to the minus twenty-six newtons. The electric force is something like a hundred million times bigger than gravity here. So gravity is utterly negligible, and ignoring it is justified, not lazy. But — and this matters for the exam — only ignore gravity when the question tells you to, or when, like here, the electric force dominates by many orders of magnitude. Don't develop a reflex of always dropping gravity; justify it.

NARRATOR: Let me also clear two sign-related confusions the spec flags, because they rattle students. First, for an electron moving through a voltage, the algebra of charge times voltage can throw up a negative sign, and people panic that the field is doing "negative work" and somehow slowing the electron. Keep the physics in front of the algebra. If the electron is moving toward the positive plate — which is the direction the force pushes it — then the field is doing positive work on it and its kinetic energy is increasing, full stop. Handle the magnitudes for the number and reason about direction separately; don't let a stray minus sign convince you the electron is slowing down when it's plainly speeding up. Second, do not, under any circumstances, import magnetic-field language here. The electric force is parallel to the field, has a fixed direction, is independent of velocity, and it does do work. The "perpendicular to velocity, does no work, goes in a circle" story belongs to magnetic fields next episode. Mixing them is a classic way to torpedo an answer.

NARRATOR: Now let's nail the extended response, because this is the actual point of the episode. The spec gives us a weak answer and a strong answer, and hearing them side by side teaches the marking boundary faster than anything. Here's a typical question: "Explain why a positive charge projected horizontally into a uniform electric field follows a parabolic path, and describe its energy changes."

NARRATOR: The weak answer — the band-four version — is: "The particle accelerates because of the electric field." That's it. Why does it earn almost nothing? No force law, no Newton's second law, no mention that the force is constant because the field is uniform, no splitting into components, no reason for the parabola, no sign convention, and nothing at all about energy. It names a cause and stops. The marker has nothing to award.

NARRATOR: Now the strong answer. Listen for the chain — Field, Force, Constant force, constant Acceleration, Path — and then the energy. "A uniform electric field between parallel charged plates exerts a constant electrostatic force on the charge, equal to the charge times the field strength. For a positive charge this force acts in the direction of the field, toward the negative plate; for a negative charge it would act opposite to the field. Because the field is uniform, this force is constant in magnitude and direction, so by Newton's second law the charge undergoes a constant acceleration along the field. The motion then resolves into two independent components: perpendicular to the field no force acts, so that velocity component stays constant, while parallel to the field the constant force produces uniform acceleration, so that velocity component increases steadily. The combination of uniform velocity in one direction and uniform acceleration in the perpendicular direction produces a parabolic trajectory — directly analogous to a projectile in a uniform gravitational field, where gravity plays the role of the electric force. As the charge is displaced along the field through a potential difference, the field does work on it equal to the charge times the potential difference, and with no other forces this work is converted entirely into kinetic energy, one-half mass times speed squared — so the charge speeds up as it falls through the potential difference, exactly as a mass gains kinetic energy falling through a gravitational field."

NARRATOR: Hear the difference? Every link is named: the force law, the reason it's constant, Newton's second law, the component split, the reason for the parabola, the gravity analogy, the work done, the energy conversion, and the sign convention stated for both charges. That answer would clean up the marks. And it's not memorised word-for-word magic; it's just walking down the five-link chain and then doing the energy. If you have the chain, you can rebuild that answer cold.

NARRATOR: Quick recap of today, phrased the way you'd write it. A uniform electric field between parallel plates has constant strength, equal to the voltage divided by the plate separation. It exerts a constant force, charge times field, on any charge in it — in the direction of the field for a positive charge, opposite for a negative charge, and always state both the sign and the field direction. Constant force gives constant acceleration by Newton's second law. Fire the charge across the field and you get a parabola, by exactly the projectile-motion logic from Module Five; release it from rest or fire it along the field and you get straight-line acceleration. The field does work on the charge equal to charge times voltage, and that work becomes kinetic energy, one-half mass times speed squared — that's what "falling through a potential difference" means. And the deep idea: the electric force is independent of velocity, which is precisely why it can do work and make a parabola — unlike the magnetic force coming next time. Remember the chain with "Find Five Constant Accelerating Paths": Field, Force, Constant force, Acceleration, Path.

NARRATOR: Let's finish with exam-style questions in real NESA format. Five of them, easy to hard. Pause after each, attempt it properly, then play for the model answer.

QUESTION: Question one. Outline the direction of the electrostatic force on an electron and on a proton placed in the same uniform electric field. Pause now, write it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The electron, being negatively charged, experiences a force opposite to the direction of the field, toward the positive plate. The proton, being positively charged, experiences a force in the direction of the field, toward the negative plate. The two forces are in opposite directions because the charges have opposite signs. Notice I named the sign of each charge and the field direction in the same sentence — that's the whole mark, and it's the trap-proof habit.

QUESTION: Question two. A uniform electric field is produced by two parallel plates two hundred volts apart and separated by four centimetres. Calculate the field strength and state its units. Pause, calculate, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Field strength equals voltage divided by separation: two hundred volts divided by zero point zero four metres equals five thousand volts per metre, equivalently five thousand newtons per coulomb. The mark-earners are converting four centimetres to zero point zero four metres before dividing, and stating the unit correctly — volts per metre or newtons per coulomb, not plain volts.

QUESTION: Question three. An electron is accelerated from rest through a potential difference of two hundred volts. Calculate its final speed. Take the electron mass as nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms. Pause, work it through, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The work done equals charge times voltage: one point six times ten to the minus nineteen times two hundred, giving three point two times ten to the minus seventeen joules. This equals the kinetic energy, one-half mass times speed squared, so speed squared equals two times that energy divided by the mass: two times three point two times ten to the minus seventeen, divided by nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one, which is about seven point oh times ten to the thirteen. The square root is about eight point four times ten to the six metres per second. Sanity check: a few times ten to the six metres per second, exactly the order of magnitude we expect for an electron through a couple of hundred volts. Full working method mirrors Listing Four.

QUESTION: Question four. Explain why a charged particle released from rest in a uniform electric field travels in a straight line, whereas the same particle projected at right angles to the field follows a parabolic path. Pause, build your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. In both cases the uniform field exerts a constant force, charge times field, producing a constant acceleration in the direction of the field. When the particle is released from rest, its entire motion is along the field, so it simply accelerates uniformly in a straight line. When the particle is instead projected at right angles to the field, its motion has two independent components: perpendicular to the field there is no force, so that velocity component is constant, while parallel to the field the constant force produces uniform acceleration. The combination of constant velocity in one direction and uniform acceleration in the perpendicular direction produces a parabolic path — the same structure as projectile motion under gravity. The contrast word the marker wants is "whereas," and the key idea is that the field is the same in both cases; only the initial velocity direction differs.

QUESTION: Question five. The big one. With reference to forces, acceleration and energy, explain the motion of a positive charge projected horizontally into a uniform electric field directed downward, and account for the change in its kinetic energy. Pause, give it a real attempt — aim for five or six sentences — then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The downward uniform field exerts a constant electrostatic force on the positive charge in the direction of the field — that is, downward — equal to the charge times the field strength. Because the field is uniform, this force is constant, so by Newton's second law the charge has a constant downward acceleration equal to the force divided by its mass. The motion resolves into two independent components: horizontally there is no force, so the horizontal velocity remains constant, while vertically the constant force produces uniform downward acceleration, so the vertical velocity increases steadily from zero. The combination of constant horizontal velocity and uniformly increasing vertical velocity produces a parabolic trajectory, exactly analogous to a projectile in a gravitational field, with the electric force playing the role of gravity. As the charge is displaced downward through the potential difference, the field does work on it equal to the charge times that potential difference; with no other significant force acting — gravity being negligible for a sub-atomic particle here — this work is converted entirely into kinetic energy, so the charge's kinetic energy, one-half its mass times its speed squared, increases as it falls through the potential difference. That answer names every link in the chain and both halves of the energy story, and it's exactly the structure a five-or-six-mark extended response is marked against.

NARRATOR: That's the conceptual picture of charged particles in electric fields. Constant force, constant acceleration, parabola when fired across, straight line when released from rest, and work done equals kinetic energy gained as the charge falls through a potential difference — with the sign convention stated every single time.
