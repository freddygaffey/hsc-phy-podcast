NARRATOR: Then last episode, Charge-to-Mass, the Elementary Charge, and the Plum Pudding Model, we got quantitative. Thomson's two-stage method. Stage one, cross an electric field and a magnetic field, perpendicular to each other, and tune them until the beam passes straight through, undeflected. When it's undeflected the electric force exactly balances the magnetic force, and when you set those equal the charge cancels, leaving the speed equal to the electric field strength divided by the magnetic field strength. That's the velocity selector you already knew from Module 6. Stage two, switch the electric field off, let the magnetic force alone bend the beam into a circular arc, measure the radius, and out comes the charge-to-mass ratio, because the magnetic force is providing the centripetal force. The number, about one point seven six times ten to the eleven coulombs per kilogram, was the shock, roughly eighteen hundred times bigger than the charge-to-mass ratio of a hydrogen ion. Either the charge was enormous or the mass was tiny, and Thomson concluded the mass was tiny. The first subatomic particle.

NARRATOR: Then Millikan, with his oil-drop experiment, suspended charged oil drops between charged plates so the upward electric force balanced gravity, and found that charge only ever comes in whole-number multiples of one smallest amount, one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, the elementary charge. NARRATOR: And that brings us to the model. With the electron discovered, Thomson proposed the plum pudding model. It included the newly discovered electrons, it made the whole atom electrically neutral, and it was consistent with the atom being divisible. But it had a soft spot, an Achilles heel: there was no direct experimental evidence for the positive part at all. Nobody had ever tested how the positive charge was actually arranged. Thomson just spread it out smoothly because that was the simplest guess. Hold that thought, because today we test it, and it does not survive.

NARRATOR: Now the apparatus, and every piece of it is there for a reason. You have a radioactive source emitting a narrow beam of alpha particles. In the refined nineteen-eleven apparatus the source was polonium. What is an alpha particle? It's a helium nucleus, two protons and two neutrons, so it carries a positive charge of plus two e, that is plus two elementary charges, about plus three point two times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. It is relatively massive, around seven thousand three hundred times the mass of an electron, and it's moving fast. Positive, heavy, fast. Hold onto those three adjectives, because they do all the work in the argument. The beam is fired at an extremely thin sheet of gold foil. Gold, because it can be hammered into a foil only a few hundred atoms thick, so an alpha particle passes through just a thin curtain of atoms rather than a deep slab.

NARRATOR: The whole apparatus is evacuated, the air pumped out. That matters, and it's an easy mark to grab: if there were air in the chamber, the alpha particles would scatter off air molecules and you couldn't trust that any deflection came from the gold. And the detector is a movable scintillation screen with a microscope. The screen is coated so that every time an alpha particle strikes it, there's a tiny flash of light, a scintillation, that you can see through the microscope. And, critically, the screen rotates. You can swing it around to any angle and count the flashes at that angle. That's the whole experimental genius: you can ask "how many alpha particles end up coming off at ten degrees? at ninety degrees? at a hundred and seventy degrees, almost straight back?" QUESTION: Quick one before we go on. Why does the screen need to be movable? Why not just put a flat screen behind the foil?

NARRATOR: Because the entire discovery is in the rare large angles. A fixed screen behind the foil only catches the particles that go roughly straight through, and almost all of them do. The shocking result is the handful that come back at steep angles, and you can only count those if you can swing the detector around to the side and even back toward the source. A flat screen behind the foil would have missed the very thing that mattered. Good experimental design is the unsung hero of this story.

NARRATOR: Now, the predictions. What did everyone expect? On the plum pudding model, the atom is a soft, diffuse cloud of positive charge with tiny embedded electrons. A fast, heavy, positive alpha particle ploughing through that is like a cannonball through fog. The diffuse positive charge is spread so thin it can only nudge the alpha by a tiny amount, and the electrons are about seven thousand times lighter than the alpha, far too light to deflect it noticeably. So plum pudding makes a clean, confident prediction: every alpha particle should pass through with only a very small deflection. A slight fuzzing of the beam. Nothing dramatic. That prediction is what gets tested.

NARRATOR: And here's what they actually saw. Three observations. This is the core of the episode, so I'm going to give you a memory hook to lock the three down and keep them in order. Picture the alpha particles as tiny bullets and the gold atom as a fortress, and remember the three outcomes as "Through, Turned, Thrown back." Through: most sail straight through. Turned: some get turned through a large angle. Thrown back: a tiny few are thrown almost straight back at you. Through, Turned, Thrown back. Three T's. Now let's take each one and chain it to a conclusion, because that chaining is the marks.

NARRATOR: Observation one. The great majority of alpha particles passed straight through the foil with little or no deflection. What does that force you to conclude? That the atom is mostly empty space. Think about it. The beam is hitting a curtain of hundreds of atoms, and almost every alpha sails through as if nothing were there. The only way that happens is if each atom is overwhelmingly empty, with nothing in the alpha's path to push it off course. So observation one gives you the size and emptiness conclusion: the atom is mostly empty space, and whatever does the deflecting must be tiny, because the alpha almost always misses it.

NARRATOR: Observation two. A small fraction of the alpha particles were deflected through large angles, well off their original path. Now we reason carefully. The alpha is positive. To push a fast, positive particle hard off course, you need a strong force, and the obvious candidate is the electric force between charges. Like charges repel. So there must be a region of positive charge strong enough and concentrated enough to repel the alpha through a big angle by electrostatic repulsion. And here is the killer detail for the plum pudding model: a diffuse, spread-out positive charge can't do this. Spread the same total positive charge thinly through the whole atom and at no point is it dense enough to deliver that kick. The large deflections only make sense if the positive charge is squeezed into a small, concentrated central region. So observation two gives you the charge conclusion: the positive charge of the atom is concentrated in a tiny central region, not spread out.

NARRATOR: And let me hammer the single most common trap in this whole topic, because it's worth marks every single year. The alpha particles are repelled, not attracted. Both the alpha and the central charge are positive, and like charges repel. If you ever write that the alpha particles were "attracted to the nucleus," you've contradicted the physics and you'll lose the mark. Say it with me: positive alpha, positive nucleus, electrostatic repulsion. The closer the alpha's path comes to that central charge, the stronger the repulsion and the bigger the deflection. That's why most alphas, passing far from the centre, barely turn, and only the rare ones aimed almost dead-on get turned hard.

NARRATOR: Observation three, the famous one. A very few alpha particles, about one in eight thousand, were deflected by more than ninety degrees. Some came almost straight back the way they came. Now think about what it takes to do that. You have a fast, heavy alpha particle, and you have to stop it and reverse it. This is a momentum argument. A light object can't reverse a heavy fast one, the same way a ping-pong ball can't bounce a bowling ball backwards. To throw that alpha back, the thing it hits must be both extremely dense and carry almost all the atom's mass, concentrated in a tiny volume. So observation three gives you the mass conclusion: that concentrated central positive charge also contains almost all of the atom's mass, packed into a tiny dense core. This is where Rutherford's line comes from. He said it was as incredible as if you fired a fifteen-inch artillery shell at a sheet of tissue paper and it came back and hit you. A shell does not bounce off tissue paper, unless, hidden in that paper, there's something incredibly small, hard and massive.

NARRATOR: Now notice what I just did, because this is exactly the structure markers reward. I didn't lump the observations together. Each observation proved a different, specific property. Let me give you the clean pairing, and a hook to keep them straight. Through proves empty space and tiny size. Turned proves concentrated positive charge. Thrown back proves that charge is also massive and dense. Three observations, three distinct conclusions: emptiness, charge, mass. Through, Turned, Thrown back, mapping to empty, charged, massive. If you can reproduce that mapping in the exam, you've essentially written the answer.

QUESTION: Hang on, isn't this splitting hairs? Don't the large deflections and the rebounds basically prove the same thing, that there's a nucleus?

NARRATOR: It feels like splitting hairs, but it's exactly the distinction that separates a band-four answer from a band-six answer, so let me be precise. The large-angle deflections prove the positive charge is concentrated, the charge property. They don't, on their own, prove anything about mass, because in principle a concentrated charge could be light. It's the rebounds, the more-than-ninety-degree scattering, that prove the concentrated charge is also massive and dense, because only a massive object can reverse a massive projectile. Markers award marks per pairing. If you say "some bounced back, so there's a nucleus," you've collapsed three marks into one vague statement. Keep the observations separate and pair each to its own property. We'll see that contrast spelled out, weak answer versus strong answer, in a minute.

NARRATOR: So, the conclusion Rutherford drew. From those three observations he proposed the nuclear model of the atom, also called the planetary model or the solar-system model. Here it is. The atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at its centre. That nucleus contains essentially all of the atom's mass, around ninety-nine point nine percent of it, and all of its positive charge, packed into a minuscule volume. The electrons orbit the nucleus at relatively enormous distances, like planets orbiting the sun, which is where "planetary model" comes from. And the atom is overwhelmingly empty space. How empty? The nucleus is roughly one ten-thousandth to one hundred-thousandth of the diameter of the whole atom. To put that in your head: if the atom were the size of a sports stadium, the nucleus would be a marble at the centre circle, and the electrons would be specks of dust drifting up in the back rows. That's the scale. Almost all of the atom is nothing. The reference figures, the one-in-eight-thousand fraction, the ninety-nine point nine percent of the mass, the size ratio, are all in Listing 2.

NARRATOR: And the model explained the gold foil results exactly, which is the whole point. Most alphas pass far from the tiny nucleus and barely deflect, that's "through." The rare ones passing close to the nucleus get repelled through large angles, that's "turned." And the very rare near-direct hits get repelled almost straight back, that's "thrown back." The plum pudding model could not produce any of the large angles, so the gold foil experiment falsified it. This is one of the cleanest examples in the whole HSC of evidence killing a model. The plum pudding's untested soft spot, the unverified positive part, was exactly where the alpha particles drove the dagger in.

NARRATOR: Let me make a cross-link explicit here, because it's worth marks and it ties the module together. The physics of the deflection, a charged particle pushed off course by an electric force, is exactly the charged-particle-in-a-field physics from Module 6. The strength of that repulsion follows Coulomb's law, the electric force between two charges equals the Coulomb constant times the product of the charges divided by the separation squared. So as the alpha approaches the nucleus, the separation shrinks, the force shoots up as one over the separation squared, and the deflection grows. It's the same electrostatic force law that governed the deflection plates in the cathode-ray tube last episode; here it's between the alpha and the nucleus instead of between a charge and a plate. Same physics, new arena. Coulomb's law is in Listing 3.

NARRATOR: Now, you should also know the story behind the story, because the HSC loves the history-of-physics framing and because it makes this stick. He'd handed Marsden what he thought was a dead-end check, "just see if any of them scatter at big angles," fully expecting the answer to be none. The fact that any came back at all detonated the plum pudding model. NARRATOR: One nuance to protect you from over-claiming, and it's a real trap. The gold foil experiment established the nucleus, the concentrated positive charge, the mass, the empty space. NARRATOR: Now to the second half of the lesson, and it's the part that sets up everything that follows. Rutherford's nuclear model is gorgeous and it explained the scattering perfectly. But it has a fatal flaw, and the flaw isn't experimental, it's theoretical. The model contradicts itself when you apply the physics you already know. This is the "limitations of the nuclear model" question, and it's examined just as often as the gold foil one.

NARRATOR: Here's the chain, and it's pure cause and effect, so I'll walk it slowly. Step one. In Rutherford's model the electron orbits the nucleus in a circle. From Module 5, circular motion, you know that anything moving in a circle is constantly changing the direction of its velocity, which means it is constantly accelerating, even at steady speed. That's centripetal acceleration, directed toward the centre, equal to the speed squared divided by the orbital radius. So the orbiting electron is an accelerating charge. Lock that in: orbiting means accelerating.

NARRATOR: Step two, and this is the cross-link that does the damage, straight from Module 6, Electromagnetism. Maxwell's theory of electromagnetism tells us that an accelerating electric charge radiates electromagnetic energy. It emits electromagnetic waves. This is not some exotic rule; it's the very principle behind every radio transmitter. You make a radio wave by accelerating charges, electrons sloshing up and down an antenna. Remember the case study where Hertz verified Maxwell, case study Hertz verifies Maxwell, generating and detecting electromagnetic waves with a spark gap? That confirmed exactly this: accelerate charges, and out come electromagnetic waves carrying energy away. So Maxwell's electromagnetism, the triumph of Module 6, turns around and dooms Rutherford's atom.

NARRATOR: Step three. Put those together. The orbiting electron is an accelerating charge, so by Maxwell it must continuously radiate electromagnetic energy. But energy radiated away is energy lost. So the electron continuously loses energy. Step four. An electron losing energy can't maintain its orbit; it spirals inward, getting closer and closer to the nucleus. And the maths says it does this absurdly fast, in something like ten to the minus eleven seconds, a hundred-billionth of a second. The electron should spiral into the nucleus almost instantly, and the atom should collapse. Which gives the first contradiction, and you state it plainly for the mark: this predicts that atoms are unstable, that matter itself should collapse in a fraction of a second. But atoms are observably stable. You, the gold foil, the planet you're standing on, all stable. So the model's prediction is flatly wrong.

NARRATOR: And there's a second contradiction, just as important, and many students forget it, so it's a marks differentiator. As the electron spirals inward, it orbits faster and faster, in a tighter and tighter circle. The frequency of the electromagnetic radiation it emits depends on how fast it's orbiting, so as it spirals in, the emitted frequency would rise smoothly and continuously through every value. That means the atom should emit a continuous smear of frequencies, a continuous spectrum, a smooth rainbow with everything in it. But that is not what we see. Excited atoms emit a discrete line spectrum, only certain sharp, specific frequencies, and each element has its own unique fingerprint of lines. So the nuclear model gets the spectrum wrong too. It predicts a continuous smear; reality gives discrete lines.

NARRATOR: Let me give you a memory hook for these two failures, because they're a pair you must produce together. Call them the two S's: Stability and Spectrum. The nuclear model fails on Stability, atoms should collapse but don't, and it fails on Spectrum, atoms should emit a continuous smear but actually emit discrete lines. Stability and Spectrum, the two S's. Both failures trace back to the same root cause: the accelerating electron radiating, by Maxwell. If you remember the two S's and the Maxwell root, you have the full limitations answer.

QUESTION: Question four. "Explain why Rutherford's nuclear model, despite successfully explaining the scattering results, could not be the final model of the atom." Pause and answer.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Although the nuclear model explained the gold foil scattering, it was internally inconsistent with established physics.
