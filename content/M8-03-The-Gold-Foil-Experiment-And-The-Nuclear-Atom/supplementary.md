# Supplementary Materials

Key facts, the observation-to-conclusion structure, the supporting relations, and model extended-response answers for this episode. Nothing here is spoken in the audio — it is the read-along reference.

### Listing 1 — The Geiger–Marsden (gold foil) apparatus
```text
SOURCE      Radioactive source emitting a narrow beam of alpha particles
            (polonium in the 1911 apparatus).
ALPHA       Helium nucleus = 2 protons + 2 neutrons.
            Charge = +2e = +3.2 × 10^-19 C.
            Mass ≈ 7300 × electron mass. Fast-moving.
TARGET      Extremely thin gold foil (a few hundred atoms thick).
            Gold chosen because it is highly malleable → ultra-thin foil.
CHAMBER     Whole apparatus EVACUATED, so alphas are not scattered by air.
DETECTOR    Movable (rotatable) scintillation screen + microscope.
            Each alpha strike → a flash (scintillation) counted by angle.
            Movable → can count rare large-angle and back-scattered alphas.
PEOPLE      Hans Geiger + Ernest Marsden did the experiment (~1909).
            Ernest Rutherford supervised and interpreted; proposed the
            nuclear model in 1911.
```

### Listing 2 — Observation → conclusion chain (the exam answer), and key values
```text
"Through, Turned, Thrown back"  →  "empty, charged, massive"

OBSERVATION 1 (Through):
  Most alphas pass straight through, little/no deflection.
  → Atom is MOSTLY EMPTY SPACE; the deflector is tiny (alpha usually misses it).

OBSERVATION 2 (Turned):
  A small fraction deflected through LARGE angles.
  → Positive charge is CONCENTRATED in a small central region.
    (Like charges repel: positive alpha repelled by concentrated positive charge.
     A diffuse charge could not produce a large deflection.)

OBSERVATION 3 (Thrown back):
  ~1 in 8000 deflected by more than 90°, some almost straight back.
  → That central region is DENSE and holds almost ALL the atom's MASS.
    (Only a tiny, massive object can reverse a fast, massive alpha — momentum.)

CONCLUSION: nuclear / planetary model.
  Tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus; ~99.9% of the mass; all positive charge.
  Electrons orbit at relatively large distances. Atom mostly empty space.

KEY VALUES
  Fraction deflected >90° ............ ~1 in 8000
  Nucleus holds ...................... ~99.9% of atomic mass
  Atom : nucleus diameter ratio ...... ~10 000 to 100 000
  Atom : nucleus radius ratio (H) .... ~2.5 × 10^4
```

### Listing 3 — Supporting relations (Coulomb repulsion and centripetal acceleration)
```text
Electrostatic (Coulomb) force between two charges:
  F = k q1 q2 / r^2
    F  = force (N)
    k  = Coulomb constant = 8.99 × 10^9 N·m^2·C^-2
    q1, q2 = charges (C);  for the alpha, q = +2e = +3.2 × 10^-19 C
    r  = separation (m)
  As the alpha approaches the nucleus, r shrinks → F rises as 1/r^2
  → larger deflection. Closest approaches give the largest angles.

Centripetal acceleration of an orbiting electron (why it must radiate):
  a = v^2 / r
    a = acceleration (m·s^-2)
    v = orbital speed (m·s^-1)
    r = orbital radius (m)
  An orbiting electron is ALWAYS accelerating (direction of v changes),
  so by Maxwell it must radiate EM energy → spiral collapse.

Reference constants:
  Elementary charge  e = 1.602 × 10^-19 C
  Electron mass        = 9.109 × 10^-31 kg
  Speed of light     c = 3.0 × 10^8 m·s^-1

(No required quantitative calculation in this episode — it is conceptual /
 extended-response. These relations support the reasoning only.)
```

### Listing 4 — Model answer: "Explain how the gold foil observations led to the nuclear model" (6 marks)
```text
WEAK (band 4): "Rutherford fired alphas at gold foil. Most went through but
some bounced back, so there must be a nucleus."
  Faults: credits Rutherford with the experiment; lumps observations together;
  one vague conclusion; no observation-to-property pairing.

STRONG (full marks):
  - In the Geiger–Marsden experiment, positive alpha particles were fired at a
    thin gold foil and detected by a movable scintillation screen.
  - Most alphas passed straight through, almost undeflected
        → the atom is mostly empty space.
  - A small fraction were deflected through large angles; because alphas are
    positive, this is electrostatic repulsion (like charges repel)
        → the positive charge is concentrated in a small central region,
          NOT spread out as in the plum pudding model.
  - About 1 in 8000 were deflected by more than 90°, some almost straight back;
    reversing a fast, massive alpha needs a tiny, very dense, very massive object
        → that central region holds almost all the atom's mass.
  - Rutherford proposed the nuclear (planetary) model: a tiny, dense, positively
    charged nucleus with ~99.9% of the mass, electrons orbiting far out, atom
    mostly empty space.
  - Plum pudding (diffuse positive charge) could not produce large-angle
    scattering or rebounds, so it was rejected.

MARKING: one mark per observation→property pairing; one for naming/describing the
nuclear model; one for the explicit contrast that plum pudding fails.
```

### Listing 5 — Model answer: "Limitations of Rutherford's nuclear model" (5 marks) — the two S's
```text
ROOT CAUSE: Maxwell's electromagnetism — an accelerating charge radiates EM energy.

CHAIN (state every link):
  1. Electron orbits nucleus in a circle (nuclear model).
  2. Circular motion → centripetal acceleration → electron is an accelerating charge.
  3. By Maxwell, it continuously radiates EM energy → continuously loses energy.
  4. Losing energy, it spirals into the nucleus in ~10^-11 s.

TWO CONSEQUENCES (the two S's — both contradict observation):
  S1 STABILITY:  predicts atoms collapse almost instantly (unstable),
                 but atoms are observed to be STABLE.
  S2 SPECTRUM:   as it spirals in and speeds up, emitted frequency rises
                 continuously → a CONTINUOUS spectrum,
                 but atoms emit a DISCRETE line spectrum, unique per element.

CONCLUSION: the nuclear model is internally inconsistent; it explains neither
atomic stability nor line spectra → replaced by Bohr's stationary-state model.

TRAP: writing "the electron loses energy" WITHOUT naming the cause (accelerating
charge radiates, by Maxwell) loses the mechanism mark. Name Maxwell explicitly,
and land BOTH consequences.
```

### Listing 6 — Atomic model timeline and what each experiment did / did not establish
| Model / event | Who & when | Key idea | What it did NOT do |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plum pudding | Thomson, ~1904 | Diffuse positive sphere with embedded electrons; neutral, divisible atom | No test of the positive charge's arrangement |
| Gold foil experiment | Geiger & Marsden ~1909; Rutherford interpreted 1911 | Established the nucleus: small, dense, positive, holds ~99.9% of mass; atom mostly empty | Did NOT discover proton, neutron, or electron |
| Proton identified | Rutherford, ~1919 | Positive nuclear constituent | — |
| Neutron discovered | Chadwick, 1932 | Neutral nuclear constituent (conservation-of-momentum argument) | — |
| Bohr model (next episode) | Bohr, 1913 | Stationary states fix instability + line spectra | Works mainly for hydrogen |
