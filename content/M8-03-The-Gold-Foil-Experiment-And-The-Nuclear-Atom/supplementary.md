# Supplementary Materials

Key facts, the observation-to-conclusion structure, the supporting relations, and model extended-response answers for this episode. Nothing here is spoken in the audio — it is the read-along reference.

### Listing 1 — The Geiger–Marsden (gold foil) apparatus

- **SOURCE** — radioactive source emitting a narrow beam of alpha particles (polonium in the 1911 apparatus).
- **ALPHA** — helium nucleus = 2 protons + 2 neutrons. Charge \(= +2e = +3.2 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}\). Mass \(\approx 7300 \times\) electron mass. Fast-moving.
- **TARGET** — extremely thin gold foil (a few hundred atoms thick). Gold chosen because it is highly malleable \(\to\) ultra-thin foil.
- **CHAMBER** — whole apparatus EVACUATED, so alphas are not scattered by air.
- **DETECTOR** — movable (rotatable) scintillation screen + microscope. Each alpha strike \(\to\) a flash (scintillation) counted by angle. Movable \(\to\) can count rare large-angle and back-scattered alphas.
- **PEOPLE** — Hans Geiger + Ernest Marsden did the experiment (\(\sim\)1909). Ernest Rutherford supervised and interpreted; proposed the nuclear model in 1911.

### Listing 2 — Observation → conclusion chain (the exam answer), and key values

"Through, Turned, Thrown back" \(\to\) "empty, charged, massive"

**OBSERVATION 1 (Through):** Most alphas pass straight through, little/no deflection. \(\to\) Atom is MOSTLY EMPTY SPACE; the deflector is tiny (alpha usually misses it).

**OBSERVATION 2 (Turned):** A small fraction deflected through LARGE angles. \(\to\) Positive charge is CONCENTRATED in a small central region. (Like charges repel: positive alpha repelled by concentrated positive charge. A diffuse charge could not produce a large deflection.)

**OBSERVATION 3 (Thrown back):** \(\sim\)1 in 8000 deflected by more than \(90^\circ\), some almost straight back. \(\to\) That central region is DENSE and holds almost ALL the atom's MASS. (Only a tiny, massive object can reverse a fast, massive alpha — momentum.)

**CONCLUSION:** nuclear / planetary model. Tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus; \(\sim\)99.9% of the mass; all positive charge. Electrons orbit at relatively large distances. Atom mostly empty space.

KEY VALUES:

- Fraction deflected \(> 90^\circ\): \(\sim\)1 in 8000
- Nucleus holds: \(\sim\)99.9% of atomic mass
- Atom : nucleus diameter ratio: \(\sim\)10 000 to 100 000
- Atom : nucleus radius ratio (H): \(\sim 2.5 \times 10^{4}\)

### Listing 3 — Supporting relations (Coulomb repulsion and centripetal acceleration)

Electrostatic (Coulomb) force between two charges:

$$
F = \frac{k\, q_1 q_2}{r^{2}}
$$

- \(F\) = force (\(\mathrm{N}\))
- \(k\) = Coulomb constant \(= 8.99 \times 10^{9}\ \mathrm{N\cdot m^{2}\cdot C^{-2}}\)
- \(q_1, q_2\) = charges (\(\mathrm{C}\)); for the alpha, \(q = +2e = +3.2 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}\)
- \(r\) = separation (\(\mathrm{m}\))

As the alpha approaches the nucleus, \(r\) shrinks \(\to F\) rises as \(1/r^{2}\) \(\to\) larger deflection. Closest approaches give the largest angles.

Centripetal acceleration of an orbiting electron (why it must radiate):

$$
a = \frac{v^{2}}{r}
$$

- \(a\) = acceleration (\(\mathrm{m\cdot s^{-2}}\))
- \(v\) = orbital speed (\(\mathrm{m\cdot s^{-1}}\))
- \(r\) = orbital radius (\(\mathrm{m}\))

An orbiting electron is ALWAYS accelerating (direction of \(v\) changes), so by Maxwell it must radiate EM energy \(\to\) spiral collapse.

Reference constants:

- Elementary charge \(e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}\)
- Electron mass \(= 9.109 \times 10^{-31}\ \mathrm{kg}\)
- Speed of light \(c = 3.0 \times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{m\cdot s^{-1}}\)

(No required quantitative calculation in this episode — it is conceptual / extended-response. These relations support the reasoning only.)

### Listing 4 — Model answer: "Explain how the gold foil observations led to the nuclear model" (6 marks)

**WEAK (band 4):** "Rutherford fired alphas at gold foil. Most went through but some bounced back, so there must be a nucleus." Faults: credits Rutherford with the experiment; lumps observations together; one vague conclusion; no observation-to-property pairing.

**STRONG (full marks):**

- In the Geiger–Marsden experiment, positive alpha particles were fired at a thin gold foil and detected by a movable scintillation screen.
- Most alphas passed straight through, almost undeflected \(\to\) the atom is mostly empty space.
- A small fraction were deflected through large angles; because alphas are positive, this is electrostatic repulsion (like charges repel) \(\to\) the positive charge is concentrated in a small central region, NOT spread out as in the plum pudding model.
- About 1 in 8000 were deflected by more than \(90^\circ\), some almost straight back; reversing a fast, massive alpha needs a tiny, very dense, very massive object \(\to\) that central region holds almost all the atom's mass.
- Rutherford proposed the nuclear (planetary) model: a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus with \(\sim\)99.9% of the mass, electrons orbiting far out, atom mostly empty space.
- Plum pudding (diffuse positive charge) could not produce large-angle scattering or rebounds, so it was rejected.

MARKING: one mark per observation→property pairing; one for naming/describing the nuclear model; one for the explicit contrast that plum pudding fails.

### Listing 5 — Model answer: "Limitations of Rutherford's nuclear model" (5 marks) — the two S's

ROOT CAUSE: Maxwell's electromagnetism — an accelerating charge radiates EM energy.

CHAIN (state every link):

1. Electron orbits nucleus in a circle (nuclear model).
2. Circular motion \(\to\) centripetal acceleration \(\to\) electron is an accelerating charge.
3. By Maxwell, it continuously radiates EM energy \(\to\) continuously loses energy.
4. Losing energy, it spirals into the nucleus in \(\sim 10^{-11}\ \mathrm{s}\).

TWO CONSEQUENCES (the two S's — both contradict observation):

- **S1 STABILITY:** predicts atoms collapse almost instantly (unstable), but atoms are observed to be STABLE.
- **S2 SPECTRUM:** as it spirals in and speeds up, emitted frequency rises continuously \(\to\) a CONTINUOUS spectrum, but atoms emit a DISCRETE line spectrum, unique per element.

CONCLUSION: the nuclear model is internally inconsistent; it explains neither atomic stability nor line spectra \(\to\) replaced by Bohr's stationary-state model.

TRAP: writing "the electron loses energy" WITHOUT naming the cause (accelerating charge radiates, by Maxwell) loses the mechanism mark. Name Maxwell explicitly, and land BOTH consequences.

### Listing 6 — Atomic model timeline and what each experiment did / did not establish
| Model / event | Who & when | Key idea | What it did NOT do |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plum pudding | Thomson, ~1904 | Diffuse positive sphere with embedded electrons; neutral, divisible atom | No test of the positive charge's arrangement |
| Gold foil experiment | Geiger & Marsden ~1909; Rutherford interpreted 1911 | Established the nucleus: small, dense, positive, holds ~99.9% of mass; atom mostly empty | Did NOT discover proton, neutron, or electron |
| Proton identified | Rutherford, ~1919 | Positive nuclear constituent | — |
| Neutron discovered | Chadwick, 1932 | Neutral nuclear constituent (conservation-of-momentum argument) | — |
| Bohr model (next episode) | Bohr, 1913 | Stationary states fix instability + line spectra | Works mainly for hydrogen |
