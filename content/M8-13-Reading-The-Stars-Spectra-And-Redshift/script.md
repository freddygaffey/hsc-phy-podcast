NARRATOR: And just before that, half-life and binding energy and the Bohr model — and that last one is the real key tonight, so let me reach a little further back. The Bohr model, from earlier in this module: electrons sit only in allowed energy levels, and when an electron drops from a higher level to a lower one, the atom emits a single photon whose energy is exactly the gap between the levels — the photon energy equals the difference in the energy levels. Drop down, emit a photon; absorb a photon of exactly the right energy, jump up. Because every element has its own unique ladder of energy levels, every element emits and absorbs its own unique set of wavelengths. That uniqueness — that atomic fingerprint — is the single most important idea we're going to use tonight. Tonight we point a telescope at a star a thousand light-years away and read its temperature, what it's made of, and how fast it's moving toward us or away, all from the light alone. And the only reason any of that is possible is the Bohr model you already know.

NARRATOR: So here's the hook. You will never visit a star. You will never scoop up a sample of the Sun, or weigh a distant galaxy, or fly out and clock its speed. A star's spectrum is a message, and tonight you learn to read it. NARRATOR: First we have to get the three types of spectrum straight, because everything builds on them. There are exactly three, and the rules for which one you get were worked out by Kirchhoff and Bunsen back in eighteen fifty-nine. Here's the memory hook for all three at once: think of a candle behind a curtain of cold gas. The candle flame is hot and dense — that gives you the first type. The cold gas glowing on its own would give you the second. And the candle seen through the cold curtain gives you the third. Hot and dense, thin and glowing, cold curtain in front. Let me make each one precise.

NARRATOR: Type one, the continuous spectrum, also called a black-body spectrum. It's produced by a hot, dense glowing object — a hot solid, a hot liquid, or a dense high-pressure gas. Think of the glowing filament of an old light bulb, or the hot dense plasma deep inside a star. Every wavelength is present, smoothly, with no gaps — spread it through a prism and you get an unbroken rainbow. The key words for the marker: hot and dense.

NARRATOR: Type two, the emission spectrum, also called a bright-line spectrum. It's produced by a hot but low-density, rarefied gas glowing on its own. The atoms get excited, their electrons jump up to higher energy levels, and then fall back down — and exactly as the Bohr model says, each fall emits a photon of a specific wavelength. So instead of a full rainbow you get a few bright coloured lines on an otherwise dark background — only the wavelengths the atoms emit, nothing else. Key words: hot and thin. You'd see this from an emission nebula, or a neon sign, or a quasar.

NARRATOR: Type three, the absorption spectrum, also called a dark-line spectrum. This one is the candle through the cold curtain. You start with a continuous spectrum — the full rainbow from a hot dense source behind — and you pass that light through a cooler gas in front of it. The atoms in that cool gas absorb exactly their own characteristic wavelengths, kicking their electrons up the energy ladder, removing those specific wavelengths from the light passing through. So you get the full rainbow with thin dark lines cut into it — gaps, exactly where the cool gas swallowed its colours. Key words: continuous source behind, cooler gas in front.

QUESTION: Hang on — those dark absorption lines and the bright emission lines. Are they at the same wavelengths or different ones, for the same element?

NARRATOR: Beautiful question, and the answer is the punchline of the whole atomic story: they're at exactly the same wavelengths. Sodium absorbs the precise colours it would emit, because it's the same energy-level gaps either way — a jump up and a fall down between the same two levels involve the same photon energy, the same wavelength. So a given element's emission lines and absorption lines line up perfectly. That's why a dark line at a particular wavelength fingerprints the same element as a bright line at that wavelength. Same ladder, read two directions. This is the Bohr model from earlier in the module doing real work in astrophysics — that's interleaving link number one, and it's the backbone of everything tonight.

NARRATOR: Now, the question the HSC loves to ask, and it catches people out every year: why does a star like the Sun show an absorption spectrum, and not an emission spectrum? You need the geometry, and you need to say it in cause and effect. The hot, dense plasma in the star's interior radiates a continuous black-body spectrum — hot and dense, type one. As that light streams outward, it passes through the star's cooler, less-dense outer atmosphere. The atoms in that cooler atmosphere absorb their own characteristic wavelengths out of the continuum, cutting dark lines into it. So what reaches us is a continuous spectrum crossed by dark absorption lines. That's why a star shows absorption, not emission: dense hot interior making the continuum, cooler thin atmosphere imprinting the dark lines on the way out.

NARRATOR: And here's the trap, stated loudly, because it's a classic mark-loser. Do not write "the Sun is a hot gas, so it makes an emission spectrum." Wrong. A hot, low-density gas makes emission lines, yes — but a hot, dense body makes a continuous spectrum, and the Sun's dense interior is the dense body. The emission-versus-continuous split is about density, not just temperature. Hot and thin gives bright lines; hot and dense gives the full rainbow. Get the density word in and you've got the mark.

NARRATOR: Right. So a star's spectrum is a continuous rainbow with a forest of dark absorption lines crossing it. And that one image carries three separate readings. Here is your master memory hook for the entire episode, and I want you to write it at the top of the page the moment a stellar-spectrum question appears. The three things a stellar spectrum tells you are Temperature, Composition, and Velocity — and the hook is "Tall Cats Vanish." Tall for Temperature, Cats for Composition, Vanish for Velocity. Three readings, three different features of the spectrum, three different mechanisms. Tall Cats Vanish. Let me take them one at a time, and the whole game is keeping them distinct.

NARRATOR: Reading one — Temperature, from the shape of the continuous background. The continuous part of the spectrum is black-body radiation, and the hotter the object, the more its peak — its most intense wavelength — shifts toward the shorter, bluer end. A cooler star peaks at longer, redder wavelengths. So a star's colour is a direct readout of its surface temperature. A blue star is hot; a red star is cool. Notice — and this is the trap people reverse under pressure — hotter means shorter wavelength, bluer. Cooler means longer wavelength, redder. Blue hot, red cold. It feels backwards because on Earth we call red "hot" and blue "cold" on a tap, but in physics it's the opposite, and the exam knows you might flip it. Blue is hotter.

NARRATOR: Now, an important honesty note, because the HSC is qualitative here. There is no formula for this on your data sheet for this module — no Wien's law calculation expected. You read temperature from the colour, from where the continuous spectrum peaks, qualitatively. Shorter-wavelength peak means hotter; that's the whole statement. If you've seen Wien's law in the Nature of Light module — and you have, that's interleaving link number two — that's the quantitative version of this exact idea, but for Module Eight spectra you keep it qualitative: bluer is hotter, redder is cooler.

NARRATOR: And the colour-to-temperature relationship is what underlies the spectral classification of stars — the famous sequence. Stars are sorted into seven main classes, in order from hottest to coolest, and the letters are O, B, A, F, G, K, M. The hottest, class O, are blue and burn at tens of thousands of degrees; the coolest, class M, are red, a few thousand degrees. Our Sun sits in the middle, class G, yellow, about five and a half thousand degrees on the surface. The memory hook is the classic one, and you've met it before: "Oh Be A Fine Girl, Kiss Me." O B A F G K M. Use that exact one — it's the standard, and you'll see it again when we build the H-R diagram next episode. Write it down at the start of the exam: O B A F G K M, hottest blue to coolest red.

QUESTION: Why isn't the order alphabetical? O, B, A — that's not in order, and it's not by abundance either. What's going on?

NARRATOR: Good catch, and it's a small historical trap worth knowing. The order is by temperature — hottest to coolest — full stop. It is not alphabetical and it is not by how common the stars are. The letters got assigned earlier, before anyone understood that the sequence was a temperature sequence, based on how strong the hydrogen lines looked; later, when astronomers realised the real ordering principle was temperature, they kept the old letters but rearranged them into temperature order. So you're left with this odd-looking O B A F G K M, which is purely hottest-to-coolest. If an exam asks what the sequence orders stars by, the answer is surface temperature.

NARRATOR: Reading two — Composition, from the identity of the absorption lines. This is where the Bohr fingerprint pays off completely. Each element absorbs a unique, characteristic set of wavelengths, fixed by its own allowed electron energy-level transitions — the very same transitions from the Bohr episode. Astronomers measure these fingerprints in the laboratory first: take a known gas, hydrogen, or helium, or calcium, shine light through it, and record exactly which wavelengths it absorbs. That's the reference catalogue. Then you take the star's spectrum, look at where its dark absorption lines fall, and match them against the laboratory fingerprints. A line at hydrogen's wavelength means hydrogen is present. A line at calcium's wavelength means calcium. By matching the pattern of dark lines to the known fingerprints, you identify which elements are in the star's atmosphere — without ever sampling the star. The dark lines in the Sun's spectrum even have a name: the Fraunhofer lines, after the man who first carefully mapped hundreds of them.

NARRATOR: Now here's the crucial distinction, and it's where the number-one trap of this whole topic lives. Composition comes from the identity of the lines — which wavelengths the dark lines sit at. That's a completely different reading from temperature, which comes from the shape of the continuous background, and from velocity, which — as you're about to see — comes from the shift of the lines. Same spectrum, three different features. Composition is about where the lines are at rest. Keep that word: at rest. Because the third reading is about what happens when the lines move.

NARRATOR: Reading three — Velocity, from the Doppler shift of the lines. The light has a wavelength, so motion of the source shifts it, exactly like the pitch of an ambulance siren dropping as it passes you. If the star is moving away from us, the wavelengths get stretched — the whole pattern of lines slides toward the red, longer-wavelength end. That's a redshift. If the star is moving toward us, the wavelengths get compressed — the whole pattern slides toward the blue. That's a blueshift. And the size of the shift tells you the speed: a bigger shift means a faster motion. Here's the memory hook, and it's already half-built into the words: Red means Receding, Blue means... approaching — call it "blue is barrelling toward you." Red recedes, blue barrels in.

NARRATOR: Two things the marker is watching for here. First, notice what shifts: the whole pattern of lines moves together, sideways, while keeping the same spacing. The fingerprint doesn't change its internal pattern — every line slides by the same fractional amount. That's how you can tell a Doppler shift from a genuinely different element: it's the same recognisable pattern, just displaced bodily toward red or blue. Second, and this is a subtle exam point: the Doppler shift only measures the radial velocity — the part of the motion along the line of sight, straight toward you or straight away. A star moving purely sideways across the sky, tangentially, produces no Doppler shift at all, because it's neither approaching nor receding. So don't ever claim the Doppler shift gives you the star's full speed. It gives you the speed along the line of sight only. Radial velocity — that's the term, and getting "radial" in earns the mark.

Composition comes from the identity of the absorption lines — which wavelengths the dark lines fall at, matched to laboratory fingerprints of the elements. Velocity comes from the shift of those same lines — redshifted means receding, blueshifted means approaching, and the size of the shift gives the radial speed. Three readings, three features: shape of the continuum, identity of the lines, shift of the lines. If you said all three and kept them separate, that's the full structure of the canonical extended response, and you're most of the way to top marks already. NARRATOR: Let me hammer that trap, because it is the single biggest mark-loser in this topic. The number-one mistake is conflating the three readings. Students use line positions — which is Doppler, velocity — when the question asks about composition, which is line identity. Or they talk about lines when the question is about temperature, which is the continuum shape. Burn the mapping in: continuum shape for temperature, line identity for composition, line shift for velocity. Shape, identity, shift. NARRATOR: Now we go deeper on composition, because there's a named dot-point here that the exam asks by name: the Payne-Gaposchkin spectral analysis technique. And this is a genuinely beautiful piece of physics, with a story attached — remember the Cecilia Payne story, because that's the case study this was written for. Here's the problem she solved.

NARRATOR: You'd think reading composition is simple: strong lines of an element mean lots of that element. More iron, stronger iron lines. Reasonable — and wrong. The strength of an element's absorption lines depends on two things, not one. Yes, it depends on how much of the element is present — its abundance. But it also depends, very strongly, on the temperature of the star. Why? Because temperature controls which electron energy levels are populated, and which ionisation states the atoms are in — whether they've had electrons stripped off. And only atoms in the right state, with electrons sitting in the right starting level, can make a given absorption line at all. So if a star is at the wrong temperature for, say, hydrogen's main absorption lines, hydrogen can be wildly abundant and still show only weak lines. Temperature can mask abundance.

NARRATOR: This had led astronomers badly astray. The Sun's spectrum is crammed with iron lines — hundreds of them — and since the Earth and the meteorites that fall to it are rich in iron, the natural conclusion in the early nineteen-hundreds was that the Sun, too, was largely iron. Then in nineteen twenty-five, Cecilia Payne — later Payne-Gaposchkin — working at Harvard, applied the new physics of ionisation and excitation to the line strengths. She accounted properly for the temperature-dependence: she worked out, for the Sun's temperature, how strong each element's lines should be for a given abundance. And when she corrected the iron lines and the hydrogen lines for that temperature effect, the answer flipped completely. The Sun, and the stars generally, are overwhelmingly hydrogen and helium — the lightest elements — with only a sprinkle of everything else. The iron lines were strong not because iron was abundant, but because the Sun's temperature happens to excite iron's lines efficiently. Hydrogen was there in vast quantities all along; it just wasn't shouting as loudly at that temperature.

NARRATOR: So the exam-ready statement of her contribution, and this is exactly how to phrase it for the named dot-point: the strength of an absorption line depends not only on the abundance of the element but strongly on the star's temperature, because temperature sets which energy levels and ionisation states are populated and therefore which lines can appear. By correcting line strengths for this temperature dependence, Payne-Gaposchkin showed that stars are composed overwhelmingly of hydrogen and helium, overturning the earlier belief that the Sun was largely iron. The one-line lesson for you: line strength must be interpreted through temperature, not taken at face value.

NARRATOR: And here's the trap on her specifically. Don't write that she "discovered hydrogen lines," or "discovered hydrogen in the Sun" — she didn't discover the lines; they were already catalogued. What she did was correctly interpret the line strengths using the temperature-dependent physics, and that reinterpretation is the contribution. Interpretation, not discovery. Notice too how this loops back to temperature, reading one: the same surface temperature that you read off the continuum colour is the thing that controls the line strengths. The three readings aren't fully independent in practice — temperature even shapes how composition shows up — which is exactly why she had to untangle them. That's the depth a top-band answer shows.

NARRATOR: Now we turn the same Doppler tool from single stars onto whole galaxies, and that's where the universe cracks open. This is the named dot-point: the evidence that led to the discovery of the expansion of the universe — Hubble's work. Let me build the cause-and-effect chain, because the marker wants the chain, not just the conclusion.

NARRATOR: Step one of the chain: when you take the spectra of distant galaxies, their absorption lines — the same recognisable fingerprint patterns, hydrogen, calcium and so on — are redshifted. The whole pattern is shifted toward longer wavelengths. By the Doppler interpretation, redshift means receding, so the galaxies are moving away from us.

NARRATOR: Step two, and this is the discovery, the part that makes it more than "stuff is moving": in nineteen twenty-nine, Edwin Hubble found that the redshift is greater for more distant galaxies. The further away a galaxy is, the faster it's receding — and not just vaguely, but proportionally. Recession velocity is proportional to distance. That is Hubble's law: the speed at which a galaxy is receding equals a constant — Hubble's constant — multiplied by its distance. Twice as far, twice as fast.

NARRATOR: Step three, the interpretation that earns the top band: a velocity-proportional-to-distance pattern is exactly, precisely, what you get if space itself is expanding and carrying the galaxies apart. Picture dots painted on a balloon as you inflate it. Every dot moves away from every other dot, and a dot twice as far away recedes twice as fast — because there's twice as much rubber stretching between you and it. That's the signature: speed proportional to distance is the unmistakable fingerprint of uniform expansion. So the galaxies aren't flying through static space like shrapnel from an explosion. Rather, the space between them is stretching, and that stretching carries them apart. The light's wavelength is stretched by the expansion of the very space it travels through — the longer the journey, the more space has stretched in transit, the greater the redshift. That's why it's called cosmological redshift: it's not really a Doppler shift through space, it's space itself doing the stretching.

QUESTION: Wait — if everything is rushing away from us, doesn't that put us at the centre of the universe?

NARRATOR: No, and this is a lovely trap to dismantle, because it's the heart of why expansion is different from an ordinary explosion. On the inflating balloon, stand on any dot you like — every other dot still rushes away from you, and the far ones still recede fastest. There is no centre, no special dot. Every observer in an expanding universe sees exactly the same thing: everything receding, faster with distance. We're not at the centre; there is no centre. Avoid writing "galaxies move away from a central point" — that's the explosion picture, and it's wrong. Space expands everywhere at once. Every raisin in the rising loaf sees every other raisin moving away.

NARRATOR: And step four, the grand payoff: run the expansion backwards in time. If everything is flying apart now, then long ago everything was closer together — and far enough back, everything converges to a single hot, dense beginning. That was the first real evidence that the universe had a beginning — the Big Bang. The other major piece of evidence, which I'll just link to, is the cosmic microwave background: a faint glow of microwave radiation coming from every direction in the sky, at about two point seven kelvin — that's about minus two hundred and seventy degrees Celsius — the cooled-down afterglow of that hot dense beginning. For tonight, the spectroscopic evidence is the redshift-distance relationship; the microwave background is the partner piece. Together they're why we say the universe began.

NARRATOR: Now I owe you the weak-versus-strong contrast on this, because it's exactly the band boundary and the exam tests it directly. The weak answer to "how does galaxy redshift give evidence for an expanding universe" is: "redshift means the galaxies are moving away from us." That's it — and it earns almost nothing, maybe one mark, because moving-away by itself doesn't tell you the universe is expanding; it's just motion. The strong answer adds the two things the weak one is missing. One: the redshift increases with distance — more distant galaxies are more redshifted, so they recede faster, in proportion to distance. Two: this specific relationship — velocity proportional to distance — is what uniform expansion of space predicts, with no special centre, which ordinary motion through static space would not produce. Hear the difference? The weak answer states the observation. The strong answer states the observation, the distance relationship, and the interpretation that pins it to expanding space rather than mere motion. Observation, distance-dependence, interpretation. Get all three and you've got the marks.

NARRATOR: Now — there is exactly one quantitative thing in this whole episode, and it's worth doing properly because the HSC can examine it as a calculation: Hubble's law, used to estimate the age of the universe. Everything else tonight is qualitative, but this is real arithmetic, so let me walk it step by step. The full working is in Listing 1, and the unit conversions I'll use are in Listing 2.

NARRATOR: Hubble's law in words: a galaxy's recession velocity equals Hubble's constant times its distance. Rearrange it and Hubble's constant is the recession velocity divided by the distance — which is just the gradient of the velocity-versus-distance graph. So if you measure how fast a galaxy is receding and how far away it is, you can find Hubble's constant. And here's the elegant part: if the universe has been expanding at a steady rate, then the age of the universe is simply one divided by Hubble's constant. The reason is intuitive — a galaxy now at distance d, receding at velocity v, took a time equal to distance over velocity to get there from a point start, and distance over velocity is just one over Hubble's constant. So age equals one over Hubble's constant.

NARRATOR: Let me do the canonical worked example, using Hubble's own nineteen twenty-nine numbers. We have a galaxy at a distance of two million parsecs, receding at about eleven hundred kilometres per second. Find Hubble's constant, and then the age of the universe.

NARRATOR: Step one — fix the units, because this is where marks die. Distance in parsecs is useless until we convert it; one parsec is three point zero nine times ten to the thirteen kilometres. So two million parsecs — that's two times ten to the six parsecs — times three point zero nine times ten to the thirteen kilometres per parsec, gives a distance of about six point two times ten to the nineteen kilometres. Hold that.

NARRATOR: Step two — Hubble's constant is velocity over distance. Eleven hundred kilometres per second, divided by six point two times ten to the nineteen kilometres. The kilometres cancel, leaving per-second, and the number comes out at about one point seven seven times ten to the minus seventeen per second. So Hubble's constant is roughly one point eight times ten to the minus seventeen per second. Notice the units: per second, an inverse time — that's the giveaway that one over it will be a time.

NARRATOR: Step three — the age is one over Hubble's constant. One divided by one point seven seven times ten to the minus seventeen per second gives about five point six times ten to the sixteen seconds. That's the age in seconds.

NARRATOR: Step four — convert seconds to years, because nobody quotes the age of the universe in seconds. One year is about three point one six times ten to the seven seconds. So five point six times ten to the sixteen seconds, divided by three point one six times ten to the seven seconds per year, gives about one point eight times ten to the nine years. So Hubble's original data gives an age of roughly one point eight billion years — call it about two billion years to one significant figure.

NARRATOR: And here's the teaching twist, which is itself an exam-worthy point: that answer is badly wrong, and you should say so. Two billion years is younger than the Earth, which we know from radioactive dating is about four and a half billion years old — and the universe can't be younger than a planet inside it. So something was off. The error was in the distances: in the nineteen fifties, Walter Baade discovered there are two different populations of the Cepheid variable stars used as Hubble's distance markers, and recalibrating them roughly halved Hubble's constant — which doubled the age. The modern value, from precise measurements, is about thirteen point eight billion years. So when you present this calculation, present the two-billion-year figure as historical, flag that it's too young, and note that better distance measurements pushed the age up to today's thirteen point eight billion years. That little caveat is exactly the kind of evaluation the top band rewards. Don't present two billion years as fact.

QUESTION: Question one, three marks. Account for the production of a continuous spectrum, an emission spectrum, and an absorption spectrum. Pause, write all three, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. A continuous spectrum is produced by a hot, dense object — a hot solid, liquid, or high-pressure gas — which emits black-body radiation at all wavelengths. An emission spectrum is produced by a hot, low-density gas: its excited electrons fall to lower energy levels, each transition emitting a photon of a specific wavelength, giving bright lines on a dark background. An absorption spectrum is produced when light with a continuous spectrum passes through a cooler gas in front of it; the atoms in that gas absorb their own characteristic wavelengths, cutting dark lines into the continuous spectrum. One mark each, and the marker is checking for "hot and dense," "hot and low-density," and "continuous source through cooler gas."

QUESTION: Question two, two marks. Explain why a star such as the Sun produces an absorption spectrum rather than an emission spectrum. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The hot, dense plasma in the star's interior emits a continuous black-body spectrum. As this light passes outward through the star's cooler, less-dense outer atmosphere, atoms there absorb their characteristic wavelengths from the continuum, producing dark absorption lines. Because the dense interior produces a continuous spectrum and the cooler thin atmosphere only removes specific wavelengths, the result is a continuous spectrum crossed by dark absorption lines — an absorption spectrum. The marks are for the dense-interior continuum and the cooler-atmosphere absorption geometry. Don't say "the Sun is a hot gas so it emits lines" — that's the density trap.

QUESTION: Question three, four marks. Explain how the spectrum of a star can be used to determine its surface temperature, its chemical composition, and its translational velocity. Pause, write a full response keeping the three distinct, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, three clean parts. Surface temperature is determined from the shape of the continuous black-body component: the wavelength of peak intensity depends on temperature, with hotter stars peaking at shorter, bluer wavelengths and cooler stars at longer, redder wavelengths, so the star's colour is a direct measure of its surface temperature — the basis of the O B A F G K M classes. Chemical composition is determined from the identity of the absorption lines: each element absorbs a characteristic set of wavelengths set by its electron energy-level transitions, so matching the dark lines to known laboratory spectra identifies the elements present. Translational velocity is determined from the Doppler shift of those lines: if the whole line pattern is shifted toward longer red wavelengths the star is receding, toward shorter blue wavelengths it is approaching, and the size of the shift gives the radial velocity along the line of sight. Three separate readings — continuum shape, line identity, line shift — kept distinct. That structure, three labelled mechanisms, is what earns all four marks. Conflating them is the standard way to drop marks.

QUESTION: Question four, three marks. Describe the contribution of Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin to determining the chemical composition of stars. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Payne-Gaposchkin showed that the strength of an element's absorption lines depends not only on the element's abundance but strongly on the star's temperature, because temperature determines which electron energy levels and ionisation states are populated and therefore which lines can appear. By correctly accounting for this temperature dependence of line strengths, she demonstrated that stars, including the Sun, are composed overwhelmingly of hydrogen and helium, overturning the earlier belief that the Sun was largely iron. The marks are for: line strength depends on temperature as well as abundance; the temperature-corrected interpretation; and the hydrogen-and-helium conclusion. Remember — she interpreted the lines correctly, she didn't "discover" them.

QUESTION: Question five, the big one, five marks. Explain how the redshift of galaxies provides evidence that the universe is expanding, and outline how Hubble's law can be used to estimate the age of the universe. Pause, attempt the whole thing, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The absorption-line spectra of distant galaxies are redshifted — the whole line pattern is shifted toward longer wavelengths — which by the Doppler interpretation means the galaxies are receding from us. Hubble found that the redshift, and hence the recession velocity, increases in proportion to a galaxy's distance: recession velocity equals Hubble's constant times distance. A velocity-proportional-to-distance relationship is exactly what is produced if space itself is expanding uniformly and carrying the galaxies apart — the wavelength of the light being stretched by the expansion of the space it travels through — rather than galaxies merely moving through static space, which would have no special centre and would not give this relationship. This is the evidence for an expanding universe. For the age: since the age equals one over Hubble's constant, you measure a galaxy's recession velocity and distance, calculate Hubble's constant as velocity over distance with consistent units so it comes out in inverse seconds, and take its reciprocal to get the age in seconds, then convert to years. The full marks here come from the distance-dependence, the proportionality, the expanding-space interpretation, and the one-over-Hubble's-constant age method. The weak version — "redshift means galaxies are moving away" — gets a single mark; everything above the line is what lifts it to full marks.
