NARRATOR: Let's start with Maxwell, the theory side. James Clerk Maxwell, Scottish physicist, born eighteen thirty-one, died eighteen seventy-nine. NARRATOR: Here's your hook for the four laws, in that order, the order that tells the story: think "Gauss's Glorious Fields Are Mine." NARRATOR: Now here is the moment. Maxwell read the speed straight out of his wave equation, and it was not some new mysterious quantity. NARRATOR: Let me make sure you know what those two constants actually are, because the whole argument hinges on where they come from. The permittivity of free space, epsilon-nought, is a constant that measures how easily an electric field forms in a vacuum. The permeability of free space, mu-nought, is the magnetic partner: it measures how easily a magnetic field forms in a vacuum, and it shows up whenever you do magnetism, the field around a current-carrying wire, the field inside a coil. Its value is four pi times ten to the minus seven, which works out to about one point two six times ten to the minus six.

NARRATOR: And here is the crucial, mark-earning point, the one that turns a B-grade answer into a top answer. Both of those constants are measured in purely electric and magnetic experiments. Epsilon-nought you get from charging up a capacitor and measuring it, an electricity experiment. Mu-nought you get from running a current through a coil and measuring the magnetic force, a magnetism experiment. Neither experiment has anything whatsoever to do with light. You could measure both of these constants in a dark room with a battery, some wire, and a couple of plates, never thinking about light for a second. Epsilon-nought and mu-nought were not chosen to give the speed of light; they were already pinned down by completely separate experiments. The speed of light fell out of his theory as a consequence. NARRATOR: But detecting the waves wasn't enough. Hertz had to show these new waves really were the same kind of thing as light, just invisible, and then measure their speed. NARRATOR: Here's the polarisation test. Hertz rotated his receiver loop relative to the transmitter and watched what happened to the spark. When he rotated the loop to be perpendicular to the transmitter, ninety degrees across, the spark vanished completely, no spark at all. And at angles in between, he got an in-between spark. That is exactly the behaviour of light passing through a polariser and an analyser, and it tells you the wave has a definite orientation in space, a single plane of vibration, which only a transverse wave can have. NARRATOR: And finally, the measurement of speed, which is the bit that actually confirms Maxwell's predicted number. Hertz set up standing waves. He moved his detector through this pattern and found the nodes, and from the spacing of the nodes he could read off the wavelength. He also knew the frequency, because the frequency was set by his oscillating circuit, by the electrical properties of his transmitter. So he had wavelength, and he had frequency, and he used the wave equation: speed equals frequency times wavelength. The answer equalled the speed of light, c. Maxwell's prediction, confirmed by measurement. The waves were the same nature as light, but at a much lower frequency. Today we call them radio waves. And the unit of frequency, the hertz, one cycle per second, is named in his honour.

NARRATOR: Now, the third dot-point: measuring the speed of electromagnetic waves yourself, in the school lab, and assessing the accuracy of your value. This is a required practical, so you need to be able to describe the method and, crucially, discuss the errors, because "assess the accuracy" is a NESA verb that demands you make a judgement backed by error analysis. The method is a scaled-down, modern version of exactly what Hertz did with his standing waves.

NARRATOR: Here's the apparatus. You take a microwave transmitter, which emits microwaves of a single known frequency, a few gigahertz typically, and you point it straight at a flat metal reflector plate, a few tens of centimetres away. The microwaves travel to the plate, reflect, and come back. The outgoing wave and the reflected wave superpose and form a standing wave in the space between the transmitter and the plate, just like Hertz's. Then you take a small probe or receiver and slide it slowly along the line between transmitter and plate, watching its output meter. As you move it, the reading rises and falls: maxima where the waves reinforce, the antinodes, and minima where they cancel, the nodes.

NARRATOR: And here's the one piece of physics you absolutely cannot get wrong, because it's the most common slip in the whole experiment. The distance between two adjacent nodes is half a wavelength, not a whole wavelength. Half. So to find the wavelength, you measure the distance from one node to the next and double it. Better, you measure across many nodes, say ten node-spacings, and divide, then double, to average out your error. The wavelength equals two times the node spacing. If you forget that factor of two, you halve your answer for the speed, and you'll get one and a half times ten to the eight instead of three times ten to the eight, and wonder where it went wrong. Two times the node spacing gives the wavelength.

NARRATOR: Then the speed is just the wave equation: speed equals frequency times wavelength. You know the frequency, it's printed on the transmitter or set by the apparatus. You've measured the wavelength. Multiply them, and you get a value for the speed of electromagnetic waves. Let me give you the worked numbers, and they're in Listing two as well. Say the transmitter runs at ten point five gigahertz, that's one point zero five times ten to the ten hertz, and you find adjacent nodes fourteen point three millimetres apart. Wavelength is two times fourteen point three millimetres, which is twenty-eight point six millimetres, or two point eight six times ten to the minus two metres. Speed is frequency times wavelength: one point zero five times ten to the ten, times two point eight six times ten to the minus two, equals three point zero zero times ten to the eight metres per second. There's your speed of light, measured on a bench.

NARRATOR: Now the part that earns the real marks: assess the accuracy. You compare your measured value against the accepted value, three point zero zero times ten to the eight metres per second, and you compute the percentage error. In this case the agreement is excellent, well under one percent. But "assess the accuracy" doesn't mean just quote a percentage; it means make a judgement and identify your sources of error. So name them. The dominant one is uncertainty in locating the node positions: the nodes are not razor-sharp points, they're broad, shallow minima, the reading dips over a range of positions, so pinning the exact centre of a node is hard and that uncertainty feeds straight into your wavelength. You reduce it by averaging over many node spacings rather than one. Other sources: drift or instability in the transmitter's frequency; stray reflections off nearby walls, equipment, or the experimenter's own body, which distort the standing-wave pattern; and simple error in reading the probe's position off the ruler. A strong answer classifies these: node-location and reading errors are largely random, reducible by repetition and averaging; a miscalibrated frequency would be systematic, shifting every result the same way. That distinction, random versus systematic, is exactly what "assess the accuracy" is fishing for.

NARRATOR: Now follow the logic, because this flips how the metre and the second relate to c, and it's examinable. Once you've defined c as an exact number, you can use it to define the metre. QUESTION: So does that mean scientists are still trying to measure the speed of light more and more accurately with better equipment?

NARRATOR: No, and this is a favourite trap, so lock it in. Since nineteen eighty-three, c cannot be measured more accurately, because it is no longer measured at all. It's defined, fixed, exact. Any experiment you do now that seems like a "better measurement of the speed of light" is, in reality, a better realisation of the metre. When the transmitter sparked, a spark also jumped the gap of the receiver loop, which could only occur if an electromagnetic wave had travelled across the space and induced a current in the loop, demonstrating that electromagnetic waves exist and carry energy through empty space. Hertz then confirmed these waves share the properties of light by showing they could be reflected off a metal sheet, refracted through a prism, and polarised, the spark being maximum when the loop was parallel to the transmitter and absent when perpendicular. Finally he measured their speed using standing waves and the relationship speed equals frequency times wavelength, obtaining the speed of light, confirming Maxwell's prediction. Full marks need: the transmitter as accelerating charges, the detector sparking with no power source, the reflection-refraction-polarisation confirmations, and the measured speed equal to c.

QUESTION: Question four, worth three marks. A student measures the speed of electromagnetic waves using a microwave transmitter of frequency nine point four times ten to the nine hertz aimed at a metal reflector. They find adjacent nodes are sixteen millimetres apart. Calculate the speed, and identify the main source of error in this experiment.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The wavelength is twice the node spacing, because adjacent nodes in a standing wave are half a wavelength apart, so the wavelength is two times sixteen millimetres, which is thirty-two millimetres, or three point two times ten to the minus two metres. The speed is frequency times wavelength: nine point four times ten to the nine, times three point two times ten to the minus two, equals three point zero times ten to the eight metres per second. The main source of error is uncertainty in locating the node positions, because the nodes are broad, shallow minima rather than sharp points, making the exact position hard to pin down; this is reduced by averaging the spacing over many nodes. Two marks for the correct calculation, including the crucial factor of two for the wavelength, and one mark for a valid, well-explained source of error. The working is in Listing two.

QUESTION: Question five, worth two marks. A textbook states: "Modern atomic-clock experiments now let scientists measure the speed of light more precisely than ever before." Assess whether this statement is correct.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The statement is not correct. Since nineteen eighty-three the speed of light has been defined to be exactly two hundred and ninety-nine million, seven hundred and ninety-two thousand, four hundred and fifty-eight metres per second, so it is a fixed, exact value with no measurement uncertainty and cannot be measured more precisely. The metre is now defined from this fixed value of c together with the caesium-based definition of the second. Therefore improved experiments refine the definition of the metre, not the value of c. One mark for stating c is defined exactly and so cannot be measured more accurately, one mark for explaining that better experiments now refine the metre rather than c.
