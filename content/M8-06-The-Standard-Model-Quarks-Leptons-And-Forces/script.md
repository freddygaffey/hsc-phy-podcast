NARRATOR: Five episodes back, cathode rays — the birth of the electron. The Maltese cross casting a sharp shadow, telling us cathode rays go in straight lines. The paddle wheel turning, telling us they carry momentum, so they have mass. The beam bending towards the positive plate, telling us they're negatively charged. A stream of negative particles with mass: the electron. Hold onto the electron, because in today's episode it turns out to be one of the truly fundamental particles — it has no parts inside it at all.

NARRATOR: Then Thomson and Millikan. Thomson's crossed-fields trick pinned down the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron, the same value for every gas and every metal — the electron is universal. Millikan's oil drop pinned down the charge itself, one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, and showed charge only comes in whole-number multiples of that. Charge is quantised. Now keep that elementary charge in your pocket, because today, for the first time, we're going to meet particles that carry a fraction of it — two-thirds, and minus one-third. The first dent in "charge only comes in whole units."

NARRATOR: Then the gold foil experiment. Geiger and Marsden firing alpha particles at gold. Most go straight through — mostly empty space. About one in eight thousand bounces straight back — a tiny, dense, massive, positively charged nucleus. Out goes the plum pudding, in comes the nuclear atom. And that nucleus, the thing the alpha particle bounced off, is exactly what we're going to take apart today.

NARRATOR: Then the Bohr model. Stationary states — Stations, Steps, Spin. The electron sits in discrete energy levels without radiating; it jumps between them by emitting or absorbing a single photon, with the photon energy equal to Planck's constant times its frequency, equal to the gap between the levels. A hybrid of classical and quantum physics that nailed hydrogen's line spectrum.

NARRATOR: And last episode, matter waves — de Broglie. Every particle has a wavelength equal to Planck's constant divided by its momentum. Electrons diffract; Davisson and Germer proved it. A whole number of electron wavelengths has to fit around a Bohr orbit, which is finally *why* the energy levels are quantised — the standing-wave condition. So here's the thread that runs through all five: the atom is built from a small number of particles, and matter, at the bottom, behaves in quantised, discrete chunks. Today we ask the last question in that sequence. The electron is fundamental — fine. But is the proton fundamental? Is the neutron? And the answer, which took two-mile-long machines to find, is no. They have parts inside.

NARRATOR: Here's today, and why it matters for the exam. By the end of this episode you'll be able to do five things. Name the two families of fundamental particles — leptons and quarks — and list them. Describe a baryon in terms of quarks, and *derive* the charge of the proton and the neutron from the fractional quark charges — that's the signature calculation and it's nearly guaranteed. Describe the structure of an atom purely in terms of quarks and leptons. Name the four fundamental forces and their carrier particles, and explain the role of the strong force in holding nucleons together. And explain how particle accelerators are used to discover and study these particles. That's a lot, but it's mostly naming and one piece of fraction arithmetic. Let's go.

NARRATOR: Start with the big picture. The Standard Model sorts every fundamental particle into two great tribes. On one side, the matter particles — the building blocks, the things stuff is made of. These are called fermions. On the other side, the force-carrier particles — the things that transmit the forces between the matter particles. These are called bosons. Matter on one side, forces on the other. Fermions build; bosons bind. Hold that division, because it organises the whole episode: first the matter particles, then the forces.

NARRATOR: The matter particles, the fermions, split again into two families. Leptons and quarks. Let's take leptons first, because you already know one of them.

NARRATOR: Leptons are fundamental particles with no internal structure — you cannot break a lepton into smaller pieces, full stop. There are six of them, arranged in three pairs we call generations. The first and most important is the electron — the one that orbits in atoms and decides an element's chemistry. Then there's a heavier cousin called the muon, and a heavier one still called the tau. Each of those three carries a charge of minus one, in units of the elementary charge. And each of those three has a partner: a neutrino. The electron neutrino, the muon neutrino, and the tau neutrino — three neutrinos, all electrically neutral, all with almost no mass, almost ghostly. So six leptons total: three charged ones — electron, muon, tau — and their three neutrinos.

NARRATOR: Now quarks. Quarks are also fundamental — also no internal structure — but here's the property that makes them strange, and it's the property the exam loves: quarks are never found alone. You will never isolate a single quark. We covered the deep reason in the case study — remember the particle accelerators and quarks story, where pulling a quark away just conjures new particles out of empty space to keep it company. So quarks always come bound together, and when they bind, they build the heavier particles called hadrons. The proton is a hadron. The neutron is a hadron.

NARRATOR: There are six quarks, and like the leptons they come in three generations, lightest to heaviest. Their names are deliberately whimsical — physicists having fun. Up and down. Charm and strange. Top and bottom. And here's the memory hook you must lock in, because naming the six quarks is a straight recall mark. Take the first letters — U, D, C, S, T, B — and remember the sentence: "Up Dogs Chase Strange Tabby-cats Below." Up, down, charm, strange, top, bottom. Up Dogs Chase Strange Tabbycats Below. Say it once at the start of your exam and dump the six names on the page.

NARRATOR: Now the property that matters most about quarks: their charge is fractional. This is the dent in charge quantisation I flagged in the recap. Three of the quarks — up, charm, and top — carry a charge of plus two-thirds of the elementary charge. The other three — down, strange, and bottom — carry minus one-third of the elementary charge. There's a tidy pattern there: the top row of each generation, up-charm-top, is plus two-thirds; the bottom row, down-strange-bottom, is minus one-third. And every quark has an antiquark with the opposite charge — the anti-up is minus two-thirds, the anti-down is plus one-third. We'll need those antiquarks in a moment.

QUESTION: Hang on — if charge only comes in whole multiples of the elementary charge, like Millikan showed, how can a quark have two-thirds of one?

NARRATOR: Sharp question, and it's exactly the apparent contradiction the exam might probe. The resolution is this: Millikan measured the charge on things you can actually isolate — oil drops, free particles. And every *isolatable* particle does have a whole-number charge. But quarks can never be isolated. They only ever appear bound together inside hadrons, and when you add up the fractional charges of the quarks inside any hadron, they always sum to a whole number. So the fractional charges are real, but they're hidden — they only exist inside particles whose total charge is still a whole number. Millikan's rule survives for anything you can hold; the fractions live one level deeper, where you can never reach in and grab just one. Which brings us straight to the heart of the episode.

NARRATOR: Hadrons — the particles built from quarks — come in two types, and confusing these two is the single most common trap in this topic, so we're going to nail it now and reinforce it later. The two types are baryons and mesons. A baryon is three quarks bound together. A meson is one quark plus one antiquark — a pair. Three versus a pair. The syllabus dot-point is about baryons specifically, because the two most important particles in the universe — the proton and the neutron — are baryons. But you must know the meson exists and that it's a quark-antiquark pair, because the classic mistake is calling a proton a meson, or saying a baryon is two quarks. It is three. Here's the hook to keep them straight: "Baryons are Big — three quarks; mesons are the Middle — a pair." Big-three, Middle-pair. Or just remember the B in Baryon stands for "Bunch of three."

NARRATOR: Now the signature calculation — the one you can almost guarantee will appear in some form, and the one that separates a band-six answer from a band-four. We're going to derive the charge of the proton and the neutron from their quark content. This is worth doing slowly, out loud, because the marks are in showing the arithmetic, not just stating the answer.

NARRATOR: The proton is a baryon, so it's three quarks. Its composition is up, up, down — two up quarks and one down quark. We write it u-u-d. Now add the charges. The first up is plus two-thirds. The second up is plus two-thirds. The down is minus one-third. So you're computing plus two-thirds, plus two-thirds, minus one-third. Put them over a common denominator of three: that's plus two, plus two, minus one, all over three. Two plus two minus one is three. So it's three-thirds, which is plus one. The proton has a charge of plus one — that is, plus one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. The fractional pieces add up to exactly the whole-number charge we measure. I've written this out step by step in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now the neutron — also a baryon, also three quarks. Its composition is up, down, down — one up quark and two down quarks. We write it u-d-d. Add the charges: the up is plus two-thirds, the first down is minus one-third, the second down is minus one-third. Common denominator of three: plus two, minus one, minus one, all over three. Two minus one minus one is zero. So it's zero-thirds — zero. The neutron is electrically neutral. Which is exactly what we already knew — the neutron has no charge — and now we've derived it from its parts. That's also in Listing one.

NARRATOR: And here's the contrast that ties them together and that the exam loves. Look at what's different between the proton and the neutron. The proton is up-up-down. The neutron is up-down-down. They differ by a single quark — swap one up quark for one down quark, and a proton becomes a neutron. That single swap, plus two-thirds becoming minus one-third, is exactly a change of one in the charge — from plus one down to zero. Hold that thought hard, because in two episodes, when we do beta decay, that exact quark swap — one up turning into one down inside a nucleon — is what the weak force does to turn a proton into a neutron, or the reverse. The proton-neutron pair *is* a single quark flip apart. Here's the memory hook to stop you reversing them: the Proton is more Positive, and it has the extra Plus — the extra up quark. P for Proton, P for Positive, P for the Plus-two-thirds up quark it has the extra of. Proton: more ups. Neutron: more downs.

QUESTION: Before I go on, pause the player and do this yourself. The pion-plus is a meson, made of an up quark and an anti-down quark. What is its charge? Work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. It's a meson, so it's a quark plus an antiquark — up quark and anti-down. The up quark is plus two-thirds. The anti-down is the antiparticle of the down, so its charge is the opposite of minus one-third, which is plus one-third. Add them: plus two-thirds plus one-third equals three-thirds, which is plus one. The pion-plus has a charge of plus one. If you got that, you've nailed two things at once — you used the meson rule, quark-plus-antiquark, and you flipped the sign correctly for the antiquark. That working is in Listing two. If you called the pion a baryon and tried three quarks, that's the trap — re-burn it: baryon is three, meson is a pair.

NARRATOR: Now let's zoom back out and answer a named dot-point cleanly: describe the structure of an atom in terms of quarks and leptons. This is a satisfying one, because it pulls the whole picture together. An atom is a nucleus surrounded by electrons. The nucleus is protons and neutrons. Each proton is two up quarks and a down. Each neutron is an up quark and two downs. And the electrons orbiting outside are leptons. So follow it down: the entire atom — and therefore everything you can see, touch, breathe, or stand on — is built from just three fundamental particles. Up quarks, down quarks, and electrons. That's it. Two quarks and one lepton, all from the first generation. The muon, the tau, the charm, strange, top and bottom quarks, the neutrinos — none of them are needed to build ordinary stable matter. They show up in cosmic rays, in accelerators, in decays, but they're not in your atoms. Here's the mark-earning sentence: all ordinary, stable matter is composed of only first-generation fermions — up quarks and down quarks bound into the protons and neutrons of the nucleus, with electrons, which are leptons, around the outside. Say that and you've described atomic structure in terms of quarks and leptons exactly as the dot-point asks.

NARRATOR: That's the matter half of the Standard Model done — fermions, leptons and quarks, building atoms. Now the other half: the forces. Because quarks don't just sit next to each other politely. Something has to hold them together, and something has to hold the protons and neutrons together in the nucleus. That something is a force, and in the Standard Model every force is carried by a particle — a boson.

NARRATOR: There are four fundamental forces in nature, and you need to name all four, name their carrier particles, and — this is the one students forget — flag the exception with gravity every single time. Let me give you the four with a memory hook, then we'll go through each one.

NARRATOR: The four forces are the strong nuclear force, the electromagnetic force, the weak nuclear force, and gravity. Here's the hook to remember all four and their relative strength, strongest to weakest: "Strong Elephants Will Grumble" — Strong, Electromagnetic, Weak, Gravity. And that order is also roughly their strength order: the strong force is the strongest, then electromagnetic, then the weak force, and gravity — astonishingly — is by far the weakest of the four. Strong Elephants Will Grumble. Now the carriers.

NARRATOR: The strong nuclear force is carried by particles called gluons — and the name is perfect, because they glue the quarks together. The electromagnetic force, which acts between any charged particles, is carried by the photon — the same photon of light we've been talking about since Module 7, the same packet Einstein used for the photoelectric effect and Bohr used for his jumps. The weak nuclear force, which is responsible for certain kinds of radioactive decay, is carried by particles called the W and Z bosons. And gravity — here's the exception you flag every time — gravity, in the Standard Model, has no confirmed carrier particle at all. The Standard Model does not explain gravity. There's a hypothetical particle people talk about called the graviton, but it has never been detected, it is not part of the confirmed Standard Model, and it is not on your data sheet. So if a question asks about the forces in the Standard Model, the correct, mark-earning statement is: gravity is the one fundamental force the Standard Model does not yet describe, and it has no confirmed force-carrier. Here's the hook for the carriers: "Gluttons Photograph Weak Zebras" — Gluons for strong, Photons for electromagnetic, W and Z for weak — and Gravity gets no photo at all. Listing three is the full forces-and-carriers table; rehearse it from that.

NARRATOR: One more particle you should be able to place, because it makes headlines and examiners like it: the Higgs boson. The Higgs is a boson, but it is *not* a force carrier — that's the trap. It's a different kind of boson, and its job is to give the other particles their mass through its interaction with something called the Higgs field. Peter Higgs predicted it in the nineteen sixties; it was finally identified at CERN, the big European laboratory, in twenty-thirteen. So if a question mentions the Higgs: it gives particles mass, and it is not one of the four force carriers. Don't list it as a fifth force.

NARRATOR: Now let's go deep on the strong force, because it earns the most marks and it's the one the dot-point singles out. The strong force does two jobs, at two scales, and you should distinguish them. Job one: inside a single proton or neutron, gluons carry the strong force that binds the three quarks tightly together. That's why you can never pull a quark out — the strong force between quarks is ferociously powerful and, oddly, doesn't weaken much with distance, so the quarks are confined. Job two: between the protons and neutrons in a nucleus. This is a leftover, a *residual* effect of the strong force that's busy inside the nucleons — a bit like how molecules attract each other through the residual electrical effects of their atoms even though the atoms are neutral overall. This residual strong force is what binds protons and neutrons to one another and holds the nucleus together.

NARRATOR: And here is the crucial property — the one that makes nuclei possible at all. The strong force is the strongest of the four forces, but it acts only over an extremely short range — about ten to the minus fifteen metres, which is roughly the size of a nucleus itself. It's a nearest-neighbour force: a nucleon only feels the strong pull of the nucleons right next to it. Now contrast that with the electromagnetic force. Inside a nucleus you've got a crowd of protons, all positively charged, all repelling each other by the Coulomb force — and that repulsion is long-range, reaching right across the nucleus. So why doesn't the nucleus blow itself apart? Because at the tiny separations inside a nucleus, the short-range strong-force attraction between neighbouring nucleons is far stronger than the Coulomb repulsion. The strong force wins locally, and the nucleus holds. We'll build this into the full nuclear-stability story next episode — that tug-of-war between the short-range strong force and the long-range Coulomb repulsion is exactly what decides which nuclei are stable and which fall apart.

NARRATOR: This sets up a comparison the exam asks directly — distinguish the strong nuclear force from the weak nuclear force — and it's a classic place students lose marks by mixing the two up. So let's do it properly, point by point, with the contrast language markers reward.

QUESTION: So how would I answer "compare the strong nuclear force and the weak nuclear force" for full marks?

NARRATOR: Build it as an explicit contrast, one feature at a time, using "whereas" and "in contrast." Here's the model answer. The strong nuclear force is the force that binds quarks together inside nucleons and binds nucleons together in the nucleus; whereas the weak nuclear force is the force responsible for certain radioactive decays, in particular beta decay, where it changes one type of quark into another. The strong force is carried by gluons, in contrast to the weak force, which is carried by the W and Z bosons. The strong force is the strongest of the four fundamental forces; whereas the weak force is much weaker — far weaker even than electromagnetism. Both, however, act only over extremely short ranges within the nucleus. In short: the strong force *holds matter together*, in contrast to the weak force, which *transforms* particles by changing quark type and drives decay. Hear the structure? Every sentence pairs the two forces and contrasts one property — carrier, strength, and above all role. The single most important contrast, and the one to lead with, is the role: strong binds, weak transforms. Get that one down and you've got the central mark. We come back to the weak force properly in two episodes, when it turns a down quark into an up quark and flips a neutron into a proton in beta decay — that's the same quark swap we spotted earlier between the proton and the neutron.

NARRATOR: That's the forces. Now the last big piece, and it's a named dot-point of its own: how do we actually *know* any of this? You can't see a quark. You can't isolate one. So how did anyone discover quarks, leptons, and the force-carrying bosons? The answer is particle accelerators, and explaining how they work is worth real marks. Let me give you the full chain — there are five steps, and there's a hook for them.

NARRATOR: The five steps are: Accelerate, Steer, Collide, Convert, Detect. Here's the hook — "All Scientists Crave Cool Data." Accelerate, Steer, Collide, Convert, Detect. Walk through them.

NARRATOR: Step one, Accelerate. You take charged particles — protons, electrons, ions — and you give them repeated energy kicks using electric fields. Every time the particle crosses a gap with an electric field across it, the field does work on it and speeds it up. The more kicks, the higher the energy. Now this should ring a bell — it's exactly the physics from Module 6. Remember from charged particles in electric fields: an electric field exerts a force on a charge and accelerates it, doing work and increasing its kinetic energy. Same idea, just done over and over to reach enormous energies.

NARRATOR: Step two, Steer. To keep the particles on a circular or curved path — so you can keep kicking them, or aim them — you use magnetic fields to bend them. And here's the cross-link, and the trap: it's the electric field that speeds the particles up, and the magnetic field that steers them. The magnetic field does no work — it only changes direction. Remember from Module 6, charged particles in magnetic fields: a magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it bends the path into a circle without changing the speed. This is exactly the cyclotron and the mass spectrometer you studied — electric field to accelerate, magnetic field to curve. A modern accelerator like the Large Hadron Collider at CERN is a twenty-seven-kilometre ring doing precisely this: electric fields kick, superconducting magnets steer, lap after lap, up to seven tera-electronvolts per beam. Don't ever write that the magnetic field speeds the particle up. It steers. The electric field accelerates.

NARRATOR: Step three, Collide. Once the particles are at huge energy, you smash them — into a fixed target, or, better, head-on into another beam coming the other way. Step four, Convert — and this is the conceptual heart of it. In the collision, that enormous kinetic energy is converted into mass, creating brand-new particles that weren't there before. The rule is Einstein's mass-energy equivalence: energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. We met it at the end of Module 7 with relativistic mass-energy, and we'll go deep on it in episode nine. What it means here: a particle of a given mass can only be created if the collision provides at least that mass times the speed of light squared in energy. So the more collision energy you have, the more massive the particles you can make. This is the whole reason you build bigger accelerators — to reach the energy needed to materialise heavier, rarer particles that don't exist in ordinary matter. The mass isn't created from nothing; it's converted from the kinetic energy of the collision, via energy equals m c squared.

NARRATOR: Step five, Detect. The new particles fly out of the collision and pass through layers of detectors that record their tracks, their charges, their momenta, and their energies. From those tracks — how much a particle's path curves in a magnetic field, how much energy it deposits — physicists reconstruct what each particle was. The very first detectors were cloud chambers and bubble chambers, where a charged particle leaves a visible trail of droplets or bubbles. Modern detectors at CERN and Fermilab are giant onion-layered machines, each layer tuned to catch a different kind of particle. That's the full chain — All Scientists Crave Cool Data: Accelerate, Steer, Collide, Convert, Detect.

NARRATOR: And what did this actually discover? Everything in today's episode. Firing high-energy electrons into protons and watching them scatter off something hard hidden inside — that was the evidence for quarks, the internal structure of the proton, at the SLAC laboratory in nineteen sixty-eight. That's the case study — remember the particle accelerators and quarks story, the two-mile machine aimed at a single proton. Accelerators also produced the W and Z bosons, the carriers of the weak force. They found the top quark at Fermilab in nineteen ninety-five. And they identified the Higgs boson at CERN. Particle accelerators are *the* tool that built and tested the entire Standard Model. So when the exam asks "explain the role of particle accelerators in obtaining evidence for the Standard Model," the answer is: they accelerate charged particles to enormous energies and collide them, converting collision energy into the mass of new particles that detectors then identify — and this is how quarks, leptons, and the force-carrying bosons were all discovered and the model validated.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the second big extended-response model now, because "explain how particle accelerators are used to discover new particles" is a stock question and you want the full-mark version ready.

QUESTION: So what does the full-mark answer to that look like?
