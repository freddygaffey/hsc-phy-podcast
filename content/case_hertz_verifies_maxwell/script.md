NARRATOR: On the fifth of November, eighteen seventy-nine, in a house in Cambridge, England, a forty-eight-year-old Scottish physicist died of abdominal cancer. His name was James Clerk Maxwell. And he died believing something extraordinary. He believed that light itself — the light streaming through his window, the light of every star in the sky — was nothing more than a ripple of electricity and magnetism, travelling through empty space at three hundred thousand kilometres every second. He had worked it out on paper. The mathematics was airtight. There was just one problem. Nobody had ever seen these waves. Nobody had ever made one in a laboratory, or caught one, or measured it. Maxwell had predicted an entire invisible universe, and then he had died without ever holding a single piece of evidence in his hand.

NARRATOR: Eight years later, in a darkened lecture hall in Germany, a young physicist held up a loop of copper wire. He watched a tiny spark jump across a gap in that wire — a spark that came from nowhere, with no battery and no wire connecting it to anything. And in that flicker of light, smaller than the width of a human hair, James Clerk Maxwell was finally, completely, vindicated.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how Heinrich Hertz proved that light is an electromagnetic wave — and how, in doing so, he accidentally built the foundation of the entire wireless world, while insisting the whole time that his discovery was, in his own words, of no use whatsoever.

NARRATOR: To understand why Maxwell's prediction was so daring, you have to understand what the world of physics looked like in the middle of the nineteenth century. By the eighteen sixties, scientists had spent decades carefully cataloguing the behaviour of electricity and magnetism. They knew that an electric current flowing through a wire created a magnetic field around it — that was Oersted and Ampere. They knew that a changing magnetic field could push electric charges into motion, creating a current — that was Michael Faraday's great discovery of electromagnetic induction. Electricity could make magnetism. Magnetism could make electricity. They were clearly connected. But they were still treated as two separate things, two cousins who happened to live next door to each other.

NARRATOR: James Clerk Maxwell looked at all of these scattered experimental results and did something that almost nobody else in history has managed to do. He saw, underneath all of it, a single unified structure. He took everything that was known about electricity and magnetism and compressed it into a set of equations. His great paper, A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field, was read to the Royal Society in eighteen sixty-four and published in the Philosophical Transactions the following year. In its original form there were twenty equations in twenty unknowns — a dense, forbidding wall of mathematics. Years later, a self-taught English engineer named Oliver Heaviside would boil these down to the four elegant equations we now call Maxwell's equations, and Hertz himself would help express them in modern vector form. But the physics was all there in Maxwell's original work.

NARRATOR: Now, the first two of those equations describe how electric and magnetic fields behave around charges and magnets. The first is Gauss's law, which says electric field lines begin and end on electric charges, and that the electric flux through a closed surface depends only on the charge inside it. Buried in that law is a constant called the permittivity of free space — remember that number, because it comes back to bite. The second is Gauss's law for magnetism, which says the magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero — that you can never isolate a single magnetic pole, a lone north or a lone south, because they always come in pairs. The third equation is Faraday's law — it says that a changing magnetic field creates a swirling electric field around it, an electric field that forms a closed loop rather than starting and ending on a charge. That was already known from experiment. But the fourth equation, the Ampère-Maxwell law, is where Maxwell did something genuinely new. It describes how a magnetic field is created. Everyone knew an electric current made a magnetic field. But Maxwell added a single extra term — a term that said a changing electric field, all by itself, with no current flowing at all, also creates a magnetic field. This term is called the displacement current, and it is the most important addition in the entire theory. And it carries a second constant, the permeability of free space.

QUESTION: Why does that one extra term matter so much?

NARRATOR: Because of what it makes possible. Think carefully about what those equations now say together. A changing magnetic field creates an electric field. And a changing electric field creates a magnetic field. So imagine you could somehow get an electric field changing in empty space. That changing electric field would generate a magnetic field. But that magnetic field is also changing — it's growing as the electric field grows — and a changing magnetic field generates an electric field. Which generates a magnetic field. Which generates an electric field. Each one continually regenerating the other, leapfrogging through empty space, neither one ever fading, because each is being constantly recreated by its partner. You have a self-sustaining disturbance — an electromagnetic wave — that can travel through a perfect vacuum, needing no air, no water, no medium of any kind. Just the two fields, holding each other up, racing forward forever.

NARRATOR: This was already a staggering claim. But Maxwell pushed further. He used his equations to calculate how fast such a wave would have to travel. And the answer was not some arbitrary number. The speed came out of two constants that physicists had already measured in the laboratory — two numbers that had nothing obvious to do with light at all. One is the permittivity of free space, which describes how strongly empty space responds to electric fields. The other is the permeability of free space, which describes how it responds to magnetic fields. Maxwell's equations said that the speed of an electromagnetic wave equals one divided by the square root of the permittivity multiplied by the permeability.

QUESTION: And what number did that give him?

NARRATOR: When Maxwell plugged in the measured values — values that came from experiments on charged jars and current-carrying coils, experiments that had nothing to do with optics — he got a speed of about three hundred and ten thousand kilometres per second. Now hold that number next to the best measured value for the speed of light at the time, which was about three hundred thousand kilometres per second. They matched. To within a percent or two, they were the same number. This was no coincidence — it could not possibly be a coincidence. Maxwell wrote, in a line that should be carved into the wall of every physics department, that we can scarcely avoid the inference that light consists of the transverse modulations of the same medium which is the cause of electric and magnetic phenomena.

NARRATOR: Read that again slowly. Maxwell was saying: light is an electromagnetic wave. The thing you see with your eyes is the same thing as the invisible disturbance in his equations. Electricity, magnetism, and light — three phenomena that humanity had treated as completely separate for thousands of years — were all the same thing. And there was one more consequence, perhaps the most thrilling of all. If light was just an electromagnetic wave of a particular frequency, then his equations allowed for waves of any frequency. Visible light, he realised, was only a narrow band — a thin slice the human eye happens to catch. There must be electromagnetic waves with frequencies far below visible light, and far above it — invisible waves, undiscovered, filling the universe, waiting to be found. Today we know the full family, running from the shortest wavelengths to the longest: gamma rays, X-rays, ultraviolet, visible light, infrared, microwaves, and radio waves. Every one of them is the same kind of wave Maxwell wrote down, the same self-sustaining ripple of electric and magnetic field, travelling at the one speed, c, in a vacuum. They differ only in frequency and wavelength. But in eighteen sixty-five, only visible light and infrared had ever been observed. All the rest were a prediction on a page.

NARRATOR: It was one of the most beautiful pieces of theoretical physics ever produced. And here is the part that's hard to believe. For more than a decade, almost nobody paid attention to it. The mathematics was too difficult. The idea of waves travelling through nothing at all offended physicists who were certain there had to be a medium — they called it the luminiferous aether, an invisible jelly filling all of space, and they spent years hunting for it. Maxwell's prediction of invisible electromagnetic waves sat there, on the page, untested. And then, in November of eighteen seventy-nine, the same year the prize was announced to test his ideas, Maxwell died. He was forty-eight years old. He never saw a single electromagnetic wave generated, detected, or measured. He died on faith in his own equations.

NARRATOR: Now we cross to Germany, and we meet the second hero of our story. Heinrich Rudolf Hertz was born in Hamburg in eighteen fifty-seven, the son of a lawyer who would become a senator of the city. He was, by every account, brilliant — fluent in languages, gifted with his hands, a natural in both the workshop and at the chalkboard. He went to study physics at the University of Berlin, where he came under the wing of one of the most powerful figures in German science: Hermann von Helmholtz. Helmholtz spotted Hertz's talent immediately and took him on as his personal assistant. Hertz earned his doctorate in eighteen eighty, ahead of schedule, with the highest distinction.

NARRATOR: And here is where the threads of our story first touch. In that very year, eighteen seventy-nine, the Berlin Academy of Sciences — under Helmholtz's influence — had announced a prize. The prize would go to whoever could produce solid experimental evidence for the kind of electromagnetic effects that Maxwell's theory predicted. Helmholtz, who admired Maxwell's work and wanted it tested, brought this challenge directly to his brilliant young student. He more or less said: Hertz, here is the great open question of our age. Go and settle it.

QUESTION: So did Hertz take the prize on immediately?

NARRATOR: No. And this is one of the most wonderful, human details in the whole story. Hertz looked carefully at what the experiment would actually require — the apparatus, the sensitivity, the timescales involved — and he concluded that it simply could not be done with the equipment available. The effects were too small, too fast, too delicate. He talked his own supervisor out of it. He convinced Helmholtz that the experiment was, for the moment, impossible. And so the great test of Maxwell's theory was set aside, and Hertz went off to build an ordinary academic career. He lectured at Kiel. Then, in eighteen eighty-five, at the age of twenty-eight, he was appointed full Professor of Experimental Physics at the Karlsruhe Polytechnic. And it was there, in that ordinary provincial laboratory, that lightning struck.

NARRATOR: It happened in the autumn of eighteen eighty-six, and it happened, like so many great discoveries, completely by accident. Hertz was working with a pair of coils — an apparatus for demonstrating electrical induction. He would build up a charge in a glass jar, a Leyden jar, and then let it discharge through one of the coils. The discharge happened in a violent, sudden burst. And one day, Hertz noticed something out of the corner of his eye that he could not explain. When that first coil discharged, a tiny spark jumped across a small gap in a second, completely separate coil sitting nearby. There was no wire connecting them. Nothing physical bridged the space between them. And yet, when he fired the first, the second answered with a spark of its own.

QUESTION: What could possibly carry the effect across that empty gap?

NARRATOR: That was exactly the question that seized Hertz. Something had travelled across open space from one coil to the other. And a physicist trained in Helmholtz's laboratory, who had once stared at Maxwell's prediction and turned away from it as impossible — that physicist suddenly realised what he might be looking at. The thing crossing the gap might be an electromagnetic wave. The very thing Maxwell had predicted. The very thing nobody had ever caught. The challenge he had walked away from six years earlier had just walked back into his laboratory and tapped him on the shoulder. And this time, he did not turn away.

NARRATOR: Over the next two years, from eighteen eighty-six to eighteen eighty-eight, Hertz conducted one of the most careful and beautiful experimental campaigns in the history of physics. To find these invisible waves, he first had to build a machine that could make them. And to understand what he built, you have to remember how Maxwell said electromagnetic waves are produced. They come from accelerating electric charges. If you take a charge and shake it back and forth — make it oscillate — it produces an oscillating electric field, which produces an oscillating magnetic field at right angles to it, and the whole self-sustaining package radiates outward as a wave. And here's the geometry that matters: in that wave the electric field and the magnetic field are perpendicular to each other, and both are perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. That's what makes it a transverse wave — and it's exactly why, later, Hertz would be able to polarise it. So Hertz needed to make charges oscillate back and forth, very fast.

NARRATOR: His solution is called a spark-gap oscillator, and it is a marvel of simplicity. He took two straight copper rods and laid them end to end in a line, each about one metre long, leaving a tiny gap between their inner ends — a gap of about seven and a half millimetres. On the far outer end of each rod he attached a large metal sphere, a zinc ball about thirty centimetres across. Then he connected the two rods to a Ruhmkorff induction coil — a device that could generate enormous voltages, around thirty thousand volts. Here is how it worked. The induction coil piled up electric charge on the rods, positive on one side, negative on the other, building the voltage higher and higher. The two zinc spheres acted like the plates of a capacitor, storing the charge. And the air in that tiny seven-and-a-half-millimetre gap acted like an insulator, holding the two sides apart — until the voltage got so high that the air could no longer hold. Then the air broke down, and a fat blue spark leapt across the gap.

QUESTION: And the spark is the wave?

NARRATOR: The spark is the engine of the wave. When that spark jumps, it suddenly connects the two charged sides, and all that stored charge comes rushing across. But it doesn't just flow across once and stop. It overshoots, and sloshes back, and overshoots again — the charge surges back and forth along the two-metre rod many millions of times in a tiny fraction of a second before it finally dies away. In the language of electronics, the rod and spheres form what's called an L-C circuit — an inductor and a capacitor — and such a circuit has a natural resonant frequency at which it wants to oscillate. For Hertz's apparatus, that frequency was enormous, in the range of tens of millions of cycles per second — what we would today call the V-H-F band, roughly the frequencies of broadcast television. And every time the charge sloshed back and forth, it was an accelerating charge. Which means, according to Maxwell, every oscillation flung an electromagnetic wave out into the room.

NARRATOR: So now Hertz had his transmitter — a machine that, with each crack of its spark, radiated invisible electromagnetic waves. But a wave you cannot see and cannot feel is useless unless you can detect it. He needed a receiver. And his receiver is the loop antenna — the copper ring that began our story.

NARRATOR: Hertz took a piece of copper wire and bent it into a loop — in his early work, a rectangle roughly a metre and a bit on its longest side; later, neat circular rings. He bent the wire almost all the way around, leaving just a tiny gap between the two ends, with a fine adjusting screw — a micrometer — so he could set that gap to be unbelievably small. That was the whole detector. No battery. No power source. Just a loop of wire with a microscopic gap. Here is the physics of why it works, and it is pure Faraday's law. When an electromagnetic wave passes through the loop, its oscillating magnetic field passes through the area of the loop. A changing magnetic field through a loop induces a voltage around that loop — that is exactly Faraday's law of induction. And Hertz tuned his loop so that it would resonate at the same frequency as his transmitter. At resonance, that induced voltage builds up just enough to push a spark across the tiny gap. So when the transmitter fired, and its waves washed across the room and through the receiving loop, a second spark — a faint, faint echo of the first — jumped across the gap in the ring.

QUESTION: And you could see this spark clearly?

NARRATOR: No — and this is one of the most important and least appreciated facts in the whole story. Forget the romantic image of a glowing ring lighting up a room. Hertz's receiver sparks were almost nothing. He described them as scarcely a hundredth of a millimetre long, lasting about a millionth of a second. They were so faint that he had to switch off all the lights, sit in total darkness, and let his eyes adjust for many minutes before he could see them at all. He would lean in close, in the blackness, squinting at the gap in the wire, waiting for the tiniest possible flicker. It was painstaking, eye-straining, patient work. But each one of those tiny flickers was a piece of an electromagnetic wave that had travelled across his laboratory through empty space. Maxwell's invisible waves, made just barely visible.

NARRATOR: And once Hertz could detect the waves, he could begin to interrogate them. He set out to prove, point by point, that these things behaved exactly the way Maxwell said light should behave. He found that the sparks got weaker as he moved the loop farther from the transmitter — the waves spread out and weakened with distance. He found he could block them with a sheet of metal, but they passed straight through a wooden door — just as you'd expect of a wave that interacts with conductors. He found he could reflect them off a large zinc sheet, like light off a mirror. He could even refract them, bending their path by sending them through an enormous prism made of solid pitch, a block of asphalt over a metre tall. And crucially, he found they were polarised. When he rotated his receiving loop to a certain orientation, the spark died completely; rotate it back, and the spark returned. That only happens for a transverse wave — a wave whose oscillations are at right angles to its direction of travel. Exactly what Maxwell predicted. One by one, every property of light — reflection, refraction, diffraction, polarisation — Hertz reproduced with his invisible waves. They weren't just like light. They were light, in every way that mattered, just at a frequency too low for the human eye.

NARRATOR: But the crown jewel — the measurement that connects everything back to Maxwell's lonely prediction — was the speed. Hertz had to prove that these waves travelled at the speed of light. And to do that, he used one of the most elegant tricks in all of wave physics: the standing wave.

QUESTION: How do you measure the speed of something invisible that crosses a room in a few billionths of a second?

NARRATOR: You don't try to time it directly — that's hopeless, it's far too fast. Instead, you use interference. Hertz set up his spark-gap transmitter at one end of his lecture hall. At the other end, about twelve metres away, he stood up a large zinc sheet, a metal reflector. Now think about what happens. The transmitter sends a wave travelling toward the zinc sheet. The sheet reflects it straight back. So in the space between the transmitter and the sheet, you have two waves of the same frequency travelling in opposite directions — the outgoing wave and the reflected wave — passing through each other. And when two such waves overlap, they create what's called a standing wave: a fixed pattern in space that doesn't travel. At certain points, called nodes, the two waves always cancel each other out perfectly — there the field is dead, zero, forever still. Halfway between the nodes, at points called antinodes, the two waves always add together — there the field swings to its maximum.

NARRATOR: And here is the beautiful part. The distance between one node and the next node is exactly half a wavelength. It's a fixed, frozen ruler laid out in the air, and its spacing is set by the wavelength of the wave. So Hertz took his little copper detector loop and walked it slowly through the space between the transmitter and the zinc sheet, watching the tiny spark in the dark. At the antinodes, the spark blazed as bright as it ever got. At the nodes, it vanished — dead silence, no spark at all. By marking where the sparks died and measuring the distance between those dead spots, Hertz was directly measuring half the wavelength of his invisible waves. He found the wavelength to be roughly four metres.

NARRATOR: Now he had everything he needed, because of the single most fundamental equation in all of wave physics: the speed of any wave equals its frequency multiplied by its wavelength. He had the wavelength — about four metres — measured directly from the spacing of his standing-wave nodes. And he had the frequency, which he calculated from the known electrical properties of his oscillator, the inductance and capacitance of those rods and spheres. He multiplied the frequency by the wavelength. And out came a speed in the neighbourhood of three hundred thousand kilometres per second. The speed of light. The exact number Maxwell had pulled out of his equations, from constants measured in jars and coils, more than twenty years earlier — now confirmed by sparks jumping across a copper ring in a darkened German lecture hall.

NARRATOR: Maxwell was right. Light is an electromagnetic wave. The invisible waves filling the universe were real, and you could make them, and catch them, and measure them. In November of eighteen eighty-seven, Hertz wrote up his first results and sent them to his old mentor Helmholtz at the Berlin Academy — the very man who had handed him this challenge eight years before. Helmholtz's reply was a single word, scrawled in delight: Bravo. The student had solved the problem the master had set him.

NARRATOR: Now, in the interest of honesty — because this is physics, not mythology — I have to tell you that the real story was messier than the clean version you'll read in a textbook. Hertz's first attempts to pin down the speed gave troubling results. At one early stage he thought the waves might travel infinitely fast. Worse, one of his published measurements suggested that the waves travelling through the air moved much faster than waves travelling along a wire — a discrepancy that actually seemed to contradict Maxwell's theory, and that puzzled physicists for years before it was sorted out by later, more careful experiments. So the idea that Hertz cleanly measured the speed of light to be exactly three times ten to the eight metres per second on the first go is a tidied-up retrospective. What Hertz really proved, beyond any doubt, was something arguably more powerful than a single precise number. He proved that these invisible waves travelled at roughly the speed of light, and that they shared every single property of light — reflection, refraction, polarisation, interference. It was the whole package together, not any one measurement, that nailed the case shut. Maxwell's electromagnetic theory of light was no longer a beautiful guess. It was established fact.

NARRATOR: There's another extraordinary thing that happened during these same experiments, almost as a footnote, that I have to mention. While Hertz was peering at his receiver sparks in the dark, he noticed something annoying. When the bright light from the transmitter spark was allowed to shine on the receiver's spark gap, the receiver spark jumped more easily — it could leap a slightly wider gap. He investigated, and found it was specifically the ultraviolet light from the transmitter doing it. He even found that a sheet of glass blocked the effect, while a slab of quartz let it through — because glass absorbs ultraviolet and quartz does not. Hertz wrote that the effect was striking and yet totally puzzling, published it as a curiosity, and moved on, because it had nothing to do with his main quest. He had no idea what he was looking at. He had just observed, for the first time in history, the photoelectric effect — the phenomenon that, eighteen years later, Albert Einstein would explain by proposing that light comes in particles called photons, the work that won Einstein his Nobel Prize and helped launch quantum physics. Hertz brushed past the doorway to the quantum world without ever stepping through it.

QUESTION: So Hertz had proven Maxwell right, and stumbled onto quantum physics. What did he think his discovery was good for?

NARRATOR: Nothing. According to the famous story, when a student at Karlsruhe asked Hertz what use these electromagnetic waves could possibly have, he is said to have replied: It's of no use whatsoever. This is just an experiment that proves Maestro Maxwell was right. We have these mysterious electromagnetic waves that we cannot see with the naked eye, but they are there. And when pressed on what would come of it, he supposedly answered: Nothing, I guess. Now, I have to be straight with you — historians flag this quote as probably apocryphal. There's no rock-solid primary source written in Hertz's own hand. But everyone who knew him agrees it captures his genuine attitude perfectly. Hertz was a pure scientist. He wanted to know if Maxwell was right. He had found out that Maxwell was right. As far as he was concerned, the job was done. The idea of using these waves to send messages across the world simply did not interest him.

NARRATOR: And then the story takes its final, tragic, and triumphant turn. In eighteen eighty-nine, Hertz moved on to a professorship at Bonn. But within a few years his health began to collapse. He suffered from terrible migraines and a painful illness in his bones and blood vessels — a rare vascular disease that the doctors of his day could not treat. He endured operation after operation. He wrote to his parents, with extraordinary grace: If anything should happen to me, you should not be sorrowful, but rather feel some pride. On the first of January, eighteen ninety-four — New Year's Day — Heinrich Hertz died. He was thirty-six years old. Like Maxwell before him, he died young, and he died without ever seeing what his discovery would become.

NARRATOR: But this time, the torch passed almost instantly. In the days after Hertz's death, obituaries and accounts of his work spread across Europe. And in Italy, a twenty-year-old named Guglielmo Marconi read about Hertz's electromagnetic waves. Where Hertz had seen only a beautiful confirmation of a theory, Marconi saw something else entirely. He saw a telegraph with no wires. He reportedly thought: if these waves can cross a laboratory, why not a valley? Why not a country? Why not an ocean? He told his father he had a feeling these waves would soon give humanity a powerful new means of communication. And he was right. Marconi took Hertz's spark-gap transmitter and loop receiver — the very same physics — and relentlessly improved them: better detectors, taller antennas, grounded circuits, tuned resonance. By eighteen ninety-six he was in London with a patent. And on the twelfth of December, nineteen oh-one, Guglielmo Marconi sent a wireless signal — three faint dots, the letter S in Morse code — across more than three thousand kilometres of open Atlantic ocean, from Cornwall in England to Newfoundland, on the eastern edge of North America. The thing Hertz said was of no use whatsoever had just connected two continents through thin air.

NARRATOR: Everything came from that copper ring in the dark. Radio. Television. Radar that won wars. Microwave ovens. Mobile phones. Wi-Fi. G-P-S satellites guiding ships and aircraft and the phone in your pocket. Bluetooth. Every wireless signal that has ever carried a voice, an image, or a message through empty space is a direct descendant of the waves Hertz first generated and caught at Karlsruhe. We honoured him in the most fitting way imaginable. In nineteen thirty, the international community made the unit of frequency — one cycle per second — the hertz. Every time you read that your radio station broadcasts at a certain number of megahertz, or that your processor runs at gigahertz, you are saying his name. The man who thought his discovery was useless gave his name to the heartbeat of the entire electronic age.

NARRATOR: And so the circle closes. James Clerk Maxwell, working alone with nothing but mathematics, predicted that light was an electromagnetic wave and that an invisible spectrum of such waves must exist — and he died without proof. Heinrich Hertz, working with two copper rods, a spark, and a ring of wire, generated those invisible waves, detected them, reflected them, polarised them, and measured their speed to be the speed of light — and he died thinking it amounted to nothing. Between the two of them, dying twenty years and a few hundred miles apart, both convinced their work was finished and largely unappreciated, they handed the human race the entire wireless world.

NARRATOR: In the New South Wales H-S-C Physics course, in Module Seven, The Nature of Light, you are asked to explain how a spark-gap oscillator and a loop antenna verified the predicted speed of electromagnetic waves — to explain Maxwell's contribution to electromagnetism, including the unification of electricity and magnetism, the prediction of electromagnetic waves, and the prediction of their velocity, and to relate the production and propagation of those waves to Maxwell's theory. You are asked to know that every form of electromagnetic radiation, from gamma rays to radio waves, travels at that one constant speed in a vacuum. This is that story. The spark-gap oscillator is the transmitter, where charge oscillates across a gap to radiate waves from accelerating charges. The loop antenna is the receiver, where Faraday's law induces a spark from the passing wave. And the standing-wave measurement between transmitter and reflector — frequency multiplied by wavelength — is how Hertz confirmed that the speed of these waves matched the speed of light that Maxwell calculated from one over the square root of the permittivity times the permeability of free space. When the exam asks you how we know light is an electromagnetic wave, this is the evidence. This is the experiment. This is Heinrich Hertz, in the dark, proving Maxwell right.
