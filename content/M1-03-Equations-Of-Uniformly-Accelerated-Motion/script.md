NARRATOR: Today we get the equations, the four equations of uniformly accelerated motion that you will use in more exam questions than almost any other tool in the course. Students call them the suvat equations, after the five quantities they connect, and the single skill that separates a confident student from a flustered one is not memorising them, it's knowing which one to reach for. By the end of today you'll know all four, you'll have a foolproof method for choosing the right one every time, and you'll be able to handle the headline application, an object thrown straight up under gravity, signs and all. But first, as always, a quick recap, because today's equations are built directly on the last two episodes.

NARRATOR: Two episodes ago we set the vocabulary: displacement, velocity, acceleration, the vector trio D-V-A, each with a direction; and we chose a positive direction and stuck to it. Last episode we put motion on graphs, and the key result for today was the velocity-time graph: its gradient is the acceleration, and the area under it is the displacement. I told you then that the equations we meet today come straight out of that area, and they do. For uniformly accelerated motion the velocity-time graph is a straight sloping line, and the area under it is a trapezium. The area of that trapezium, average height times width, is one half the sum of the initial and final velocities, times the time, and that is literally one of today's four equations: displacement equals one half, times the quantity initial velocity plus final velocity, times time. So these equations aren't new magic; they're the graph from last episode, written as algebra. Hold that, it makes them far less intimidating.

QUESTION: Quick check before the new material. On a velocity-time graph, what does the gradient give you, and what does the area under the line give you? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: The gradient of a velocity-time graph gives the acceleration, and the area under it gives the displacement. That area, for the straight line of constant acceleration, is exactly where today's equations come from. Now let's meet the five quantities and the four equations.

NARRATOR: First, the five quantities, because the word suvat is your memory hook and it names them. S is the displacement, the vector change in position. U is the initial velocity, the velocity at the start, the "u" for the velocity you begin with. V is the final velocity, the velocity at the end. A is the acceleration, which for these equations must be constant. And T is the time taken. S, U, V, A, T. Suvat. Write those five letters down at the start of any motion problem, because the entire method is: figure out which three you're given, what you're asked to find, and which one is missing, and then pick the equation that doesn't contain the missing one. That's the whole game. Five quantities, and every problem hands you three, asks for a fourth, and leaves one out.

NARRATOR: Now the four equations, and crucially, which quantity each one leaves out, because that's how you choose. The list is in Listing one. The first equation: final velocity equals initial velocity plus acceleration times time. V equals u plus a t. Look at it, it has v, u, a, and t, but no s, no displacement. So you use it when displacement is the quantity you neither have nor want. The second equation: displacement equals initial velocity times time, plus one half acceleration times time squared. S equals u t plus one half a t squared. It has s, u, a, and t, but no v, no final velocity. The third: final velocity squared equals initial velocity squared plus two times acceleration times displacement. V squared equals u squared plus two a s. It has v, u, a, and s, but no t, no time. And the fourth, the one straight from the graph's trapezium: displacement equals one half, times initial plus final velocity, times time. S equals one half, u plus v, times t. It has s, u, v, and t, but no a, no acceleration. Four equations, each missing exactly one of the five quantities: the first has no s, the second no v, the third no t, the fourth no a.

NARRATOR: So here is the method, and it never fails. Step one: write down suvat, the five letters, as a little list. Step two: read the problem and fill in the three values you're given, with their signs, using your chosen positive direction. Step three: identify the quantity you're asked to find, and the one quantity that is neither given nor asked for, the one that's missing. Step four: choose the equation that does not contain that missing quantity. Then substitute and solve. That's it. You never have to guess. The missing variable points you straight to the right equation. Let me say the matching out loud once more: missing displacement, use v equals u plus a t; missing final velocity, use s equals u t plus half a t squared; missing time, use v squared equals u squared plus two a s; missing acceleration, use s equals half, u plus v, times t.

QUESTION: Let me have you use the method right now. A car accelerates from ten metres per second to thirty metres per second over a distance of one hundred metres. You're asked for the acceleration. Which of the five quantities is missing, and so which equation do you use? Pause the player, decide, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. List suvat. You're given u, the initial velocity, ten; v, the final velocity, thirty; and s, the displacement, one hundred. You're asked for a, the acceleration. So the quantity that is neither given nor asked for is t, the time, it's missing. The equation with no t is the third one, v squared equals u squared plus two a s. So you use that, rearrange for a, and solve. The full working is in Listing two, and the answer comes out at four metres per second per second. Notice you didn't have to try equations at random, the missing quantity, time, told you immediately which one to use. That is the method, and it works on every single problem.

NARRATOR: Let me do that worked example fully, because the marks are in the method. The car: u is ten metres per second, v is thirty metres per second, s is one hundred metres, find a. Time is missing, so use v squared equals u squared plus two a s. Substitute: thirty squared equals ten squared plus two times a times one hundred. That's nine hundred equals one hundred plus two hundred a. Subtract one hundred from both sides: eight hundred equals two hundred a. Divide: a equals four metres per second per second. Done. Write the equation, substitute with units, rearrange, solve. The marker rewards each of those steps, and the full layout is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Let me show you where two of these come from, because understanding the derivation makes them impossible to forget, and it's the velocity-time graph from last episode doing all the work. Picture the straight sloping velocity-time line for constant acceleration, starting at the initial velocity u and rising to the final velocity v over time t. The gradient of that line is the acceleration, rise over run, which is v minus u, over t. Rearrange that and you get v equals u plus a t, the first equation, just the gradient definition turned around. Now the displacement is the area under that line, which is a trapezium. You can find the trapezium's area two ways: as the average height times the width, which gives one half, u plus v, times t, that's the fourth equation; or as a rectangle of height u plus a triangle on top of height a t, which works out to u t plus one half a t squared, the second equation. So three of the four equations fall straight out of the graph, gradient gives the first, the trapezium area gives the second and fourth, and the third comes from combining them to eliminate time. The derivations are set out in Listing four. You don't need to reproduce them in most exams, but seeing them means the equations stop being four things to memorise and become one graph you understand.

NARRATOR: Let me do a second worked example, a deceleration, because the signs of acceleration matter and this is a common type. A car travelling at thirty metres per second brakes uniformly and comes to rest in five seconds; find the braking distance. Take the direction of motion as positive. Knowns: u is thirty, v is zero because it comes to rest, t is five. We want s, and the acceleration a is missing, we're not given it and not asked for it, so use the equation with no a: s equals one half, u plus v, times t. Substitute: s equals one half, times thirty plus zero, times five, which is one half times thirty times five, seventy-five metres. The braking distance is seventy-five metres. Notice we didn't even need to find the acceleration, the missing-variable method took us straight to the equation that skips it. If the question had also asked for the acceleration, it's negative: using v equals u plus a t, zero equals thirty plus a times five, so a is minus six metres per second per second, negative because the car is slowing while moving in the positive direction, exactly the sign logic from episode one.

NARRATOR: One practical tip on the two equations that both give displacement, because students freeze choosing between them. Use s equals one half, u plus v, times t when you know both velocities and the time but not the acceleration, it skips acceleration entirely. Use s equals u t plus one half a t squared when you know the acceleration and the time but not the final velocity. They're not rivals, they're for different given-information; the missing-variable method picks for you, whichever quantity you're missing, acceleration or final velocity, points to one of the two. And if you ever have a choice because you've already computed the missing quantity, prefer the equation that uses only the original given data, not a value you calculated, so that an early slip doesn't cascade into the rest of your answer. That's good exam hygiene: build each result from the question's data where you can, not from your own intermediate numbers.

NARRATOR: Now there's one condition on all four equations that you must never forget, and it's a common exam trap: they only work when the acceleration is constant. Uniformly accelerated motion means constant acceleration. If the acceleration changes during the motion, none of these four equations applies to the whole journey, you'd have to break it into segments where the acceleration is constant in each. So before you reach for suvat, check the acceleration is constant. The good news: the most important application in the course, motion under gravity, has exactly constant acceleration, so suvat is tailor-made for it.

NARRATOR: Motion under gravity, this is the headline application, so let's do it carefully. Near the Earth's surface, ignoring air resistance, every object falls with the same constant acceleration due to gravity, about nine point eight metres per second per second, directed downward. We call it g. This is why suvat shines: the acceleration is constant and known, it's g. The only thing you have to get right is the signs, and this is where last episode's sign convention earns its keep. Choose a positive direction and commit to it. The standard choice for a thrown object: take up as positive. Then a thrown ball's initial velocity, going up, is positive, but the acceleration due to gravity, pointing down, is negative, minus nine point eight. That single sign, gravity is negative when up is positive, is the thing students get wrong, so decide your direction first and apply it to every quantity.

NARRATOR: Let me work the classic thrown-ball problem in full, because it appears constantly and it tests the signs. The full working is in Listing three. A ball is thrown straight up at twenty metres per second. Find, first, the maximum height it reaches, and second, the total time until it returns to the thrower's hand. Take up as positive. So u, the initial velocity, is plus twenty. The acceleration a is minus nine point eight, because gravity points down and down is negative. Now, the maximum height. Here's the insight that makes it solvable: at the very top of its flight, for an instant, the ball is momentarily at rest, its velocity is zero, before it starts falling back. So at the top, v equals zero. List suvat for the trip up: u is twenty, v is zero, a is minus nine point eight, find s, and time is missing, we don't have it and don't need it. Missing time means use v squared equals u squared plus two a s. Substitute: zero squared equals twenty squared plus two times minus nine point eight times s. That's zero equals four hundred minus nineteen point six s. So nineteen point six s equals four hundred, and s equals about twenty point four metres. That's the maximum height.

NARRATOR: Now the total time of flight, back to the hand. There are two clean ways, and I'll show the one that drills the signs. Consider the whole journey, up and back down to the same height it started. Its displacement over the whole trip is zero, because it ends where it began, that's the displacement-is-zero-for-a-round-trip idea from episode one. So for the whole flight: u is plus twenty, a is minus nine point eight, s is zero, find t. Final velocity v isn't given, so it's missing, use the equation with no v: s equals u t plus one half a t squared. Substitute: zero equals twenty t plus one half times minus nine point eight times t squared, that's zero equals twenty t minus four point nine t squared. Factor out t: zero equals t times, twenty minus four point nine t. So either t equals zero, that's the launch instant, or twenty minus four point nine t equals zero, giving t equals about four point one seconds. The total time of flight is four point one seconds. Notice the symmetry: it takes about two seconds to rise and about two seconds to fall, the up and down halves are mirror images, which is a useful check.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. When a ball is thrown straight up, what is its velocity at the very highest point, and what is its acceleration at that same highest point? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: At the highest point, the ball's velocity is zero, it's momentarily at rest as it changes from going up to coming down. But its acceleration is still nine point eight metres per second per second downward, gravity never switches off. That catches people: they think because the velocity is zero, the acceleration is zero too. No. The velocity is instantaneously zero, but gravity is still pulling, the acceleration is unchanged at g downward, which is exactly why the ball doesn't hang there, it immediately starts falling. Velocity zero, acceleration still g. If you separated those two, you've got the most important subtlety in projectile-style problems, and it's a guaranteed mark.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for a typical "calculate" question, because there's a method the marker rewards even on a numerical question. Take "a stone is dropped from rest from a cliff and hits the ground after three seconds; find the height of the cliff." The strong answer lays out: take down as positive, so a equals plus nine point eight; list the knowns, u equals zero because it's dropped from rest, t equals three seconds, a equals nine point eight, find s, with v missing; choose the equation with no v, s equals u t plus half a t squared; substitute, s equals zero times three plus half times nine point eight times three squared, which is half times nine point eight times nine, about forty-four metres. The marks are awarded for stating the sign convention, listing the known quantities, selecting the correct equation with a reason, substituting with units, and giving the answer to sensible significant figures. That layout, suvat list, equation choice, substitution, answer, is the band-six structure for any motion calculation.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the difference. A weak answer just writes "s equals half a t squared equals forty-four metres" with no setup. Even if the number is right, it risks marks because it doesn't show the sign convention, doesn't justify the equation choice, and doesn't state that u is zero because the stone was dropped. If the student had picked the wrong equation, there'd be no working to earn method marks. The strong answer is bulletproof because every step is shown; the weak answer is fragile because it's just an answer. Show the suvat list and the reasoning, always, even when you can see the answer.

NARRATOR: A couple of common traps, gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: using a suvat equation when the acceleration isn't constant, they only apply to uniform acceleration. Trap two: sign errors, especially making gravity positive when you've taken up as positive, decide your direction and apply it to every quantity including g. Trap three: thinking the velocity being zero at the top means the acceleration is zero, it's still g. Trap four: forgetting that "dropped" or "from rest" means the initial velocity u is zero, that's a given value you must supply yourself. Trap five: forgetting the displacement of a complete up-and-down trip is zero, which is what lets you find the total time. Avoid those five and motion-under-gravity problems are routine.

QUESTION: Question four. Explain why the four equations of motion can be applied to an object in free fall under gravity, but cannot be applied to a car whose acceleration is increasing. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The four equations of uniformly accelerated motion are valid only when the acceleration is constant. Three marks: the equations require constant acceleration; free fall has constant acceleration g; the car's changing acceleration violates the condition.
