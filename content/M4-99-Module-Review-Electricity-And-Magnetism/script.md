QUESTION: Let's start with pure retrieval, no calculation. Name the three ways an object can be charged. Then state Kirchhoff's two circuit laws. Pause the player, write all five, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The three charging methods are friction, rubbing transfers electrons; conduction, contact giving the same sign; and induction, no contact giving the opposite sign. Kirchhoff's two laws: the current law, the currents into a junction equal the currents out, which is conservation of charge; and the voltage law, the voltages around a loop sum to zero, which is conservation of energy. Friction, conduction, induction; current law and voltage law. A quick way to keep the charging methods straight: friction and conduction both involve touching, friction by rubbing two insulators, conduction by contact with a charged conductor, while induction is the odd one out that works at a distance and leaves the opposite sign. If any were slow, that's the gap to drill, and they're all in the registry in Listing three. Remember the conduction-versus-induction sign rule especially: contact gives the same sign, induction the opposite.

NARRATOR: Now an integrated electrostatics problem combining the field and the force, it's in Listing two. Two parallel plates are separated by zero point zero two metres with a voltage of four hundred volts between them. First find the field strength, then the force on a charge of five times ten to the minus six coulombs placed between them. The field is the voltage over the separation, E equals V over d, four hundred over zero point zero two, twenty thousand newtons per coulomb. The force is then F equals E times q, twenty thousand times five times ten to the minus six, which is zero point one newtons. Notice the structure: E equals V over d gives the field from the plates, then F equals E q gives the force on a specific charge in it. That two-step, field first, then force, is the standard parallel-plate question, and it's exactly the setup you'll accelerate charged particles with in Module Six.

NARRATOR: Let me push into Coulomb's law with the inverse-square reasoning, because proportional thinking is heavily tested. Suppose two point charges experience a certain force. If you triple one charge and halve the distance between them, what happens to the force? Take it in two steps. Tripling one charge multiplies the force by three, because force is proportional to the product of the charges. Halving the distance multiplies the force by one over a half squared, that's four, because of the inverse square. Combine them: three times four is twelve. So the force becomes twelve times larger. The trap is to forget to square the distance factor, it's four, not two, for halving. That inverse-square dominance is the heart of Coulomb's law, and it's in Listing two.

QUESTION: Now a circuits retrieval. Distinguish, in one sentence each, between electric current and voltage, giving the equation for each. Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. Electric current is the rate of flow of charge past a point, I equals q over t, measured in amperes. Voltage, or potential difference, is the energy transferred per unit charge, V equals W over q, measured in volts. Current is about flow, how much charge per second; voltage is about energy, how much energy per charge. They are completely different quantities, and saying "voltage pushes the current" is the weak answer that loses marks. Current is flow, I equals q over t; voltage is energy per charge, V equals W over q. That distinction is the single most tested conceptual point in the circuits half of the module.

NARRATOR: Now a full network problem, the core circuit skill, worked in Listing two. A twelve-volt supply is connected to a three-ohm resistor in series with a parallel pair of six-ohm resistors. Find the total resistance, the supply current, and the voltage across the parallel section. First reduce the parallel pair: two six-ohm resistors in parallel give one over the equivalent equals one over six plus one over six, two over six, one third, so the equivalent is three ohms. Now it's a three-ohm resistor in series with that three-ohm section, total six ohms. The supply current is the supply voltage over the total resistance, twelve over six, two amperes, which flows through the series resistor. The voltage across the parallel section is the current times its resistance, two times three, six volts. Check with Kirchhoff's voltage law: the series resistor drops two times three, six volts, and six plus six equals twelve, the full supply. The method, reduce, find the current, work back, with a conservation check, is the universal recipe, and it's in the decision guide in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Let me show the weak-versus-strong contrast on a classic extended response, "compare current and voltage in series and parallel circuits", because structure earns the marks. The weak answer: "in series it adds up and in parallel it splits". Too vague to score well, it never says which quantity does what. The strong, band-six answer: in a series circuit there is a single path, so the current is the same through every component while the voltage divides across them in proportion to resistance, and the total resistance is the sum of the resistances. In a parallel circuit there are multiple paths, so the voltage is the same across each branch while the current divides between them, and the total resistance from the reciprocal sum is less than the smallest branch. The marks live in stating, for each, which quantity is constant, which divides, and how the resistances combine. Say which quantity is constant and which divides, every time, that's the structure, and it's in the registry in Listing three.

QUESTION: Now a magnetism retrieval, because direction is everything here. A long straight wire carries a conventional current directed to your right, horizontally. Using the right-hand grip rule, state the direction of the magnetic field directly above the wire, and directly below it. Pause the player, work it out with your right hand, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Point the thumb of your right hand to the right, along the current; your fingers curl around the wire. Above the wire, your fingers point out of the page towards you; below the wire, they point into the page away from you. So the field is out of the page above the wire and into the page below it. The field forms complete circles around the wire, and which way it points at any spot is set by the grip rule, thumb along the current, fingers curling the way of the field. Getting this right is a recurring exam mark and it's essential for the motor effect next year. The grip rule is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Let me cross the central conceptual distinctions of the module, because the exam tests them relentlessly, and they're all in the registry in Listing three. Charge versus current versus voltage: charge is the property, current is its rate of flow, voltage is energy per charge, three different things. Field versus force: the field is force per unit charge, a property of the location; the force is what a specific charge feels in it. Conduction versus induction: contact giving the same sign versus no contact giving the opposite sign. Ohmic versus non-ohmic: constant resistance, straight I-V line, versus changing resistance, curved line. Series versus parallel: same current, voltage divides, versus same voltage, current divides. Wire field versus solenoid field: concentric circles versus a bar-magnet pattern with poles. Each of those is a one-mark discriminator, and knowing which side is which is what lifts a band four to a band six.

QUESTION: A rapid-fire conceptual check, five of them. One: charging by induction gives the same or opposite sign to the charging object? Two: is the filament lamp ohmic or non-ohmic? NARRATOR: Here are the answers. One: induction gives the opposite sign to the charging object, because the charges separate and earthing removes the like charge. NARRATOR: Let me work one more integrated circuit problem that brings in power and energy, since the energy story is half the module, it's in Listing two. A heater of resistance twenty ohms is connected to a two hundred and forty volt supply. Find the current it draws, the power it dissipates, and the energy it converts in five minutes. The current is the voltage over the resistance, I equals V over R, two hundred and forty over twenty, twelve amperes. The power is voltage times current, P equals V I, two hundred and forty times twelve, two thousand eight hundred and eighty watts; or you could use P equals V squared over R, two hundred and forty squared over twenty, the same two thousand eight hundred and eighty watts. The energy in five minutes, that's three hundred seconds, is power times time, W equals P t, two thousand eight hundred and eighty times three hundred, eight hundred and sixty-four thousand joules. The skill is choosing the power formula that fits what you're given, and remembering energy is power times time. That choice is in the decision guide in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Let me give a weak-versus-strong on the magnetism side, "explain how a piece of iron becomes magnetised", because the domain answer is so often done poorly. The weak answer: "the iron lines up with the magnet". It gestures at the truth but earns little. The strong, band-six answer: the iron contains many tiny regions called domains, each acting as a small magnet because its atoms' fields are aligned; in unmagnetised iron these domains point in random directions so their fields cancel and there is no overall magnetism; when the iron is placed in an external magnetic field the domains rotate to align with that field, and their now-aligned fields add together to give the iron a strong net magnetism. The marks are for domains as small magnets, random orientation cancelling, and alignment with an external field adding up. Name the domains and the random-to-aligned change, that's the difference between bands.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the single deepest thread of the whole module, the one that ties it to the rest of physics: fields and conservation. Two ideas have done all the work. The first is the field: a charge sets up an electric field that exerts a force on other charges, and a current sets up a magnetic field that will, next year, exert forces too. Fields let forces act across empty space, and the same field-line language describes both the electric and the magnetic case, lines showing direction and strength, never crossing. The second idea is conservation: charge is conserved, which gives Kirchhoff's current law and governs how current splits and recombines; energy is conserved, which gives Kirchhoff's voltage law and the whole power-and-energy story. These are the same conservation laws you met in Dynamics and in thermodynamics, now running the circuits. So when a problem looks unfamiliar, ask two questions, "what field is acting, and what force does it produce?", and "where is the charge and energy going, and what's conserved?". Those two questions, fields and conservation, will open almost anything in this module, and they're the spine of Module Six too.

NARRATOR: Let me also re-surface the practical investigations, because the exam often asks you to design or evaluate one, and they thread through the module. For electrostatics, charging rods by friction and testing attraction and repulsion, and using an electroscope to show induction without contact. For circuits, the central experiment is measuring a component's current-voltage characteristic, ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel, and plotting current against voltage, a straight line for an ohmic conductor, a curve for a filament lamp. For networks, confirming that series currents are equal everywhere while voltages add, and that parallel branches share the voltage while currents add. For magnetism, mapping fields with iron filings or compasses, showing the circular field around a current-carrying wire and reversing it with the current, and building a nail-and-coil electromagnet to test how its strength depends on current and turns. The recurring evaluation points: correct meter placement and why, and that the magnetic field grows with current and turns. Knowing the variable changed, the quantity measured, and the key point for each is exactly what an investigation question rewards.

NARRATOR: Let me do one fully conceptual integrated question, because not every exam item is a calculation. Consider this: a charged balloon held near a thin stream of water bends the stream towards it, and a current-carrying wire held near a compass deflects the needle. What do these two observations have in common, at the level of physics? Both are demonstrations of a field exerting a force at a distance without contact. The balloon's charge sets up an electric field that polarises the water and pulls it, an electric force; the wire's current sets up a magnetic field that exerts a force on the compass's magnetic poles, a magnetic force. In both cases an invisible field, created by charge, reaches across a gap and produces a force, which is the unifying theme of the whole module. Recognising that electric and magnetic effects are both field-mediated forces is exactly the conceptual maturity the exam rewards, and it's the thread into Module Six, where electric and magnetic fields turn out to be two aspects of one thing.

QUESTION: Question one. State whether each of the following is conserved, and where relevant name the law: electric charge in a circuit junction, and energy around a circuit loop. Then state the basic rule for the force between two magnetic poles. Three marks. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Electric charge is conserved at a circuit junction: the current flowing in equals the current flowing out, which is Kirchhoff's current law. Energy is conserved around a circuit loop: the voltages around the loop sum to zero, the supply equalling the total of the drops, which is Kirchhoff's voltage law. For magnetic poles, like poles repel and unlike poles attract. Three marks: charge conserved at a junction, Kirchhoff's current law; energy conserved around a loop, Kirchhoff's voltage law; and like-repel, unlike-attract for poles. The trap is not naming the conservation principle behind each Kirchhoff law.

QUESTION: Question two. A charge of one hundred and twenty coulombs flows through a resistor in forty seconds, and the potential difference across it is twelve volts. Calculate the current, the resistance, and the power dissipated. Four marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. The current is the charge over the time, I equals q over t, one hundred and twenty over forty, three amperes. The resistance is the voltage over the current, R equals V over I, twelve over three, four ohms. The power is voltage times current, P equals V I, twelve times three, thirty-six watts. Four marks: the current of three amperes from I equals q over t; the resistance of four ohms from Ohm's law; the power of thirty-six watts from P equals V I; with correct working. The trap is mixing the formulas; keep current as charge per time, resistance as voltage over current, power as voltage times current.

QUESTION: Question three. Two point charges exert a force of twelve newtons on each other. Calculate the new force if one charge is doubled and the distance between them is also doubled, explaining your reasoning. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By Coulomb's law the force is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance. Doubling one charge multiplies the force by two. Doubling the distance multiplies the force by one over two squared, that is one quarter. Combining, the force is multiplied by two times one quarter, which is one half. So the new force is one half of twelve newtons, six newtons. Three marks: doubling the charge gives a factor of two; doubling the distance gives a factor of one quarter from the inverse square; and combining to six newtons. The trap is thinking the two doublings cancel; the distance is squared, so it wins.

QUESTION: Question four. A nine-volt supply is connected to a two-ohm resistor in series with a parallel combination of a three-ohm and a six-ohm resistor. Calculate the total resistance of the circuit and the current drawn from the supply. Four marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. First reduce the parallel combination: one over the equivalent equals one over three plus one over six, which is two over six plus one over six, three over six, one half, so the parallel section is two ohms. This is in series with the two-ohm resistor, so the total resistance is two plus two, four ohms. The current drawn from the supply is the supply voltage over the total resistance, nine over four, two point two five amperes. Four marks: reducing the parallel section to two ohms; adding the series resistor for a total of four ohms; applying Ohm's law; and the current of two point two five amperes. The trap is forgetting to take the reciprocal when finding the parallel resistance.

QUESTION: Question five, the hardest, fully integrated. A solenoid of three hundred turns and length zero point two metres carries a current of four amperes, with the current supplied by a twelve-volt source. Calculate the magnetic field strength inside the solenoid, taking the permeability of free space as four pi times ten to the minus seven. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. The magnetic field inside the solenoid is B equals mu-nought N I over L, that's four pi times ten to the minus seven, times three hundred, times four, divided by zero point two. The numerator is four pi times ten to the minus seven times one thousand two hundred, about one point five one times ten to the minus three; divided by zero point two gives about seven point five times ten to the minus three tesla. The resistance of the wire is the voltage over the current, R equals V over I, twelve over four, three ohms. The power dissipated is voltage times current, P equals V I, twelve times four, forty-eight watts. If an iron core were inserted, the magnetic field would become much stronger, because the iron is ferromagnetic: its domains align with the solenoid's field and add their own magnetism, multiplying the field, this is how an electromagnet is made. Five marks: the field of about seven point five times ten to the minus three tesla from B equals mu-nought N I over L; the resistance of three ohms from Ohm's law; the power of forty-eight watts from P equals V I; the field becoming much stronger with an iron core; because the iron's domains align and add to the field. This single question pulls together the solenoid field, Ohm's law, power, and ferromagnetism, the whole module in one.
