NARRATOR: In October nineteen forty-five, a small British technical magazine called Wireless World printed an article by a twenty-seven-year-old man who was not a scientist. He was a Royal Air Force flight lieutenant, an enthusiast, a member of a society of dreamers who believed humans would one day travel into space. His name was Arthur C. Clarke, and in four pages he described something that did not exist and would not exist for another eighteen years. He worked out, using nothing more than the physics of Isaac Newton, the exact height above the Earth at which a satellite would circle the planet at precisely the same rate the planet turns. A satellite at that height would appear to hang motionless in the sky. It would never rise. It would never set. You could point an antenna at a fixed spot above the horizon and leave it there forever.

NARRATOR: Clarke calculated that this magic altitude was about thirty-five thousand eight hundred kilometres above the equator. He proposed putting three crewed space stations up there, spaced evenly around the globe, to relay radio and television signals to the entire world. Nobody did anything with the idea for nearly two decades. And yet today, almost every television broadcast you have ever watched from a satellite dish, every weather forecast that shows clouds sweeping across a continent, and the entire global navigation system that lives inside your phone, all of it depends on the physics that Clarke wrote down in that magazine, and on Newton's laws written down two hundred and fifty years before him. This is the story of how a science fiction writer's afternoon of arithmetic became the backbone of the modern world, and of the precise, unforgiving physics that holds it all in the sky.

NARRATOR: Let us go back further than Clarke. Let us go back to a thought experiment that Isaac Newton published, posthumously, in seventeen twenty-eight, though he had worked it out decades earlier. Imagine, Newton said, a cannon placed on top of an impossibly tall mountain, so tall its peak rises above the atmosphere. The cannon fires a ball horizontally. With a small charge, the ball curves down and strikes the ground a few kilometres away. Load more powder, fire it faster, and it travels further before it lands. Now imagine firing it faster and faster still. Here is the key insight. The Earth is round. Its surface curves away beneath the ball. If you fire the ball fast enough, then in the time it takes to fall toward the ground, the ground has curved away by exactly the same amount. The ball is always falling, but it never lands. It falls around the Earth, forever. That is an orbit.

NARRATOR: And if you keep loading on powder, there comes a point where the ball is moving so fast that it does not fall around the Earth at all but flies off and never returns, breaking free of the planet's gravity entirely. The minimum launch speed for that great escape is called the escape velocity, and it depends only on the gravitational constant and on the mass and radius of the Earth, not on the mass of the ball. For the Earth it comes out to about eleven point two kilometres per second, roughly forty thousand kilometres per hour. Below that speed you orbit; at or above it you leave. But for everything we are about to discuss, we want the satellite to stay, so we keep it below escape velocity and let it fall forever.

QUESTION: So a satellite in orbit is not somehow being held up against gravity. It is actually falling the whole time?

NARRATOR: Exactly. This is the single most important and most counter-intuitive idea in the whole subject. A satellite in orbit is in a state of continuous free fall. Gravity is pulling on it just as hard as gravity pulls on you right now. The strength of gravity, what physicists call the gravitational field strength, follows an inverse square law. It equals the gravitational constant times the mass of the Earth divided by the square of your distance from the Earth's centre, and so it weakens only gradually as you climb. At the altitude of the International Space Station, around four hundred kilometres up, the field strength is still close to nine newtons per kilogram, roughly ninety percent of its value at the surface. That is the key to clearing up a stubborn misconception. The astronauts inside are not weightless because gravity has switched off. It very nearly has not switched off at all. They are weightless because they, and their spacecraft, are all falling together, around the curve of the Earth, at the same rate. There is nothing pushing up on them from below. Weightlessness is not a zero-gravity situation. It is the sensation of experiencing only gravity, with no supporting force underneath you. The satellite stays up not by resisting gravity, but by moving sideways so fast that it perpetually misses the planet.

NARRATOR: Now, how fast is fast enough? This is where Newton's law of universal gravitation meets the physics of circular motion, and the answer falls out with beautiful simplicity. For an object to move in a circle, there must be a force pulling it constantly toward the centre. We call that the centripetal force. For a planet or a satellite, the only thing providing that inward pull is gravity. So gravity is the centripetal force. We can write that down: the gravitational force pulling the satellite inward equals the centripetal force needed to keep it on its circular path.

NARRATOR: Newton's law of gravitation says the gravitational force equals the gravitational constant, big G, times the mass of the Earth, times the mass of the satellite, all divided by the square of the orbital radius. The centripetal force needed equals the mass of the satellite times its speed squared, divided by the orbital radius. Now watch what happens when we set those two equal to each other. The mass of the satellite appears on both sides, so it cancels out completely. One factor of the radius cancels too. After tidying up, we are left with a clean, powerful statement: the orbital speed equals the square root of, the gravitational constant times the mass of the Earth, divided by the orbital radius. The full derivation is in Listing one.

QUESTION: Wait. The mass of the satellite cancelled out entirely. Does that mean a tiny satellite and a giant one orbit at the same speed if they are at the same height?

NARRATOR: Yes. And that is one of the most profound consequences of the whole topic. The orbital speed depends only on the mass of the body you are orbiting and how far you are from its centre. It does not depend at all on the mass of the orbiting object. A bolt that falls off a spacecraft, the spacecraft itself, and a thousand-tonne space station, if they are at the same altitude, all travel at exactly the same orbital speed. This is the same reason Galileo's heavy and light balls hit the ground together. Gravity accelerates everything equally regardless of mass.

NARRATOR: Look closely at that equation and you will see something else. The orbital radius is in the bottom of the fraction, under a square root. That means the bigger the orbit, the smaller the speed. Satellites that are close to the Earth must travel extremely fast. Satellites that are far away travel slowly. Low Earth orbit, where the International Space Station lives, runs from roughly two hundred kilometres up to about two thousand kilometres. Down there, a satellite has to scream around the planet at about seven point seven kilometres per second. That is around twenty-seven thousand six hundred kilometres per hour. The space station completes a full lap of the Earth in roughly ninety-two minutes. The astronauts aboard see a sunrise and a sunset about every forty-five minutes, sixteen sunrises and sixteen sunsets in a single day.

QUESTION: Why don't they just orbit lower, closer to the ground, where it would be easier to reach?

NARRATOR: Two reasons, and they bracket low Earth orbit from below and from above. Below about two hundred and fifty kilometres, there is still enough wisp of atmosphere to cause drag. The thin gas molecules up there slap against the satellite, stealing its energy and momentum, and its orbit slowly decays. It spirals down and eventually burns up. The space station, even at four hundred kilometres, feels this drag and gradually loses altitude, which is why visiting cargo ships have to periodically fire booster engines to nudge it back up. The Russian space station Mir came down in two thousand and one largely because of this relentless atmospheric drag, with its descent managed by booster rockets so that it splashed down safely in the Pacific. So you keep satellites above the thickest air to make them last. But you also do not want to go too high within low Earth orbit, because above roughly a thousand kilometres you run into the Van Allen radiation belts, doughnut-shaped regions of charged particles trapped by the Earth's magnetic field that can fry electronics and endanger astronauts. So low Earth orbit is really the band of clear sky between the atmosphere below and the radiation belts above. But the deeper reason to climb higher connects us back to Clarke. Because if speed depends on altitude, then so does the time it takes to go around. And that means there is one very special altitude, far higher than the space station, where something remarkable happens.

NARRATOR: To find it, we need the second great law of orbits, which Johannes Kepler discovered by staring at decades of planetary observations a full lifetime before Newton explained why it was true. Kepler's third law, the law of periods, says that the square of the time it takes to complete an orbit is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius. Square of the period, cube of the radius. Newton later showed exactly what the constant of proportionality is. If you take the orbital speed equation and combine it with the simple fact that speed equals the circumference of the orbit divided by the period, the algebra rearranges into Kepler's law with the constant filled in. The radius cubed divided by the period squared equals the gravitational constant times the mass of the Earth, divided by four pi squared. That whole expression is fixed by the mass of the Earth alone. The derivation is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Now here is the move that made Arthur Clarke famous. Suppose you do not start by choosing an altitude. Suppose instead you start by choosing a period. You demand that the satellite take exactly one day to go around, the same time the Earth takes to spin once on its axis. If you fix the period and use Kepler's law to solve for the radius, there is only one answer. One single altitude where the orbital period equals the Earth's rotation.

QUESTION: And if the satellite goes around in exactly one day, while the Earth turns underneath it in exactly one day, then from the ground it would appear to stay completely still?

NARRATOR: That is the whole trick. The satellite is racing around the planet at over three kilometres per second, but the planet is turning at exactly the same angular rate beneath it. The two motions are perfectly locked together. From your back garden, the satellite seems frozen in the sky. We call that a geostationary orbit. Let us actually find its height. There is one subtlety the textbooks insist on, and it matters. The period you must use is not the twenty-four hour day measured against the Sun. It is the sidereal day, the time the Earth takes to spin once relative to the distant stars. That is about three minutes and fifty-six seconds shorter than the solar day, eighty-six thousand one hundred and sixty-four seconds in total. Drop that period into Kepler's law along with the mass of the Earth, and solve for the radius. The full calculation is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: The orbital radius comes out to about forty-two thousand one hundred and sixty-four kilometres from the centre of the Earth. But that is measured from the centre. To get the altitude above the ground, you subtract the radius of the Earth, about six thousand three hundred and seventy-eight kilometres. And what you are left with is thirty-five thousand seven hundred and eighty-six kilometres. That is the altitude. That is the precise number Arthur Clarke worked out in nineteen forty-five with pencil and paper. At that height the satellite glides along at about three point zero seven kilometres per second, slow and stately compared to the frantic space station, because it is so much further out. There is exactly one such altitude. Go a little higher and the satellite takes longer than a day, so it drifts slowly eastward across the sky. Go a little lower and it laps the Earth faster than the planet turns, drifting westward. Only at thirty-five thousand seven hundred and eighty-six kilometres does it hang truly still.

NARRATOR: There is one more condition, and it is a condition that trips up almost everyone. The orbit must lie directly over the equator. It must have zero tilt. If the orbit is tilted relative to the equator, even at exactly the right altitude and exactly the right period, the satellite will not sit still. Instead, viewed from the ground, it traces out a slow figure-eight pattern in the sky over the course of each day, drifting north and south. An orbit like that, with the right period but a tilt, is called geosynchronous. It is synchronised with the Earth's rotation, but it is not stationary. A truly geostationary orbit is a special case of geosynchronous: same period, but with zero tilt and a perfectly circular path. All geostationary satellites are geosynchronous, but not all geosynchronous satellites are geostationary. Hold onto that distinction, because it is about to matter in the real history.

NARRATOR: Now, back to the young man with the pencil. In nineteen forty-five Arthur Clarke was no professor. He was a flight lieutenant in the Royal Air Force, a military rank, not a scientific one. During the war he had worked as a radar instructor, and that posting gave him an intimate understanding of radio and its frustrating limitations. Radio waves do not bend around the curve of the Earth. To send a signal across a continent you needed a chain of expensive relay towers, each one passing the message along to the next, hop after hop. Clarke had also been reading the technical press about the German V-two rockets, the first objects humanity had ever flung beyond the atmosphere. He did the mathematics himself and saw the future. Build something far bigger than a V-two, push it up to that one magic altitude, and leave it there. From thirty-six thousand kilometres up, a single station could see nearly half the planet at once. Three of them, spaced evenly around the equator, could blanket the entire globe in radio coverage.

NARRATOR: His article, which the editor titled Extra-Terrestrial Relays, can Rocket Stations Give Worldwide Radio Coverage, proposed three crewed stations. He placed them at specific longitudes, one over Africa and Europe, one over China and the Pacific, one over the Americas. He imagined crews living aboard, because in nineteen forty-five the idea of reliable, unattended electronics surviving in space for years was simply unthinkable. Someone would have to be up there to fix things. He even costed the power. Only about one point two kilowatts for broadcasting, he reckoned, or a mere fifty watts for a focused point-to-point link. A fifty-metre solar mirror, he calculated, could gather over ten thousand kilowatts from sunlight. He worked out that the stations would pass through the Earth's shadow only for a couple of days each year, with the longest eclipse lasting just over an hour. It was extraordinarily thorough for a four-page magazine article by an amateur.

QUESTION: If he worked all of this out, why didn't Clarke become rich? Why didn't he patent it?

NARRATOR: He thought about it. He seriously considered filing a patent. But he decided the whole concept was simply too far from anything that could actually be built. The rockets did not exist. The electronics did not exist. A patent would expire long before the technology caught up. Late in life, when people asked him about the fortune he might have made, he gave a wry answer. A patent, he said, is really a licence to be sued. And there is an honesty we should respect here. Clarke never claimed to have invented the geostationary orbit itself. The orbit is just a consequence of Newton's physics. A German rocketry pioneer, Hermann Oberth, had described stations at that height back in nineteen twenty-three. And in nineteen twenty-eight, a Slovenian engineer writing under the pen name Hermann Noordung, whose real name was Herman Potočnik, had described a crewed station in a circular orbit at around thirty-six thousand kilometres, synchronised with the Earth's spin, a full seventeen years before Clarke. Clarke acknowledged them both. What Clarke contributed, and the reason his name is attached to the orbit today, was the specific vision of using it for global telecommunications. He saw the use. That is why the International Astronomical Union now officially calls that band of sky the Clarke Orbit, and the ring of satellites circling within it, the Clarke Belt.

NARRATOR: And then nothing happened. For eighteen years, Clarke's brilliant idea sat in print, gathering dust, while the world caught up. The obstacle was never the physics. Newton's laws had been settled for centuries. The obstacles were engineering. You needed a rocket powerful enough to lift a payload not just to space but all the way out to thirty-six thousand kilometres. You needed electronics small enough, light enough, and reliable enough to work, unattended, for years in the radiation and vacuum of deep space. Neither of those existed in nineteen forty-five.

NARRATOR: By the late nineteen fifties, with Sputnik launched in nineteen fifty-seven and the space race underway, the pieces were finally arriving. But inside the American aerospace industry there was a fierce argument about whether a geostationary satellite was even worth attempting. The doubters pointed out that thirty-six thousand kilometres is a very long way. Radio signals fade with the square of distance, so the signals would be desperately weak by the time they returned to Earth. And the round trip for the signal, up and back down, would introduce a noticeable delay, a quarter of a second of lag in any conversation. Many engineers argued for swarms of satellites in low orbit instead. One engineer at Hughes Aircraft Company in California disagreed completely. His name was Harold Rosen, and history would come to call him the father of the geostationary satellite. Rosen and his small team, including engineers Don Williams and Thomas Hudspeth, were convinced they could build a geostationary relay light enough to launch and clever enough to work.

NARRATOR: NASA backed the gamble and the project was named Syncom, short for synchronous communications. And here we should pause on just how much harder it is to reach that altitude, because this is the other half of the physics. Lifting a satellite all the way out to thirty-six thousand kilometres is not just a matter of building a taller rocket. It is a matter of energy. Every satellite in orbit has a total mechanical energy that is the sum of two parts: its kinetic energy from its speed, and its gravitational potential energy from its height. By the convention physicists use, where the potential energy is taken to be zero at infinite distance and negative everywhere closer in, the total energy of any circular orbit works out to be negative, equal to minus the gravitational constant times the mass of the Earth times the mass of the satellite, all divided by twice the orbital radius. The important thing is that a higher orbit, a larger radius, is a less negative number, which means it holds more total energy. So to move a satellite from a low parking orbit up to the geostationary belt, you must add energy. Engineers do this with a transfer orbit, an elongated elliptical path that kisses the low orbit at its closest point and the high orbit at its farthest, fired up with two carefully timed rocket burns. Counter-intuitively, as the satellite climbs to the higher orbit it actually slows down, even though it ends up with more total energy, because so much of the added energy goes into gravitational potential energy and the higher orbit is a slower one. That is the strange accounting of orbital mechanics. The first attempt, Syncom one, launched in February nineteen sixty-three, and died on the way up when its electronics failed before it reached orbit. The second attempt, Syncom two, launched in July nineteen sixty-three, and it worked, but here is where our earlier distinction comes alive. Syncom two reached the right altitude and the right period, but its orbit was tilted relative to the equator. It was geosynchronous, but not geostationary. From the ground it traced that slow figure-eight in the sky each day, and the antennas tracking it had to slowly move to follow it. Even so, it was a triumph. In August nineteen sixty-three, Syncom two carried the first live two-way satellite telephone call between heads of state, between the President of the United States and the Prime Minister of Nigeria.

NARRATOR: Then came Syncom three, launched in August nineteen sixty-four. This one was placed in an orbit directly over the equator, with the tilt removed. Syncom three was the world's first true geostationary satellite. It hung, motionless, over the Pacific Ocean. And just weeks later it proved its worth to the entire planet, by broadcasting the nineteen sixty-four Tokyo Olympic Games live across the Pacific to the United States, the first time a major event had ever been beamed live across an ocean by satellite. Nineteen years and ten months after Arthur Clarke published his article, the thing he had described became real. Within a year, in nineteen sixty-five, the first commercial geostationary communications satellite, nicknamed Early Bird, was carrying telephone calls and television between North America and Europe. The communications satellite industry had been born, and it has only grown since. Today the geostationary arc is so crowded with satellites carrying television, weather imaging, and communications that an international body has to carefully ration the orbital slots so the satellites do not interfere with one another.

NARRATOR: But the story does not end with communications hanging motionless in the sky. Because there is a second great use of orbits, one that turns the whole logic upside down. Instead of using satellites that stay still so you can find them, what if you used satellites that move in precisely known ways, so they can find you? This is the Global Positioning System, GPS, and it lives in a third region of space we have not yet visited.

NARRATOR: GPS satellites do not orbit low like the space station, and they do not orbit at the geostationary height. They sit in between, in what is called medium Earth orbit, at an altitude of about twenty thousand two hundred kilometres. There are a constellation of them, between twenty-four and thirty-two operational satellites, arranged in six orbital planes with several satellites in each. Their orbits are tuned so that each satellite completes a lap in about eleven hours and fifty-eight minutes, which is exactly half a sidereal day. That means each satellite passes over the same patch of ground twice a day, like clockwork. The constellation is designed so that from anywhere on the surface of the Earth, at any moment, you can always see at least four of them.

QUESTION: Why exactly four? What is special about that number?

NARRATOR: To answer that, we have to understand how GPS actually finds you, because almost everyone gets this wrong. People say GPS works by triangulation, measuring angles. It does not. GPS works by trilateration, measuring distances. Here is the principle. Every GPS satellite carries an extraordinarily precise atomic clock, and it continuously broadcasts two things: its own exact position in space, and the exact time at which it sent the signal. The signal travels to you at the speed of light, about three hundred million metres per second. Your receiver notes the time the signal arrives, subtracts the time it was sent, and gets the travel time. Multiply that travel time by the speed of light, and you have the distance to that satellite.

NARRATOR: Now think about what one distance tells you. If you know you are, say, twenty-one thousand kilometres from one particular satellite, then you must be somewhere on the surface of an enormous sphere of that radius centred on the satellite. One measurement, one sphere. Add a second satellite, and you are on a second sphere. Two spheres intersect in a circle, so now you are somewhere on that circle. Add a third satellite, a third sphere, and it cuts that circle down to just two points. Usually one of those two points is absurd, out in space or deep inside the Earth, so you can throw it away. So with three satellites you can, in principle, pin down your position in three dimensions.

QUESTION: Then why do we need a fourth? Three spheres already gave us a point.

NARRATOR: Because of the clocks. The satellites carry atomic clocks worth a fortune, accurate to billionths of a second. But your phone does not. Your phone has a cheap quartz clock, and it is slightly wrong. And remember, the whole method depends on measuring time and multiplying by the speed of light. Light travels so fast that an error of just one millionth of a second, one microsecond, in your clock translates into a position error of three hundred metres. Your phone's clock is far worse than that. So your three distance measurements are all corrupted by the same unknown clock error. Here is the elegant solution. You treat your own clock error as a fourth unknown, alongside your three spatial coordinates. Four unknowns require four equations, and each satellite gives you one equation. So you need a minimum of four satellites. With four, the mathematics can solve simultaneously for your latitude, your longitude, your altitude, and the exact error in your own clock. The system corrects your cheap clock for free, every single time it gives you a fix. That is why the answer is four, not three.

NARRATOR: Now we arrive at the most astonishing part of the whole story, the part where two completely different chapters of physics, separated by two centuries, collide inside a device in your pocket. Because GPS depends on measuring time with almost unimaginable precision. And time, it turns out, does not tick at the same rate everywhere. This is where Einstein walks into the room.

NARRATOR: Einstein's theory of relativity makes two separate predictions about clocks, and both of them apply to a GPS satellite. The first comes from special relativity, the physics of motion. A clock that is moving fast, as seen by a stationary observer, runs slow. GPS satellites move at about three point nine kilometres per second relative to the ground. That is fast enough that, due to their motion, their atomic clocks tick slightly slower than identical clocks on the Earth's surface. The amount works out to about seven point two microseconds lost per day. Their clocks run slow.

NARRATOR: But there is a second effect, and it pulls in the opposite direction and is even larger. It comes from general relativity, Einstein's theory of gravity. General relativity predicts that clocks deeper in a gravitational field, where gravity is stronger, run slower than clocks higher up where gravity is weaker. A clock on the ground sits deep in the Earth's gravity. A GPS satellite, twenty thousand kilometres up, sits in much weaker gravity. So the satellite's clock, being higher in the gravitational field, runs faster than the ground clock. This gravitational effect amounts to about forty-five point nine microseconds gained per day. The satellite clock runs fast.

QUESTION: So one effect slows the satellite clock down, and the other speeds it up. Do they just cancel out?

NARRATOR: They do not cancel, because they are not equal. The gravitational effect, the speeding up, is much larger than the motional effect, the slowing down. Subtract the seven point two microseconds the satellite loses from motion from the forty-five point nine microseconds it gains from weaker gravity, and the net result is that a GPS satellite clock runs fast by about thirty-eight microseconds every single day, compared to clocks on the ground. The full calculation is in Listing five. Now, thirty-eight millionths of a second sounds laughably tiny. Who could possibly care about thirty-eight microseconds a day? But remember the brutal arithmetic of GPS. You multiply time by the speed of light. So let us convert thirty-eight microseconds of timing error into distance. Thirty-eight microseconds, times the speed of light, equals about eleven and a half kilometres.

NARRATOR: Let that sink in. If the engineers had ignored relativity, every GPS fix would drift by more than ten kilometres on the very first day. The next day it would be off by over twenty kilometres. Within a week your navigation would place you in the wrong city. The system would be completely, catastrophically useless. When GPS was being designed in the early nineteen seventies, some engineers genuinely doubted that these relativistic effects mattered, or were even real. A physicist named Neil Ashby, at the University of Colorado, was among those who worked through the numbers in detail and showed that the corrections were not optional. They were the difference between a working system and a broken one.

NARRATOR: And the solution the engineers chose is one of the most beautiful pieces of practical physics ever deployed. They knew exactly how fast the clocks would run once in orbit. So before launch, they deliberately built the atomic clocks to run slow, by precisely the right amount, here on the ground. The clocks were set to tick at a frequency of ten point two two nine nine nine nine nine nine five four three megahertz, instead of their nominal ten point two three megahertz. A clock built deliberately wrong. Then, once it is hauled up to twenty thousand kilometres and relativity speeds it up by exactly that predicted amount, it ticks at precisely the right rate. Engineers built a clock that is wrong on Earth so that it would be right in space. Every time your phone tells you where you are, it is silently relying on Einstein being correct to within a fraction of a microsecond.

NARRATOR: The aftermath of all this reshaped daily life. GPS reached full operational capability in nineteen ninety-three. For years, the United States military deliberately degraded the civilian signal, an intentional scrambling that limited ordinary accuracy to about a hundred metres. There was a turning point. Back in nineteen eighty-three, a Korean airliner, Flight zero zero seven, carrying two hundred and sixty-nine people, strayed off course into Soviet airspace and was shot down, a navigation error that GPS could have prevented. In its wake, President Reagan promised that once GPS was finished it would be opened to the world. And in May of the year two thousand, President Clinton ordered the deliberate scrambling switched off. Overnight, civilian accuracy leapt from a hundred metres to ten or twenty metres. Within a few years GPS was in cars, in ships, in tractors guiding themselves across fields, in the time-stamps that synchronise the world's power grids and financial trading and the internet itself. The first iPhone, in two thousand and seven, carried a GPS chip, and the world has navigated by satellite ever since. Other nations built their own constellations, Russia's, Europe's, China's, all running on the very same physics.

NARRATOR: So when you step back, the whole sky above you is organised by altitude, and the altitude is dictated entirely by the equation that fell out of setting gravity equal to centripetal force. Low Earth orbit, a few hundred kilometres up, fast-moving, short-lived, perfect for the space station, for high-resolution imaging, for watching the weather up close. Medium Earth orbit, twenty thousand kilometres up, home to the navigation constellations that find you by trilateration. And the Clarke Belt, thirty-five thousand seven hundred and eighty-six kilometres up over the equator, where satellites hang motionless and pour television and weather imagery down onto fixed dishes. Three regions, one law. Newton wrote the law. Kepler found its rhythm. Clarke saw its use. And Einstein supplied the final, tiny, indispensable correction without which none of it would point you to the right street.
