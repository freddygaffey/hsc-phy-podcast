NARRATOR: Welcome to Module Three, Waves and Thermodynamics. We're changing scene from the motion of solid objects to two new worlds: waves, which carry energy without carrying matter, and heat, which is energy on the move because of temperature. Today we start with waves, the properties common to every wave in the universe, whether it's a ripple on water, a sound, a wave on a string, or light itself. By the end you'll be able to define the wave quantities, distinguish transverse from longitudinal and mechanical from electromagnetic waves, read the two kinds of wave graph, and use the wave equation, speed equals frequency times wavelength. But first, a quick recap, because the one idea that carries straight from Module Two into waves is energy.

NARRATOR: Last module, Dynamics, ended with two great conservation laws, energy and momentum. The energy one is the bridge to today: we said energy is the capacity to do work, it comes in many forms, and it's transformed but never destroyed. Here's the link, and it's the defining feature of a wave: a wave is a way of transferring energy from one place to another without transferring matter. When a wave travels across the ocean, the water itself doesn't travel across the ocean, it just bobs up and down while the energy moves along. So waves are an energy-transport mechanism, and that single idea, energy moves, matter doesn't, is the headline of the whole topic. Hold the energy concept from last module; today it travels as a wave.

QUESTION: Quick check before we start. From last module, what's the one thing that's conserved and transformed but never destroyed, and what does a wave transfer from place to place? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Energy is conserved and transformed but never destroyed, and a wave transfers energy from place to place. The crucial addition today: it transfers that energy without transferring matter. The particles of the medium oscillate about fixed points; they don't travel with the wave. Lock that, because "energy not matter" is the single most important thing to say about what a wave is. Let's build the wave concept properly.

NARRATOR: A wave is a disturbance that travels through a medium, or through space, transferring energy without a net transfer of matter. Drop a stone in a pond: the ripple, the energy, spreads outward, but a leaf floating on the surface just bobs up and down in place, it doesn't get carried to the edge. That's the signature of a wave. The medium is whatever the wave travels through, water, air, a string, and its particles oscillate, vibrate back and forth, passing the energy along to their neighbours, but each particle stays, on average, where it started. So when you describe a wave, never say the medium travels; say the energy, or the disturbance, travels while the particles oscillate.

NARRATOR: Let me say a little more about how a wave actually propagates, because it explains the "energy not matter" point mechanically. Each particle in the medium is connected to its neighbours, like a row of people linked by springs. Disturb the first particle and it pulls on the next, which pulls on the next, so the disturbance, and the energy, passes along the chain, while each particle just oscillates about its own spot and returns there. The energy is handed from particle to particle; no particle travels the length of the wave. That's why a cork on water bobs in place as waves pass, and why a Mexican wave travels around a stadium while every person stays in their seat. The speed of this hand-off depends on the medium, how stiff and how dense it is, which is why sound travels faster in water than in air, and faster still in steel: a stiffer medium passes the disturbance along more quickly. So the wave speed is a property of the medium, set before the source ever vibrates.

NARRATOR: And one more quantity the syllabus names, the wave number, which is just another way of describing the wavelength: it's the number of wavelengths that fit into a given distance, related to the spatial repetition of the wave. You won't use it heavily at this level, but know that it, like frequency for time, captures how rapidly the wave repeats, in space rather than in time. Frequency is repetition per unit time; wave number is repetition per unit distance. And amplitude, worth repeating, is tied to energy: a wave's energy depends on its amplitude, a bigger-amplitude wave carries more energy, which is why a loud sound, large amplitude, carries more energy than a quiet one of the same pitch, and a bright light more than a dim one of the same colour. The relationship is steeper than you might guess, the energy a wave carries is proportional to the square of its amplitude, so doubling the amplitude quadruples the energy, which is why a small increase in the height of a wave can mean a large increase in the energy it delivers, the reason a slightly bigger ocean swell hits so much harder.

NARRATOR: Now the first big classification: transverse versus longitudinal waves, defined by the direction the particles oscillate relative to the direction the wave travels. In a transverse wave, the particles oscillate perpendicular, at right angles, to the direction the wave moves. A wave on a string is transverse: the string moves up and down while the wave travels along it. All electromagnetic waves, including light, are transverse. In a longitudinal wave, the particles oscillate parallel to, along, the direction the wave travels, creating regions where the particles bunch up, compressions, and regions where they spread out, rarefactions. Sound is the classic longitudinal wave: the air molecules move back and forth along the direction the sound travels. So the test is simple: particle motion perpendicular to travel is transverse, particle motion parallel to travel is longitudinal. That distinction is in Listing three, and it's a guaranteed exam item.

NARRATOR: The second classification: mechanical versus electromagnetic waves, defined by whether they need a medium. A mechanical wave needs a medium to travel through, it's a disturbance of that medium's particles, so it cannot travel through a vacuum. Sound, water waves, and waves on a string are all mechanical, which is why there's no sound in space, no air to carry it. An electromagnetic wave is an oscillation of electric and magnetic fields, it needs no medium and travels through the vacuum of space, which is how light from the Sun reaches us across empty space. You'll study electromagnetic waves in depth in Module Seven; for now, the distinction is mechanical needs a medium, electromagnetic doesn't. So sound and light are both waves, but sound is a mechanical longitudinal wave that needs air, while light is an electromagnetic transverse wave that needs nothing.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. Classify each of these two ways: a sound wave, and a light wave. Is each transverse or longitudinal, and mechanical or electromagnetic? Pause the player, classify both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. A sound wave is longitudinal, the air molecules oscillate along the direction of travel, with compressions and rarefactions, and it is mechanical, it needs a medium like air, so there's no sound in a vacuum. A light wave is transverse, the electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction of travel, and it is electromagnetic, it needs no medium and travels through empty space. So sound is longitudinal and mechanical; light is transverse and electromagnetic. If you got both classifications for both waves, you've nailed the two big distinctions of the topic. They come up constantly, often as "explain why you can see a distant explosion before you hear it", the light, electromagnetic and fast, arrives before the sound, mechanical and far slower.

NARRATOR: Now the quantities that describe any wave, and there are several, so let's be precise, they're all in Listing one. Wavelength is the distance for one complete cycle of the wave, from one crest to the next crest, say, measured in metres, and its symbol is the Greek letter lambda. Amplitude is the maximum displacement of a particle from its rest position, how big the oscillation is, and it relates to the energy the wave carries, bigger amplitude means more energy. Period is the time for one complete cycle, measured in seconds. Frequency is the number of complete cycles per second, measured in hertz, and frequency and period are reciprocals: frequency equals one over the period, f equals one over T. A wave of frequency two hertz completes two cycles per second, so each cycle takes half a second, that's the period. And velocity is the speed at which the wave, the energy, travels through the medium.

NARRATOR: Now the relationship that ties them together, the wave equation, and it's one you'll use throughout this module and again in Module Seven. The speed of a wave equals its frequency times its wavelength: v equals f lambda. It makes intuitive sense: in one period the wave advances exactly one wavelength, so the speed, distance over time, is one wavelength divided by one period, which is the wavelength times the frequency since frequency is one over the period. So speed equals frequency times wavelength. Rearrange it for whichever quantity you need. There's a worked example in Listing two. The wave equation links the three headline quantities, and almost every wave calculation in the course uses it.

QUESTION: Let me have you use it. A wave has a frequency of fifty hertz and a wavelength of two metres. What is its speed? And if the frequency doubled while the speed stayed the same, what would happen to the wavelength? Pause the player, work both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The speed is frequency times wavelength, fifty times two, one hundred metres per second. For the second part: if the speed stays the same, since speed equals frequency times wavelength, frequency and wavelength are inversely related, so doubling the frequency would halve the wavelength, to one metre. The speed of a wave is set by the medium, not by the source, so if you shake the source faster, higher frequency, the wavelength shortens to keep the speed the same. That inverse relationship between frequency and wavelength at fixed speed is a favourite exam point, and it's why a higher-pitched sound, higher frequency, has a shorter wavelength than a low one in the same air. The working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: A subtle distinction worth a mark: the wave speed is not the same as the particle speed. The wave speed is how fast the disturbance, the pattern, travels through the medium, the v in the wave equation. The particle speed is how fast an individual particle of the medium oscillates back and forth about its rest position. These are completely different. On the ocean, the wave might race along at several metres per second while any given water molecule just bobs up and down slowly in place. Don't confuse "how fast the wave moves" with "how fast the particles move", the wave equation gives the wave speed, the speed of the energy through the medium, not the speed of the particles. That distinction trips students who think the medium travels with the wave; it doesn't, and the two speeds are unrelated.

NARRATOR: A quick forward look at electromagnetic waves, because it primes Module Seven. All electromagnetic waves, radio, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays, are transverse oscillations of electric and magnetic fields, and they all travel through a vacuum at the same enormous speed, the speed of light, three times ten to the eight metres per second. They differ only in frequency and wavelength, linked by the very same wave equation, the speed of light equals frequency times wavelength. So a radio wave and a gamma ray are the same kind of thing travelling at the same speed, just with vastly different wavelengths and frequencies. That family is the electromagnetic spectrum, and you'll meet it properly next year, but notice it's built entirely on today's ideas: transverse, electromagnetic, and v equals f lambda. The wave vocabulary you're learning now is the language of all of light. Here's a hook to remember the spectrum in order of increasing frequency, and so decreasing wavelength: Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible, Ultraviolet, X-ray, Gamma. Many students remember it as "Raging Martians Invaded Venus Using X-ray Guns". As you go along that list the frequency rises, the wavelength shrinks, and because energy rises with frequency, the energy per packet of the wave climbs too, which is why gamma rays and X-rays are the dangerous, ionising end and radio waves are the gentle end. But the speed is identical for all of them in a vacuum, the speed of light, because speed depends on the medium, here empty space, not on the frequency. Keep that picture: one speed, a whole range of frequencies and wavelengths, all stitched together by v equals f lambda.

NARRATOR: Now the two kinds of graph, because reading them correctly is a guaranteed exam skill and students mix them up. A wave can be graphed two ways, and they look similar but mean different things, so read the horizontal axis. A displacement-versus-time graph plots one particle's displacement as time passes; from it you read the period, the time for one cycle, and hence the frequency. A displacement-versus-position graph, sometimes called a snapshot, plots the displacement of all the particles at one instant, frozen in time; from it you read the wavelength, the distance for one cycle. So the rule: a graph with time on the horizontal axis gives you the period and frequency; a graph with position or distance on the horizontal axis gives you the wavelength. Both graphs can show you the amplitude, the height of the wave, but only the time graph gives period and only the position graph gives wavelength. Check the horizontal axis first, every time. The graph rules are in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Let me work a graph-reading example, because combining both graphs to get the wave speed is examinable. Suppose a displacement-time graph for one particle shows a complete cycle every zero point four seconds, and a displacement-position snapshot of the same wave shows a wavelength of zero point eight metres. Find the speed. From the time graph, the period is zero point four seconds, so the frequency is one over zero point four, two point five hertz. From the position graph, the wavelength is zero point eight metres. Then the speed is frequency times wavelength, two point five times zero point eight, two metres per second. Notice you needed both graphs: the time graph for the frequency, the position graph for the wavelength, then the wave equation to combine them. That's a classic multi-step question, and the trick is knowing which graph gives which quantity, time gives period and frequency, position gives wavelength.

NARRATOR: One more skill on the graphs, because the marks hide in careful reading. When you measure the period or the wavelength off a graph, read across a whole number of cycles and divide, don't eyeball a single bump. If a displacement-time graph shows four complete cycles in one point two seconds, the period is one point two divided by four, zero point three seconds, much more accurate than guessing one cycle by eye, and the frequency is one over that, about three point three hertz. Same idea on a position graph: count several wavelengths across a known distance and divide. Two more reading points. First, the amplitude is measured from the rest position, the centre line, up to a crest, not from the bottom of a trough up to the top of a crest, that full height is twice the amplitude, a classic dropped mark. Second, a crest is a point of maximum positive displacement and a trough a point of maximum negative displacement, and the distance from any point on the wave to the matching point one cycle later, crest to crest, trough to trough, or any repeat, is one wavelength. Read across many cycles, measure amplitude from the centre, and identify crest-to-crest as one wavelength, and the graph questions become routine.

NARRATOR: A practical note, because the syllabus wants you to create and investigate waves. You can make transverse waves on a stretched slinky or rope by flicking one end side to side, and longitudinal waves by pushing and pulling the slinky along its length, watching the compressions travel. With a ripple tank you generate water waves and measure their wavelength and frequency to find the speed; with a signal generator and an oscilloscope you can display a sound wave's displacement-time graph and read its period. In every case you're measuring the same quantities, frequency, wavelength, speed, and confirming the wave equation. A key observation from these investigations: the speed depends on the medium, not on how hard or fast you drive the source, change the frequency and the wavelength adjusts to keep the speed constant, exactly the inverse relationship we calculated. Being able to describe such an investigation, and what it shows, is examinable.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "describe the difference between transverse and longitudinal waves, with an example of each". The band-six version: in a transverse wave, the particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction in which the wave transfers energy; an example is a wave on a string, or any electromagnetic wave such as light. In a longitudinal wave, the particles oscillate parallel to the direction of energy transfer, producing compressions and rarefactions; an example is a sound wave in air. In both, energy is transferred without a net transfer of matter. The marks are for the perpendicular-versus-parallel distinction, a correct example of each, and the point that both transfer energy not matter.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the gap. A weak answer says "a transverse wave goes up and down and a longitudinal wave goes side to side". NARRATOR: Let me connect this to a few everyday situations, because the exam likes applied questions. You can measure the speed of sound with an echo: shout at a cliff a known distance away, time the echo's return, and the sound travelled there and back, twice the distance, in that time, so speed is twice the distance over the time. That's the wave equation's cousin, speed equals distance over time, applied to a pulse. The inverse relationship between frequency and wavelength explains musical pitch: a higher-pitched note has a higher frequency and, in the same air, a shorter wavelength. Amplitude explains loudness and brightness: a louder sound or brighter light has a larger amplitude and carries more energy. And the difference between wave types explains why, in a thunderstorm, you see the lightning, electromagnetic, almost instantly but hear the thunder, mechanical sound, seconds later, the light covers the distance in a flash while the much slower sound lags, and counting the gap tells you how far away the strike was. Every one of these is just the wave properties from today applied to the world.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: saying a wave transfers matter, it transfers energy, the medium's particles only oscillate. Trap two: confusing transverse and longitudinal, perpendicular is transverse, parallel is longitudinal. Trap three: thinking all waves need a medium, electromagnetic waves don't. Trap four: reading wavelength off a displacement-time graph, that gives the period; wavelength comes from a displacement-position graph. Trap five: confusing amplitude with wavelength, amplitude is the height, wavelength is the length of a cycle. Avoid those and wave basics are secure.

QUESTION: Question three. Explain, using the properties of waves, why sound cannot travel through the vacuum of space but light can. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

QUESTION: Question four. A displacement-time graph of a single particle shows a complete cycle taking zero point two five seconds. Calculate the frequency. Then explain what additional graph you would need to find the wavelength. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A sound wave travels at three hundred and forty metres per second in air. A particular sound has a frequency of six hundred and eighty hertz. Calculate its wavelength. Then explain what happens to the wavelength if the same sound passes into water, where its speed increases to about one thousand five hundred metres per second, while its frequency stays the same. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. In air, from the wave equation, wavelength equals speed over frequency, three hundred and forty over six hundred and eighty, zero point five metres. When the sound passes into water, its frequency stays the same, because frequency is set by the source, but its speed increases to one thousand five hundred metres per second. Since speed equals frequency times wavelength, and the frequency is unchanged while the speed increases, the wavelength must increase: wavelength equals speed over frequency, one thousand five hundred over six hundred and eighty, about two point two metres. So the wavelength increases from zero point five metres to about two point two metres. Four marks: wavelength in air zero point five metres; frequency stays the same across the boundary; the wave equation gives a longer wavelength; and the value about two point two metres. The key concept is that frequency is fixed by the source, so when the speed changes between media, it's the wavelength that changes, an idea that returns as refraction in the light episodes.
