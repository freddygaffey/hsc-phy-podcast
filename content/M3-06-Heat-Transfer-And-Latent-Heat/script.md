NARRATOR: Welcome back to Module Three, Waves and Thermodynamics. Last episode we laid the foundations of thermal physics: temperature is the average kinetic energy of the particles, heat is energy transferred because of a temperature difference, and the energy to change a substance's temperature is Q equals m c delta T. Today we finish the thermal story, and the module, with two things. First, how heat actually moves from place to place, the three modes of heat transfer, conduction, convection, and radiation. Second, latent heat, the surprising energy involved when a substance changes state, melts or boils, at constant temperature. By the end you'll be able to describe and compare the three transfer modes, use the thermal-conductivity relationship, explain why temperature stays constant during a change of state, and calculate latent heat with Q equals m L. But first, a recap, because today builds directly on last episode.

NARRATOR: Last episode we established that heat flows from a hotter object to a colder one until they reach thermal equilibrium, and that the energy to raise a mass through a temperature change is Q equals m c delta T, with the specific heat capacity c measuring how much energy each kilogram needs per kelvin. We also drew the sharp line between temperature, the average particle kinetic energy, and internal energy, the total energy of all the particles. Hold both of those, because today we ask the next two questions: by what mechanisms does that heat actually travel from hot to cold, and what happens to the energy when, instead of changing temperature, a substance changes state? The second question is where Q equals m c delta T runs out and a new equation takes over.

QUESTION: Quick check from last episode. Which way does heat always flow on its own, and what does the equation Q equals m c delta T calculate? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Heat always flows spontaneously from the hotter object to the colder object, never the reverse on its own, until they reach thermal equilibrium. And Q equals m c delta T calculates the heat energy needed to change the temperature of a mass m of a substance by an amount delta T, using its specific heat capacity. Heat flows hot to cold, and Q equals m c delta T is the temperature-change energy. Lock those, because today's first half is about the mechanism of that hot-to-cold flow, and today's second half is about energy that does not change the temperature at all.

NARRATOR: There are exactly three ways heat is transferred, and the syllabus wants all three with their mechanisms: conduction, convection, and radiation. They're listed for you in Listing one. Let's take them one at a time, because exam questions love to ask you to name the mechanism and say whether each needs a medium.

NARRATOR: First, conduction. Conduction is the transfer of heat through a material by direct particle-to-particle interaction, without the material itself moving. Here's the mechanism in particle terms: at the hot end of, say, a metal rod, the particles vibrate vigorously, lots of kinetic energy; they collide with their slower neighbours and pass energy along, which collide with theirs, so the energy travels through the solid from hot to cold while each particle stays put. Conduction is the main way heat moves through solids. Metals are especially good conductors because, on top of this vibration mechanism, they have free electrons that move through the metal and carry energy quickly, which is why a metal spoon in hot soup heats along its whole length fast, and why metals feel cold to touch even at room temperature, they conduct heat away from your hand so rapidly that your skin cools, whereas wood at the very same temperature feels warmer because it draws heat away slowly. Materials that conduct heat poorly, like wood, plastic, air, and wool, are called insulators; they slow the transfer, which is why saucepan handles are plastic and why we wear wool in winter. Conduction, particle-to-particle in solids, is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: The rate of conduction is captured quantitatively by thermal conductivity. The rate at which heat energy is conducted through a slab of material, the energy per unit time, equals the thermal conductivity of the material, times the cross-sectional area, times the temperature difference across it, divided by the thickness. In symbols, Q over t equals k A delta T over d. So heat is conducted faster through a material with a high conductivity k, like a metal, faster through a larger area, faster when the temperature difference is bigger, and slower through a thicker slab. That's exactly why home insulation is made thick, large d, from low-conductivity materials, small k, to cut the rate of heat loss, and why a large, thin window loses heat quickly. The thermal-conductivity relationship is in Listing two, with a worked example.

QUESTION: A retrieval check on conduction. In particle terms, how does heat travel by conduction through a metal rod? And name one property of metals that makes them especially good conductors. Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. In conduction, the particles at the hot end vibrate with high kinetic energy and collide with their slower-moving neighbours, transferring kinetic energy to them; those particles then collide with theirs, and so the energy passes along the rod from hot to cold while the particles themselves stay in place. And the property that makes metals especially good conductors is that they have free, or delocalised, electrons that move through the metal and carry energy rapidly, in addition to the vibration mechanism. Particle collisions pass the energy along, and free electrons make metals fast conductors. That free-electron point is worth a mark.

NARRATOR: Second, convection. Convection is the transfer of heat by the bulk movement of a heated fluid, a liquid or a gas, so unlike conduction the material itself moves. Here's the mechanism: when part of a fluid is heated, it expands, becomes less dense than the surrounding cooler fluid, and so rises; the cooler, denser fluid sinks to take its place, gets heated in turn, and rises, setting up a circulating flow called a convection current. So convection carries energy by physically moving hot fluid from one place to another. It's why hot air rises above a radiator and warms a room, why the sea breeze blows, why a pot of water heated from the bottom circulates and cooks evenly, and why warm air rising drives weather. The key phrase to remember is "hot rises, cool sinks", driven by the change in density with temperature. Convection needs a fluid that can flow, so it happens in liquids and gases but not in solids. Convection, bulk fluid movement with hot rising and cool sinking, is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Third, radiation. Radiation, more fully thermal radiation, is the transfer of heat by electromagnetic waves, and it is profoundly different from the other two because it needs no medium at all, it travels through a vacuum. Every object emits thermal radiation because of the motion of its charged particles, and the hotter the object, the more it radiates and the shorter the wavelength. This is how the Sun's energy crosses the empty vacuum of space to reach the Earth, conduction and convection simply can't cross a vacuum, only radiation can. It's why you feel the warmth of a fire or a heater on your face across the room, infrared radiation travelling straight to you, and why a black, dull surface, which absorbs and emits radiation well, gets hotter in the sun than a shiny, white one, which reflects it. Radiation links directly to the light topics: thermal radiation is electromagnetic radiation, the same family as visible light, and next year in Module Seven you'll study how a hot object's radiation spectrum depends on its temperature, black-body radiation. Radiation, electromagnetic waves needing no medium, is in Listing one.

QUESTION: A retrieval one worth stopping for. For each of the three modes, conduction, convection and radiation, state in a word or two whether it requires a medium, and which single mode can transfer heat across the vacuum of space. Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Conduction requires a medium, a material whose particles pass energy along, usually a solid. Convection requires a medium too, specifically a fluid that can flow. Radiation requires no medium, it travels as electromagnetic waves through a vacuum. So the single mode that can cross the vacuum of space is radiation, which is exactly how the Sun's heat reaches the Earth. Conduction and convection need a medium, radiation does not. That medium distinction is a classic exam discriminator, get it crisp.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "compare the three methods of heat transfer". The band-six version: conduction is the transfer of heat through a material by particle-to-particle collisions, with no bulk movement of the material, and it occurs mainly in solids, requiring a medium. Convection is the transfer of heat by the bulk movement of a heated fluid, in which warmer, less dense fluid rises and cooler, denser fluid sinks, setting up convection currents; it occurs in liquids and gases and requires a fluid medium. Radiation is the transfer of heat by electromagnetic waves, which requires no medium and can travel through a vacuum, as with the Sun's energy reaching the Earth. The marks are for the mechanism of each, and the medium requirement of each, especially that radiation alone needs no medium. The weak answer, "heat moves by conduction, convection and radiation", names them but gives no mechanism or medium, and earns little. Name the mechanism and the medium for each.

NARRATOR: A lovely way to see all three modes at once is the vacuum flask, the Thermos that keeps a drink hot or cold for hours, because it's designed to defeat every mode of heat transfer. It has double walls with a vacuum sealed between them: the vacuum carries no particles, so it stops both conduction and convection dead, neither can cross empty space. The walls are made of, or coated with, shiny, silvered material, which reflects thermal radiation back rather than emitting or absorbing it, defeating the one mode that can cross the vacuum. And the stopper is made of plastic or cork, a poor conductor, an insulator, to block conduction up through the top. So a single everyday object is engineered against conduction, convection and radiation together, and asking you to explain how each feature blocks a particular mode is a favourite exam question. The same logic, low-conductivity materials, reflective surfaces, and trapped air or vacuum to kill convection, is how a house is insulated and how a survival blanket works.

NARRATOR: Now the second big idea: latent heat, and the change of state. Last episode, adding energy raised the temperature, Q equals m c delta T. But watch what happens if you heat a block of ice steadily. The ice warms up to zero degrees Celsius, and then, as it melts, something strange: you keep adding energy, but the temperature stays at zero, fixed, until all the ice has turned to water. Only then does the temperature start rising again. The same thing happens at the boiling point: the water heats to one hundred degrees, and then sits at one hundred while it boils into steam, however much more energy you pour in, until it's all vapour. During a change of state, the temperature does not change even though heat is being added. The energy you're adding during those flat stretches is called latent heat, from the Latin for "hidden", because it produces no temperature rise. Latent heat is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Why does the temperature stay constant? This is the key conceptual question, and it comes straight from the particle model. Remember temperature is the average kinetic energy of the particles. During a change of state, the energy you add does not go into speeding the particles up; instead it goes into breaking the bonds, the attractive forces, that hold the particles together in the solid or liquid structure. Melting ice means breaking the rigid bonds of the solid so the particles can move past one another as a liquid; boiling water means completely separating the particles so they fly free as a gas. That energy increases the particles' potential energy, the energy stored in their separation, not their kinetic energy, so the average kinetic energy, and hence the temperature, stays constant until the change of state is complete. Adding energy without raising the temperature is the signature of a phase change: the energy goes into the bonds, not into motion. That explanation is the most examinable point in the episode, and it's in Listing three.

QUESTION: A retrieval one. When you add heat to ice that is melting at zero degrees, the temperature stays at zero. Explain, in terms of the particles, where the added energy goes and why the temperature doesn't rise. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The added energy does not increase the kinetic energy of the particles; instead it goes into breaking the bonds, the attractive forces, that hold the water molecules in the rigid solid structure of ice, allowing them to move more freely as a liquid. Because the energy is increasing the particles' potential energy, by separating them, rather than their kinetic energy, the average kinetic energy of the particles stays the same. Since temperature is the average kinetic energy of the particles, the temperature stays constant at zero degrees until all the ice has melted. The energy goes to breaking bonds, not to motion, so the temperature holds steady. That's the whole idea of latent heat in one answer.

NARRATOR: Now the quantitative side. The energy needed to change the state of a substance, with no temperature change, is the mass times the specific latent heat: Q equals m L. The specific latent heat, L, is the energy needed to change the state of one kilogram of the substance, measured in joules per kilogram. There are two kinds. The specific latent heat of fusion is the energy to melt one kilogram of a solid into liquid, or equally the energy released when it freezes; for water it's about three hundred and thirty-four thousand joules per kilogram. The specific latent heat of vaporisation is the energy to boil one kilogram of liquid into gas, or released when it condenses; for water it's much larger, about two million two hundred and sixty thousand joules per kilogram, because separating the particles completely into a gas takes far more energy than just loosening them into a liquid. So melting and boiling each have their own L, and Q equals m L gives the energy. The two latent heats and the equation are in Listing three, with a worked example in Listing four.

QUESTION: Let's use it. How much energy is needed to completely melt two kilograms of ice at zero degrees into water at zero degrees? Take the specific latent heat of fusion of water as three hundred and thirty-four thousand joules per kilogram. Pause the player, set up Q equals m L and solve, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing four. Use Q equals m L. The mass is two kilograms and the latent heat of fusion is three hundred and thirty-four thousand joules per kilogram. So Q equals two times three hundred and thirty-four thousand, which is six hundred and sixty-eight thousand joules, about six hundred and sixty-eight kilojoules. Notice there's no temperature term, because the temperature doesn't change during melting, you use Q equals m L, not Q equals m c delta T. That's the key decision: if the temperature is changing, use m c delta T; if the state is changing at constant temperature, use m L. Choosing the right equation is half the battle.

NARRATOR: That choice deserves spelling out, because the standard hard problem combines both, heating a substance through a temperature change and a change of state. Picture heating ice at minus ten degrees all the way to steam. The process has stages: first warm the ice to zero, m c delta T with the specific heat of ice; then melt it at zero, m L with the latent heat of fusion, temperature constant; then warm the water from zero to one hundred, m c delta T with the specific heat of water; then boil it at one hundred, m L with the latent heat of vaporisation, temperature constant; and finally warm the steam, m c delta T again. You add up the energy for each stage. On a temperature-versus-time graph this shows as sloping sections, where the temperature rises, separated by flat plateaus, where the state changes at constant temperature. The plateaus are the latent-heat stages. Being able to read or sketch that heating curve, and to total the energy across the stages, is a classic exam task, and the heating-curve picture is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can measure the specific latent heat of fusion of ice by adding ice to warm water in an insulated cup and using energy conservation, the heat lost by the water as it cools equals the heat gained melting the ice plus warming the melt-water. You can show the constant-temperature plateau directly by heating a beaker of crushed ice with a thermometer and plotting temperature against time, the flat stretch at zero degrees is the latent heat of fusion in action. You can demonstrate the three transfer modes simply: conduction by heating one end of a metal rod with wax-stuck pins that fall off in sequence; convection by dropping a coloured crystal into heated water and watching the current; radiation by feeling the warmth from a heater or comparing how dull-black and shiny surfaces warm in sunlight. A key examinable point for the latent-heat experiments is insulation, since heat exchanged with the surroundings is the main source of error. Describing such an investigation and its main error is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: thinking the temperature rises during melting or boiling, it stays constant; the energy goes into bonds, not motion. Trap two: using Q equals m c delta T for a change of state, use Q equals m L there, with no temperature term. Trap three: confusing conduction and convection, conduction is particle-to-particle with the material staying put, convection is the bulk movement of a fluid. Trap four: thinking radiation needs a medium, it doesn't, it's the only mode that crosses a vacuum. Trap five: forgetting that the latent heat of vaporisation is much larger than that of fusion, because fully separating particles into a gas takes far more energy than loosening a solid into a liquid. Avoid those five and heat transfer is secure.

QUESTION: Question three. Explain why the temperature of a substance does not change while it is melting, even though energy is continuously supplied. Four marks. Pause, answer, then play.
