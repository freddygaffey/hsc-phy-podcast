NARRATOR: Right now, as you listen to this, particles are raining down through your body. Thousands of them, every minute, through every hand-sized patch of the ground you're standing on. They were born about fifteen kilometres above your head, in the cold, thin air at the edge of space. They are travelling at almost the speed of light. And by every rule of classical physics, not a single one of them should be here.

NARRATOR: These particles are called muons. And the muon has a problem. A muon, sitting at rest in a laboratory, lives for an average of two point two microseconds before it falls apart. Two point two millionths of a second. That is its whole life. After that, it decays into smaller particles and is gone. Two point two microseconds is not very long. Even moving at the absolute cosmic speed limit, the speed of light, a particle that lives only two point two microseconds can travel at most six hundred and sixty metres before it dies. Six hundred and sixty metres. Not even a kilometre.

NARRATOR: So here is the impossible thing. The muons are created fifteen kilometres up. They can only travel six hundred and sixty metres before decaying. And yet we catch them at sea level, fifteen thousand metres below where they were born. A particle that should die after six hundred and sixty metres is routinely travelling fifteen kilometres. That is more than twenty times further than it has any right to go.

QUESTION: So what is it? Is the lifetime wrong? Is the distance wrong? Is something feeding them energy on the way down?

NARRATOR: None of those. The lifetime is right, measured a thousand times over. The distance is right. Nothing is feeding them. What's wrong is something far stranger, and far more beautiful, than any of those answers. The muons are getting away with the impossible because time itself runs differently for them than it does for us. This is the story of how a falling particle became one of the cleanest, most direct pieces of evidence ever found for Albert Einstein's theory of relativity. This is the story of the muon, and of time dilation made real.

NARRATOR: Let's go back to the beginning, to nineteen thirty-six, and to a mountain in Colorado. Two physicists from the California Institute of Technology, Carl Anderson and Seth Neddermeyer, hauled a piece of equipment called a cloud chamber up Pikes Peak, more than four thousand metres above sea level. A cloud chamber is a wonderful thing. It's a sealed box full of supersaturated vapour, and when a charged particle shoots through it, it leaves a trail of tiny droplets behind it, like the vapour trail of a jet across the sky. You can photograph the trail. And if you put the whole chamber inside a magnetic field, the particle's path curves. From how much it curves, you can work out the particle's mass and charge.

NARRATOR: Anderson and Neddermeyer were studying cosmic rays. Cosmic rays are high-energy particles, mostly protons, that come streaming in from deep space, from exploding stars and other violent events across the galaxy. When these rays smash into the top of our atmosphere, they create showers of new particles, and those showers were what rained down through the cloud chamber on Pikes Peak. And in among the tracks, Anderson and Neddermeyer saw something they couldn't explain. Tracks that curved the wrong amount. Too gently curved to be electrons. Too sharply curved to be protons. The particle making these tracks had to have a mass somewhere in the middle. About two hundred times the mass of an electron, but still far lighter than a proton.

NARRATOR: They had found a genuinely new particle. Anderson called it a mesotron, from the Greek word meso, meaning middle, because its mass sat in the middle. Today we call it the muon. Now, at first, everyone was thrilled, because a few years earlier a Japanese physicist, Hideki Yukawa, had predicted that a particle of roughly this mass should exist, a particle that carried the strong nuclear force holding atomic nuclei together. For a while, physicists thought the muon was Yukawa's particle. But it wasn't. When they tested it, the muon barely interacted with atomic nuclei at all. It was the wrong particle entirely. The physicist Isidor Rabi, on hearing that this new thing didn't fit any theory and wasn't needed by anyone, supposedly threw up his hands and asked, who ordered that?

NARRATOR: The muon, it turned out, is a lepton. It's a heavy cousin of the electron. Same negative charge, but about two hundred and seven times the mass. And like a lot of heavy particles, it's unstable. It decays, through the weak nuclear force, into an electron and a pair of ghostly particles called neutrinos. The average time it takes to do this, measured in the muon's own resting frame, is that magic number: two point two microseconds. To be precise, two point one nine seven microseconds. Physicists call this the proper lifetime, because it's the time measured in the muon's own rest frame, the frame in which the two events, birth and decay, happen at the same place. Hold on to that word, proper. It's going to matter enormously. And a quick note for the exam, because your textbook quotes a slightly different number. The two point two microsecond figure is the mean lifetime, the average. The muon's half-life, the time for half a population to decay, is shorter, about one point five microseconds, and you'll sometimes see that value used instead. They describe the same decay, just measured two different ways, and either one delivers the same verdict for our story.

NARRATOR: So now we understand what a muon is, and where the cosmic ones come from. A cosmic ray proton slams into a nitrogen or oxygen nucleus high in the atmosphere, around fifteen kilometres up. That collision creates a spray of particles called pions, and the charged pions decay almost instantly into muons. The muons come streaming downward at tremendous speed, somewhere between ninety-nine and ninety-nine point nine-nine percent of the speed of light. And then the clock starts ticking. Two point two microseconds. Go.

NARRATOR: Let's do the classical calculation, the one any nineteenth-century physicist would have done, the one that gets the answer catastrophically wrong. We have a particle. It lives two point two microseconds. It's moving at, let's be generous, the full speed of light, three times ten to the eight metres per second. How far does it get? Distance equals speed times time. Three times ten to the eight metres per second, multiplied by two point two times ten to the minus six seconds. That comes out to about six hundred and sixty metres. The full working is in Listing one. And remember, that's the absolute best case, assuming the muon somehow hits light speed exactly. A realistic cosmic muon at ninety-eight percent of light speed manages only about six hundred and fifty metres.

NARRATOR: Six hundred and sixty metres, against a journey of fifteen thousand metres. If classical physics were right, the muons would die more than fourteen kilometres before reaching the ground. The number that should arrive at sea level isn't small, it's essentially zero. The muon decay follows an exponential curve, and after a journey twenty times longer than its lifetime, the fraction surviving is a tiny sliver of a fraction of a percent. We should detect almost nothing.

NARRATOR: And yet. About ten thousand muons strike every square metre of the Earth's surface every single minute. Hold your hand out flat. Hundreds of muons are passing through it as you do. The detectors don't lie. The muons are here, in enormous numbers, having travelled a distance that classical physics says is flatly impossible. So one of our cherished assumptions has to break. And the assumption that breaks is the one nobody had ever thought to question: that time is the same for everyone.

NARRATOR: Here's where Einstein comes in. In nineteen oh five, Einstein published the special theory of relativity, built on two simple-sounding postulates. One, the laws of physics are the same in every inertial reference frame, every frame moving at constant velocity. And two, the speed of light in a vacuum is the same for every observer, no matter how fast they're moving or how fast the light's source is moving. That second postulate sounds harmless. It is not harmless. If everyone, regardless of their own motion, measures light to travel at exactly the same speed, then something else has to give. And the thing that gives is time.

NARRATOR: The consequence is called time dilation, and it works like this. If a clock is moving relative to you, you will measure that clock to tick more slowly than your own. Not because anything's wrong with the clock. Because moving clocks genuinely run slow, as measured from a frame they're moving through. The faster the clock moves, the bigger the effect. And the size of the effect is captured by a single number, the Lorentz factor, written as the Greek letter gamma.

NARRATOR: Gamma is one divided by the square root of one minus v squared over c squared, where v is the speed of the moving thing and c is the speed of light. You don't need to feel that equation in your bones right now, just feel what it does. When v is small, much slower than light, gamma is almost exactly one, and time dilation is negligible. That's why you've never noticed it. But as v climbs toward the speed of light, the thing under the square root shrinks toward zero, and gamma blows up. At ninety-eight percent of light speed, gamma is about five. At ninety-nine point nine-seven percent, gamma is around forty. The faster you go, the more dramatically your clock slows down relative to a stationary observer.

NARRATOR: Now, watch what this does for our doomed muon. From our point of view, standing on the ground, the muon is screaming past at, let's say, a speed that gives a gamma of about fifteen. That corresponds to about ninety-nine point seven-eight percent of light speed. The muon's internal clock, the clock that decides when it decays, is running slow by a factor of fifteen, as we measure it.

QUESTION: Hang on. So how long does the muon last, from our point of view on the ground? Pause the player, work it out with a gamma of fifteen, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. In the muon's own frame, it lives two point two microseconds. That never changes. But in our frame, on the ground, the time we measure is gamma times that proper lifetime. Fifteen times two point two microseconds. That's thirty-three microseconds. As measured by us, the muon survives not for two point two microseconds, but for thirty-three. It's living fifteen times longer, from our perspective, because its clock is running fifteen times slow.

NARRATOR: And thirty-three microseconds changes everything. Let's redo the distance. The muon travels at about ninety-nine point seven-eight percent of light speed, near enough to three times ten to the eight metres per second, for thirty-three microseconds. Distance equals speed times time. That gives roughly ten thousand metres, ten kilometres. The full calculation is in Listing two. Suddenly the muon isn't capped at six hundred and sixty metres. It's covering something on the order of ten kilometres, comfortably the same scale as the journey down through the atmosphere. The impossible trip is no longer impossible. Time dilation bought the muon a stay of execution, and that stay is exactly long enough to reach the ground.

QUESTION: But wait. This feels like a cheat. From the muon's own point of view, it still only lives two point two microseconds. So how does the muon itself reach the ground? It doesn't get the extra time.

NARRATOR: That is the most beautiful question in this whole story, and the answer is the part that convinces you relativity is real and not some accounting trick. You're absolutely right that the muon does not experience thirty-three microseconds. In its own frame, the muon's clock ticks normally, and it lives its full proper lifetime of two point two microseconds, no more. From the muon's perspective, time dilation isn't happening to it at all. So how does it cross fifteen kilometres in two point two microseconds, when even light couldn't do that?

NARRATOR: It doesn't have to. Because in the muon's frame, the fifteen kilometres isn't fifteen kilometres anymore. Special relativity has a second consequence that travels hand in hand with time dilation, and it's called length contraction. Just as moving clocks run slow, moving distances shrink, along the direction of motion, by that same factor of gamma. So from the muon's point of view, it's sitting still and the Earth is rushing up to meet it at ninety-nine point seven-eight percent of light speed. And the entire atmosphere, all fifteen kilometres of it, is squashed flat in the direction of motion. Fifteen kilometres divided by gamma, divided by fifteen, is just one kilometre. The muon only has to cross one kilometre of contracted atmosphere. At nearly the speed of light, that takes a bit over three microseconds, and a good fraction of muons survive that. The full working is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Do you see what just happened? We have two completely different stories. In our frame, the muon's clock runs slow and the muon lives long enough to cross the full fifteen kilometres. In the muon's frame, its clock runs perfectly normally, but the atmosphere is contracted to one kilometre, so a normal-length life is enough to cross it. Two different explanations. Time dilation for us, length contraction for the muon. And they give exactly the same answer: the muon reaches the ground. The two frames disagree about why, but they agree completely about what. That self-consistency, that the universe never contradicts itself no matter whose stopwatch you trust, is the deep signature that relativity is correct.

NARRATOR: Now, this is a gorgeous argument on paper. But physics lives and dies on measurement. Could anyone actually catch the muons in the act, and prove the numbers? The first people to do it were Bruno Rossi and David Hall, in nineteen forty and forty-one. Rossi was an Italian physicist who'd fled Mussolini's Italy. With Hall, he carried particle detectors up into the Colorado Rockies and compared the muon count high on a mountain with the count lower down. The idea was simple and brilliant: count the muons high up, count them lower down, and see how many died in between. If classical physics were right, almost all of them should have decayed over that height difference. And here is where your textbook lays out the numbers beautifully. The journey between the two detectors took about six point five microseconds according to Earth-based clocks. But the muons decayed as though only about zero point seven microseconds had passed. Divide those two times and you get the Lorentz factor directly, a gamma of roughly nine point three, which corresponds to a muon speed of about ninety-nine point four percent of the speed of light. Rossi and Hall found that far more muons survived than classical physics allowed, exactly the survival rate the relativistic prediction demands. And notice the two-frame story is right there in the data: from Earth's frame the muons' clocks ran slow and their lifetime stretched out, while from the muons' own frame the tall mountain was length-contracted down to a small hill. The full Rossi and Hall numbers, the ones your textbook works through, are in Listing eight. It was the first experimental evidence of time dilation acting on a moving clock. But it was, honestly, a bit rough around the edges.

NARRATOR: The truly definitive version, the one that became legendary, came twenty years later, in nineteen sixty-two and sixty-three. Two physicists from MIT, David Frisch and James Smith, decided to make the muon experiment so clean, so visual, so undeniable, that you could teach relativity with it. And they picked a spectacular stage: Mount Washington, in New Hampshire. Mount Washington is famous for having some of the worst weather on Earth; the wind speed record stood there for decades. Its summit sits at nineteen hundred and seventeen metres.

NARRATOR: Frisch and Smith took their muon detector to the summit of Mount Washington. They set it to count only muons in a particular narrow band of speed, around ninety-nine point five percent of the speed of light, so they knew exactly how fast their muons were going. At the summit, they counted about five hundred and sixty-five muons per hour passing through their detector. Then, and this is the part I love, they physically loaded the apparatus into a vehicle, drove it down the mountain, all the way to Cambridge, Massachusetts, essentially sea level, and counted again.

QUESTION: Here's the test. Of those five hundred and sixty-five muons per hour seen at the summit, how many should still be alive at the bottom, if there were no time dilation? And how many did they actually find?

NARRATOR: Let's do the classical prediction first. The muons take about six point three microseconds to make the trip from the summit down to Cambridge, as measured on the ground. Six point three microseconds is nearly three muon lifetimes. Without time dilation, after three lifetimes, the exponential decay leaves you with only about five percent of your particles. Five hundred and sixty-five per hour at the top should collapse to roughly twenty-seven per hour at the bottom. Classical physics says: almost all of them die on the way down. Twenty-seven survivors.

NARRATOR: But relativity says the muons' clocks are running slow by their Lorentz factor, which at this speed is about ten. So the six point three microsecond journey, as far as the muons' decay is concerned, feels like only about zero point seven microseconds of internal time. Less than a third of a lifetime. Hardly any of them should die. Relativity predicts that something like four hundred per hour should survive the trip.

NARRATOR: Two predictions, wildly different. Twenty-seven per hour if Newton is right. Around four hundred per hour if Einstein is right. They drove the detector down to Cambridge, they counted, and they found four hundred and nine muons per hour, plus or minus nine. Four hundred and nine. Almost exactly Einstein's number, and nowhere remotely near Newton's. From their data they could extract the time dilation factor directly. They measured a dilation of eight point eight, plus or minus zero point eight, against a predicted value of around eight point four. Agreement, dead on, within experimental error. The numbers are laid out in Listing four.

NARRATOR: And the most charming part: Frisch and Smith filmed the whole thing. They made an educational movie in nineteen sixty-two, called Time Dilation, an Experiment with Mu-Mesons, part of a series made specifically to teach physics to students. There's footage of a working scientist standing on the side of Mount Washington, in the wind, explaining Einstein's relativity while the muons pour down out of the sky and into his detector. Decades later, Mount Washington was named a historic site by the American Physical Society for this experiment. You could see relativity happening. You could count it, muon by muon.

NARRATOR: So that's the muons. But the muons are particles, born of cosmic rays, far removed from ordinary life. Could you ever see time dilation acting on something human-scale? On an actual clock, a real clock, that you could hold? In nineteen seventy-one, two Americans set out to do exactly that, and the way they did it is one of the great stories of experimental physics.

NARRATOR: The physicist was Joseph Hafele, of Washington University in St. Louis. The astronomer was Richard Keating, of the United States Naval Observatory in Washington. Hafele had worked out something remarkable: atomic clocks, the most precise clocks ever built, had finally become accurate enough that they could detect the tiny time dilation produced by an ordinary commercial airliner. Not a particle accelerator. Not a cosmic ray. A passenger jet, the kind you could buy a ticket on. The U.S. Office of Naval Research gave them eight thousand dollars to test it.

NARRATOR: Of that eight thousand dollars, seven thousand six hundred went on airfares. Because here was their plan. They had four caesium-beam atomic clocks. A caesium clock keeps time by counting the oscillations of caesium atoms, more than nine billion of them per second, with staggering precision. The clocks were too large and too delicate to put in the cargo hold, so Hafele and Keating bought each clock its own seat on the plane. On the passenger manifest, the clocks were booked under the name Mister Clock. And they took turns, Hafele and Keating, flying around the entire world with these clocks strapped into the seats beside them. First they flew all the way around the world heading east, then all the way around heading west, while back at the Naval Observatory an identical set of clocks stayed on the ground, ticking, as the reference.

NARRATOR: Now here's where it gets subtle, and genuinely clever, because there are two relativistic effects fighting each other on those flights. The first is special relativity, time dilation from motion. The plane is moving fast relative to the centre of the Earth, so its clock should tick slow. But here's the twist: the Earth itself is rotating, carrying the ground clocks eastward too. A plane flying east adds its speed to the Earth's rotation, so it's moving faster relative to the Earth's centre, and its clock runs slower. A plane flying west moves against the Earth's rotation, so it's actually moving slower relative to the centre, and that effect reverses.

NARRATOR: And then there's a second effect, from general relativity, Einstein's theory of gravity, which says clocks run faster where gravity is weaker. The plane flies at around ten kilometres up, higher than the ground clocks, where Earth's gravity is very slightly weaker. So altitude makes the flying clocks tick faster, on both flights, east and west alike. Two effects, sometimes adding, sometimes subtracting. The predictions were precise and, frankly, bold: the eastbound clocks should lose about forty nanoseconds compared to the ground, and the westbound clocks should gain about two hundred and seventy-five nanoseconds.

QUESTION: Nanoseconds. Billionths of a second, after flying all the way around the world. Could clocks really be read accurately enough to catch a difference that small? And did it work?

NARRATOR: It worked. They flew east, they flew west, they brought the clocks home, and they compared them against the clocks that had stayed at the Naval Observatory. The eastbound clocks had lost fifty-nine nanoseconds. Predicted, around forty, within the margin of error. The westbound clocks had gained two hundred and seventy-three nanoseconds. Predicted, two hundred and seventy-five. The westbound result agreed with Einstein to within a tenth of a standard deviation. Essentially perfect. The numbers, both effects broken out separately, are in Listing five.

NARRATOR: Think about what that means. Two clocks, set ticking together. One flies around the world, the other stays home. They are brought back together, side by side, and they no longer agree. They are reading different times. Not because one is faulty. Because they genuinely experienced different amounts of time. The travelling clock literally aged less, or more, than the one that stayed behind. There is no putting it back, no correction to apply. Time really did pass differently for them. This is the twin paradox, made out of metal and caesium and airline tickets, and it came out exactly the way Einstein said it would, half a century earlier.

NARRATOR: There were sceptics, of course. Some physicists worried that tiny systematic errors in the caesium clocks could account for the result. So in nineteen ninety-six, the experiment was repeated with far more precise clocks, and the results were confirmed to even tighter tolerance. Time dilation, on a passenger jet, is simply real.

NARRATOR: And the muons and the atomic clocks are only two strands of a much thicker rope. The syllabus asks you to weigh the evidence for relativity, and the evidence runs far wider than these two cases. In particle accelerators, unstable particles are routinely flung around at near light speed and live measurably longer than their resting cousins, exactly as time dilation says they must. In two thousand and fourteen, a team measured the transition frequencies of atoms travelling at a third of the speed of light in an accelerator and matched Einstein's time dilation to the highest precision ever achieved. And out in the cosmos, the very cosmic rays that make our muons are protons and alpha particles screaming across the galaxy at near light speed; from their point of view, almost no time passes on journeys we would clock at millions of years. Muon, atomic clock, accelerator, cosmos, four independent witnesses, all telling the same story.

NARRATOR: And now we arrive at the part where this stops being a curiosity and starts running your daily life. Because there is a system, right now, in orbit above your head, that would fail completely, within minutes, if its engineers had not taken Einstein's relativity seriously. That system is GPS, the Global Positioning System.

NARRATOR: GPS works by triangulation using time. A network of satellites, orbiting about twenty thousand kilometres up, each carries an atomic clock, and each constantly broadcasts the precise time. Your phone picks up signals from several satellites at once, compares the tiny differences in their arrival times, and from those differences works out exactly where you are. The whole thing depends on knowing the time, on every satellite, to within billionths of a second. And here, both of relativity's clock effects show up at full strength.

NARRATOR: The satellites are moving fast, about three point nine kilometres per second. Special relativity says their clocks run slow because of that motion, by about seven point two microseconds per day. But the satellites are also way up high, twenty thousand kilometres up, where Earth's gravity is much weaker. General relativity says their clocks run fast because of that weaker gravity, by about forty-five point eight microseconds per day. The two effects don't cancel. Put them together and the satellite clocks run fast, overall, by about thirty-eight microseconds every single day, compared to clocks on the ground.

QUESTION: Thirty-eight microseconds a day. That sounds tiny. Who cares about thirty-eight millionths of a second?

NARRATOR: You do, because GPS measures position using the time it takes light to travel, and light covers about thirty centimetres every nanosecond. Thirty-eight microseconds is thirty-eight thousand nanoseconds. If you let that error accumulate, untreated, for a single day, your position fix drifts off by about ten kilometres. Ten kilometres a day, and growing. Within a couple of minutes of switching on an uncorrected system, your GPS would already be putting you on the wrong street. Within hours it would be useless, then absurd. Every navigation app, every aircraft, every shipping container, every emergency call traced to a location, would simply stop working.

NARRATOR: So the engineers built relativity directly into the system. Before launch, they deliberately set the satellite clocks to tick at the wrong frequency. The target frequency is ten point two-three megahertz, but they tune each clock, on the ground, to ten point two-two-nine-nine-nine-nine-nine-five-four-three megahertz. A hair slow. So that once the satellite is up in orbit, moving fast and floating in weaker gravity, relativity speeds the clock up by exactly the right amount, and it ticks at the correct rate as seen from the ground. They literally pre-compensate for Einstein before the rocket leaves the pad. GPS is the first and most economically important machine humanity has ever built that would not work without the theory of relativity. Every time your phone shows a blue dot on a map, that blue dot is in the right place because somebody took time dilation seriously.

NARRATOR: Let's step all the way back. We started with an impossibility. A particle that lives only long enough to cross six hundred and sixty metres, somehow crossing fifteen kilometres to reach the ground. The resolution was not a trick or a loophole. It was a discovery: that time is not the rigid, universal thing we feel it to be. Time runs slow for things that move fast. Distances shrink along the direction of motion. The faster you go, the more pronounced it gets, and at the speeds cosmic muons travel, the effect is enormous, a factor of fifteen, a factor of forty. The muon survives its impossible journey because, from our frame, its clock crawls, and from its own frame, the world it falls through is squashed thin. Both stories agree on the ending, and that agreement is relativity proving itself consistent.

NARRATOR: And the muons are not alone. Rossi and Hall first caught the effect on a Colorado mountain. Frisch and Smith pinned it to two significant figures on Mount Washington and put it on film. Hafele and Keating flew atomic clocks around the world in airline seats booked under the name Mister Clock, and watched them disagree on landing. And every GPS satellite overhead carries a clock deliberately mistuned to account for the fact that time, up there, runs fast. What began as a strange equation Einstein wrote down in nineteen oh five, an idea so counterintuitive that great physicists resisted it for years, is now confirmed every minute, by particles falling through your body, and by the map in your pocket finding its way home.
