NARRATOR: So far in Dynamics we've explained motion with forces, net force equals mass times acceleration. Today we meet a second, often quicker, way to analyse motion: energy. Work, kinetic energy, potential energy, the conservation of mechanical energy, and power. The energy approach is powerful because it lets you connect the start and end of a motion without tracking every force at every instant, and because conservation of energy is one of the deepest principles in all of physics. By the end you'll be able to calculate work and power, apply the work-energy theorem, and use conservation of mechanical energy to solve problems that would be messy with forces alone. But first, a quick recap, because today is the energy view of the very motion we've been forcing our way through.

NARRATOR: Last episode we put a block on an incline and resolved its weight: the down-slope component, m g sine theta, drives it, and friction opposes it. We found the net force along the slope and, from the second law, the acceleration, then handed that to the suvat equations. And the episode before set up the whole module: net force equals m a, and the distinction between mass, the scalar amount of matter, and weight, the gravitational force m g. Hold the incline picture especially, because today we'll re-solve that same sliding block with energy instead of forces, and get the answer faster: the block loses gravitational potential energy and gains kinetic energy. Forces and energy are two lenses on the same motion, and a strong student picks whichever is quicker for the question.

QUESTION: Quick check before we start. From last episode, what drives a block down a frictionless incline, and what is its acceleration in terms of g and the slope angle? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: The down-slope component of the weight, m g sine theta, drives it, and the acceleration is g sine theta, independent of mass. Hold that, because today we'll show that the same block, dropping a certain height down the slope, arrives at the bottom with a speed you can get straight from energy conservation, no angle needed. Let's build the energy toolkit, starting with work.

NARRATOR: Work, in physics, has a precise meaning, not the everyday one. Work is done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force. Quantitatively, the work done is the force times the displacement in the direction of the force: work equals force times distance, and when we mean the net force over a displacement, work equals net force times displacement, W equals F net times s. Work is a scalar, measured in joules, one joule is one newton acting over one metre. Three things follow that the exam tests. First, no displacement means no work: if you push against a wall that doesn't move, you do zero work on it, however tired you get. Second, a force perpendicular to the motion does no work: carrying a tray horizontally, the upward force you exert is perpendicular to the horizontal motion, so it does no work. Third, work transfers energy: doing work on an object changes its energy, which is the bridge to everything else today.

NARRATOR: That bridge is kinetic energy, the energy of motion. An object of mass m moving at speed v has kinetic energy equal to one half m v squared, in joules. And here's the link to work, the work-energy theorem: the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy. If you do positive net work on an object, you speed it up, increasing its kinetic energy; negative net work, like friction, slows it down. This is why work and energy connect so neatly to the forces of the last two episodes: the net force does work over a distance, and that work shows up as a change in kinetic energy. The work-energy theorem is in Listing one, and it's an alternative to "net force gives acceleration, then suvat", often a shortcut.

NARRATOR: Next, gravitational potential energy, the energy stored by virtue of position in a gravitational field. Lift an object up and you do work against gravity, and that work is stored as gravitational potential energy. The change in gravitational potential energy is m g times the change in height, written delta U equals m g delta h, in joules. Lift a one-kilogram object one metre and you store about nine point eight joules. Note it's the change in height that matters, so you measure from whatever reference level the problem gives, the ground, a table, wherever. Lower the object and it gives that energy back. So gravitational potential energy is the energy banked by lifting, ready to be released as the object falls.

NARRATOR: Now the powerful principle that ties kinetic and potential energy together: conservation of mechanical energy. Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic and gravitational potential energy. In the absence of friction and air resistance, mechanical energy is conserved, it stays constant, energy just transforms back and forth between kinetic and potential. A ball thrown up slows as it rises: kinetic energy converts to potential energy. At the top, it's all potential. Coming down, potential converts back to kinetic. The total stays the same throughout. This lets you solve motion problems by equating energy at two points, without any forces or times. For a falling object, the gravitational potential energy lost equals the kinetic energy gained: m g delta h equals one half m v squared. Notice the mass cancels, so the speed gained depends only on the height dropped, not the mass, the energy version of "all objects fall at the same rate".

QUESTION: Let me have you use conservation of energy. A ball is dropped from a height of five metres. Using energy conservation, find its speed just before it hits the ground. Take g as nine point eight, and ignore air resistance. Pause the player, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and watch how clean energy makes it. The gravitational potential energy lost equals the kinetic energy gained: m g delta h equals one half m v squared. The mass cancels from both sides, leaving g delta h equals one half v squared, so v squared equals two g delta h, and v equals the square root of two g times the height. Put in the numbers: two times nine point eight times five is ninety-eight, and the square root of ninety-eight is about nine point nine metres per second. So the ball hits at about nine point nine metres per second. Notice we never used time or the equations of motion, we just equated energy at the top and the bottom, and the mass cancelled. You'd get the identical answer from suvat with v squared equals u squared plus two a s, because energy conservation and the equations of motion are consistent, but the energy route was faster. The working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: This is exactly why energy beats forces for the incline. That block sliding down a frictionless slope: instead of resolving the weight, finding the acceleration, and using suvat, just say the gravitational potential energy lost, m g times the vertical height dropped, equals the kinetic energy gained, one half m v squared. The speed at the bottom depends only on the vertical height it descended, not on the angle or the length of the slope or the mass. A steep short slope and a gentle long slope to the same vertical drop give the same final speed, because the same potential energy was converted. That's a result that's awkward with forces but obvious with energy, and the exam rewards choosing the energy method when height is involved.

NARRATOR: Now, what about friction? Friction transforms mechanical energy into heat, so when friction acts, mechanical energy is not conserved, some of it becomes thermal energy. But, and this is the crucial exam phrasing, energy overall is still conserved; it's just been transformed into a form, heat, that's no longer mechanical. So never say energy is "lost" or "used up", say it's transformed, in this case to heat and sound. The work done against friction equals the friction force times the distance, and that's the amount of mechanical energy converted to heat. For the block on a rough incline: the potential energy lost equals the kinetic energy gained plus the energy converted to heat by friction. Energy bookkeeping always balances; you just have to include every form.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. A student says "the car's kinetic energy is lost when it brakes to a stop". What's wrong with that statement, and how should it be phrased? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The kinetic energy is not lost, it's transformed. When the car brakes, friction in the brakes converts the car's kinetic energy into heat, warming the brake pads and discs, and a little into sound. The total energy is conserved; it has simply changed from kinetic energy into thermal energy. So the correct phrasing is "the kinetic energy is transformed into heat by friction in the brakes", not "lost" or "used up". This matters because the law of conservation of energy says energy can never be destroyed, only transformed, and markers specifically penalise "lost" or "used up". Transformed, not destroyed, every time. If you caught that, you've got the single most important piece of energy language in the course.

NARRATOR: Let me step back and name the forms of energy and the conservation law properly, because it's the conceptual spine of the topic. Energy is the capacity to do work, and it's a scalar, in joules. It comes in many forms: kinetic, the energy of motion; gravitational potential, stored by height; elastic potential, stored in a stretched spring or trampoline; thermal, the energy of particle motion; chemical, stored in fuels and food; light, sound, electrical, and nuclear energy. The law of conservation of energy says the total energy of an isolated system is constant, energy can be transformed from one form to another but never created or destroyed. A trampolinist is a parade of transformations: chemical energy in their muscles to kinetic as they jump, kinetic to gravitational potential as they rise, potential to kinetic coming down, kinetic to elastic potential as the mat stretches, then back to kinetic, with a little lost to heat and sound each bounce, which is why they'd slowly stop without re-pushing. The forms and the transformations are listed in Listing four. Every problem in this topic is really energy moving between these forms while the total stays fixed.

NARRATOR: Let me work the work-energy theorem directly, because it's a shortcut worth having. The theorem says the net work done equals the change in kinetic energy. Suppose a net force of one hundred newtons acts on a ten-kilogram object over a distance of twenty metres, starting from rest. The net work is force times distance, one hundred times twenty, two thousand joules. By the theorem, that equals the change in kinetic energy, so the final kinetic energy is two thousand joules, since it started from rest. Then one half m v squared equals two thousand, so v squared equals four thousand over ten, four hundred, and v is twenty metres per second. Notice we found the final speed without ever computing the acceleration or the time, the work went straight to kinetic energy. That's the work-energy theorem as a shortcut: net work in equals kinetic energy gained, and it sidesteps the suvat equations entirely when you care about speed over a distance.

NARRATOR: Finally, power, the rate of doing work or transferring energy. Power equals the energy transferred, or work done, divided by the time taken, P equals delta E over time, measured in watts, one watt is one joule per second. A more powerful motor does the same work in less time, or more work in the same time. There's a second, very useful form: since work is force times distance, and distance over time is velocity, power also equals force times velocity, P equals F v. This is handy for things moving at steady speed against a resistance, a car cruising against air resistance, a winch hauling at constant speed: the power equals the driving force times the speed. So two formulas for power, energy over time, and force times velocity, and they're in Listing one. Applications the syllabus names: lifting objects against gravity, and working against air resistance, rolling resistance and friction.

NARRATOR: Let me work a power example with the force-times-velocity form, because it's the one students forget. A car drives at a constant one hundred kilometres per hour, about twenty-eight metres per second, against a total resistance force, air resistance plus rolling resistance, of seven hundred newtons. What power must the engine deliver to maintain that speed? Because the speed is constant, the driving force exactly balances the resistance, so the driving force is seven hundred newtons. The power is force times velocity, seven hundred times twenty-eight, about nineteen thousand six hundred watts, roughly twenty kilowatts. Notice the logic: at constant speed there's no acceleration, so the net force is zero, which means the engine's driving force equals the resistance, and the power delivered just replaces the energy being transformed to heat by air and rolling resistance. That's why a faster cruising speed needs disproportionately more power, the resistance rises with speed and the power is force times that speed. The worked power examples are in Listing three.

NARRATOR: A word on positive and negative work and the named applications, because the syllabus lists them. Work can be negative: when a force acts opposite to the motion, like friction or air resistance, it does negative work, removing kinetic energy. So "work done against friction" or "work done against air resistance" is the magnitude of energy transformed out of the mechanical store, friction force times distance. The syllabus names several contexts to analyse with energy and power: uniformly accelerated motion, where the net work equals the kinetic energy gained; objects raised against gravity, where the work equals m g delta h and the power is that over time; and work done against air resistance, rolling resistance and friction, where the work done against the resistance equals the resistive force times the distance, and it ends up as heat. In every case the bookkeeping is the same: energy in equals useful energy out plus energy transformed to heat. That balance is what an energy analysis is, and it's why energy is often the cleanest way to handle motion with resistance.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "use conservation of energy to determine the speed of a ball at the bottom of a frictionless ramp". The band-six version: state that in the absence of friction, mechanical energy is conserved, so the gravitational potential energy lost equals the kinetic energy gained; write m g delta h equals one half m v squared; cancel the mass and rearrange to v equals the square root of two g delta h; substitute the height and g to get the speed; and note that the result is independent of mass and of the ramp's angle, depending only on the vertical height. The marks are for naming conservation of mechanical energy, equating the two energies, the correct algebra, and the insight that mass and angle don't matter, only the height.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the gap. A weak answer says "the energy turns into speed, so it goes faster at the bottom". That earns little: it doesn't name conservation of mechanical energy, doesn't equate potential to kinetic, doesn't give the formula or the algebra. The strong answer states the principle, writes m g delta h equals one half m v squared, and solves it. Name the conservation principle, equate the energies, solve. That's the difference.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: saying energy is "lost" or "used up", it's transformed, conserved overall. Trap two: forgetting work needs displacement in the direction of the force, no movement or a perpendicular force means zero work. Trap three: using the slope length instead of the vertical height in m g delta h, potential energy depends only on the change in height. Trap four: confusing power with energy, power is energy per unit time, watts not joules. Trap five: forgetting that with friction present, mechanical energy isn't conserved, you must add the heat term. Avoid those and the energy method is clean.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the threads together. Today is the energy lens on the forces of the last two episodes: the net force does work, and that work changes kinetic energy, the work-energy theorem linking Dynamics's forces to energy. Back to Module One, there's a lovely graph connection: work is the area under a force-displacement graph, the same "area under the graph" idea as displacement being the area under a velocity-time graph. Forward, energy is everywhere: in Module Three, thermal energy and the conservation of energy across heat transfers; and the deepest extension of all, in Module Seven and Eight, Einstein's mass-energy equivalence, energy equals mass times the speed of light squared, where even mass is a form of energy and the conservation law becomes conservation of mass-energy. The principle you learn today, energy is conserved and only transforms, is one you'll use for the rest of the course and it never once breaks.

NARRATOR: A word on strategy, because the exam often lets you choose between the forces method and the energy method, and picking the right one saves time. Reach for energy when the question involves speeds and heights or distances but doesn't mention time, and when you only care about the start and end states, not the journey in between, conservation of mechanical energy connects two points in one line. Reach for forces and the second law when you need the acceleration itself, or a force, or anything involving time, since energy is timeless and can't directly give you a duration. And the two must always agree: the speed at the bottom of a ramp comes out the same whether you use m g h equals one half m v squared or resolve the forces and apply v squared equals u squared plus two a s, because the work-energy theorem is the second law integrated over distance. So they're not rival theories, they're two routes to the same physics, and the skill is choosing the shorter route. When you see "height" and "speed" with no time, think energy first; when you see "acceleration", "force", or "time", think Newton's laws.

NARRATOR: Let me recap the takeaways, phrased for marks. One: work is force times displacement in the direction of the force, a scalar in joules, and it transfers energy; no displacement or a perpendicular force means no work. Two: kinetic energy is one half m v squared, and the net work done equals the change in kinetic energy, the work-energy theorem. Three: gravitational potential energy change is m g delta h, depending only on the vertical height. Four: in the absence of friction, mechanical energy, kinetic plus potential, is conserved, so potential lost equals kinetic gained, and the result is independent of mass; with friction, energy is transformed to heat, never lost. Five: power is the rate of energy transfer, P equals energy over time, also force times velocity, in watts. Hold those and the energy method is yours.

QUESTION: Question one. Define work and state its unit. Then explain why no work is done when a person holds a heavy box stationary. Three marks. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Work is the product of the force applied to an object and the displacement of the object in the direction of the force; its unit is the joule. No work is done on a stationary box because there is no displacement, and work requires the object to move in the direction of the force; with zero displacement, the work is zero, regardless of the force exerted. Three marks: work as force times displacement; the unit joule; and zero work because there's no displacement. The everyday sense of "work" as effort is the trap, physically, no displacement means no work.

QUESTION: Question two. A two-kilogram object falls from a height of ten metres. Using conservation of energy, calculate its speed just before impact. Take g as nine point eight, ignore air resistance. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By conservation of mechanical energy, the gravitational potential energy lost equals the kinetic energy gained: m g delta h equals one half m v squared. The mass cancels, so v squared equals two g delta h, which is two times nine point eight times ten, one hundred and ninety-six, so v is the square root of one hundred and ninety-six, fourteen metres per second. Three marks: equating potential to kinetic energy; the mass cancelling; and the answer fourteen metres per second. The two-kilogram mass is a distractor, it cancels, the speed depends only on the height.

QUESTION: Question three. A crane lifts a five-hundred-kilogram load to a height of twenty metres in ten seconds at constant speed. Calculate the work done against gravity and the power output. Take g as nine point eight. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing three. The work done against gravity equals the gain in gravitational potential energy, m g delta h, five hundred times nine point eight times twenty, which is ninety-eight thousand joules. The power is the work divided by the time, ninety-eight thousand joules over ten seconds, nine thousand eight hundred watts, or nine point eight kilowatts. Three marks: work as m g delta h equals ninety-eight thousand joules; power as work over time; nine thousand eight hundred watts. Note "constant speed" means the kinetic energy doesn't change, so all the work goes into potential energy.

QUESTION: Question four. Explain, in terms of energy transformations, what happens to the kinetic energy of a car as it brakes to a stop, and why it is incorrect to say the energy is destroyed. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. As the car brakes, friction between the brake pads and discs transforms the car's kinetic energy into thermal energy, heating the brakes, with a small amount becoming sound. The kinetic energy is therefore transformed into other forms, not destroyed. It is incorrect to say the energy is destroyed because the law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another; the total energy is conserved throughout. Three marks: kinetic energy transformed to heat by friction; the conservation-of-energy principle; and that energy is transformed, not destroyed. This is the "transformed, not lost" point, worth a guaranteed mark.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A four-kilogram trolley moving at six metres per second along a frictionless surface runs up a ramp. Using conservation of energy, calculate the maximum vertical height it reaches. Then explain what would change if the ramp were rough. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By conservation of mechanical energy on the frictionless ramp, all the kinetic energy converts to gravitational potential energy at the highest point, where the trolley momentarily stops: one half m v squared equals m g delta h. The mass cancels, so delta h equals v squared over two g, thirty-six over nineteen point six, about one point eight four metres. So the trolley rises about one point eight metres vertically. If the ramp were rough, friction would transform some of the kinetic energy into heat as the trolley climbs, so less energy would be available to become gravitational potential energy, and the trolley would reach a lower maximum height. Four marks: equating kinetic to potential energy; the mass cancelling; the height of about one point eight metres; and the explanation that friction transforms some energy to heat so the height is reduced. Notice energy conservation solved the height in one line, where forces would have needed the ramp angle, and the friction discussion is the "transformed, not lost" idea again.
