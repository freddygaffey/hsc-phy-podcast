NARRATOR: In the spring of eighteen ninety-seven, in a cramped basement laboratory in Cambridge, a forty-year-old physicist with poor eyesight and famously clumsy hands was pointing a beam of invisible rays at a magnet. The rays themselves were a mystery. Nobody knew what they were. They poured out of a metal plate at the negative end of a glass tube, raced down its length, and made the far glass glow a soft, ghostly green. For nearly twenty years, the cleverest people in Europe had argued about what these rays actually were. Were they waves, like light? Or were they particles, like tiny bullets? The physicist in that basement was about to settle the argument. But in doing so, he would prove something so strange, so unwelcome, that when he announced it, much of his audience simply did not believe him.

NARRATOR: He had discovered that the atom — the thing that every chemist and physicist on Earth agreed was the smallest possible object, indivisible by definition, the ultimate full stop of matter — was not the smallest thing at all. Inside it was something smaller. A particle nearly two thousand times lighter than the lightest atom known. A piece of matter in a state nobody had ever imagined. His name was Joseph John Thomson, and the particle he found is the reason you can hear this podcast at all.

QUESTION: So how do you find something smaller than the smallest thing there is? And how do you even begin, when the thing you are chasing is completely invisible?

NARRATOR: To understand what Thomson did, we have to go back further, to a piece of laboratory equipment that, in the eighteen fifties, was the most advanced toy in physics. It started with a German glassblower named Heinrich Geissler. Geissler built vacuum pumps, and in eighteen fifty-five he made one good enough to suck the air out of a sealed glass tube until only about one ten-thousandth of the normal amount remained. This mattered enormously, because everyone believed that gases were perfect electrical insulators. Air did not conduct electricity. That was settled science.

NARRATOR: But Geissler's friend, a physicist named Julius Plücker, took one of these nearly empty tubes, sealed a metal plate into each end, and connected them to a high-voltage source — thousands of volts. And to everyone's astonishment, a current flowed. Through what was very nearly nothing, electricity passed. And as it passed, the glass at one end of the tube began to glow with a pale, eerie green light. Something was streaming through the empty tube and slamming into the glass.

NARRATOR: Whatever this something was, it came from the negative plate, the cathode. So in eighteen seventy-six, the German physicist Eugen Goldstein gave the streaming something a name. He called it cathode rays. The name stuck. But naming a thing is not the same as understanding it, and for the next two decades cathode rays would be one of the great unsolved puzzles of physics.

QUESTION: What did these early experimenters actually see? What were the clues?

NARRATOR: The greatest clue-gatherer was an Englishman named William Crookes. In the eighteen seventies, Crookes built the finest cathode ray tubes in the world and started torturing the rays to give up their secrets. He found, first, that the rays travelled in dead straight lines. He proved this with a beautiful trick. He placed a small metal cross, shaped like a Maltese cross, inside the tube, standing up in the path of the rays. And on the glowing far wall of the tube, a sharp, crisp shadow of the cross appeared — a dark cross surrounded by green glow. The rays were being blocked by the metal and casting a shadow, exactly as light would. They went in straight lines.

NARRATOR: Crookes did something even more theatrical. He built a tiny glass paddle wheel, a little mill with vanes, mounted on rails inside the tube. He aimed the cathode rays at it. And the wheel turned. It rolled along its rails, away from the cathode, as if it were being pushed by a wind of tiny particles striking the vanes. To Crookes, this was the clincher. The rays were a stream of particles — bullets of something — hammering the paddle wheel and shoving it along. He called this stuff radiant matter, a fourth state of matter beyond solid, liquid, and gas.

NARRATOR: Now, hold that paddle wheel in your mind, because there is a twist coming that almost nobody knew at the time, and that many textbooks still get wrong today. We will return to it. For now, just note the evidence was piling up on the side of particles. The rays were deflected by magnets — bring a magnet near the tube, and the green spot on the glass would swing to one side. Light does not do that. And the rays seemed to carry energy, heating whatever they struck and making materials fluoresce.

QUESTION: That sounds like a pretty strong case for particles. So why was there a debate at all?

NARRATOR: Because the rays also did things that particles should never do. And this is where the argument turned into a genuine intellectual war, fought largely between the British and the Germans. The British physicists, led by Crookes, said: particles, obviously. The German physicists said: nonsense, these are waves, some new kind of disturbance in the ether, like an extreme form of light.

NARRATOR: And the Germans had good reasons. In eighteen ninety-two, the brilliant German physicist Heinrich Hertz — the same Hertz who had just proved that radio waves and light were the same thing — showed that cathode rays could pass straight through thin sheets of metal foil. Hertz's student Philipp Lenard built tubes with a tiny aluminium window and showed the rays could even leak out into the open air. Now think about that. If the rays were particles, solid little bullets, how could they pass through solid metal? Surely a bullet would be stopped by a metal wall. But a wave — a wave might slip through. To the Germans, the foil experiment screamed wave.

NARRATOR: And there was a second, far more damning problem. If cathode rays were charged particles — little bullets carrying electric charge — then an electric field should push them around. Bring two charged plates near the beam, one positive and one negative, and a stream of charged particles should bend toward the plate of opposite charge. It is the most basic test imaginable. And Hertz had done it. He had set up charged plates, sent the cathode rays between them, and watched. And the rays sailed straight through. No deflection. Nothing.

NARRATOR: This was a catastrophe for the particle camp. The magnet bent the rays, yes — but a magnet can bend a wave too, if the wave is exotic enough. The decisive test, the electric field, showed no charge at all. To Hertz and the Germans, the conclusion was clear. Cathode rays carry no charge. They are not charged particles. They must be waves.

QUESTION: So you had one camp saying particles, one camp saying waves, and the evidence genuinely pointing both ways. How does anyone break a deadlock like that?

NARRATOR: There was one more piece of the puzzle, and it deepened the paradox rather than resolving it. In eighteen ninety-five, a young French physicist named Jean Perrin caught the cathode rays in a small metal cup connected to an instrument that measures charge. And the cup steadily collected negative charge. The rays were depositing negative electricity wherever they landed. So now the contradiction was unbearable. Perrin showed the rays carry negative charge. Hertz showed that electric fields do not deflect them. But if a thing carries charge, an electric field must push it. Both could not be true. One of these careful, respected experiments had to be wrong. The question was: which one?

NARRATOR: And this is the thread that Joseph John Thomson took hold of and pulled. So let us meet him properly, because he is one of the most unlikely heroes in the history of science.

NARRATOR: J.J. Thomson was born in December eighteen fifty-six, in Cheetham Hill, on the outskirts of Manchester. His father was a bookseller, and the plan for young Joseph was that he would become an engineer — specifically, he was put on a waiting list for an apprenticeship building locomotives. The family was going to pay the apprenticeship fee, and that would be his life: railways and engines. But then his father died, when Thomson was sixteen, and the family could no longer afford the fee. He stayed in education only because he had won scholarships. Physics, in a real sense, was Thomson's second choice — the path he fell into because the first path closed. He himself said as much later in life.

NARRATOR: He was a mathematical prodigy. He went up to Trinity College, Cambridge, and in the famous, gruelling mathematics exams there, he came second in his entire year — Second Wrangler, as they called it. And then something genuinely shocking happened. In eighteen eighty-four, when the great Lord Rayleigh stepped down as head of the Cavendish Laboratory — Cambridge's temple of physics, one of the most prestigious positions in all of science — the job went to Thomson. He was twenty-seven years old. He had never run a laboratory. He was known as a theorist who scribbled equations, not an experimenter who built apparatus. The Cambridge physics establishment was stunned.

NARRATOR: Thomson knew exactly how unlikely it was. He later described his feelings with a wonderful image. He said he felt, quote, like a fisherman who had casually cast a line in an unlikely spot and hooked a fish much too heavy for him to land. And here is a delicious irony about this great experimental physicist: he was famously bad with his hands. His assistants used to gently keep him away from the delicate glassware, because his touch could ruin a careful setup. What Thomson had was not manual dexterity. It was something rarer. He had an instinct — an almost uncanny instinct — for what an experiment meant, for which result was the one that mattered, and for the assumption everyone else had failed to question.

QUESTION: So how did Thomson resolve the contradiction? How could the rays carry charge and yet ignore an electric field?

NARRATOR: Thomson started by reproducing Hertz's experiment exactly. He sent cathode rays between charged plates, and at first — exactly like Hertz — he saw no deflection. The rays ignored the field. But Thomson did not conclude that the rays were uncharged. Instead, he asked a different question: why is there no field acting on them? And he had a suspicion. He suspected the problem was not the rays. The problem was the leftover gas in the tube.

NARRATOR: Here is the insight. In Hertz's tubes, the vacuum was not very good. There was still a fair amount of gas rattling around inside. And when you fire cathode rays through a gas and put thousands of volts across some plates, the rays smash into the gas molecules and knock electrons off them, creating charged fragments — ions. Those ions are free to move. The positive ones rush to the negative plate, the negative ones rush to the positive plate, and they pile up on the plates and cancel out the field. In effect, the gas short-circuits your electric field almost as fast as you can apply it. So inside the tube, in the region where the rays are flying, there is no electric field at all. No wonder the rays were not deflected. There was nothing pushing them.

NARRATOR: So Thomson set about making a better vacuum than anyone had managed before. This took patience that borders on obsession. He would run the discharge through the tube for days on end, slowly baking gas molecules off the glass walls and the metal electrodes, pumping them away, getting the tube emptier and emptier. And when the vacuum was good enough — when there was almost no gas left to ionise — he switched on the plates again. And this time the beam of cathode rays bent. It swung cleanly toward the positive plate. The rays were negatively charged after all. Hertz had been right about his result and wrong about its meaning. The rays carried charge. His tube had simply been hiding it.

NARRATOR: Now Thomson had the rays deflecting in an electric field and deflecting in a magnetic field. And that combination is the key that unlocks everything. Because Thomson realised he could use the two fields against each other to measure things about the rays that no one had ever measured — to weigh the invisible.

QUESTION: How can you weigh something you cannot see, cannot catch, and cannot even identify?

NARRATOR: This is the heart of the whole story, so let us go slowly and carefully. Thomson built a special tube. Inside it, the cathode rays were squeezed through narrow slits to make a thin, sharp beam. Then the beam passed between two parallel metal plates — that gave him an electric field he could switch on. And wrapped around the outside of the tube, in the same region, were coils that produced a magnetic field he could switch on. The crucial detail is the geometry. The electric field pushed the beam one way — say, upward. The magnetic field, by the way it was arranged, pushed the beam the opposite way — downward. The two forces fought each other.

NARRATOR: Now watch what Thomson does. First, he turns on only the electric field. The beam bends — let us say it bends up — and the green spot on the screen moves. Good. The rays feel the electric force. The electric force on the beam is simply the charge of each particle times the strength of the electric field. Call the charge e and the field strength E. The force is e times E.

NARRATOR: Then Thomson turns on the magnetic field as well, and he carefully adjusts its strength until the beam goes back to dead centre — perfectly straight, as if no fields were on at all. At that precise moment, the magnetic force exactly cancels the electric force. They are equal and opposite. Now, the magnetic force on a moving charge depends on three things: the charge, the speed of the particle, and the strength of the magnetic field. It is the charge e, times the speed v, times the magnetic field B. So when the beam is balanced, the electric force equals the magnetic force. In words: charge times electric field equals charge times speed times magnetic field.

NARRATOR: And look what happens. The charge appears on both sides of that equation, so it cancels out entirely. What you are left with is beautifully simple: the speed of the particles equals the electric field strength divided by the magnetic field strength. Thomson knew both of those numbers — he had set them himself. So for the first time, he had measured how fast the cathode rays were travelling. And the answer was staggering. These particles were moving at tens of thousands of kilometres per second. In some of his tubes, the speed came out to something like one tenth, even up to a third, of the speed of light. Faster than anything anyone had ever put a number to. Far, far slower than light itself, which was another nail in the coffin of the wave theory — but unimaginably fast for a piece of matter. This arrangement, two fields balanced against each other, is now called a velocity selector, and it is in the supplementary notes as Listing one.

QUESTION: So now he knows the speed. But speed alone does not tell you what the particle is. How does he get from speed to the actual nature of the thing?

NARRATOR: This is the second, decisive move. Having measured the speed from the balanced fields, Thomson now leaves only one field acting on the beam. In the version of the experiment the textbooks build on, he switches the electric field off and lets the magnetic field act alone. A magnetic force always pushes at right angles to the direction of motion, so it cannot speed the particle up or slow it down — it can only bend the path into the arc of a circle. Thomson measures the radius of that circle. And here is why the radius is gold. For a charged particle curving in a magnetic field, the magnetic force is providing the centripetal force that holds it on the circle — the same kind of force that keeps a ball on a string going round. Setting the magnetic force equal to the centripetal force, and using the speed he has already measured, the radius of the curve hands him exactly one quantity.

NARRATOR: That quantity is the ratio of the particle's charge to its mass. Now think about why it has to be a ratio and not the charge or the mass on its own. A strong force bends the beam more. But a heavier particle bends less, because heavy things are hard to push — that is just inertia, mass resisting a force. And the charge sets how strongly the field grips the particle in the first place. When you do the algebra, the charge and the mass never separate; they come out welded together as charge divided by mass. We call it the charge-to-mass ratio. Thomson could not get the charge on its own, and he could not get the mass on its own — but he could get the two of them locked together in a single number. The full derivation, both from the magnetic curving and from the simpler picture of the beam deflecting like a thrown ball in the electric field alone, is written out in Listing two of the supplementary notes.

NARRATOR: And when Thomson worked out that number, he got a shock. The charge-to-mass ratio of his cathode ray particles was enormous — far larger than for any object anyone had ever measured. To put it in context, physicists already knew the charge-to-mass ratio of the lightest charged thing in the chemistry laboratory: the hydrogen ion, a hydrogen atom that has lost its electron, the lightest atom there is. Thomson's particles had a charge-to-mass ratio about a thousand times bigger than the hydrogen ion. The modern, more precise figure is closer to one thousand eight hundred times bigger. Either way, it was off the scale.

QUESTION: A charge-to-mass ratio nearly two thousand times bigger than hydrogen. What could possibly explain that? There seem to be two ways it could happen.

NARRATOR: Exactly. And Thomson saw both possibilities clearly. The charge-to-mass ratio is charge divided by mass. For that ratio to be enormous, either the charge on top must be gigantic, or the mass on the bottom must be tiny. Either these particles carry a thousand times more charge than a hydrogen ion, or they have a thousand times less mass. The mathematics alone cannot tell you which. Thomson needed an independent argument to break the tie. And he found one, using an old result from his German rivals.

NARRATOR: Remember Lenard, who showed that cathode rays could travel through thin metal foils and even out into the air? Lenard had measured how far the rays could travel through a gas before being scattered and stopped — what physicists call the mean free path. And the cathode ray particles travelled astonishingly far before being knocked off course. Compare them to ordinary gas molecules, which collide with each other after an incredibly short distance. The cathode ray particles slipped through gaps that no ordinary atom could fit through. They travelled hundreds of thousands of times farther than a molecule before hitting anything.

NARRATOR: Now reason it through. A particle that carries a gigantic charge would be a fierce thing — it would grab and shove every atom it passed, interacting strongly, and it would be stopped almost immediately. That is not what these particles do. They glide through matter as if it were mostly empty space. The only way to make sense of that is that the particle is not enormously charged. It is enormously small. The mass is what is tiny. These particles are far, far smaller than an atom. And that was the conclusion Thomson could see no way around. The cathode rays were pieces of matter smaller than the smallest atom. Subatomic. A word that, until that moment, had no meaning, because nothing was supposed to be smaller than an atom.

QUESTION: Surely the most obvious objection is — maybe these particles are just bits of whatever metal the cathode is made of, or bits of whatever gas is in the tube? How did Thomson know they were something universal?

NARRATOR: This is the masterstroke, and it is the part that turns a curious result into a revolution. Thomson did the experiment over and over, changing everything he could change. He swapped the gas in the tube — air, then hydrogen, then carbon dioxide. He swapped the metal of the cathode — aluminium, then platinum. If the particles were fragments of the gas, the charge-to-mass ratio should change when you change the gas. If they were chips of the metal electrode, it should change when you change the metal. Thomson measured, and measured, and measured. And the number never changed. Air, hydrogen, carbon dioxide, aluminium, platinum — the same charge-to-mass ratio came out every single time.

NARRATOR: There was only one possible meaning. These particles were not made of any particular gas or any particular metal. They were the same particle no matter where they came from. Which meant they were a constituent of all of those substances. They were a building block present inside every kind of matter Thomson could lay his hands on. As Thomson put it in his paper, this was matter in a new state — a state in which the subdivision of matter is carried very much further than in the ordinary gaseous state. He had found a universal ingredient of all atoms. A piece, the same piece, inside everything.

NARRATOR: He called these particles corpuscles. His own word, borrowed from biology, meaning little bodies. He pointedly did not call them electrons. The word electron already existed — it had been coined by the Irish physicist George Johnstone Stoney back in eighteen ninety-one for the fundamental unit of electric charge in chemistry. Thomson did not want to muddle his new particle with Stoney's unit of charge, so he stubbornly stuck with corpuscles for years. The name electron, applied to Thomson's particle, was pushed by another Irishman, George Francis FitzGerald, after Thomson published — and it was that name, electron, that the world eventually chose.

QUESTION: So when did he tell the world? And how did the world react?

NARRATOR: On the evening of Friday, the thirtieth of April, eighteen ninety-seven, Thomson stood up to give a Friday Evening Discourse at the Royal Institution in London. These discourses were a grand tradition, running since the eighteen twenties — formal evening lectures for an audience of educated people and distinguished scientists, Fellows of the Royal Society in the front rows. The lights, the reputation, the weight of the room. And there, Thomson laid out his case: cathode rays are streams of negatively charged corpuscles; here is their speed; here is their charge-to-mass ratio; the ratio is the same whatever the gas or the metal; and the only conclusion he could reach was that these corpuscles are pieces of matter far smaller than the atom, present in all matter.

NARRATOR: The reaction was not applause and acclaim. The reaction was hesitation and disbelief. Thomson himself recalled that he was met with scepticism. The atom had been the bedrock of science for the best part of a century. The word atom comes from the Greek for uncuttable, indivisible. To say there was something inside it, something smaller, was very nearly a contradiction in terms. One account holds that a respected member of the audience told Thomson afterward that he thought Thomson had been pulling their legs. The great Lord Kelvin, the most eminent physicist in Britain, was deeply sceptical. These were not foolish men. They were defending one of the most successful ideas in the history of science, and Thomson was telling them it was incomplete.

NARRATOR: But Thomson had something better than rhetoric. He had numbers. Careful, repeatable numbers, measured many times over with different gases and different metals, all agreeing. In October of eighteen ninety-seven he published the full paper, titled simply Cathode Rays, in the Philosophical Magazine, with all the data laid bare. And the line at the heart of that paper is one of the great sentences in physics. He wrote that since the cathode rays carry a charge of negative electricity, are deflected by an electrostatic force as if negatively charged, and are acted on by a magnetic force in just the way such a force would act on a negatively charged body, he could, in his words, see no escape from the conclusion that they are charges of negative electricity carried by particles of matter. No escape. The data left no other door open. And within a few years, as other laboratories reproduced the work, the scepticism melted away.

QUESTION: There was still a loose end though, wasn't there? Thomson had the charge-to-mass ratio, but not the charge and the mass separately. How did he finish the job?

NARRATOR: He made a start on it himself. In eighteen ninety-nine, using a cloud-droplet method developed partly by his Cavendish colleague C.T.R. Wilson, the inventor of the cloud chamber, Thomson and his team made a rough first measurement of the actual charge on a single corpuscle. And they found something profound: the charge on the corpuscle was, to the accuracy they had, the same as the charge on a hydrogen ion in chemistry. The very same fundamental unit. Now the logic snaps shut. If the charge is the same as hydrogen's, but the charge-to-mass ratio is about eighteen hundred times bigger, then since the charge on top is identical, the mass on the bottom must be about eighteen hundred times smaller. The corpuscle weighs roughly one eighteen-hundredth of a hydrogen atom. The mass of a single electron, in modern units, is about nine times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms — almost nothing. The smallest piece of ordinary matter anyone had ever found.

NARRATOR: But Thomson's charge figure was crude, and the precise, beautiful measurement came a decade later, from across the Atlantic. In nineteen oh nine, the American physicist Robert Millikan built one of the most elegant experiments in all of physics — the oil-drop experiment. He sprayed a fine mist of tiny oil droplets between two horizontal charged plates. The droplets picked up a few electrons, and a small X-ray beam knocked extra electrons onto them, so each drop carried a little negative charge. Then Millikan did something simple and powerful. He turned up the voltage between the plates until the upward electric force on a single droplet exactly balanced the downward pull of gravity — until the droplet hung dead still in mid-air, suspended. At that moment the weight equals the electric force, and since he could work out the tiny mass of the drop from how fast it fell with the field off, he could solve for the charge on that one drop. He did it for thousands of drops. And here is the result that mattered: the charge always came out as a whole-number multiple of one smallest packet. Charge does not come in any old amount; it comes in indivisible lumps. That smallest lump, the elementary charge, the charge on a single electron, was about one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. Millikan won the Nobel Prize for it in nineteen twenty-three.

NARRATOR: Now combine Millikan's charge with Thomson's ratio, and you have the electron pinned down completely. The charge-to-mass ratio gave you the two quantities locked together; Millikan's charge prises them apart. Divide the known charge by the known charge-to-mass ratio and the mass of the electron falls straight out — about nine times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms, roughly one eighteen-hundredth of a hydrogen atom. Thomson found the ratio; Millikan found the charge; between the two of them, the lightest piece of ordinary matter in the universe had been weighed.

QUESTION: And once you know there are tiny negative pieces inside every atom — what does the atom actually look like? What holds it together?

NARRATOR: That was the obvious next question, and Thomson spent years on it. Atoms, in everyday life, are electrically neutral. So if every atom contains these tiny negative corpuscles, there must be positive charge somewhere to balance them. In nineteen oh four, seven years after the discovery, Thomson proposed his model of the atom. He pictured the negative corpuscles embedded in a smeared-out sphere of positive electric charge — like seeds suspended in a soft, positively charged jelly. The positive charge filled the whole atom; the electrons dotted through it.

NARRATOR: You have probably heard this called the plum pudding model — electrons scattered through the positive material like raisins through a Christmas pudding. But here is a small piece of history worth getting right: Thomson himself never called it that. The plum pudding name was coined by an anonymous journalist writing in a trade magazine called The Chemist and Druggist in nineteen oh six, who described the electrons as being like raisins in a parsimonious plum pudding. The phrase was catchy, it stuck, and Thomson's name got welded to a dessert he never mentioned. The model was wrong, as it turned out — but it was wrong in a productive way. It was a real, testable picture of the inside of an atom, the first one ever, and you need a wrong model you can test before you can find the right one.

NARRATOR: And the right one came from Thomson's own laboratory. One of the young men who had worked under Thomson at the Cavendish was a New Zealander named Ernest Rutherford. Around nineteen oh nine, in what is now called the Geiger–Marsden experiment, Rutherford's collaborators Hans Geiger and Ernest Marsden fired alpha particles at a thin sheet of gold foil and found that a tiny fraction — about one in eight thousand — bounced back through angles greater than ninety degrees, some almost straight back the way they had come. That is impossible if the positive charge is smeared out softly through the whole atom, the way the plum pudding said. The positive charge had to be concentrated into a minute, dense core. In nineteen eleven Rutherford put it together: the pudding was wrong, the atom had a nucleus, with almost all the mass packed into a tiny centre and electrons out in the surrounding space. Thomson's model was overthrown, and it was overthrown out of his own laboratory, using the very style of careful, ratio-measuring experiment that Thomson had pioneered. That is how science is supposed to work.

QUESTION: Now — that paddle wheel. You said there was a twist. What was wrong with it?

NARRATOR: I am glad you remembered, because this is one of the most persistent myths in physics teaching, and Thomson himself debunked it. Crookes's spinning paddle wheel was always presented as proof that cathode rays are particles with momentum — bullets striking the vanes and pushing them along, like wind on a sail. It is a lovely story. It is also wrong. In nineteen oh three, Thomson did the calculation. He worked out how much momentum the electrons in the beam actually carry, and how hard they could possibly push that wheel. And the answer was: nowhere near enough. The momentum of the electrons is far too small to turn the wheel at the speed it visibly turns. A modern analysis, published in two thousand and fourteen, confirmed it — the force from the electrons hitting the vanes is more than a hundred times too weak to account for the motion.

NARRATOR: So what actually turns the wheel? Heat. The cathode rays strike the vanes and warm them. The warm surface heats the tiny amount of gas right next to it; that hot gas expands and pushes back harder than the cooler gas on the other side. It is the same effect that drives the little spinning radiometer you sometimes see in shop windows, the one with the black-and-white vanes that turns in sunlight. The paddle wheel is a beautiful demonstration that cathode rays carry energy — they clearly heat what they hit. But it is not evidence that they carry momentum, and it never was. Thomson's real proof that the rays were particles with mass and charge came from the fields, the deflection, the charge-to-mass ratio — not from the wheel. It is worth knowing, because it is a perfect lesson in how an experiment can give you a true conclusion for completely the wrong reason.

QUESTION: So in the end — how much did this one discovery actually change?

NARRATOR: It is difficult to overstate it. Begin with the obvious. Once you know that every atom contains these light, mobile, negatively charged particles, you can finally explain electricity itself — an electric current is electrons flowing. You can explain chemistry — atoms bond by sharing and trading their outer electrons; the entire periodic table gets a physical explanation in terms of how electrons arrange themselves. The electron is the actor at the centre of all of chemistry and all of electronics.

NARRATOR: And electronics is the word. Every device that controls a flow of electrons traces directly back to that basement in Cambridge. The cathode ray tube itself became the television screen and the computer monitor for the entire twentieth century — those bulky old TVs were literally Thomson's apparatus, a beam of electrons steered by fields onto a glowing screen. The vacuum tube gave us radio and early computers. The transistor that replaced it, the chip in your phone, the entire digital world — all of it is, at bottom, applied electron physics. The science of moving electrons around on purpose.

NARRATOR: Thomson's own techniques kept giving. In nineteen twelve, he and his assistant Francis Aston used these very same cathode ray methods — bending charged particles with fields and measuring where they land — to discover that the element neon comes in two different weights. They had found isotopes, and invented the mass spectrometer, an instrument now used everywhere from cancer research to airport security. Thomson won the Nobel Prize in nineteen oh six. And then perhaps the most poetic footnote in all of science: Thomson's son, George Paget Thomson, won his own Nobel Prize in nineteen thirty-seven — for proving that the electron is a wave. The father proved the electron is a particle. The son proved it is a wave. Both were right, and the strange truth that it is somehow both lies at the heart of quantum mechanics. As a final measure of the man: seven of the research students Thomson trained went on to win Nobel Prizes of their own. No supervisor in history has ever matched that record.

NARRATOR: Joseph John Thomson died in nineteen forty, and his ashes were buried in Westminster Abbey, a few steps from Isaac Newton. The boy who was meant to build locomotives, the theorist too clumsy to be trusted with the glassware, had reached into the supposedly uncuttable atom and pulled out the first piece. He had weighed the unweighable and found, hiding inside the smallest thing in existence, something smaller still.

NARRATOR: For the New South Wales HSC Physics course, this story is the evidence behind a key part of the matter, energy and the cosmos topic, modelling the atom. Start with where cathode rays come from: in a cathode ray tube, the huge voltage rips electrons off the cathode and the leftover gas, and those electrons stream from the cathode across to the anode — that is the origin of the rays. Then come the observations you are asked to describe inside a low-pressure tube. The Maltese cross casts a sharp shadow, showing the rays travel in straight lines. The paddle wheel rolls along its track, showing the rays carry energy. The rays bend in an electric field and bend in a magnetic field, and the direction of bending — toward the positive plate — shows they are negatively charged. Taken together, these observations are the evidence that cathode rays are particles with both mass and charge.

NARRATOR: Above all there is Thomson's charge-to-mass experiment, in which crossed electric and magnetic fields first reveal the speed, and then the curving of the beam in the magnetic field reveals the charge-to-mass ratio of the particle — the derivation the syllabus asks you to be able to reproduce. That ratio, about eighteen hundred times that of the hydrogen ion, is the hard quantitative proof that cathode rays are not waves but tiny particles, each carrying both mass and charge — the electron. Pair it with Millikan's oil-drop experiment, which measured the charge on a single electron directly, and you can finally separate the charge from the mass and weigh the electron itself. That same body of evidence — light negative particles inside every atom — is what the plum pudding model was built to explain, and what the Geiger–Marsden gold-foil experiment then overturned in favour of the nuclear atom. The equations, the values, and a full worked example are waiting for you in the supplementary notes.
