NARRATOR: Welcome to Module Two, Dynamics. Last module, Kinematics, we learned to describe motion, displacement, velocity, acceleration, graphs, equations, vectors. But we never asked the obvious question: what causes motion to change in the first place? That's Dynamics, and the answer is forces. Today we meet the force concept and Newton's three laws of motion, the framework that underpins the whole module and most of the physics you'll do for two years. By the end you'll be able to define a force, distinguish mass from weight, state and apply all three of Newton's laws, and use net force equals mass times acceleration to connect forces back to the kinematics you already know. But first, as always, a quick recap, because today's whole point is to explain the motion you learned to describe last module.

NARRATOR: Last module gave us the language of motion. Acceleration, the vector rate of change of velocity, in metres per second per second. The equations of uniformly accelerated motion, the suvat equations, which predict motion when the acceleration is constant. And vectors: quantities with magnitude and direction, which we resolved into perpendicular components using cosine and sine, and combined with Pythagoras. Hold all of that, because here's the bridge between the modules, the single most important connection: kinematics told us that a changing velocity means an acceleration, but it never said why an object accelerates. Dynamics answers that. An object accelerates because a net force acts on it. Force is the cause; acceleration is the effect; and once a force gives you an acceleration, you hand that acceleration straight to last module's suvat equations to predict the motion. Forces and kinematics are two halves of one story.

QUESTION: Quick check before we start. From last module, what is acceleration, and what kind of quantity is it, scalar or vector? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, and it's a vector, it has magnitude and direction. Hold that it's a vector, because force is a vector too, and today's headline equation links the two: the net force and the acceleration it produces point in the same direction. Now let's define a force.

NARRATOR: A force is a push or a pull, an interaction that can change an object's motion or shape. It's a vector, with magnitude, measured in newtons, and direction, so everything we learned about resolving and adding vectors applies to forces. Forces come in two broad kinds, and the syllabus wants this distinction. Contact forces act when objects touch: the push of your hand, friction, tension in a rope, the normal force a surface pushes back with, air resistance. Field forces, or forces mediated by fields, act at a distance, without contact: gravity pulling everything down, the electrostatic force between charges, the magnetic force. You'll study those fields in detail in Module Four and Module Six, but the idea starts here, a force can reach across empty space through a field. The two kinds and key examples are in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now a distinction that costs more marks in this module than almost any other: mass versus weight. They are not the same thing, and using them interchangeably is a classic error. Mass is the amount of matter in an object, a scalar, measured in kilograms, and it's the same everywhere in the universe, your mass on the Moon equals your mass on Earth. Weight is something different: it's the gravitational force acting on that mass, a vector, measured in newtons, pointing down toward the centre of the Earth. Weight equals mass times the gravitational field strength, weight equals m g, where g is about nine point eight newtons per kilogram near the Earth's surface. So weight depends on where you are, on the Moon, where gravity is weaker, your weight is about a sixth of your Earth weight, but your mass is unchanged. Mass in kilograms, a scalar, constant; weight in newtons, a vector, equal to m g, location-dependent. Get that distinction crisp, because the exam tests it relentlessly.

NARRATOR: Now the heart of the episode, Newton's three laws. And here's a memory hook for the set: think "I, F, A", Inertia, F equals m a, Action-reaction. Three laws, three letters, in order. Let me take them one at a time, because each one is examined.

NARRATOR: Newton's first law, the law of inertia. An object at rest stays at rest, and an object moving at constant velocity stays moving at constant velocity, unless acted on by a net, unbalanced, force. In other words, an object's velocity only changes if there's a net force, no net force means no change in motion. Inertia is the name for this tendency of objects to resist changes to their motion, and it's why a passenger lurches forward when a car brakes suddenly: the passenger was moving forward, and with no force to stop them they keep moving forward while the car slows beneath them, the first law in action. The key term here is net force: forces can be acting, but if they're balanced, summing to zero, the object is in equilibrium, either at rest or moving at constant velocity. Balanced forces, no change in motion. That's the first law.

NARRATOR: Newton's second law, the famous one. The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration, net force equals m a. This is the quantitative core of the whole module. It tells you three things at once: an acceleration requires a net force; the acceleration is in the same direction as the net force; and for a given force, a larger mass gives a smaller acceleration, mass resists acceleration, that's inertia made quantitative. And here is the bridge to last module, spelled out: you find the net force, divide by the mass to get the acceleration, and then feed that acceleration into the suvat equations to predict how the object moves. Force to acceleration to motion. That chain, second law then kinematics, solves a huge fraction of mechanics problems. There's a worked example of exactly that chain in Listing two.

QUESTION: Let me have you connect the modules right now. A net force of twelve newtons acts on a four-kilogram object initially at rest. What is its acceleration, and how fast is it moving after three seconds? Pause the player, work both parts, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and notice it's Dynamics then Kinematics. First, the acceleration, from the second law: net force equals m a, so acceleration equals net force over mass, twelve newtons over four kilograms, three metres per second per second. That's the Dynamics step. Now the speed after three seconds, from last module's suvat: the object starts from rest, so u is zero, a is three, t is three, find v, use v equals u plus a t, v equals zero plus three times three, nine metres per second. That's the Kinematics step. See the chain: the force gave the acceleration, and the acceleration fed straight into the equation of motion. That two-step is the signature move of this entire module, and the full working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Newton's third law, the one everyone can quote and many get wrong. For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Stated precisely: if object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts a force on object A that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. In symbols, the force of A on B equals minus the force of B on A. Now here is the crucial point that the exam hammers, and where students lose marks: the two forces in an action-reaction pair act on different objects. The force of A on B acts on B; the reaction, B on A, acts on A. Because they act on different objects, they never cancel each other out. People wrongly think action-reaction forces cancel, and conclude nothing could ever accelerate, that's the trap. They don't cancel because they're on different bodies. When you walk, your foot pushes back on the ground, action, and the ground pushes forward on you, reaction, and it's that forward push on you that accelerates you. A rocket pushes exhaust gases down, and the gases push the rocket up. The third-law pair is always two forces, equal in size, opposite in direction, the same type of force, acting on two different objects. Examples are in Listing four.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. A book rests on a table. Name the action-reaction pair, and explain why this is different from the pair of forces that keeps the book in equilibrium. Pause the player, think it through, then play.

NARRATOR: This is a beautiful question because it separates two ideas students merge. The action-reaction pair, by Newton's third law, is: the book pushes down on the table, and the table pushes up on the book, equal and opposite, acting on different objects, the book and the table. Now, separately, the book is in equilibrium, it's not accelerating, so the net force on the book is zero. The two forces that balance on the book are: the weight of the book, gravity pulling it down, and the normal force, the table pushing it up. Those two are equal and opposite, but they are not an action-reaction pair, because they act on the same object, the book, and they're different types of force, gravity versus contact. The action-reaction partner of the book's weight is the book pulling up on the Earth by gravity; the action-reaction partner of the normal force is the book pushing down on the table. So "balanced forces on one object" and "an action-reaction pair" are different things: balanced forces act on the same object and can be different types; action-reaction forces act on different objects and are the same type. If you separated those, you've understood the third law at the level the exam rewards. Most students conflate them, and lose the mark.

NARRATOR: Let me talk about force diagrams, because they're the practical tool for every Dynamics problem and they're marked. A force diagram, sometimes called a free-body diagram, shows a single object with every force acting on it drawn as a labelled arrow, pointing in the direction of the force, with its length roughly showing the magnitude. The discipline: draw only the forces acting on that one object, not forces it exerts on other things, and label each one, weight, normal force, tension, friction, applied force. Once every force is on the diagram, you add them as vectors to get the net force, and then apply the second law. For forces in two dimensions you resolve them into components, exactly the cosine-and-sine method from last module, which we'll use heavily next episode on the inclined plane. The force diagram is where a wordy problem becomes solvable physics.

NARRATOR: Let me name the common contact forces, because force diagrams need them labelled correctly. The normal force is the push a surface exerts on an object, always perpendicular to the surface, that's what "normal" means here, at right angles. Tension is the pulling force carried along a rope, string or cable, acting along its length. Friction is the contact force that opposes relative motion or the tendency to move, acting along the surface. The applied force is whatever external push or pull you're deliberately exerting. And the weight, though a field force not a contact force, appears on nearly every diagram, acting straight down. Getting these labelled and pointing the right way, normal perpendicular to the surface, tension along the rope, friction opposing motion, weight down, is half the battle in any Dynamics problem.

NARRATOR: Let me work a net-force example where the forces aren't along one line, because forces add as vectors, last module's skill. Suppose two forces act on an object on a frictionless surface: one of three newtons east and one of four newtons north, perpendicular to each other. The net force is their vector sum, found by Pythagoras: the square root of three squared plus four squared, the square root of nine plus sixteen, the square root of twenty-five, five newtons. Its direction is the angle north of east whose tangent is four over three, about fifty-three degrees north of east. And by the second law, the acceleration is the net force over the mass, in that same direction, five newtons over the mass, pointing fifty-three degrees north of east, because acceleration is always in the direction of the net force. Notice we did not add three and four to get seven, forces are vectors, and perpendicular three and four make five. That's the vector discipline from Module One, now applied to forces, and it's exactly what you'll do on the inclined plane next episode.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the strong extended-response answer for a guaranteed exam question, "explain, using Newton's laws, what happens to a passenger when a car suddenly brakes". Here's the band-six version. By Newton's first law, an object continues in its state of motion unless acted on by a net force. While the car is moving, the passenger is moving forward at the same velocity. When the car brakes, a net backward force acts on the car, decelerating it, but this braking force acts on the car, not directly on the passenger. Because of their inertia, the passengers tend to continue moving forward at their original velocity, so they lurch forward relative to the car until a force, the seatbelt, acts on them to decelerate them too. Notice what that answer does: it names the first law, names inertia, identifies that the force acts on the car not the passenger, and explains the lurch as continued motion in the absence of a force. That's the structure, name the law, apply it to the specific objects.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the gap. A weak answer says "the passenger flies forward because of the momentum" or "because the car stops suddenly". That earns little: it doesn't name Newton's first law, doesn't use the word inertia, and doesn't explain that the passenger keeps moving because no net force acts on them until the seatbelt does. The strong answer names the law and the mechanism; the weak answer just describes the outcome. Name the law, name inertia, identify which object the force acts on. That's the difference between bands.

NARRATOR: Let me unpack the second law a little more, because the syllabus asks you to investigate it, and the relationships are examinable. The law says net force equals mass times acceleration, and that single equation hides two proportionalities. First, for a fixed mass, the acceleration is directly proportional to the net force: double the net force and you double the acceleration. If you plot acceleration against net force for a fixed mass, you get a straight line through the origin, and its gradient is one over the mass. Second, for a fixed net force, the acceleration is inversely proportional to the mass: double the mass and you halve the acceleration. In a practical investigation you'd pull a trolley with a measured constant force, vary it, and measure the acceleration with a motion sensor or ticker-timer, exactly the kinematics tools from last module, and confirm the straight-line relationship. That experiment is a classic, and being able to describe it, constant force, measure acceleration, plot a against F, get a straight line through the origin, is worth marks. The bigger the mass, the more it resists acceleration; that resistance is inertia, the same inertia from the first law, now quantified by the mass in the second law.

NARRATOR: Let me also work an equilibrium example, because the first law in two dimensions is examined. When an object is in equilibrium, the net force is zero, which means the forces balance in every direction, the horizontal components sum to zero and the vertical components sum to zero. Take a picture hanging from two wires, or a sign held by two cables at angles. The weight pulls straight down; the two cable tensions pull up and outward at angles. For the sign to hang in equilibrium, the upward components of the two tensions must together balance the weight, and their horizontal components must balance each other. You solve it by resolving each tension into horizontal and vertical components, the cosine-and-sine method from last module, and setting each direction's total to zero. So the first law, "no net force", becomes two component equations, and that's how you find unknown tensions or supporting forces. We'll use this exact resolve-and-balance method next episode on the inclined plane. Equilibrium isn't "no forces", it's forces that cancel, and the cancelling happens component by component.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: confusing mass and weight, mass is kilograms and constant, weight is newtons and equals m g. Trap two: thinking action-reaction forces cancel, they act on different objects, so they don't. Trap three: forgetting that "net force" is what matters in the first and second laws, balanced forces produce no acceleration. Trap four: drawing forces the object exerts on others in its force diagram, only draw forces acting on the object. Trap five: in the second law, using a single force instead of the net force, you must add all the forces as vectors first. Avoid those and the laws are straightforward.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the threads together. Back to last module: the second law produces the acceleration that the suvat equations then turn into motion, force to acceleration to displacement and velocity, the two modules joined. And forward: next episode we apply these laws to friction and the inclined plane, resolving forces into components, last module's vector skill, to find the net force along a slope. Forces mediated by fields, which we met today, get their own deep treatment in Module Four, electric and magnetic fields, and Module Six, where the same net-force-equals-m-a logic governs a charged particle in a field and the motor effect. And the second law is the engine of Module Five, Advanced Mechanics, where it drives projectile and circular motion. Newton's three laws are arguably the most reused idea in the whole course.

NARRATOR: Here's the unifying idea that ties the three laws into one picture, and it's worth holding above the detail. Everything comes down to the net force, the vector sum of all forces on an object. There are only two cases. Case one: the net force is zero, the forces are balanced. Then, by the first law, the motion doesn't change, the object stays at rest or keeps moving at constant velocity, it's in equilibrium. Case two: the net force is not zero, the forces are unbalanced. Then, by the second law, the object accelerates, in the direction of the net force, with an acceleration of net force over mass. That's the whole framework: compute the net force, and if it's zero you're in the first-law world of constant motion, and if it's not you're in the second-law world of acceleration, which you then hand to the kinematics equations. The third law just reminds you that each of those forces has a partner acting on another object, which matters when you analyse interacting bodies like a rocket and its exhaust or your foot and the ground. Net force zero or net force not zero, that single question decides whether an object cruises or accelerates, and it's the lens for every problem in this module.

NARRATOR: Let me recap the takeaways, phrased for marks. One: a force is a push or pull, a vector in newtons, either a contact force or a field force. Two: mass is the scalar amount of matter in kilograms, constant everywhere; weight is the gravitational force on it, a vector in newtons, equal to m g. Three: Newton's first law, an object's motion only changes under a net force, inertia is the resistance to that change. Four: Newton's second law, net force equals mass times acceleration, the acceleration is in the direction of the net force, and you feed it into the suvat equations. Five: Newton's third law, forces come in equal and opposite pairs acting on different objects, so they never cancel. Remember the hook, I, F, A, inertia, F equals m a, action-reaction. Hold those and you've got the framework for the whole module.

NARRATOR: Now let's finish with exam-style questions in real NESA format. Get a pen, because you learn this by retrieving it, not by hearing it. Five questions, easy to hard.

QUESTION: Question one. Distinguish between the mass and the weight of an object. Two marks. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Mass is the amount of matter in an object, a scalar quantity measured in kilograms, and it is constant regardless of location. Weight is the gravitational force acting on the object, a vector quantity measured in newtons, equal to the mass times the gravitational field strength, m g, and it depends on the local gravity, so it changes with location. Two marks: mass as the scalar amount of matter in kilograms, constant; weight as the gravitational force in newtons, equal to m g, location-dependent. The trap is saying weight is "how heavy something is", that's vague, name it as a force equal to m g.

QUESTION: Question two. A car of mass one thousand kilograms experiences a net forward force of two thousand five hundred newtons. Calculate its acceleration. Two marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By Newton's second law, net force equals mass times acceleration, so acceleration equals net force divided by mass, two thousand five hundred newtons divided by one thousand kilograms, which is two point five metres per second per second, in the direction of the net force. Two marks: the correct rearrangement of the second law, and the answer with units. Note the direction: the acceleration is forward, the same direction as the net force.

QUESTION: Question three. Calculate the weight of a seventy-kilogram person on Earth, where g is nine point eight newtons per kilogram. Then state their mass and weight on the Moon, where g is one point six newtons per kilogram. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing three. On Earth, weight equals m g, seventy kilograms times nine point eight, which is six hundred and eighty-six newtons, directed downward. On the Moon, the person's mass is unchanged, still seventy kilograms, because mass doesn't depend on location. Their Moon weight is m g with the Moon's gravity, seventy times one point six, which is one hundred and twelve newtons. Three marks: Earth weight six hundred and eighty-six newtons; mass unchanged at seventy kilograms on the Moon; Moon weight one hundred and twelve newtons. The whole point of the question is that mass stays the same while weight changes, that's the distinction being tested.

QUESTION: Question four. A student claims that because every force has an equal and opposite reaction force, the two forces always cancel and nothing can ever accelerate. Explain the flaw in this reasoning. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The flaw is that an action-reaction pair acts on two different objects, not on the same object, so the two forces cannot cancel each other's effect on a single object. By Newton's third law, if object A pushes on object B, then B pushes back on A with an equal and opposite force, but the action acts on B and the reaction acts on A. To find whether a particular object accelerates, you consider only the forces acting on that object, and these generally do not balance. For example, when you walk, you push back on the ground and the ground pushes forward on you; that forward force acts on you and accelerates you. Three marks: the pair acts on different objects; therefore they don't cancel on one object; with a correct example. This is the single most common third-law misconception, and naming "different objects" is the key.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A fifty-kilogram crate is pushed across a floor with a horizontal applied force of two hundred newtons, against a friction force of fifty newtons. Calculate the net force and the acceleration, and then find the crate's velocity after four seconds, starting from rest. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, and this is the full Dynamics-to-Kinematics chain, in Listing two. First the net force: the applied force is two hundred newtons forward, friction is fifty newtons backward, opposing motion, so the net force is two hundred minus fifty, one hundred and fifty newtons forward. Then the acceleration, from the second law: acceleration equals net force over mass, one hundred and fifty newtons over fifty kilograms, three metres per second per second forward. Then the velocity after four seconds, from suvat: u is zero, a is three, t is four, find v, use v equals u plus a t, v equals zero plus three times four, twelve metres per second. Four marks: net force one hundred and fifty newtons after subtracting friction; acceleration three metres per second per second from the second law; correct suvat equation; and the final velocity of twelve metres per second. Notice the structure, add the forces to get the net force, apply net force equals m a for the acceleration, then feed that acceleration into the equations of motion. That chain solves most of this module.
