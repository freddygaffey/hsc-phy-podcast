NARRATOR: Here's the mechanism of nuclear fission. Start with a uranium-235 nucleus, the fissile nucleus, the one that splits readily. A slow-moving neutron drifts in and is captured by the uranium-235, turning it momentarily into uranium-236. That uranium-236 is wildly unstable, it's like a wobbling, overfull drop of liquid, and almost instantly it splits, fissions, into two roughly equal mid-sized fragments, plus, crucially, two or three free neutrons, and a large burst of energy. NARRATOR: A word on the fuel itself, because it explains a phrase you'll meet, enriched uranium. Natural uranium dug out of the ground is overwhelmingly uranium-238, which does not readily fission with slow neutrons; only about seven-tenths of one percent of it is the fissile uranium-235 we actually want. That's too dilute to sustain a chain reaction in most reactor designs, because there simply aren't enough uranium-235 nuclei for the neutrons to find. So the fuel is enriched, processed to raise the proportion of uranium-235, typically to around three to five percent for a power reactor. A bomb needs far higher enrichment, around ninety percent. That single difference, the level of enrichment, is one of the main reasons reactor fuel cannot simply explode like a bomb: it isn't concentrated enough to go supercritical in that runaway way. Keep the distinction between uranium-235, the fissile one we enrich for, and uranium-238, the abundant one we mostly discard.

QUESTION: You said a slow neutron triggers the fission. Why slow? Wouldn't a fast, energetic neutron be better at breaking the nucleus apart?

NARRATOR: This is the counter-intuitive bit that catches people, so listen closely. Fission isn't about a neutron smashing the nucleus apart by brute force. It's about the neutron being captured, absorbed into the nucleus, to make that unstable uranium-236 in the first place. And here's the key: a slow neutron, often called a thermal neutron, spends more time in the vicinity of the nucleus as it passes, so it's far more likely to be captured. A fast neutron zips past too quickly and tends to glance off or recoil away without being absorbed. So, paradoxically, slowing the neutrons down increases the rate of fission, because capture is what matters, and slow neutrons get captured. That single fact, slow neutrons are captured more readily and so cause more fission, is the entire reason a reactor needs the component called a moderator, which we'll get to. Remember it: slow neutrons, more capture, more fission.

NARRATOR: Now the idea that turns one fission into a power source, or a bomb, the chain reaction. Look again at what one fission produces: two or three free neutrons. Each of those neutrons can, if it's slowed down and captured by another uranium-235 nucleus, trigger another fission, which releases two or three more neutrons, which trigger more fissions, and so on. That's a chain reaction, a self-propagating sequence where the neutrons from each generation of fissions set off the next. One becomes three becomes nine becomes twenty-seven, potentially, in a runaway cascade. Whether it runs away or holds steady comes down to a single number: on average, how many neutrons from each fission go on to cause exactly one more fission. A vivid way to picture it: imagine a room floored with loaded mousetraps, each holding a few ping-pong balls. Set off one trap, its balls fly out and spring others, which fling out more balls, and the whole room goes off in a flash, that's the uncontrolled version. Now imagine you could tune each trap so that, on average, its balls trip exactly one more trap, no more, the popping would roll along steadily and forever. That tuned, steady version is what a reactor achieves, and the tuning is done by the components we're about to meet.

NARRATOR: This is the crucial comparison, controlled versus uncontrolled, and it's a named exam question. In a controlled chain reaction, on average exactly one neutron from each fission goes on to cause one further fission. The reaction sustains itself at a steady, constant rate, releasing energy at a controllable pace. That's a nuclear reactor, a power station. In an uncontrolled chain reaction, on average more than one neutron from each fission causes a further fission, so the number of fissions multiplies, one to three to nine, doubling and redoubling in a tiny fraction of a second, releasing a colossal amount of energy almost instantaneously. That's a nuclear bomb. The physics of the individual fission is identical in both. The only difference is the neutron economy: held at exactly one for steady power, allowed to exceed one for an explosion. Listing five lays the comparison out side by side.

NARRATOR: There's one more concept the comparison needs: critical mass. For a chain reaction to sustain itself, you need enough fissile material packed together that the neutrons are likely to hit another nucleus before escaping out through the surface. Too small a lump, and too many neutrons leak out the sides without causing a fission, and the chain fizzles. The minimum amount of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction is the critical mass. Below critical mass, no self-sustaining chain; at or above it, the chain can run. A bomb works by suddenly assembling a supercritical mass; a reactor stays just critical, held precisely at one-neutron-per-fission. Now let's see how engineers actually hold it there, with the four components of a reactor.

NARRATOR: First, the Fuel. The fuel is the fissile material, usually uranium enriched so that it contains a higher proportion of uranium-235 than natural uranium does. Natural uranium is mostly uranium-238, which doesn't readily fission; you have to enrich it to get enough uranium-235. The fuel's job is to provide: it provides the fissioning nuclei, and therefore the energy and the neutrons that keep the chain going. Fuel provides.

NARRATOR: Second, the Control rods. These are rods made of a strong neutron-absorbing material, typically cadmium or boron. Their job is to absorb neutrons, removing them from the chain. And this is how you steer the reactor: push the control rods further into the core and they soak up more neutrons, leaving fewer to cause fission, so the reaction slows. Withdraw them and more neutrons survive to cause fission, so the reaction speeds up. By adjusting how far the rods are inserted, operators hold the chain reaction at exactly one neutron per fission, steady power. Control rods absorb. And in an emergency, you slam the rods all the way in, absorbing so many neutrons that the chain stops dead.

NARRATOR: Third, the Moderator. This is the one students most often confuse with the control rods, so be careful. The moderator, often water or graphite, does not absorb neutrons, it slows them down. Remember from earlier: the neutrons born in fission come out fast, but fast neutrons are poor at being captured, it's slow neutrons that cause fission efficiently. The moderator surrounds the fuel and slows the fast neutrons down to thermal speeds by collisions, so they're far more likely to be captured and cause the next fission. Without a moderator, the chain reaction in most reactors simply wouldn't sustain. Moderator slows. Say it with me: the moderator slows the neutrons, the control rods absorb the neutrons. Both change the reaction rate, but by completely different mechanisms, and swapping them is the single most common mark-loser in this topic.

NARRATOR: Fourth, the Coolant. The coolant, often water, sometimes a gas or liquid metal, flows through the core and carries the heat away. Remember, almost all the fission energy is the kinetic energy of the fragments, which becomes heat in the fuel. The coolant does two jobs at once: it extracts that heat and carries it out to a heat exchanger, where it boils water into steam that drives turbines to generate electricity, and at the same time it stops the core from overheating and melting. Coolant cools. Lose the coolant and the core overheats catastrophically, that's a meltdown, which brings me to a story worth holding onto.

NARRATOR: Two real stories cash in today's physics, and we'll tell them properly elsewhere. One is the Manhattan Project and the first nuclear fission bomb, which is the uncontrolled chain reaction taken to its extreme, the race to assemble a supercritical mass and let the neutron count run away. The other is Chernobyl, the physics of a meltdown, which is this reactor diagram going horribly wrong, a flawed control-rod design, a runaway power surge, and a loss of cooling. NARRATOR: Now the comparison the syllabus specifically asks for: how does the energy of nuclear fission stack up against ordinary fuels? Here's the number that should stick. A single fission of uranium-235 releases about two hundred mega-electron-volts. Burning a single atom of carbon in coal releases about ten electron-volts. Divide those and fission releases roughly twenty million times more energy per atom than chemical burning. Twenty million. That's why a few kilograms of uranium can do the work of thousands of tonnes of coal, and why a nuclear bomb dwarfs a chemical explosive of the same size. The reason, fundamentally, is that chemical reactions only rearrange the outer electrons of atoms, releasing electron-volts, while nuclear reactions reach into the nucleus and tap the binding energy, releasing mega-electron-volts, a million times more energetic per event. QUESTION: Question four. Explain the function of the moderator and the control rods in a nuclear reactor, and explain why they must not be confused. Four marks. Pause, write a full answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer.
