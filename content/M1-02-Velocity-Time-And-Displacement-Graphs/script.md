NARRATOR: Last episode we built the vocabulary of motion. Today we put that vocabulary onto graphs, and here's why this matters more than almost anything else in kinematics: motion graphs are the single most examined skill in Module One, and they're worth easy marks if, and only if, you never confuse a gradient with an area. By the end of today you'll be able to read a displacement-time graph and a velocity-time graph, pull velocity, acceleration, and displacement straight off them, and avoid the one mistake that costs more marks than any other in this topic. But first, a quick recap of last episode, because every graph today is a picture of exactly those quantities.

NARRATOR: Last episode, motion in a straight line, we drew the line between scalars and vectors. A scalar has magnitude only; a vector has magnitude and direction. The vector trio of motion is displacement, velocity, and acceleration, D-V-A, and each must carry a direction. Distance is the scalar total path; displacement is the vector net change in position, and for a full lap displacement is zero. Speed is distance over time; velocity is displacement over time. And the idea that turns into today's whole episode: the rate-of-change ladder. Velocity is the rate of change of displacement; acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. Position, then velocity, then acceleration, each one the rate of change of the step below. We also chose a positive direction and stuck with it. Today you'll see that "rate of change" is exactly what the gradient of a graph measures, and that's the key that unlocks everything.

QUESTION: Quick check before we start. From last episode: what is velocity the rate of change of, and what is acceleration the rate of change of? Pause the player, say both, then play.

NARRATOR: Velocity is the rate of change of displacement, and acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. Hold that, because "rate of change" is the gradient, the slope, of a graph, and that single connection is what makes motion graphs work. Let's start with the first graph.

NARRATOR: The displacement-time graph. Time runs along the horizontal axis, displacement up the vertical axis, with your chosen positive direction as positive. Now, the gradient of this graph, the rise over the run, is the change in displacement over the change in time. But change in displacement over change in time is exactly the definition of velocity. So here's the headline rule, the first of the three you must know cold: the gradient of a displacement-time graph is the velocity. A steep line means a large velocity; a gentle line means a small velocity; a horizontal line, zero gradient, means zero velocity, the object is at rest, its position isn't changing. And a line sloping downward, a negative gradient, means a negative velocity, the object moving in the negative direction, back toward where it started.

NARRATOR: What about a curved displacement-time graph? A curve means the gradient is changing, which means the velocity is changing, which means the object is accelerating. On a curve, the velocity at a single instant, the instantaneous velocity from last episode, is the gradient of the tangent to the curve at that point, the straight line that just grazes the curve there. A curve getting steeper means speeding up; a curve levelling off means slowing down. So a displacement-time graph tells you, at a glance: where the line is flat the object is stationary, where it's straight and sloped the velocity is constant, and where it curves the object is accelerating. You read velocity off it as the gradient, always.

NARRATOR: Now the second, and more important, graph: the velocity-time graph. Time along the bottom, velocity up the side. Apply the same logic. The gradient of this graph is the change in velocity over the change in time, and that is exactly the definition of acceleration. So the second rule: the gradient of a velocity-time graph is the acceleration. A straight sloping line means constant acceleration, the case all our equations of motion will assume next episode. A horizontal line means zero gradient, zero acceleration, so constant velocity, note that carefully, a horizontal line on a velocity-time graph is constant velocity, not at rest. A line that crosses the time axis, going from positive velocity to negative, means the object has stopped instantaneously and reversed direction.

QUESTION: That horizontal-line point trips people, so here's a retrieval question. A horizontal line on a displacement-time graph and a horizontal line on a velocity-time graph mean two completely different things. What does each one mean? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. A horizontal line on a displacement-time graph means the displacement isn't changing, so the object is at rest, not moving at all. A horizontal line on a velocity-time graph means the velocity isn't changing, so the object is moving at constant velocity, and its acceleration is zero. At rest versus constant velocity, two totally different states, and the only way to tell them apart is to know which graph you're looking at. If you got both, you've understood the most common source of confusion in the whole topic. The graph-reading rules are collected in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now the third rule, and this is the one that earns the most marks and causes the most errors: the area under a velocity-time graph is the displacement. Here's why. Velocity times time is displacement, that's just displacement equals velocity times time rearranged. On the graph, velocity is the height and time is the width, so velocity times time is height times width, which is the area under the line. So the area between the velocity-time line and the time axis is the displacement travelled. For a constant velocity that area is a rectangle, height times width. For constant acceleration, a sloping line, the area is a triangle, or a triangle plus a rectangle, and you add the pieces up. Area below the time axis, where the velocity is negative, counts as negative displacement, motion in the negative direction.

NARRATOR: So now you have the three rules, and here is the trap that the entire topic is built to catch. On a velocity-time graph, the gradient is the acceleration and the area is the displacement. They are two different operations giving two different quantities. Students under pressure read a value straight off the axis, or they find the area when the question wanted the gradient, or they find the gradient of a displacement-time graph and call it acceleration when it's actually velocity. So burn in the three rules with this hook. Gradient climbs the ladder: the gradient of a displacement-time graph gives you velocity, one rung up, and the gradient of a velocity-time graph gives you acceleration, the next rung up. Area on a velocity-time graph goes the other way, back down to displacement. Gradient climbs, area descends. Say it: slope of displacement-time is velocity, slope of velocity-time is acceleration, area under velocity-time is displacement. Three rules, no exceptions.

NARRATOR: Let me work a velocity-time graph in full, because this is the exam's favourite and the method is what's marked. The full working is in Listing two. Picture this graph: a car starts at rest, and its velocity rises in a straight line from zero to twenty metres per second over the first ten seconds. Then it holds a constant twenty metres per second for the next twenty seconds. Find the acceleration in the first phase, and the total displacement over the whole thirty seconds. First, the acceleration in the first phase. Acceleration is the gradient, rise over run: the velocity rose by twenty metres per second over ten seconds, so the gradient is twenty over ten, two metres per second per second. That's the acceleration during the first phase. In the second phase the line is horizontal, zero gradient, so zero acceleration, constant velocity, exactly as we said.

NARRATOR: Now the total displacement, which is the area under the whole line. Break it into shapes. The first phase, zero to twenty seconds, is a triangle: the area of a triangle is half the base times the height, half of ten seconds times twenty metres per second, which is one hundred metres. The second phase is a rectangle: width twenty seconds, height twenty metres per second, which is four hundred metres. Add them: one hundred plus four hundred is five hundred metres. So the car travelled five hundred metres in total. Notice what we did: gradient for the acceleration, area for the displacement, off the same graph, and we never once read a number straight off an axis and called it an answer. That's the full-mark method, and it's all in Listing two.

QUESTION: So on a displacement-time graph, can I find the acceleration the same way, by taking an area?

NARRATOR: No, and that's a sharp question that catches a real misconception, so let me be clear. On a displacement-time graph, the area under the line means nothing physical, it's not a quantity you ever use. The only thing you extract from a displacement-time graph is the gradient, which gives you velocity. To get acceleration from a displacement-time graph you'd have to find how the gradient itself is changing, which is a second step, and at this level we don't compute it numerically from a curve. The clean division is this: from a displacement-time graph you take gradients only, to get velocity. From a velocity-time graph you take the gradient for acceleration and the area for displacement. Area is only ever meaningful under a velocity-time graph. Don't go hunting for areas under a displacement-time graph; there's nothing there.

NARRATOR: Now let me push the area idea one step further, because it brings back the distance-versus-displacement distinction from last episode and it's a favourite trap. On a velocity-time graph, area above the time axis is positive displacement, motion in the positive direction, and area below the axis is negative displacement, motion in the negative direction. So if a graph has both, the total displacement is the area above minus the area below, the net result. But the total distance travelled is the area above plus the area below, because distance doesn't care about direction, it just adds up the path. There it is again: displacement is the net, distance is the total. Take an example, worked fully in Listing four. An object moves at a positive velocity, sweeping out an area of forty metres above the axis, then reverses and sweeps out an area of fifteen metres below the axis. Its displacement is forty minus fifteen, twenty-five metres in the positive direction, the net change in position. Its distance travelled is forty plus fifteen, fifty-five metres, the total ground covered. Same graph, two different answers, depending on whether the question asks for displacement or distance. Read the verb in the question carefully, and remember last episode: net for displacement, total for distance.

NARRATOR: A practical note, because the syllabus wants you to generate these graphs yourself in an investigation. A motion sensor, or a data logger with light gates, or the old ticker-timer tape, records an object's position over time, and the software, or your own plotting, turns that into a displacement-time graph directly. From there, taking gradients gives the velocity-time graph, and taking the gradient of that would give acceleration. So the three graphs are linked by exactly the rate-of-change ladder: each graph is the gradient of the one before it. When you do the prac, that's the chain you're demonstrating, position measured directly, velocity and acceleration derived as successive gradients. Knowing that chain is itself examinable, and it's why the gradient rules aren't arbitrary, they're the ladder made visible.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the strong extended-response answer for the classic "describe the motion shown by this graph" question, because it has a structure markers reward. Suppose you're shown a velocity-time graph. A strong answer walks through it in phases, and for each phase it names the velocity behaviour and the acceleration. For example: "In the first phase the velocity increases uniformly from rest, shown by the straight line of positive gradient, so the object undergoes constant positive acceleration. In the second phase the velocity is constant, shown by the horizontal line, so the acceleration is zero. The total displacement is given by the area under the graph." Hear what that does: it ties each feature of the graph, the slope, the horizontal section, the area, to a named physical quantity, velocity, acceleration, displacement. That's the mark-earning move: feature of the graph, then what it means physically.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you can hear the gap. A weak answer says: "The object speeds up, then stays the same, then the graph is flat." That earns very little, because it never names the acceleration, never says the flat line means constant velocity rather than at rest, and never mentions that the area gives displacement. It describes the picture in everyday words instead of in the language of velocity, acceleration, and displacement. The marks live in naming the physical quantity behind each feature, gradient is acceleration, area is displacement, horizontal is constant velocity, not in saying "it speeds up". Translate the shape into physics.

NARRATOR: Let me do one displacement-time reading too, quickly, because it's examined as well, and it's in Listing three. Suppose a displacement-time graph shows a straight line rising from zero to sixty metres over the first six seconds, then a horizontal line at sixty metres for four seconds, then a straight line coming back down from sixty metres to zero over the final three seconds. Phase one: gradient is sixty metres over six seconds, ten metres per second, a positive, constant velocity. Phase two: horizontal, zero gradient, the object is at rest, parked at sixty metres from the start. Phase three: gradient is the change, zero minus sixty, that's minus sixty metres, over three seconds, minus twenty metres per second, so the object moves back toward the start at twenty metres per second, the negative sign meaning the negative direction. So from a single displacement-time graph we read three different velocities, just by taking the gradient of each straight section. Gradient, every time, for velocity.

NARRATOR: One more skill the exam loves: being given one graph and asked to sketch the corresponding one. The three graphs of a single motion are linked by the gradient ladder, so their shapes correspond in a fixed way. Take the cleanest case, uniformly accelerated motion from rest. The acceleration is constant, so the acceleration-time graph is a horizontal line above the axis. The velocity grows at a steady rate, so the velocity-time graph is a straight line of constant positive gradient, sloping up from the origin. And the displacement grows faster and faster, because the velocity is increasing, so the displacement-time graph is an upward curve, getting steeper, a parabola. Read across them: a horizontal acceleration graph corresponds to a straight sloping velocity graph corresponds to a curving displacement graph. If you're shown the velocity-time straight line and asked for the displacement-time graph, you draw a curve that starts gently and steepens, because the gradient, which is the velocity, is increasing. If you're shown the curved displacement-time graph and asked for velocity-time, you read that the gradient is increasing and draw a rising straight line. Going up the ladder, from displacement to velocity to acceleration, each graph is the gradient of the one before, so a curve becomes a slope becomes a constant. Practise stepping between them in both directions, because the question can start you on any of the three.

NARRATOR: And a word on the tangent, because instantaneous velocity on a curve is examinable. When a displacement-time graph is curved, you can't just take rise over run between two far-apart points, that gives an average velocity over the interval. For the instantaneous velocity at a single moment, you draw the tangent to the curve at that exact point, the straight line that touches the curve there without crossing it, and you find the gradient of that tangent. The steeper the tangent, the faster the object is moving at that instant. This is the graphical version of last episode's "shrink the interval" idea: as you take two points closer and closer together on the curve, the line joining them approaches the tangent, and the average velocity over that shrinking interval approaches the instantaneous velocity at the point. So the tangent's gradient is the instantaneous velocity, exactly.

NARRATOR: And what if the velocity-time graph itself is curved, not a straight line? Then the acceleration isn't constant, it's changing, and the gradient at any instant is again the gradient of the tangent at that point. A velocity-time curve that's getting steeper means the acceleration is increasing; one levelling off means the acceleration is decreasing. The area rule still holds, the area under the curve is still the displacement, but now you can't use a single triangle, you estimate the area by counting the squares of the grid, or by splitting it into thin strips. So the two rules survive even when the motion is messy: gradient of the tangent for the instantaneous acceleration, area underneath for the displacement. Most exam graphs are made of straight-line segments to keep the areas as clean triangles and rectangles, but know that the rules don't break when the line bends, they just need a tangent and a square-count instead.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the threads together. Today connects straight back to last episode: the rate-of-change ladder, position to velocity to acceleration, is exactly why gradients work, because a gradient is a rate of change. And it connects straight forward to next episode, the equations of motion. In fact, the area-under-a-velocity-time-graph rule we used today is where one of those equations comes from: the displacement equals the area, and for uniformly accelerated motion that area is a trapezium, which gives displacement equals half the sum of the initial and final velocities, times time. The graph isn't separate from the equations, it's where they're born. And there's a forward link beyond this module too: in Dynamics you'll meet the idea that the area under a force-time graph is the impulse, and the area under a force-displacement graph is the work done, the exact same "area under the graph" technique, applied to new quantities. Master reading areas and gradients here and you've got a tool you'll reuse all year.

NARRATOR: Here's a quick way to never mix up gradient and area, using units, which is also good exam discipline. On a velocity-time graph the gradient is a velocity divided by a time, metres per second divided by seconds, which is metres per second per second, the unit of acceleration. So the gradient must be an acceleration, the units prove it. The area is a velocity multiplied by a time, metres per second times seconds, and the seconds cancel to leave metres, the unit of displacement. So the area must be a displacement. If you're ever unsure whether to take the gradient or the area, ask what units the answer needs: if the question wants an acceleration you take the gradient, if it wants a displacement you take the area. The units tell you which operation, every time.

NARRATOR: Let me bank the traps in one place, because motion graphs lose marks in the same few spots. Trap one: reading a value straight off an axis instead of taking a gradient or area, the most common slip. Trap two: confusing a horizontal line on a velocity-time graph (constant velocity) with one on a displacement-time graph (at rest). Trap three: forgetting the half when finding a triangular area under a velocity-time graph for accelerated motion. Trap four: calling the gradient of a displacement-time graph "acceleration", it's velocity, gradient only ever climbs one rung of the ladder. Trap five: ignoring area below the time axis, which is negative displacement, and so confusing distance with displacement. Trap six: looking for an area under a displacement-time graph, there's no meaningful area there. Every one of those is avoidable once you hold the three rules and check your units.

NARRATOR: Let me recap the takeaways, phrased for marks. One: the gradient of a displacement-time graph is the velocity, a flat line is at rest, a curve is accelerating, and the instantaneous velocity is the gradient of the tangent. Two: the gradient of a velocity-time graph is the acceleration, and a horizontal line here means constant velocity, not at rest. Three: the area under a velocity-time graph is the displacement, found by splitting it into triangles and rectangles, with area below the axis counting as negative. Four: gradient climbs the ladder, displacement-time slope gives velocity, velocity-time slope gives acceleration, and area on a velocity-time graph descends back to displacement. And the golden trap: on a velocity-time graph, gradient is acceleration and area is displacement, never mix them. Hold those and motion graphs are free marks.

QUESTION: Question one. State what the gradient of a displacement-time graph represents, and what the gradient of a velocity-time graph represents. Two marks. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The gradient of a displacement-time graph represents the velocity of the object. The gradient of a velocity-time graph represents the acceleration of the object. Two marks, one for each. The hook is "gradient climbs the ladder": the slope of displacement-time gives the next quantity up, velocity, and the slope of velocity-time gives the next up again, acceleration. The trap is saying the gradient of a displacement-time graph is acceleration, it's velocity.

QUESTION: Question two. A velocity-time graph shows a horizontal line at fifteen metres per second. Describe the motion of the object, and state its acceleration. Two marks. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The object is moving at a constant velocity of fifteen metres per second, because the velocity is not changing. Its acceleration is zero, because the gradient of the velocity-time graph is zero. Two marks: constant velocity (not "at rest"), and zero acceleration with the reason. The classic error is to say the object is stationary, that would be a horizontal line on a displacement-time graph; on a velocity-time graph a horizontal line is constant velocity.

QUESTION: Question three. A car accelerates uniformly from rest to thirty metres per second in twelve seconds. Using a velocity-time graph, calculate the acceleration and the distance travelled. Three marks. Pause, work it fully, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, full working in Listing two. The acceleration is the gradient: the velocity rose by thirty metres per second over twelve seconds, so thirty over twelve, two point five metres per second per second. The distance is the area under the line, which is a triangle: half the base times the height, half of twelve seconds times thirty metres per second, which is one hundred and eighty metres. Three marks: acceleration as the gradient, recognising the displacement is the area, and the correct triangular area of one hundred and eighty metres. The trap is multiplying thirty by twelve and forgetting the half, that gives the rectangle, not the triangle, for uniformly accelerated motion you need the triangle.

QUESTION: Question four. Explain the difference between what a horizontal line represents on a displacement-time graph and on a velocity-time graph. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. On a displacement-time graph, a horizontal line means the displacement is not changing with time, so the object is stationary, at rest. On a velocity-time graph, a horizontal line means the velocity is not changing with time, so the object is moving at a constant velocity, with zero acceleration. The difference arises because the vertical axis represents a different quantity in each graph: displacement in the first, velocity in the second. Three marks: at rest on the displacement-time graph; constant velocity on the velocity-time graph; and the reason, the different vertical-axis quantity. This is the single most tested distinction in motion graphs.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A velocity-time graph shows an object whose velocity increases uniformly from zero to twenty metres per second in five seconds, then decreases uniformly back to zero over the next ten seconds. Calculate the total displacement, and explain what the gradient of each section tells you. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The total displacement is the area under the graph, which is one large triangle spanning the whole motion, or two triangles added. The first section, zero to five seconds, is a triangle of area half times five seconds times twenty metres per second, which is fifty metres. The second section, five to fifteen seconds, is a triangle of area half times ten seconds times twenty metres per second, which is one hundred metres. The total displacement is fifty plus one hundred, one hundred and fifty metres. The gradient of the first section is twenty over five, four metres per second per second, a positive acceleration, the object is speeding up. The gradient of the second section is the change, zero minus twenty, that's minus twenty metres per second, over ten seconds, minus two metres per second per second, a negative acceleration, the object is slowing down. Four marks: the two areas summed to one hundred and fifty metres; the positive gradient as acceleration in the first phase; the negative gradient as deceleration in the second; and tying each gradient to speeding up or slowing down. Notice both accelerations came from gradients and the displacement came from areas, the two operations kept cleanly separate. That's the whole skill.
