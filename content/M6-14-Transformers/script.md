NARRATOR: Start with the structure, because the marker wants you to be able to describe the thing before you explain it. A transformer is two separate coils of insulated wire. One is the primary coil, the input, the side you connect to the source. The other is the secondary coil, the output, the side you draw your changed voltage from. Both coils are wound onto, or linked by, a common core made of soft iron. And here is the point students skate past: there is no electrical connection between the two coils. None. You could not trace a wire from the primary circuit to the secondary circuit. The only thing connecting them is magnetic. Energy crosses the gap as a changing magnetic field travelling through the iron, never as current flowing along a wire. If you write in an exam that the coils are "connected," even loosely, you've misunderstood the device.

NARRATOR: Why soft iron, and why a core at all? Two reasons, and "soft" is doing real work in that phrase. The core's job is to channel almost all the magnetic flux produced by the primary coil so that it threads, passes straight through, the secondary coil. Iron is ferromagnetic; it concentrates and guides magnetic field lines far better than air, so it acts like a pipe for flux, carrying the primary's flux around to the secondary with very little leaking away. That property, getting the primary's flux to pass through the secondary, is called flux linkage, and good flux linkage is the whole game. The "soft" part means the iron magnetises and demagnetises easily, flipping its magnetisation back and forth effortlessly as the alternating field reverses, which it has to do fifty times a second. A "hard" magnetic material would resist that and waste energy fighting itself. Soft iron, easily magnetised, good flux linkage. That's the design brief of the core.

NARRATOR: Now the mechanism, the cause-and-effect chain, and I want you to hear it as an ordered sequence because a marker scores it link by link. An alternating current is constantly reversing direction, so it produces a constantly changing magnetic field, and therefore a constantly changing magnetic flux, in the soft iron core. NARRATOR: Say that word to yourself: mutual induction. Not self-induction, that's a coil inducing an EMF in itself, which is back-EMF from a few episodes ago. NARRATOR: Now the last big piece, and it's the one that separates a band six answer from a competent one: the difference between an ideal transformer and a real one. The ideal transformer is one hundred percent efficient, power in equals power out. A real transformer is not; some power is always lost, so the secondary power is always a bit less than the primary power. Where does that lost power go? It turns into heat. That's why real transformers run warm, and why on an infra-red camera a working transformer glows. The syllabus names exactly three loss mechanisms, and you must be able to name all three, explain the mechanism of each, and say how each is reduced. Three losses, three mechanisms, three fixes. That's a six-mark answer waiting to happen.

NARRATOR: Here's your memory hook, and it's the one to keep stable across every recap: real transformers FRET. F-R-E-T. F for incomplete Flux linkage. R for Resistive heating. E for Eddy currents. Real transformers FRET. Picture an overworked transformer on a hot day, sweating and fretting about all the energy it's leaking. FRET: Flux linkage incomplete, Resistive heating, Eddy currents. Let me take them one at a time, because the marker wants the mechanism, not just the name, and there's a deadly trap in mixing up which fix goes with which loss.

NARRATOR: First, the F, incomplete flux linkage, also called flux leakage. The mechanism: not all of the flux produced by the primary actually passes through the secondary. Some of it leaks out through the surrounding air instead of staying in the iron, so the secondary links less flux than the primary produced. Less flux through the secondary means less induced EMF than the ideal, so the transferred power drops. How is it reduced? You channel the flux better. Wind the coils tightly together, often one wound directly over the other, on a closed, continuous iron core, a loop with no gaps, a rectangular or toroidal core, so the flux has an unbroken iron path the whole way round and almost none escapes into the air. Tight coils, closed core, that's the flux-linkage fix.

NARRATOR: Second, the E, eddy currents, and this is exactly the phenomenon we met two episodes ago, now showing up as a loss. The mechanism: the core itself is iron, which is a conductor, and the flux through it is constantly changing. By Faraday's law, that changing flux induces currents in the body of the iron core, and by Lenz's law they swirl so as to oppose the change. These are eddy currents, circulating loops of current right inside the core. They flow through the resistance of the iron and dissipate energy as heat, power equals current squared times resistance, heating the core. Note carefully: eddy currents flow in the iron core, not in the copper coils. How are they reduced? You laminate the core. You build it not from one solid block of iron but from many thin iron sheets, each coated with a thin insulating layer, stacked together. The insulating layers break up the wide loops the eddy currents want to flow in, so the currents are forced into thin, high-resistance paths and shrink dramatically. The laminations are oriented to cut across the eddy-current loops. Alternatively you use ferrites, materials that conduct magnetic flux well but conduct electricity poorly, so barely any eddy current can flow. Laminate the core, or use ferrites, that's the eddy-current fix.

NARRATOR: Third, the R, resistive heating, also called copper losses or current-squared-R losses. The mechanism: the copper wire making up both coils has its own electrical resistance, and any current flowing through a resistance dissipates power as heat, at a rate of current squared times resistance. That heat is just lost. How is it reduced? Use thicker wire, which has lower resistance, and good high-conductivity copper, so less power is wasted heating the windings. Thicker, lower-resistance wire, that's the resistive-heating fix.

NARRATOR: Now the trap, because this is where good students throw away marks. You must pair each loss with its correct fix, and the pairings are easy to scramble. Flux leakage is fixed by a closed, tightly-wound core. Eddy currents are fixed by laminating the core or using ferrites. Resistive heating is fixed by thicker, lower-resistance wire. Do not say lamination reduces resistive heating in the coils, it doesn't, lamination is about the core. Do not say thicker wire reduces eddy currents, it doesn't, that's the windings, not the core. And don't put eddy currents in the copper coils, they're induced in the iron core. Keep the three pairs straight: leakage with the closed core, eddy currents with lamination, resistance with thicker wire. Get the pairs right and a "describe the losses in a real transformer" question is a guaranteed full marks.
