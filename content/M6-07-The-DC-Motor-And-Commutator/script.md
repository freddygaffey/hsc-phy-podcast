NARRATOR: Before we build a motor, let's wind back through the last few lessons, because today is where all of them finally click into a single spinning machine. We've been climbing a ladder, and the DC motor is the top rung of the first half of this module.

NARRATOR: Two lessons ago, in the motor effect, we established the foundation: a current is just moving charges, so a current-carrying wire sitting in a magnetic field feels a force. The size of that force is the magnetic field strength B, times the current I, times the length L of wire in the field, times the sine of the angle between the wire and the field. That's F equals B I L sine theta. The mechanism — and this is the part the marker wants — is two fields interacting. The wire makes its own circular magnetic field, and that adds to the external field on one side of the wire and partly cancels it on the other. You get a strong field on one side, a weak field on the other, and the wire gets pushed from the strong side toward the weak side. The force is maximum when the wire sits at right angles to the field, and it drops to zero when the wire lies parallel to the field, because then the current runs along the field lines and there's nothing to push against. Hold onto that sine theta and that "perpendicular gives maximum, parallel gives zero" rule — both come straight back today.

NARRATOR: Then last lesson, in torque on a current loop, we took that single wire and bent it into a rectangular coil. Now you've got two sides of the coil that lie across the field, and the motor effect pushes on each of them. Here's the clever bit: the current runs up one side and down the other, so the two forces point in opposite directions. They don't cancel, because they act on opposite sides of the rotation axis. Two equal and opposite forces offset from each other like that form what's called a couple, and a couple produces a turning effect — a torque. We wrote the torque as tau equals n B I A cosine theta. That's the number of turns n, times the field B, times the current I, times the coil area A, times cosine theta — where theta here is the angle between the plane of the coil and the field. Torque is maximum when the coil plane lies parallel to the field, and torque is zero when the coil plane is perpendicular to the field, because at that instant the two forces pull straight outward along the axle and have no leverage to turn anything. And we listed the four ways to crank the torque up: more turns n, stronger field B, bigger current I, larger area A. They're just the four letters in the formula.

QUESTION: Wait — last lesson the angle was cosine, but in the motor effect it was sine. Aren't those going to get confused?

NARRATOR: They are, constantly, and that's exactly why I'm flagging it now. The two formulas measure their angle from different reference lines. In the force equation, F equals B I L sine theta, theta is the angle between the wire and the field — so when the wire is perpendicular to the field, sine of ninety is one and the force is biggest. In the torque equation, tau equals n B I A cosine theta, theta is the angle between the plane of the coil and the field — so when the coil plane is parallel to the field, cosine of zero is one and the torque is biggest. Same physics, two different reference lines, so you get sine in one and cosine in the other. Every time you write one down, say in your head which angle theta is. That habit alone saves marks.

NARRATOR: And going back a little further, because spaced repetition is the whole game here: the motor effect itself grew out of the single moving charge. A charge q moving with speed v through a field B feels a force q v B, always at right angles to its motion, which is why a free charge goes in a circle of radius m v over q B. Add up the force on every moving charge in a wire and you get F equals B I L. So the force that's about to spin our motor traces all the way back to the very first idea in this module: a magnetic field pushes sideways on a moving charge. One thread, the whole way through.

NARRATOR: So here's where we are. We can make a coil experience a torque. We know exactly when that torque is biggest and when it vanishes. Today we answer the question that's been hanging over the last two lessons: if the torque drops to zero every time the coil swings into the perpendicular position, why doesn't the motor just rock back and forth and stall? Why does a real motor spin round and round, continuously, in one direction? The answer is one small, ingenious component — the split-ring commutator — and understanding it is worth a guaranteed three or four marks in the exam. That's today's home topic: the components of a DC motor, what each one does, and how the commutator turns a wobble into continuous rotation.

NARRATOR: Let's be precise about what a motor even is, because the marker wants the energy language. A DC motor transforms electrical potential energy into rotational kinetic energy — mechanical energy. Electrical in, rotation out. And notice that's the exact inverse of a generator, which we'll meet later in this module — a generator takes rotation in and gives electrical energy out. Same machine run backwards. If a "compare a motor and a generator" question ever comes up, that one sentence — a motor converts electrical energy to mechanical, a generator converts mechanical to electrical — is the spine of the answer. Keep it in your back pocket.

NARRATOR: Now the components. There are four you must be able to name and, crucially, state the function of — naming alone won't get the marks. I've laid all four out, part by part, with their functions, in Listing 5. There's a clean way to hold them. Reading from the spinning centre outward: Rotor coil, Field magnets, Brushes, Commutator. Rotor, Field, Brushes, Commutator. A memory hook that sticks: "Real Friends Bring Cake." Real for Rotor coil, Friends for Field magnets, Bring for Brushes, Cake for Commutator. Silly, but at five times speed silly is exactly what survives. Real Friends Bring Cake — the four parts of a DC motor. Let me take each one and tell you what it does, because the function is where the marks live.

NARRATOR: First, the rotor coil — also called the armature coil. This is the loop of wire that carries the direct current from the battery. It's the current-carrying conductor that feels the motor-effect force, and it's the part that actually spins. A bit of vocabulary the HSC likes to test: the coil is wound onto a frame called the armature, which is a block of ferromagnetic material — usually soft iron — sitting on the axle. The armature plus the coil, the whole rotating assembly, is called the rotor. So "rotor" is the rotating part. Why soft iron in the middle? Because a ferromagnetic core concentrates and strengthens the magnetic field through the coil, which boosts the torque. Tuck that away — it's one of the torque-increasing factors we'll list shortly.

NARRATOR: Second, the field magnets — the stator. These are stationary magnets fixed to the casing of the motor. They can be permanent magnets or electromagnets, and their job is to supply the external magnetic field that the coil sits inside. By convention the field runs from the north pole across to the south pole — north to south. The stator is the stationary magnet assembly, as opposed to the rotor which spins. Here's a trap worth naming right now: students mix up rotor and stator, and they'll wrongly call the magnets "the rotor." The magnets don't move. They're the stator. The coil is the rotor. Don't swap them.

NARRATOR: Third, the brushes. These are stationary conducting contacts that press against the rotating commutator and keep the coil electrically connected to the battery as it spins. They're made of graphite — carbon — and there's a nice reason for that: graphite both conducts electricity and self-lubricates, so it slides smoothly against the spinning commutator without grinding it down. They "brush" against the commutator as it turns, which is where the name comes from, and they solve a very practical problem — without them, the supply wires would wind up and snap as the coil rotated. The brushes are stationary; the commutator rotates against them. Don't picture little metal bristles — they're solid graphite blocks.

NARRATOR: And fourth, the star of the show — the split-ring commutator. Physically, it's a metal ring that's been split into two halves, two segments, with an insulating gap between them. Each half is connected to one end of the coil, and the whole thing is mounted on the axle so it rotates with the coil. The brushes press against it from the sides. Functionally, it's a rotating mechanical switch — it automatically reverses the direction of the current through the coil every half-turn, at exactly the right moment, and that's what keeps the motor spinning one way. We're going to spend most of this lesson on precisely how and why it does that, because that's the examinable heart of the topic.

QUESTION: So why is the commutator necessary at all? What actually goes wrong without one?

NARRATOR: This is the question. Let me walk you through one rotation of a coil that does not have a commutator, so you feel the problem in your bones — and then you'll understand exactly what the commutator is fixing.

NARRATOR: Start with the coil plane lying parallel to the magnetic field. From last lesson, that's the maximum-torque position. The two active sides of the coil — the two sides lying across the field — each feel a motor-effect force, one pushing up, one pushing down, forming that couple. The torque is at its maximum and the coil starts to swing round. Good so far.

NARRATOR: As the coil rotates, it swings toward the position where its plane is perpendicular to the field. The forces on the two sides haven't changed size — the field's the same, the current's the same, the length's the same — but their leverage shrinks. The geometry, that cosine theta factor, falls. By the time the coil plane is perpendicular to the field, the two forces are pulling straight outward along the axle line — directly away from each other through the centre — and they have no turning effect at all. The torque is zero. This is the zero-torque position, sometimes called the dead point. Say that phrase to yourself — zero-torque position — because every mark in this topic hangs off it.

NARRATOR: Now, does the coil stop there? No — and this is a trap students fall into. The coil has momentum. It's spinning, it has rotational inertia, so it coasts straight through the dead point, just like a pendulum swings through the bottom of its arc. So far so good. But here's the disaster. The coil has now rotated past the dead point, and the forces on its two sides have not changed direction — same current, same field, same forces. The side that was being pushed up is still being pushed up. But that side is now on the other half of the rotation. So that same upward force, on the new side of the axis, now produces a torque in the opposite rotational sense. It's trying to push the coil back the way it came.

NARRATOR: So what happens? The coil gets shoved forward to the dead point by momentum, then immediately shoved back by a reversed torque, then forward again — it oscillates. It rocks back and forth about the zero-torque position and never completes a full turn. You don't have a motor; you have a very expensive paperweight that twitches. That is precisely the problem, and the commutator is precisely the cure.

NARRATOR: Here's the fix, and I want you to hear the full chain because the full chain is what earns full marks. The trick is: just as the coil reaches the zero-torque position, you reverse the direction of the current in the coil. If the current reverses right there, then the force on each side reverses too. So the side that was being pushed up is now pushed down. And that means past the dead point, the torque keeps acting in the same rotational direction — it keeps the coil turning the same way instead of dragging it back. Reverse the current at the dead point, every half-turn, and oscillation becomes continuous rotation. That's the whole idea.

NARRATOR: And the split-ring commutator does this automatically, mechanically, with no electronics at all — which is genuinely elegant. Watch the timing. As the coil spins, the commutator spins with it. The insulating gap between the two segments is positioned so that just as the coil swings through the zero-torque position, the gap lines up with the brushes. At that instant the brushes are sitting on the gap, so the circuit is momentarily broken — no current for a tiny moment, but it doesn't matter, momentum carries the coil through anyway. Then, as rotation continues a hair further, each brush comes into contact with the opposite commutator segment from the one it was just touching. The two halves swap which brush they're connected to. And because the segments are wired to the ends of the coil, swapping the brushes reverses the direction of current flowing through the coil. The reversal happens exactly at the dead point, exactly every half-turn — every one hundred and eighty degrees of rotation. The current flips, the forces flip, and the torque keeps driving the coil the same way around. Continuous, one-directional rotation.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the single most important contrast in this whole topic, because the HSC tests it relentlessly. The reason a DC motor needs this split-ring commutator is that the supply is direct current — it only ever flows one way out of the battery. So the only way to reverse the current in the coil at the right moment is to mechanically swap the connections, which is what the split ring does. Now contrast that with a simple AC motor. An AC supply already reverses its own direction, all by itself, fifty times a second in Australia. So an AC motor doesn't need a device to flip the current — the current flips on its own. That means an AC motor uses slip rings instead — full, continuous, unsplit rings — which simply maintain a continuous electrical connection to the rotating coil without ever reversing anything. Split-ring commutator for DC, because you have to force the reversal. Slip rings for AC, because the alternating supply reverses itself. If you remember one discriminator for "compare a DC and AC motor," remember that one. I've put the full DC-versus-AC comparison in a side-by-side table in Listing 7, which is the structure you want to reproduce for any "compare" question on this.

QUESTION: Let's lock that in. Pause the player here. Without looking at anything, say out loud: what is the difference between a split-ring commutator and a pair of slip rings, and why does a DC motor need the split ring while an AC motor uses slip rings? Pause now, answer it, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the model answer. A split-ring commutator is a single ring split into two insulated halves, used in a DC motor to reverse the direction of current in the coil every half-turn, at the zero-torque position, so the torque keeps acting in one rotational direction. Slip rings are continuous, unsplit rings used in an AC motor; they don't reverse anything, they just maintain a continuous connection to the rotating coil. A DC motor needs the split ring because its supply current flows in only one direction, so the reversal has to be done mechanically. An AC motor uses slip rings because the alternating supply already reverses direction on its own, so no mechanical reversal is needed. If you wrote roughly that, you've got the marks — including the trickiest one, which is saying why each machine uses the part it uses.

NARRATOR: A trap to call out by name right here: do not say an AC motor "has a commutator." It doesn't. A commutator, by definition, reverses current, and an AC motor has no need to. Slip rings only. Mixing those two up is one of the most common ways students throw away an easy mark on this topic.

NARRATOR: Now let's nail the model answer for the question that's almost guaranteed to appear, in some form, on this dot-point: "Explain the role of the split-ring commutator in a simple DC motor." It's worth three or four marks. I'm going to show you a weak answer and a strong answer side by side, because the gap between them is the whole lesson.

NARRATOR: The weak answer — the band-four answer — is just: "The commutator reverses the current in the coil." That's it. It's true, and it'll earn you about one mark, because you've stated what the commutator does. But it doesn't say when it reverses the current, it doesn't say why that matters, and it doesn't connect to torque or rotation at all. A one-mark answer to a four-mark question is a disaster, and it's the answer most students give.

NARRATOR: Now the strong answer — the full-marks chain. Listen for how every link connects to the next. "In a DC motor, the rotor coil carries current in a magnetic field, so the motor effect produces a force on the two coil sides that lie perpendicular to the field. These two forces act in opposite directions on opposite sides of the axis, forming a couple that produces a torque, which rotates the coil. As the coil rotates toward the position where its plane is perpendicular to the field — the zero-torque position — the torque falls to zero, and the coil's momentum carries it past this point. Without intervention, the forces on the sides would not have changed direction, so the torque would now act to push the coil back, and the coil would simply oscillate about the zero-torque position rather than rotate. The split-ring commutator prevents this. It is a metal ring split into two insulated halves, each connected to one end of the coil, that rotates with the coil while the stationary brushes maintain contact with it. Just as the coil passes through the zero-torque position, the insulating gap aligns with the brushes, momentarily breaking the circuit; as rotation continues, each brush contacts the opposite segment, reversing the direction of current through the coil. This reversal reverses the direction of the force on each side, so the torque continues to act in the same rotational direction. The commutator therefore reverses the current every half-turn at the zero-torque point, converting what would be oscillation into continuous rotation in one direction."

NARRATOR: Hear the difference? The strong answer earns every mark because it does four things: it sets up the torque, it identifies the zero-torque position as the problem, it describes the mechanism of the commutator physically, and it ties the reversal to the consequence — continuous rotation. The marker is literally ticking off: the problem, the timing, the mechanism, the result. I've put the strong answer broken into its scoring points in Listing 1, so you can see exactly which sentence wins which mark. The rule of thumb: every time you mention the commutator reversing the current, immediately attach the why and the when — every half-turn, at the zero-torque position, so the torque keeps acting one way. Never let "reverses the current" stand on its own.

NARRATOR: Let me drill the trap one more way, because deliberate redundancy is doing real work at five times speed. Three things students get wrong on this, and you should actively avoid all three. One: saying the coil "stops" at the dead point. It doesn't stop — momentum carries it through; the commutator's job is to flip the current so the torque past the dead point keeps it going the same way, not to restart a stopped coil. Two: saying the torque is constant through the rotation. It isn't — the force on each side stays the same size, but the torque varies, maximum when the coil plane is parallel to the field and zero when it's perpendicular, because the leverage changes. Markers check that you distinguish "constant force, varying torque." Three: the weak answer we just covered — "reverses the current," full stop, with no when, no why. Avoid those three and you're already ahead of most of the cohort.

NARRATOR: Now let's talk torque-boosting, because the syllabus has a specific dot-point: analyse the factors that can increase torque in a brushed electric motor. And the beautiful thing is you already know them — they're hiding in the formula. Torque equals n B I A cosine theta. So to increase the torque you turn up any of those letters. More turns on the coil — a bigger n — because each extra turn is another pair of current-carrying segments contributing force. A stronger magnetic field — a bigger B — by using stronger permanent magnets, or electromagnets, or by putting a soft-iron ferromagnetic core in the armature to concentrate the field, which is why that core is there. A larger current — a bigger I — which you get by increasing the EMF, the voltage, across the coil. And a larger coil area — a bigger A — for instance a wider coil, which pushes the sides further from the axis and gives more leverage. So: turns, field, current, area. The memory hook is the formula itself — n, B, I, A — those four letters are your four torque-boosters. If a question says "name three ways to increase the torque of a brushed DC motor," you just read three letters off tau equals n B I A. Both torque formulas, with the two angle conventions side by side and the full list of torque-boosters, are in Listing 6.

NARRATOR: There are two more sophisticated tricks worth a mention, because they show up in the better answers. First, curved or radial pole pieces — shaping the field magnets so their faces curve around the coil. This keeps the coil sides at right angles to the field for a much larger fraction of the rotation, which keeps the force near maximum and the torque smoother and more constant, instead of surging and dipping. Second, using multiple coils set at different angles on the armature, with a commutator that has more than two segments. While one coil is passing through its dead point, another coil is near its maximum-torque position, so there's always a coil pulling hard. That gives smoother running and a higher average torque. You don't need to derive these, but dropping "radial pole pieces keep the field perpendicular to the coil for longer" or "multiple coils avoid the dead-spot" into an extended response is the kind of detail that lifts a good answer to an excellent one.

QUESTION: Question three, four marks. Compare the structure and function of a simple DC motor and a simple AC motor. Pause, write it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, and structure this one as an explicit comparison — use the word "whereas," because markers reward contrast language.
