NARRATOR: Now here is the leap, and it's the kind of leap that wins a Nobel Prize. NARRATOR: Look hard at that relation, because its whole meaning is in the shape of it. The wavelength is on top with Planck's constant; the mass and the velocity are on the bottom. NARRATOR: Now for the conceptual payoff, the part of this episode that is worth the most marks and that most students completely fluff. We are going to use matter waves to fix Bohr. Remember the hole in Bohr's model: he asserted that only certain orbits are allowed but couldn't say why, and Rutherford asked how the electron knows which orbit to be in. De Broglie's matter wave answers that question, and the answer is one of the most satisfying ideas in the syllabus.

NARRATOR: Here's the picture. Don't think of the electron as a tiny ball whizzing around the nucleus. Think of it as a wave, smeared around the circular orbit, a wave wrapped into a loop. Now ask, for that wave to survive as it goes round and round, what has to be true? The wave travels around the circle and comes back to where it started. When it arrives back at the starting point, it has to join up smoothly with itself, crest meeting crest, in step. If it does, the wave reinforces itself every time round; it's stable; it persists. We call that a standing wave. But if the wave comes back out of step, crest landing on trough, then on each lap it interferes destructively with itself, cancelling itself out, and it rapidly dies away to nothing. An out-of-step orbit is self-destructing.

NARRATOR: So which orbits let the wave join up smoothly? Only the ones whose circumference is exactly a whole number of wavelengths. One wavelength, two wavelengths, three wavelengths, and so on, fitting perfectly around the loop, never a fraction left over. That's the standing-wave condition: a whole number of electron wavelengths equals the circumference of the orbit, which is two pi times the radius. A whole number, n, times the wavelength equals two pi r. Listing 4 has it written out. Any orbit that doesn't satisfy that, where the circumference is one-and-a-half wavelengths, say, makes the wave overlap out of phase, interfere destructively, and vanish. So those orbits simply cannot exist as stable electron states. Only the standing-wave orbits survive.

NARRATOR: And now feel the floor drop away, because this is the whole point. The orbits that survive, the standing-wave orbits, are exactly Bohr's allowed orbits. The quantisation Bohr had to postulate, pull out of a hat, just falls out, automatically, from requiring the electron-wave to form a standing wave. Only whole numbers of wavelengths are allowed, so only certain wavelengths are allowed, so by the de Broglie relation only certain momenta are allowed, so only certain energies are allowed. Discrete, quantised energy levels, the stationary states, drop straight out of the standing-wave condition. De Broglie didn't just assert quantisation; he explained it. That is the mechanism Bohr was missing, and it answers Rutherford's question: the electron doesn't "decide" anything; only standing-wave orbits are physically possible, the rest annihilate themselves.

NARRATOR: Here is the analogy that makes it unforgettable, and you should use it in the exam because it shows the marker you really get it. A guitar string. When you pluck a guitar string, it can't vibrate at just any frequency. It's fixed at both ends, so only certain wavelengths fit, the ones that put a whole number of half-wavelengths along the string. Those are the harmonics. Every other frequency dies instantly because it can't form a standing wave on that string. The allowed notes of a guitar string are exactly the standing waves it can hold. The allowed orbits of an electron are exactly the standing waves it can hold around the nucleus. Same physics. A plucked string sustains only certain harmonics; an electron sustains only certain orbits. If you remember "the electron orbit is a guitar string bent into a circle," you can reconstruct the entire argument.

NARRATOR: Let me make the cross-link explicit, because it's a back-reference worth marks and it ties two modules together. What de Broglie did was take that idea, only certain standing waves fit a bounded system, and apply it to an electron bound to a nucleus. The nucleus's orbit is the "bounded system," and the standing-wave condition picks out the allowed states. So the quantisation of atomic energy levels and the harmonics of a guitar string are the same mathematics. That connection is exactly the kind of thing a top-band answer draws.

NARRATOR: The problem with Bohr's model, even propped up by de Broglie, was that it was an uncomfortable mixture of classical and quantum physics. Definite classical orbits, with quantum rules bolted on. And it failed for any atom more complicated than hydrogen, it couldn't predict the relative intensities of spectral lines, and it couldn't handle the fine splitting of lines. A patchwork. In nineteen twenty-five and nineteen twenty-six, the Austrian physicist Erwin Schrödinger, spelled S-c-h-r-ö-d-i-n-g-e-r, took de Broglie's matter-wave idea seriously and to its logical end. He wrote down a wave equation, now called the Schrödinger equation, that describes the matter wave of an electron. You do not need the equation itself for the HSC; this is qualitative, "analyse the contribution." What you need is what its solutions mean.

NARRATOR: The solutions of Schrödinger's equation are called wave functions, written with the Greek letter psi. And here's the crucial interpretation, which actually came from Max Born, not Schrödinger himself: the square of the wave function, psi squared, gives the probability of finding the electron at a particular place. Not where the electron is, but how likely you are to find it there. This is a profound shift. Bohr's electron was at a definite radius on a definite circular orbit. Schrödinger's electron is described by a probability, a smear of likelihood spread through three-dimensional space. We stop saying "the electron is here" and start saying "the electron is probably somewhere in this region."

NARRATOR: So the definite circular orbit is gone. It's replaced by a three-dimensional cloud of probability, a region where the electron is likely to be found, and we call that cloud an orbital. Be careful with the words here, because it's a marking trap: an orbit, with a t, is Bohr's definite circular path; an orbital, with an a-l, is Schrödinger's fuzzy probability cloud. Different words, different physics. The textbook puts it cleanly: the best model for atoms with discrete energy levels is to treat the electron as a standing wave, and the allowable standing waves are the orbitals. So the standing-wave idea from de Broglie becomes, in Schrödinger's hands, the full three-dimensional probability cloud. And crucially, Schrödinger's is a fully quantum theory, no classical orbits bolted on, so it handles multi-electron atoms and predicts the line intensities Bohr couldn't. That's the contribution: it removed the classical-quantum patchwork and replaced definite orbits with probability orbitals.

The cricket ball has a large mass and therefore a large momentum, giving a wavelength of roughly ten to the minus thirty-four metres, far smaller than any physical gap, so it never diffracts and its wave nature is undetectable. The electron has a very small mass, so even at high speed its wavelength is about ten to the minus ten metres, the same order as the spacing between atoms in a crystal lattice; the crystal therefore acts as a diffraction grating and the electron's wave nature is observed as a diffraction pattern. That's the full reasoning, two marks for the contrast, one for naming the crystal spacing.

QUESTION: Question three, a "describe the evidence" question. "Describe how electron diffraction experiments provide evidence that electrons exhibit wave properties." Pause and answer.

NARRATOR: Model answer. In nineteen twenty-seven, Davisson and Germer fired a beam of electrons at a nickel crystal in a vacuum and measured the intensity of the reflected electrons as a function of angle. The electrons emerged in regular intensity peaks at specific angles, a diffraction pattern, which is a uniquely wave phenomenon arising from constructive and destructive interference; a stream of classical particles could not produce such maxima and minima. Crucially, the wavelength calculated from the angles of the diffraction pattern matched the wavelength predicted by the de Broglie relation, Planck's constant divided by the electrons' momentum, confirming that the electrons genuinely behave as waves with that wavelength. G. P. Thomson independently fired electrons through a thin metal foil and obtained diffraction rings, analysed with the same techniques as X-ray diffraction, again matching the de Broglie prediction. Because diffraction is a wave behaviour and the measured wavelength agreed with de Broglie's formula, electrons must exhibit wave properties. The mark is in naming the pattern and the wavelength match, not just saying "they interfered."

QUESTION: Question four, the big extended response. "Explain how treating the electron as a standing wave accounts for the existence of discrete energy levels in an atom, and how this addresses a limitation of the Bohr model." Six marks. Pause the player, write the whole thing, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The Bohr model successfully predicted hydrogen's energy levels but could only postulate that certain orbits are allowed; it offered no mechanism for why, a limitation Rutherford criticised by asking how the electron knows which orbit to occupy. De Broglie proposed that the electron has a wavelength equal to Planck's constant divided by its momentum and behaves as a wave. If the electron-wave is pictured wrapped around a circular orbit, it can persist only if it joins smoothly onto itself after each revolution, forming a standing wave; this requires the orbit's circumference to equal a whole number of electron wavelengths. If the circumference is not a whole number of wavelengths, the wave interferes destructively with itself on successive orbits and rapidly dies out, so that orbit is forbidden. Therefore only the standing-wave orbits are stable. Because each allowed orbit corresponds to a single discrete wavelength, only discrete momenta, and hence only discrete, quantised energies, are possible, exactly the stationary energy levels Bohr had assumed. The standing-wave condition thus supplies the mechanism the Bohr model lacked, in the same way that a vibrating string can sustain only certain harmonic frequencies. Full marks: it names the limitation, the de Broglie wavelength, the standing-wave condition with the whole-number-of-wavelengths requirement, why other orbits die, and the link from discrete wavelengths to discrete energies. The structure is in Listing 5.

QUESTION: Question five, to finish, on the modern model. "Analyse the contribution of Schrödinger to the model of the atom, including how it overcomes a limitation of the Bohr model." Pause and answer.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Schrödinger, in nineteen twenty-six, developed a wave equation describing the electron as a matter wave, building on de Broglie's hypothesis. Its solutions are wave functions, and the square of a wave function gives the probability of finding the electron at a given location, an interpretation provided by Max Born. This replaces Bohr's definite circular orbits with three-dimensional regions of probability called orbitals, within which the electron is likely to be found. Schrödinger's model is a fully quantum theory rather than Bohr's uncomfortable mixture of classical orbits and quantum postulates, so it successfully describes multi-electron atoms and predicts the relative intensities of spectral lines, both of which the Bohr model could not. It is consistent with Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, that an electron's position and momentum cannot be known simultaneously, which is itself why a definite orbit must be abandoned in favour of a probability distribution. The contribution, then, is the shift from definite orbits to probability orbitals in a complete quantum description. Notice the marks come from naming the wave function, the probability interpretation, the orbital replacing the orbit, and at least one specific Bohr limitation it removes.
