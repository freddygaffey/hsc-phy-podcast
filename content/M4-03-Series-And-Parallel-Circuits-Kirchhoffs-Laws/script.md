NARRATOR: Welcome back to Module Four, Electricity and Magnetism. Last episode we built the four quantities of a circuit, current, voltage, resistance and power, and tied three of them together with Ohm's law. But every example we did had a single component in a single loop. Real circuits have many components, and they're wired together in two basic ways: in series, one after another in a single path, or in parallel, side by side on separate paths. Today we work out how current, voltage and resistance behave in each, and we'll find that it all flows from two beautifully simple rules, Kirchhoff's laws, which are nothing more than conservation of charge and conservation of energy applied to circuits. By the end you'll be able to state Kirchhoff's two laws and the conservation principle behind each, apply the series and parallel rules, and solve a multi-resistor network. But first, a recap.

NARRATOR: Last episode we established that current is the rate of flow of charge, I equals q over t; voltage is the energy per unit charge, V equals W over q; resistance is R equals V over I, with Ohm's law V equals I R; and we noted that, because charge is conserved, the current is the same all the way around a single loop. We also, much earlier, met conservation of energy in the Dynamics module. Hold three things: Ohm's law, which we'll apply to every component today; conservation of charge, which becomes Kirchhoff's current law; and conservation of energy, which becomes Kirchhoff's voltage law. So today isn't really new physics, it's two conservation laws you already trust, applied to the junctions and loops of a circuit.

QUESTION: Quick check from last episode. State Ohm's law in any of its forms. And, because charge is conserved, what is true about the current at every point around a single, unbranched loop? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Ohm's law: the voltage across a conductor equals the current through it times its resistance, V equals I R, equivalently R equals V over I or I equals V over R. And because charge is conserved, in a single unbranched loop the current is the same at every point, the charge isn't used up or piled up anywhere, the same number of coulombs per second pass every point. Ohm's law, and the same current everywhere in a single loop. That second idea is exactly the series-circuit rule we're about to formalise, and it generalises, at junctions, into Kirchhoff's current law. Let's start with the two laws.

NARRATOR: Kirchhoff's two laws are the foundation of all circuit analysis, and the mark-earning move is to name the conservation principle behind each, they're in Listing one. Kirchhoff's current law says that at any junction, any point where wires meet, the total current flowing in equals the total current flowing out; equivalently, the currents at a junction sum to zero. Why? Because charge is conserved, charge can't accumulate or vanish at a junction, so whatever flows in must flow out. Kirchhoff's voltage law says that around any complete loop in a circuit, the voltages sum to zero; the total voltage supplied by the sources equals the total voltage dropped across the components. Why? Because energy is conserved, a unit of charge travelling around a loop and back to its start must have gained exactly as much energy from the sources as it lost in the components, no energy created or destroyed. So the current law is conservation of charge at a junction, and the voltage law is conservation of energy around a loop. Name those principles and you've earned the marks.

QUESTION: A retrieval one worth stopping for. State Kirchhoff's two laws, and for each, name the conservation principle it expresses. Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Kirchhoff's current law: the sum of the currents flowing into a junction equals the sum flowing out, or the currents at a junction sum to zero; this expresses the conservation of charge. Kirchhoff's voltage law: around any closed loop, the sum of the voltages is zero, the voltage supplied equals the total voltage dropped; this expresses the conservation of energy. Current law, charge conserved at a junction; voltage law, energy conserved around a loop. If you named both the law and its conservation principle, that's a full-mark answer, the conservation principle is the part students forget. Now let's see these laws produce the series and parallel rules.

NARRATOR: First, series circuits: components connected one after another, forming a single path with no branches, like beads on a single string. Three rules follow, and they're in Listing two. One, the current is the same through every component, because there's only one path and charge is conserved, exactly the single-loop idea, so the current can't change as it goes through one component after another. Two, the voltage divides among the components: the total voltage supplied by the source is shared out, with more voltage dropped across a larger resistance, and the individual voltage drops add up to the supply voltage, that's Kirchhoff's voltage law. Three, the total resistance is the sum of the individual resistances, R-total equals R-one plus R-two plus and so on, so adding components in series always increases the total resistance. A practical consequence: in a series circuit, if any one component breaks or is removed, the single path is broken and the current stops everywhere, which is why old Christmas lights all went out when one bulb blew, and why a single break anywhere in a series loop is enough to stop the whole circuit. Series: same current, voltage shares, resistances add.

NARRATOR: Now parallel circuits: components connected side by side across the same two points, providing separate, alternative paths for the current, like several lanes of a motorway between the same two junctions. Again three rules, in Listing two, and they're almost the mirror image of series. One, the voltage across each branch is the same, because every branch connects the same two points, which are at a fixed potential difference, so each branch gets the full supply voltage. Two, the current divides among the branches: the total current from the source splits at the junction, with more current flowing through the branch of lower resistance, and the branch currents add up to the total, that's Kirchhoff's current law. Three, the total resistance is found from the reciprocal sum: one over R-total equals one over R-one plus one over R-two and so on. A crucial and counterintuitive consequence: adding resistors in parallel decreases the total resistance, and the total is always less than the smallest individual branch resistance, because you're providing more paths for the current, like opening extra checkout lanes. Parallel: same voltage, current shares, resistance drops below the smallest branch.

QUESTION: A retrieval check on the two configurations. In a series circuit, which quantity is the same through every component, current or voltage? And in a parallel circuit, which quantity is the same across every branch? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. In a series circuit, the current is the same through every component, because there's a single path; the voltage divides. In a parallel circuit, the voltage is the same across every branch, because each branch spans the same two points; the current divides. Series, same current; parallel, same voltage. A neat way to never forget it: in series there's only one road, so everyone, all the charge, travels at the same rate, the current is shared; in parallel the road splits, but both lanes start and end at the same two points, so they span the same voltage. That single pair of facts is the key to the whole topic: whichever quantity is "the same" tells you immediately which configuration you're dealing with, and the other quantity is the one that divides. Lock it: series shares voltage, parallel shares current. The rules are summarised in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "compare how current and voltage behave in series and parallel circuits", because this is a guaranteed exam item. The band-six version: in a series circuit there is a single path, so the current is the same through every component, while the supply voltage is divided among the components in proportion to their resistances, and the total resistance is the sum of the individual resistances. In a parallel circuit there are multiple paths, so the voltage across each branch is the same and equal to the supply, while the total current divides among the branches with more current in the lower-resistance branch, and the total resistance is found from the reciprocal sum, making it less than the smallest branch resistance. The marks are for stating, for each configuration, which quantity is constant, which divides, and how the resistances combine. The weak answer, "in series it all adds up and in parallel it splits", is too vague to earn well. Say which quantity is constant and which divides, for each, that's the structure.

NARRATOR: Now let's actually solve a network, because reducing a circuit to a single equivalent resistance and then applying Ohm's law is the core exam skill, it's worked in Listing three. Take two resistors in series, four ohms and two ohms, connected to a twelve-volt supply. First, the total resistance: in series they add, four plus two, six ohms. Then the current from the supply, by Ohm's law, is the supply voltage over the total resistance, twelve over six, two amperes, and because it's series, that two amperes flows through both resistors. Then the voltage across each: across the four-ohm resistor, V equals I R, two times four, eight volts; across the two-ohm resistor, two times two, four volts. Check with Kirchhoff's voltage law: eight plus four equals twelve volts, the full supply, as it must. Notice the larger resistor took the larger share of the voltage, eight volts versus four. That sequence, find the total resistance, find the current, then find each voltage, is the standard method, and the check is that the voltages add to the supply.

QUESTION: Now a parallel one for you. Two resistors, six ohms and three ohms, are connected in parallel across a twelve-volt supply. First find the total resistance, then the current through each branch. Pause the player, work it through, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing three. Total resistance from the reciprocal sum: one over R-total equals one over six plus one over three. Put them over a common denominator: one over six plus two over six is three over six, which is one half. So one over R-total is one half, meaning R-total is two ohms, and notice that's less than the smallest branch, three ohms, exactly as the parallel rule promises. Now the branch currents: because it's parallel, each branch has the full twelve volts across it. Through the six-ohm branch, I equals V over R, twelve over six, two amperes; through the three-ohm branch, twelve over three, four amperes. Check with Kirchhoff's current law: the total current is two plus four, six amperes, which matches the supply voltage over the total resistance, twelve over two, six amperes. The lower-resistance branch, three ohms, carried the larger current, four amperes, as expected. Same voltage on each branch, current divides with more through the smaller resistance.

NARRATOR: It's worth seeing why this matters in the real world, because the most important everyday circuit, the wiring of your house, is built in parallel, and the reasons come straight from today's rules. Every power point and light in your home is wired in parallel across the mains supply, for three reasons. First, each appliance gets the full supply voltage, the same two hundred and thirty or two hundred and forty volts, regardless of what else is switched on, because in parallel the voltage across every branch is the same. Second, each appliance can be switched on and off independently: switching off one branch, or one blowing, doesn't break the others, because each has its own path, unlike a series circuit where one break stops everything. Third, each branch draws only the current it needs, and the currents add. That third point is also the danger: because the branch currents add at the junction, by Kirchhoff's current law, plugging in too many high-power appliances at once makes the total current very large, which is why the wiring includes fuses and circuit breakers that cut the supply if the total current exceeds a safe value. So household parallel wiring is a direct, examinable application of all three parallel rules.

NARRATOR: Let me also give you the intuition behind how things divide, because "voltage divides in series" and "current divides in parallel" are easy to state but worth understanding. In series, think of the voltage as energy handed out per coulomb: as a charge passes through each resistor it gives up some energy, and a bigger resistor takes a bigger bite, so the voltage drops are in proportion to the resistances, this is the voltage-divider idea, the resistor with the larger share of the resistance gets the larger share of the voltage. In parallel, think of the current choosing paths: faced with two branches, more charge per second flows down the easier, lower-resistance path, so the currents divide in inverse proportion to the resistances, the smaller resistor carries the larger current, this is the current-divider idea. So in series the bigger resistor dominates the voltage, while in parallel the smaller resistor dominates the current. Those two "who gets the bigger share" rules are quick sanity checks on any answer, and they're in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Let me highlight the two checks you just used, because they catch errors and earn method marks. In any series section, the individual voltage drops must add up to the voltage across that section, Kirchhoff's voltage law, conservation of energy. At any junction, the currents into it must equal the currents out, Kirchhoff's current law, conservation of charge. After any network calculation, verify both: do the voltages around the loop add to the supply, and do the branch currents add to the total? If they don't, you've made a slip. These conservation checks aren't just safety nets, an exam answer that explicitly verifies them demonstrates understanding and protects against arithmetic errors. The checks are noted in Listing one.

NARRATOR: One more layer the syllabus wants, energy conversion in the components, because the rate of energy conversion, the power, also depends on the configuration. The total power delivered by the supply is always the supply voltage times the total current it delivers, P equals V I, and by conservation of energy this equals the sum of the powers dissipated in all the individual components, another expression of Kirchhoff's voltage law as energy bookkeeping. But how the power splits differs between the configurations. In a series circuit, where the same current flows through each component, the most useful form is P equals I squared R, so the largest resistor dissipates the most power, it has the same current but the biggest resistance. In a parallel circuit, where each branch has the same voltage, the most useful form is P equals V squared over R, so the smallest resistor dissipates the most power, it has the same voltage but the lowest resistance, drawing the largest current. Notice the reversal: in series the big resistor gets the most power, in parallel the small resistor does, and in each case it's because of which quantity, current or voltage, is shared. Matching the power formula to what's constant is the same skill from last episode, now applied to networks.

NARRATOR: Let me quickly fold that into a worked figure so it's concrete. Take the earlier series circuit, four ohms and two ohms on twelve volts, with two amperes flowing. The power in the four-ohm resistor is I squared R, two squared times four, four times four, sixteen watts; in the two-ohm resistor, two squared times two, eight watts; total twenty-four watts. Check against the supply: P equals V I, twelve times two, twenty-four watts, a perfect match, conservation of energy confirmed. The bigger resistor, four ohms, dissipated the most, sixteen of the twenty-four watts, exactly as P equals I squared R predicts for a series circuit. Running that energy check, component powers adding to the supply power, is another way to catch an arithmetic slip, and it's in Listing three.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can verify all of this on a breadboard or with a circuit kit: build a series circuit and confirm the ammeter reads the same current at different points, while voltmeters across each resistor show the drops adding to the supply. Build a parallel circuit and confirm each branch has the full supply voltage while the branch currents add to the total drawn from the source. You can measure the equivalent resistance of combinations and compare with the series-sum and reciprocal-sum predictions. Remember the meter placement from last episode: ammeters in series, in the path whose current you want; voltmeters in parallel, across the component whose voltage you want. A common practical point is that real ammeters have a small resistance and voltmeters a very large one, chosen precisely so they don't disturb the circuit they measure: an ammeter goes in series, so a low resistance keeps it from adding to the total and reducing the current it's trying to read, while a voltmeter goes in parallel, so a very high resistance keeps it from drawing a significant current through itself and stealing it from the component. Ideal meters would have zero and infinite resistance respectively; real ones get close enough not to matter. Describing and interpreting such an investigation is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: swapping the series and parallel rules, series shares voltage with current constant, parallel shares current with voltage constant, keep them straight by remembering single path versus multiple paths. Trap two: thinking parallel resistance is larger, it's always smaller than the smallest branch, because extra paths ease the flow. Trap three: forgetting to take the reciprocal at the end of a parallel calculation, one over R-total gives a half, so R-total is two, not a half. Trap four: not naming the conservation principle when asked about Kirchhoff's laws, current law is charge, voltage law is energy. Trap five: assuming the voltage divides equally in series, it divides in proportion to resistance, the bigger resistor gets the bigger share. Avoid those five and circuit networks are secure.
