NARRATOR: Start with the goal. Thomson wanted to know how heavy the cathode-ray particle was and how much charge it carried. The clever and slightly frustrating thing is that he couldn't get those two numbers separately. What he could get was their ratio, the charge divided by the mass, written q over m, the charge-to-mass ratio. Why only the ratio? Because every force he could apply to the particle depends on both the charge and the mass at the same time, and they're tangled together. The electric force depends on the charge. The acceleration that force produces depends on the mass. So when you watch how the particle moves, what you're really seeing is the charge and the mass acting together, and the cleanest thing you can extract is the combination, q over m. Keep that in your head, because it's the single most examined trap in this whole topic. Thomson measured a ratio, not a charge and not a mass.

NARRATOR: Now the apparatus. Picture a cathode-ray tube. At one end, an electron gun, the cathode and anode from last episode, firing a narrow beam of electrons down the tube. The beam then enters a region where Thomson has set up two fields at the same time. There's a pair of charged parallel plates, which makes a uniform electric field across the beam. And there's a pair of coils, Helmholtz coils, which makes a uniform magnetic field across the beam. The crucial geometry is this: the electric field, the magnetic field, and the velocity of the beam are all mutually perpendicular. All three at right angles to each other. And Thomson arranged it so that the electric force on the beam and the magnetic force on the beam point along the same line but in opposite directions. One pushes the beam up, the other pushes it down. That opposition is the whole trick.

QUESTION: Hang on, why does he need two fields fighting each other? Wouldn't one field be enough to bend the beam and measure something?

NARRATOR: Good instinct, and the answer is the heart of the method. The problem is that to find the charge-to-mass ratio he needs to know how fast the particles are travelling, and he has no independent way to measure that speed. The electrons come out of the gun at some velocity he doesn't know. So the experiment runs in two stages. Stage one's only job is to measure the speed. Stage two's only job is to measure the charge-to-mass ratio, and stage two needs the speed from stage one to work. That's why you need both. Let me take them one at a time, because in the exam you must describe both stages and say why each one is needed. That second part, the why, is what separates a band-six answer from a band-four answer.

NARRATOR: Stage one. The velocity selector. Both fields are switched on, electric and magnetic, opposing each other. Now think about the two forces on a single electron. The electric force is the charge times the electric field strength. That force is fixed in size, it doesn't care how fast the electron is going. The magnetic force is the charge times the speed times the magnetic field strength, because the velocity is perpendicular to the field. Notice the magnetic force does depend on speed. A faster electron feels a bigger magnetic force; a slower one feels a smaller magnetic force. So here's what Thomson does: he adjusts the field strengths until the beam goes straight through the apparatus completely undeflected. Dead straight. No bend up, no bend down.

NARRATOR: And undeflected means something very precise. If the beam isn't bending, the net force on it is zero, so the upward electric force exactly balances the downward magnetic force. Setting them equal: the charge times the electric field equals the charge times the speed times the magnetic field. Now watch what happens. The charge appears on both sides of that equation, so it cancels clean out. What you're left with is: the speed equals the electric field strength divided by the magnetic field strength. The speed is just E divided by B. The full working for that is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: That result is gorgeous for two reasons. First, the right-hand side, the electric field over the magnetic field, is made entirely of things Thomson set himself and can read off his own dials. So he now knows the speed of the beam, with no guessing. Second, and this is the part to say out loud in an exam, because the charge cancelled, the selected speed is completely independent of the charge and the mass of the particle. Any charged particle, whatever its charge, whatever its mass, that makes it through undeflected has the same speed, E over B. That's why we call it a velocity selector. It selects one speed and lets only that speed pass straight through.

NARRATOR: One trap to nail down right now, because students throw away marks on it. When we say the charge cancels, that does not mean the charge is zero. The particle is absolutely still charged. NARRATOR: Right, stage one done. We now know the speed of the beam, E over B. On to stage two, where we actually extract the charge-to-mass ratio.

NARRATOR: Stage two. Thomson switches the electric field off. Completely off. Now only the magnetic field is acting on the beam. And here's the key fact about the magnetic force, which again you met in Module 6: the magnetic force on a moving charge is always perpendicular to its velocity. Always at right angles to the direction it's moving. A force that's always perpendicular to the motion does no work on the particle, so it can't speed it up or slow it down. All it can do is constantly change the direction of the velocity. And a force that constantly turns the velocity while keeping the speed the same is, by definition, a centripetal force. So the magnetic force acts as a centripetal force and bends the beam into a circular arc.

QUESTION: Before I go on, here's one to actually stop and think about. Why does the beam follow a circle, and not, say, a parabola like a projectile? Pause the player, work out the difference, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. A projectile, like a ball thrown horizontally, feels a force, gravity, that always points the same way, straight down, no matter which way the ball is moving. A constant-direction force gives you a parabola. The magnetic force is different. It always points perpendicular to the velocity, so as the electron turns, the force turns with it, always aiming towards the same central point. A force of constant size always pointing at a fixed centre is the recipe for uniform circular motion. That's why it's a circle and not a parabola, and it's exactly the charged-particle-in-a-magnetic-field motion from Module 6. Second deep link, and it's the spine of stage two.

NARRATOR: So let's write down stage two. The magnetic force provides the centripetal force. The magnetic force is the charge times the speed times the magnetic field. Set them equal: the charge times the speed times the magnetic field equals the mass times the speed squared over the radius. Now rearrange to get charge over mass on one side. One factor of speed cancels, and you're left with: the charge-to-mass ratio equals the speed divided by the radius times the magnetic field. The full algebra is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: And look at what's on the right-hand side. The speed, we got from stage one, it's E over B. The magnetic field, Thomson set it and reads it off the dial. NARRATOR: Now, Thomson gave us the ratio, and only the ratio. He could not tell you the charge of the electron, and he could not tell you the mass of the electron, on their own. To break the ratio apart you need one more independent measurement, of either the charge or the mass. That's where Robert Millikan comes in, about a decade later, around nineteen oh-nine, with one of the most elegant experiments in all of physics: the oil-drop experiment. Its job is to measure the charge by itself.

NARRATOR: Here's how it works. Millikan sprays a fine mist of oil droplets from an atomiser into a chamber. As they're sprayed, and with a bit of help from X-rays ionising the air, the droplets pick up a small electric charge, a few extra electrons each, making them slightly negative. A droplet then drifts into the space between two horizontal charged parallel plates, which create a uniform electric field, just like the plates in Thomson's tube. Millikan watches a single droplet through a microscope and adjusts the voltage across the plates until the droplet hangs there, perfectly still, suspended in mid-air. Held in equilibrium.

NARRATOR: And suspended is the key word, because it tells you the forces balance. When the drop hangs motionless, the upward electric force on its charge exactly balances the downward pull of gravity, its weight. So the charge times the electric field equals the mass of the drop times the acceleration due to gravity. Rearrange and the charge equals the mass of the drop times gravity, divided by the electric field. The electric field he knows, it's the voltage over the plate separation. Gravity he knows. The only thing left is the mass of the tiny oil drop, and he gets that by switching the field off and timing how fast the drop falls to its terminal velocity under gravity and air resistance, then using fluid mechanics and the known density of the oil to back out the radius and hence the mass. Once he has the mass, he has the charge on that drop.

QUESTION: So how does measuring the charge on a few oil drops tell you anything fundamental? Surely every drop just has some random amount?

NARRATOR: That's exactly the question, and the answer is the magic of the experiment. Millikan measured the charge on drop after drop after drop, hundreds of them. And the charges were never random. Every single charge he measured came out as a whole-number multiple of one smallest value. One times that value, or two times it, or three times it, but never one-and-a-half times it, never anything in between. That smallest value, the building block that every charge was a multiple of, is one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs. That is the elementary charge, the charge on a single electron. And the deep conclusion is that electric charge is quantised. It comes in discrete packets, whole-number multiples of the elementary charge, never a continuous smear. Charge is grainy, not smooth. Millikan won the Nobel Prize for this in nineteen twenty-three.

NARRATOR: Let me put numbers through Millikan too, because it's a clean exam calculation. A drop of mass six point eight times ten to the minus six grams hangs suspended between horizontal plates three point five millimetres apart with a hundred and ten volts across them. First, the field, voltage over separation: a hundred and ten volts divided by three point five times ten to the minus three metres, which is about three point one times ten to the four volts per metre. Then convert the mass to kilograms, six point eight times ten to the minus nine kilograms, watch that conversion, grams to kilograms is a classic slip. The charge is the mass times gravity, using nine point eight, divided by the field. That works out to about two point one times ten to the minus twelve coulombs. And finally, how many excess electrons is that? Divide the charge by the elementary charge and you get about one point three times ten to the seven electrons on that drop. A whole number, as it must be, because charge is quantised. The full working is in Listing five, with every unit conversion shown.

NARRATOR: Now here's the payoff that ties Thomson and Millikan together, and it's a favourite exam point. Thomson gave you the charge-to-mass ratio. Millikan gave you the charge. Divide Millikan's charge by Thomson's charge-to-mass ratio and the charge cancels out, leaving you the mass of the electron. The elementary charge, one point six times ten to the minus nineteen coulombs, divided by one point seven six times ten to the eleven coulombs per kilogram, gives a mass of about nine point one times ten to the minus thirty-one kilograms. That's the mass of the electron, and now you see why both experiments mattered. Neither alone gives you the mass. Together they do. That combination is in Listing six.

NARRATOR: Right. We've now got the full electron story. Charge, mass, and the ratio. Now, what does an atom actually look like, given that it contains these tiny negative electrons? This is where Thomson made the leap from measurement to model, around nineteen oh-four, and proposed the first real picture of the atom's internals: the plum pudding model.

NARRATOR: The picture is this. The atom is a sphere of diffuse positive charge, a kind of smeared-out positive cloud or fluid, and the tiny negative electrons are embedded throughout it, scattered through the positive sphere like plums in a pudding, or like raisins in a fruitcake if puddings aren't your thing. Hence the name. The positive charge is spread out evenly through the whole volume, and the negatives are dotted inside it.

NARRATOR: Now the syllabus asks you to evaluate the evidence supporting this model. Evaluate is a specific verb, and it demands a specific shape of answer. Not just a description. Not just a list of what's wrong. A genuine evaluate weighs the strengths against the limitation and then delivers a judgement. Let me show you the weak version first, then the strong version, so you can hear the difference, because the gap between them is two or three marks.

NARRATOR: Here's a weak answer. "The plum pudding model was wrong because the positive charge isn't spread out, it's in a nucleus, as the gold foil experiment showed." That's weak for two reasons. First, it only judges the model against later evidence Thomson didn't have, which isn't evaluating the evidence that supported it at the time. Second, it lists no strengths at all, so it isn't balanced, and evaluate demands balance. It would scrape a mark or two and stall.

NARRATOR: Now the strong answer. The plum pudding model was supported by the evidence in several genuine ways. It incorporated Thomson's newly discovered negative electrons, the particles he'd just proven existed. By balancing that negative charge with an equal amount of diffuse positive charge, it correctly made the atom electrically neutral overall, which matched the observation that ordinary atoms carry no net charge. And it was consistent with the brand-new and revolutionary idea that atoms are divisible, that they have internal parts, which Thomson's own charge-to-mass work had just established. So far, all strengths. Then the limitation, and this is the pivot that makes it an evaluate. The model's central claim, that the positive charge is spread diffusely through the whole atom, had no direct experimental evidence. The positive pudding was simply assumed; it was never tested. Nobody had probed the inside of the atom to see whether the positive charge was actually spread out or concentrated somewhere. And then the judgement: therefore the plum pudding model was a reasonable interpretation of the evidence available at the time, but its key claim was untested, and that untested positive distribution is precisely what the gold foil experiment would later overturn. Hear the difference? Strengths, then limitation, then judgement, with the linking word "but" doing the pivot. That's an evaluate.

NARRATOR: And notice the contrast structure there. When you compare, distinguish, or evaluate, markers reward explicit contrast language.
