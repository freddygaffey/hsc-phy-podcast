NARRATOR: Before we get to today's topic, let's pull the last two lessons back into your head, because today is the lesson that ties them together. This is the one comparison the HSC asks almost every single year, and it is built entirely out of what you already know from the two episodes before this one.

NARRATOR: Two episodes back, we did charged particles in a uniform electric field. The big idea there was that a uniform electric field exerts a constant force on a charge. Constant, because the field is uniform — the same strength everywhere — so the force is the same magnitude and direction everywhere too. That force is the charge q multiplied by the field strength E. For a positive charge the force points along the field; for a negative charge it points the opposite way. A constant force means a constant acceleration, and a charge fired sideways across the field does exactly what a ball does when you throw it horizontally on Earth — it keeps its sideways speed while it accelerates steadily across the field. The path is a parabola. We said it then and we'll say it again today: this is projectile motion, just with an electrical "gravity". And the energy side mattered too — when a charge moves along the field, the field does work on it, equal to the charge times the voltage it falls through, and that work shows up as kinetic energy, one half m v squared. The phrase to remember was that the charge "falls through a potential difference".

NARRATOR: Then last episode we did charged particles in a uniform magnetic field, and this is where things got interesting, because a magnetic field does something an electric field can never do. The magnetic force on a moving charge — the charge q, times its speed v, times the field strength B — is always perpendicular to the velocity. Always. And that one fact has two enormous consequences. First, because the force is always at right angles to the motion, it does no work. None. It cannot change the particle's speed or its kinetic energy. It can only change its direction. That is the single most important sentence from last episode, so let me say it the exam way: the magnetic force does no work because it acts perpendicular to the velocity at every instant, so it changes the direction of motion but never the speed. Second, a force that is always perpendicular to velocity is exactly the definition of a centripetal force — so the particle goes in a circle. The radius of that circle is the mass times the speed, divided by the charge times the field strength. Heavier or faster particles curve on bigger circles; stronger fields or bigger charges curve them tighter. And we met the cyclotron's beautiful quirk — the period of that circular motion doesn't depend on the speed at all. Faster particles trace bigger circles, but they complete them in exactly the same time.

QUESTION: So if I had to hold onto just one contrast from those two episodes, what is it?

NARRATOR: This: the electric field gives a constant force that does work and bends a sideways-launched charge into a parabola. The magnetic field gives a force that needs motion, never does work, and bends the charge into a circle. Hold those two pictures side by side — the parabola and the circle — because today we're going to put four different situations next to each other and you're going to learn to compare them the way a band-six answer does.

NARRATOR: Right. Today's topic. This is the syllabus dot-point that says: account for the similarities and differences between the paths of masses in uniform gravitational fields, satellites in orbit, charged particles in electric fields, and moving charged particles in magnetic fields. Read that again in your head. It lists four scenarios, and the word "account for" means: give the reasons, explain the cause and effect, don't just describe. This is one of the most-examined extended-response topics in the whole module — routinely worth five, six, even seven marks. And here is the thing most students get wrong before they write a single word: they think they're being asked for four descriptions. They're not. They're being asked for a comparison. There is a world of difference, and that difference is exactly what separates a band-four answer from a band-six one.

QUESTION: By the end of this, what should I actually be able to do?

NARRATOR: Three things. One: you should be able to recite, for each of the four scenarios, what causes the force, whether the force depends on velocity, whether the field does work, and what shape the path is. Two: you should be able to structure an answer point by point — by comparison axis — using the word "whereas" and the phrase "in contrast", instead of writing four separate paragraphs. And three: you should be able to explain the deep link between a satellite's orbit and a charged particle's magnetic circle, because that link is where the top marks live. Let's build all of that.

NARRATOR: Start with the four scenarios. I want you to think of each one as a little chain of cause and effect: a field exerts a force, the force does or doesn't do work, and that determines the shape of the path. Same skeleton, four times, and the differences between them are the whole exam answer.

NARRATOR: Scenario one: a mass in a uniform gravitational field. Think of a projectile near the Earth's surface — a thrown ball, a launched shell. The field is uniform, meaning over that small region the gravitational field strength g is essentially constant, the same magnitude and direction everywhere. The force on the mass is its mass m times g, and three features matter. It is proportional to the mass. It is always attractive — it always points "down", along the field lines, toward the Earth. And it is completely independent of velocity — gravity pulls just as hard on a stationary rock as on a moving one. Now here's the elegant bit: the acceleration is the force divided by the mass, which is just g, and the mass cancels out. Every mass accelerates the same. A mass dropped from rest falls in a straight line down the field. A mass launched sideways keeps a constant horizontal velocity while it accelerates vertically — and that gives a parabola. And because the force points along the field, when the mass moves along the field, gravity does work on it and changes its kinetic energy. That's the projectile case, and you've known it since Module five.

NARRATOR: Scenario two: a satellite in orbit. And here is the first trap, the one the syllabus deliberately sets by listing this separately from scenario one. A satellite's field is not uniform. It is a radial field — the gravitational force points toward the centre of the Earth, and it gets weaker with distance, falling off as one over the distance squared. The force is the gravitational constant G, times the Earth's mass M, times the satellite's mass m, divided by the distance r squared. Still attractive, still toward the central body, but no longer constant — it changes direction depending on where the satellite is, always pointing inward, and it changes strength with altitude. Now, the mechanism: gravity here provides the centripetal force. The satellite is in continuous free fall — it's falling toward the Earth constantly, but it's moving sideways so fast that the Earth's surface curves away beneath it just as fast as it falls. So it never gets closer. The path is a closed circle, or in general an ellipse. And if you equate the gravitational force to the centripetal force needed — the central body's gravity equals m v squared over r — the masses and one factor of r cancel, and you get the orbital speed: v equals the square root of G M over r. Bigger orbit, slower speed, longer period. You met that in Module five orbital mechanics.

NARRATOR: Now hold on to one crucial fact about the circular orbit, because it's going to become the bridge of this whole episode. In a circular orbit, gravity always points toward the centre, and the velocity is always tangent to the circle — perpendicular to the force. And what did we just say about a force perpendicular to velocity? It does no work. So in a circular orbit, gravity does no work, and the satellite's speed and kinetic energy stay constant. File that away. In an elliptical orbit it's different — the force has a component along the motion, so it does do work and the speed varies, speeding up near the planet and slowing far away. That's Kepler's second law. But for the clean circular case: no work, constant speed.

NARRATOR: Scenario three: a charged particle in a uniform electric field — the parallel-plate situation from two episodes ago. The field strength E is the voltage across the plates divided by their separation. The force is the charge q times E, and look how this rhymes with gravity. It's proportional to the charge, the way gravity's force is proportional to the mass. It's constant in a uniform field, the way gravity is. And it's independent of velocity — the field pushes a stationary charge just as hard as a moving one. There's one extra wrinkle the electric case has that gravity doesn't: the force can point with the field or against it, depending on the sign of the charge. Positive charge, force along the field; negative charge, force opposite the field. So an electric field can attract or repel, whereas gravity only ever attracts. But the trajectory mathematics is identical to gravity. A charge fired across the plates keeps a constant velocity across the field and accelerates uniformly along it — a parabola, governed by exactly the same projectile-motion equations. A charge released from rest accelerates in a straight line along the field; that's your electron gun. And the field does work, equal to the charge times the voltage, when the charge moves along the field — which is precisely why electric fields are used to speed particles up.

NARRATOR: Scenario four: a moving charged particle in a uniform magnetic field. And this is the odd one out, the scenario that breaks the pattern, which is exactly why the examiner includes it. The magnetic force is the charge q, times the speed v, times the field strength B, times the sine of the angle between the velocity and the field. Three things make it completely different from the other three. It acts only on a moving charge — if the speed is zero, the force is zero, full stop. It depends on the speed — faster charge, bigger force. And it is always perpendicular to both the velocity and the field. Because the force is always perpendicular to the velocity, it can never do work — speed and kinetic energy are constant — and it acts as a centripetal force, bending the particle into a circle. The radius is the mass times the speed over the charge times the field. Not a parabola. A circle. And no work, ever.

NARRATOR: Now let me show you the move that actually earns the marks, because knowing the four scenarios isn't enough — it's how you organise them. They write a paragraph on gravity, then a paragraph on the electric field, then a paragraph on the magnetic field, then maybe orbits. Four neat little descriptions. Because the verb was "compare", or "account for the similarities and differences", and a stack of descriptions contains no comparison. NARRATOR: So here's the rule, and write it on your hand: structure by comparison point, not by field. QUESTION: Can you actually show me a weak answer and a strong answer side by side, so I can hear the difference?

NARRATOR: Absolutely — this is the most useful thing I can do for you. Listen to the weak version first. A weak answer says something like: "In a gravitational field, an object experiences a force and follows a curved path. In an electric field, a charge experiences a force and also follows a curved path. In a magnetic field, a charge moves in a circle. A satellite orbits the Earth in a circle because of gravity." Now — every sentence there is true. Not one of them is wrong. But it's four descriptions glued together. It never compares anything. It doesn't say why two of the paths are parabolas and two are circles. It doesn't mention work, or velocity dependence, or the direction of the force relative to the velocity. There's no "whereas" anywhere. That answer earns maybe two of six marks, and the student walks away baffled, because everything they wrote was correct.

NARRATOR: Now the strong answer. Listen for the structure — it goes by comparison point, and it leans on the linking words. It opens on the big similarity: "A uniform gravitational field exerts a force proportional to mass, and a uniform electric field exerts a force proportional to charge. In both cases the force is constant in a uniform field, acts in a fixed direction along the field, and is independent of the particle's velocity. Consequently, a particle projected across either field follows an identical parabolic path — constant velocity across the field combined with uniform acceleration along it — and in both cases the field does work on the particle as it moves along the field, changing its kinetic energy." That's paragraph one: gravity and electric, compared as a pair, on force, on work, and on path. Then the pivot: "In contrast, the magnetic force acts only on a charge that is already moving, depends on its speed, and is always directed perpendicular to the velocity. Because it is always perpendicular to the motion, it does no work and cannot change the particle's speed — it changes only the direction, producing uniform circular motion rather than a parabola." That's paragraph two: the magnetic field, set against the other two by contrast. Then the satellite, as the bridge: "A satellite in a circular orbit resembles the magnetic case more than the gravitational parabola, even though it too arises from gravity: the gravitational force here acts as a centripetal force, perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work, the speed is constant, and the path is a closed circle. The difference from a projectile is that the projectile moves across a uniform field, whereas the satellite moves through a radial field that continuously redirects it." Hear the difference? Same physics, but organised so that every sentence is a comparison. That's a six-out-of-six answer.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the three highest-value comparison sentences explicitly, because if you memorise the shape of these three, you can reconstruct the whole answer in the exam. Sentence one, the strong similarity: both gravitational and electric fields, when uniform, exert a constant force — proportional to mass and to charge respectively — directed along the field and independent of velocity, so a particle projected across either follows an identical parabola. Sentence two, the key difference: the magnetic force, by contrast, acts only on a moving charge, depends on its speed, and is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it does no work and produces a circle instead of a parabola. Sentence three, the unifying link: a circular orbit and a magnetic circle are deeply analogous — in both, the field supplies a centripetal force perpendicular to the velocity, so the speed is constant and the path is a closed circle; the difference is only the origin of the force, gravitational attraction versus the magnetic interaction, and that gravity also acts on stationary masses while the magnetic force requires motion.

QUESTION: You keep saying the orbit is like the magnetic circle. Isn't that strange? They're completely different forces.

NARRATOR: It is strange, and it's exactly the insight that impresses a marker, so let me make it precise. Forget where the force comes from for a second and just ask: is the force perpendicular to the velocity? In a circular orbit, gravity points to the centre and the satellite moves tangentially — perpendicular. In a magnetic field, the force is perpendicular to the velocity by its very nature. In both cases, "force perpendicular to velocity" gives you the same three consequences: no work, constant speed, circular path. The mechanism is different — gravity depends on mass and falls off with distance, the magnetic force depends on charge and speed — but the geometry is identical, and the geometry is what sets the path. Meanwhile, the projectile and the sideways-launched charge are the opposite case: there the force stays fixed in a constant direction while the velocity swings around, so the force is sometimes along the motion and sometimes across it, the field does work, and you get a parabola. So the whole comparison reduces to one question — does the force stay fixed in direction, or does it stay perpendicular to the velocity? Fixed direction gives a parabola and work done. Perpendicular gives a circle and no work. That single distinction is the engine of the entire answer, and if you can say it that cleanly, you've shown the marker you understand the physics, not just the four pictures.

NARRATOR: And notice what unifies all four despite the differences: in every case, the force in question is the net force on the particle, so Newton's second law — force equals mass times acceleration — applies to all four without exception. The differences live entirely in two places: the force law itself, and the direction of that force relative to the velocity. Everything else follows from those two. That's a lovely sentence to put in an extended response, because it shows you see the common structure underneath.

NARRATOR: Now let's make the electric-versus-magnetic contrast vivid with numbers, because the cleanest way to feel the difference between "does work" and "does no work" is to run the same electron through both fields and watch what happens. NARRATOR: Stage one. Take an electron, starting from rest, and accelerate it through a potential difference of one hundred volts — this is an electron gun, the electric-field case. The work the field does on the electron is the charge times the voltage: one point six times ten to the minus nineteen, times one hundred, which is one point six times ten to the minus seventeen joules. NARRATOR: One more number worth carrying, for the orbit case, so the satellite doesn't feel abstract. NARRATOR: Now let's collect the traps, because this question has a graveyard of predictable mistakes and the examiner knows every one of them. Trap one, the big one, we've already hammered: answering field by field instead of by comparison point. Structure by axis, use "whereas", or you cap low. Trap two: saying the magnetic force speeds the particle up. It does not. The magnetic force does no work, always, because it's perpendicular to the velocity; it changes direction, never speed. NARRATOR: Trap four: claiming all four paths are "the same maths". They are not. Only the two uniform fields — gravity and electric — give identical parabolic projectile maths.
