NARRATOR: Start with the most recent: relativistic momentum and mass-energy equivalence. The big idea was that momentum is no longer just mass times velocity. The relativistic momentum is the Lorentz factor times the rest mass times the velocity, and the Lorentz factor blows up towards infinity as the speed approaches the speed of light. So the faster you push something, the more its inertia grows, and the more force you need for the same gain in speed. Reaching the speed of light would need infinite energy, which is why no object with mass can ever get there. Only massless photons travel at the speed of light. NARRATOR: One episode before that: time dilation and length contraction. The light-clock argument. Because light must travel at the same speed in every frame, a moving clock's light has to cover a longer diagonal path, so a moving clock ticks slow. Length goes the other way: a moving object is contracted along its direction of motion, the contracted length being the proper length divided by the Lorentz factor. Proper length is measured in the object's own rest frame. Time stretches, length shrinks, and the Lorentz factor is greater than or equal to one, always. The evidence we cashed in was the muons and the Hafele-Keating atomic clocks.

NARRATOR: Pillar two, interference, Young's double slit. Light from two coherent sources travels two different paths to the screen, and the path difference decides everything. NARRATOR: Pillar three, polarisation, and this is the clincher. A polarising filter passes only one plane of oscillation of the electric field. Maximum kinetic energy: the wave model predicts energy rising with intensity, whereas in the photon model the maximum kinetic energy equals the photon energy minus the work function, so it rises with frequency only. Therefore the wave model accounts for one observation whereas the photon model accounts for all four, which is why the model was modified.
