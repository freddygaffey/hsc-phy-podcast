NARRATOR: Friction is a contact force that opposes relative motion, or the tendency to move, between two surfaces. It always acts along the surface, parallel to it, and always in the direction that opposes the motion or the attempted motion. There are two kinds the syllabus wants. Static friction acts when the object isn't yet moving; it adjusts itself to match whatever force is trying to move the object, up to a maximum, which is why a heavy box doesn't budge until you push hard enough. Kinetic friction, or sliding friction, acts once the object is moving, and it's roughly constant. The key exam points: friction opposes motion, it acts along the surface, and it depends on how hard the surfaces are pressed together, the normal force, and on the nature of the surfaces, but not on the contact area or the speed. So friction isn't a fixed number, it's a responsive force that resists motion, and you include it as one of the forces in your net-force calculation. The facts are in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Let me ground friction with a flat-surface example before we tilt anything, because it shows the static-to-kinetic transition the exam likes. The full working is in Listing three. Imagine pushing a twenty-kilogram crate on level ground. You push gently with ten newtons and it doesn't move: static friction rises to match your ten newtons, the net force is zero, and by the first law the crate stays put. You push harder, twenty newtons, still nothing: static friction has matched twenty. But static friction has a maximum; push beyond it, say the maximum is fifty newtons, and at fifty-one newtons the crate breaks free and starts sliding. Now kinetic friction takes over, which is usually a bit less than the maximum static friction, say forty-five newtons, and acts backward while the crate slides. If you keep pushing with sixty newtons, the net force is sixty minus forty-five, fifteen newtons forward, so by the second law the crate accelerates at fifteen over twenty, zero point seven five metres per second per second. That's the whole story of friction on the flat: it matches your push up to a maximum, then once sliding it's a roughly constant kinetic value, and the net force is your push minus that kinetic friction.

NARRATOR: One more idea the syllabus expects qualitatively: friction depends on how hard the surfaces press together, the normal force, and on the roughness of the surfaces, captured by a number called the coefficient of friction, but it does not depend on the contact area or, to a good approximation, the speed. So a heavier object, pressing down with a larger normal force, experiences more friction, which is exactly why the friction on an incline depends on m g cosine theta, the normal force, as we'll see. You won't always be given a coefficient at this level, friction is often just stated as a force in newtons, but know what it depends on: the normal force and the surfaces, not the area.

NARRATOR: Now the inclined plane, and the single decision that makes it easy or hard: how you choose your axes. The temptation is to use horizontal and vertical, but that's the hard way on a slope. The smart choice is to tilt your axes to line up with the slope: one axis along the slope, in the direction the object would slide, and one axis perpendicular to the slope, into it. Why? Because then the acceleration is purely along the slope, it has no perpendicular component, the object stays on the surface, so the whole problem collapses to one direction. The catch is that the weight, which points straight down, no longer lines up with either axis, so we have to resolve the weight into components along these tilted axes. That resolution is the heart of every incline problem.

NARRATOR: Here's the resolution, and it's the thing to memorise. For a slope at angle theta to the horizontal, the weight, m g straight down, splits into two components: a component along the slope, pulling the object down the slope, equal to m g times the sine of theta; and a component perpendicular to the slope, pressing the object into the surface, equal to m g times the cosine of theta. Down the slope, sine; into the slope, cosine. Here's the memory hook, because students swap them under pressure: as the slope gets steeper, theta approaches ninety degrees, the object slides more easily, the down-slope pull should grow, and sine grows toward one as the angle approaches ninety, so the down-slope component is the sine one. And at ninety degrees, a vertical cliff, the whole weight is down-slope and nothing presses into the surface, sine of ninety is one, cosine of ninety is zero, which matches. So: down-slope is m g sine theta, into-slope is m g cosine theta. Lock that with the check, steeper means more sliding means sine for the slope. The full resolution is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now assemble the forces on a block on an incline. Perpendicular to the slope: the into-slope weight component, m g cosine theta, presses in, and the normal force, N, pushes back out; since the block doesn't accelerate into or out of the surface, these balance, so the normal force equals m g cosine theta. Notice the normal force on a slope is not the full weight, it's only the cosine component, a common error. Along the slope: the down-slope weight component, m g sine theta, pulls the block down the slope, and friction, if present, acts up the slope opposing the slide. So the net force along the slope is m g sine theta minus the friction force. Apply the second law along the slope: that net force equals m a, giving the acceleration down the slope. The perpendicular direction just fixes the normal force; the along-slope direction gives the motion.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. On an inclined plane, why is the normal force equal to m g cosine theta rather than the full weight m g? Pause the player, explain it, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The normal force is the surface pushing back perpendicular to itself, and it must balance only the component of the weight that presses into the surface, not the whole weight. The weight points straight down, but on a slope only part of it, the perpendicular component m g cosine theta, presses into the surface; the other part, m g sine theta, points along the slope and isn't supported by the surface at all, it's what makes the block slide. Since the block doesn't accelerate perpendicular to the surface, the normal force balances just the perpendicular component, so it equals m g cosine theta, which is less than m g for any real slope. On flat ground, theta is zero, cosine of zero is one, and the normal force equals the full weight m g, as you'd expect. So the normal force shrinks as the slope steepens, because less of the weight presses into the surface. If you saw that the surface only supports the perpendicular component, you've understood the incline.

NARRATOR: Let me also do the equilibrium case on a slope, because it's examined and it ties friction together with the first law. Suppose a block sits on a rough incline and does not slide. Why not? Because static friction, acting up the slope, is balancing the down-slope weight component, m g sine theta. The block is in equilibrium, net force zero, so the friction force must equal m g sine theta exactly, no more, no less. As you imagine tilting the slope steeper, m g sine theta grows, and static friction grows to match it, until the slope reaches the angle where the down-slope component equals the maximum static friction. At that angle, the block is on the verge of sliding, and any steeper and it breaks free and accelerates. This is actually how you can measure friction: find the angle at which an object just begins to slide. So on a slope, if the object is stationary, friction equals the down-slope weight component; if it's sliding, friction is the kinetic value and the object accelerates by the difference. Equilibrium first, then check whether the down-slope pull has exceeded what friction can hold.

NARRATOR: And a real-world tie, because the inclined plane is one of the oldest machines. A ramp lets you raise a heavy load with less force than lifting it straight up, because you only need to overcome the down-slope component m g sine theta, which is less than the full weight m g, at the cost of pushing over a longer distance. That's why loading ramps, wheelchair ramps, and winding mountain roads exist: a gentle slope, small theta, means a small sine, so a small force needed, though you travel further. It's a preview of the energy idea next episode, the work you do, force times distance, is the same either way, you trade a smaller force for a longer distance. The incline isn't just an exam contrivance; it's a machine for trading force against distance, and the physics is exactly the resolution we just did.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "a block is released from rest on a rough inclined plane; describe and analyse its motion". The band-six version: resolve the weight into a component along the slope, m g sine theta, and a component perpendicular to the slope, m g cosine theta. The normal force balances the perpendicular component, so N equals m g cosine theta. Along the slope, the net force is the down-slope weight component minus the friction force, m g sine theta minus friction; by Newton's second law this equals m a, so the block accelerates down the slope at a equals g sine theta minus the friction per unit mass. If the down-slope component exceeds the maximum static friction, the block accelerates; otherwise it remains in equilibrium. Notice the structure: resolve into the two axes, balance the perpendicular one for the normal force, apply the second law along the slope. That's the full-mark method, and it's the template for every incline question.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the gap. A weak answer says "the block slides down because of gravity, and friction slows it". That structure is the difference between bands.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: using m g instead of m g sine theta for the force along the slope, you must resolve. NARRATOR: A practical note, because the syllabus asks you to investigate motion on inclined planes. A standard experiment: let a cart or trolley roll down a track set at a measured angle, and record its motion with a motion sensor, light gates, or a ticker-timer, the same tools from Module One. From the data you find the acceleration, the gradient of the velocity-time graph, and compare it with the prediction g sine theta. If the measured acceleration is a bit less than g sine theta, the difference is friction, and you can work the friction force back out from the second law. You can also vary the angle and plot the acceleration against the sine of the angle: theory predicts a straight line through the origin with gradient g, for a frictionless ideal, so the experiment both tests the model and reveals the friction as the shortfall. Being able to describe that, vary the angle, measure the acceleration, compare with g sine theta, is examinable, and it ties today's force analysis straight back to the kinematics graphs you mastered in Module One.
