NARRATOR: Before we crack open the nucleus today, let's spend a few minutes back over the last handful of lessons, because everything we're about to do leans on them.

NARRATOR: Two episodes back, in the Standard Model, we built the modern catalogue of fundamental particles. The matter particles split into two families. The leptons — the electron, the muon, the tau, and their three neutrinos — are genuinely fundamental, with no internal structure we can find. The quarks — up, down, charm, strange, top, bottom — carry the odd fractional charges, either plus two-thirds or minus one-third of the elementary charge. You combine three quarks to make a baryon: a proton is up-up-down, which adds to a charge of plus one, and a neutron is up-down-down, which adds to exactly zero. Hold onto that, because today a neutron is going to turn into a proton in front of your eyes, and it'll happen at the level of a single quark. We also met the four fundamental forces and their carrier particles — the force-carrying bosons. The strong force is carried by gluons and binds quarks together; that's the one that holds the nucleus, and it's the headline act today.

NARRATOR: Before that, in matter waves, we had de Broglie's idea that every particle has a wavelength — lambda equals Planck's constant divided by momentum. Electrons, fired at a crystal, diffract — that's the Davisson-Germer and G.P. Thomson result, the wave nature of matter made real. And those standing-wave orbits, where only a whole number of electron wavelengths fits around the atom, gave us a physical reason for Bohr's quantised energy levels.

NARRATOR: And the Bohr model itself, before that: electrons sit in stationary states and don't radiate, and a photon is emitted or absorbed only when an electron jumps between levels — the photon energy equals the difference between the two energy levels. Unique energy levels give unique line spectra, the fingerprint of each element. Keep that idea of "excited then dropping back down and shedding a photon" warm in your mind — gamma decay is going to be exactly that idea, just played out in the nucleus instead of the electron cloud.

NARRATOR: So here's where those threads tie together. We've built the atom up from cathode rays to quarks. We know the nucleus is tiny, dense, and positive — that's Rutherford's gold foil. We know it's made of protons and neutrons, which are themselves made of quarks. Now we ask the questions that the whole nuclear half of this module hangs on: what actually holds that nucleus together, why do some nuclei fall apart, and when they fall apart, what exactly comes flying out? That's radioactivity, and it's today.

NARRATOR: By the end of this episode you'll be able to do five exam things cleanly. One: relate an isotope's mass number to what's inside it. Two: explain nuclear stability as a tug-of-war between the strong force and electrostatic repulsion — that's a guaranteed extended-response. Three: compare alpha, beta, and gamma radiation across every property a marker asks for. Four: write and balance decay equations using conservation of mass number, charge, and mass-energy. And five: describe the role of the weak force in beta decay, including why a ghostly particle called the neutrino had to exist. Let's go.

NARRATOR: Start with the bookkeeping, because if you get the notation wrong, every decay equation after it falls over. Any nuclide — any specific kind of nucleus — is written with the element symbol and two numbers. The top number is the mass number, written A. The bottom number is the atomic number, written Z. The atomic number Z is simply the number of protons. And here's the first mark: the atomic number sets the element's identity. Carbon is carbon because it has six protons. Change the number of protons and you have literally changed which element it is — that's what the syllabus means by transmutation. The mass number A is the total number of nucleons — protons plus neutrons together. So the number of neutrons is just A minus Z.

NARRATOR: That gives us the dot-point "relate the isotopic mass number to its subatomic components" almost for free. The mass number A counts the nucleons: it equals the number of protons plus the number of neutrons. Isotopes are nuclides of the same element — same number of protons, same Z — but different mass numbers, because they have different numbers of neutrons. Carbon-12 and carbon-14 are both carbon, both six protons, but carbon-14 has two extra neutrons, so its mass number is fourteen instead of twelve. Same chemistry, different nucleus, and as we'll see, very different stability.

QUESTION: Quick one — if a nucleus has a mass number of two hundred and thirty-eight and an atomic number of ninety-two, how many neutrons does it have?

NARRATOR: Two hundred and thirty-eight minus ninety-two is one hundred and forty-six neutrons. That's uranium-238, and it's going to be our worked example shortly. Notice already: a hundred and forty-six neutrons against only ninety-two protons. Way more neutrons than protons. That imbalance is not an accident, and explaining it is the heart of nuclear stability.

NARRATOR: So let's do nuclear stability properly, because this is the conceptual core of the episode and a classic extended-response. Picture the nucleus: a clump of protons and neutrons jammed into a space about a femtometre across — that's ten to the minus fifteen metres. The protons are all positive, and they're crammed unbelievably close together. Now, what do like charges do? They repel. By the electrostatic force — the Coulomb force — every proton pushes every other proton apart, and that push gets stronger the closer they are. Acting on its own, the Coulomb repulsion inside a nucleus would blow it to pieces instantly.

NARRATOR: So something has to be holding it together, and it has to be strong enough to beat that repulsion. Could it be gravity? Gravity between the nucleons is attractive, yes — but it's absurdly, hopelessly weak at this scale, trillions of trillions of times too feeble. So there must be another force. And here's a trap worth flagging now: it is not the weak force. The weak force has a job, and we'll meet it later in beta decay, but holding the nucleus together is not it. The force that does the holding is the strong nuclear force.

NARRATOR: The strong nuclear force has three properties you need to be able to state, because the marks are in the detail. First, it attracts all nucleons — protons and neutrons alike, regardless of charge. It doesn't care about electric charge at all; a neutron is pulled in just as hard as a proton. Second, it is incredibly short-ranged — it only reaches across about one femtometre, ten to the minus fifteen metres, roughly the size of a nucleon itself. So it's effectively a "nearest-neighbour" force: a nucleon feels the strong pull only from the handful of nucleons immediately touching it, and beyond that it just switches off. Third — and this is the subtle one — at extremely short distances the strong force actually becomes repulsive. You can't squash two nucleons into the same spot; it pushes back. So it pulls nucleons together until they're touching, then refuses to let them get any closer.

NARRATOR: A memory hook for those three. Picture the strong force as Velcro-covered magnets. The Velcro grips hard, but only when surfaces are touching — that's the short range. It grabs anything fuzzy regardless of which way it's facing — that's "all nucleons, charge doesn't matter." And try to crush two together and the magnet cores repel — that's the short-range repulsion. Velcro-covered magnets: short, indiscriminate, and won't be crushed.

NARRATOR: Now the tug-of-war, which is the whole answer to "what determines stability." You have two opposing effects. The Coulomb repulsion is long-ranged — every proton pushes every other proton, no matter how far across the nucleus, and all those pushes add up. The strong-force attraction is short-ranged — each nucleon only pulls on its immediate neighbours. A nucleus is stable when, locally, the short-range strong attraction among close-packed neighbours outweighs the total long-range Coulomb repulsion. When the strong force wins, the nucleus holds together. When it can't, the nucleus is unstable — and an unstable nucleus decays, throwing out particles or energy to shift itself toward a more stable arrangement.

QUESTION: Here's the one to really stop for. Pause the player. Using the strong force and the Coulomb force, explain in your own words why a large nucleus needs more neutrons than protons to stay stable. Work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Right. Here's the chain of reasoning a marker wants. As you add protons, the Coulomb repulsion grows fast — because it acts between every single pair of protons, and it's long-range, so a new proton repels all the others across the whole nucleus. The strong-force attraction does not grow as fast, because it's only nearest-neighbour — a new nucleon only bonds to the few touching it. So repulsion is winning the arms race as the nucleus gets bigger. The fix is neutrons. A neutron adds strong-force attraction — it's a nucleon, it grips its neighbours — but it adds zero Coulomb repulsion, because it has no charge. So neutrons are pure glue with no downside. For small nuclei, roughly equal numbers of protons and neutrons is enough. But as the proton count climbs, you need progressively more neutrons than protons to supply enough extra strong-force glue to counter all that accumulating repulsion. That's why the band of stable nuclei — the valley of stability — curves upward, demanding more and more neutron excess. And beyond about eighty-three protons — that's bismuth — no amount of extra neutrons can do it. Every nucleus bigger than that is unstable and will eventually decay. That's the answer, and it's worth full marks if you name both forces, their ranges, and the no-charge point about neutrons.

NARRATOR: Let me tie that straight back to two earlier ideas. Remember the gold foil experiment — Rutherford fired alpha particles at gold, and about one in eight thousand bounced almost straight back. They bounced because they were being repelled by a tiny, concentrated, positive nucleus. That repulsion they were running into is the exact same Coulomb repulsion we're now saying tries to tear the nucleus apart from the inside. And the strong force is the residual, leftover reach of the same strong interaction we met two episodes ago binding quarks inside each nucleon — the gluon force. So the force holding the whole nucleus together is the spillover of the force holding each proton together. One interaction, two scales.

NARRATOR: Okay. Some nuclei are unstable. When they decay, three kinds of radiation can come out — alpha, beta, and gamma. This is the classic "compare" table, and the HSC loves it. We'll go through composition, charge, mass, ionising ability, and penetration for each, and I'll give you a hook to lock the ordering.

NARRATOR: Alpha radiation, the alpha particle. Composition: it is a helium-4 nucleus — two protons and two neutrons, bound together and ejected as a unit. Charge: plus two, because two protons and no electrons. Mass: about four atomic mass units — it's the heavyweight of the three, relatively massive. Because it's big, slow, and highly charged, it interacts ferociously with any matter it passes through, ripping electrons off atoms — so it is very highly ionising. And the flip side of being so good at ionising is that it loses its energy almost immediately: alpha has very low penetration. A sheet of paper stops it. A few centimetres of air stops it. The dead outer layer of your skin stops it. Alpha travels at only about five to seven percent of the speed of light, roughly two times ten to the seven metres per second.

NARRATOR: Beta-minus radiation, the beta particle. Composition: it is a fast-moving electron, created in and ejected from the nucleus. Now flag this immediately, because it's a guaranteed trap: the nucleus does not contain electrons sitting around waiting to leave. The electron is created at the instant of decay, when a neutron turns into a proton. We'll do the mechanism shortly. Charge: minus one. Mass: tiny — about one eighteen-hundredth of an atomic mass unit, basically negligible next to a nucleon. Because it's light and fast and less charged than alpha, it ionises moderately and penetrates moderately — stopped by roughly a centimetre of aluminium, or about a metre of air. Beta particles come out across a range of speeds, some up to about ninety-nine percent of the speed of light — and that range of speeds is going to matter enormously when we get to the neutrino.

NARRATOR: Gamma radiation. Composition: it is not a particle of matter at all — it's a high-energy photon, electromagnetic radiation, a packet of light from the very top of the spectrum. Remember the EM spectrum order, increasing frequency — Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible, Ultraviolet, X-ray, Gamma — "Raging Martians Invaded Venus Using X-ray Guns." Gamma is the last word, the highest frequency, the most energetic. Charge: zero. Mass: zero. It travels at the speed of light, because it is light. Being neutral, it barely interacts with matter as it passes — so it is very weakly ionising. And the flip side again: weakly ionising means it sails straight through. Gamma has extremely high penetration — you need many centimetres of lead, or metres of concrete, to seriously cut it down.

NARRATOR: Now the single most important relationship in this whole comparison, and you must state it explicitly in a compare answer: ionising power and penetrating power are inverses of each other. The charged particles — alpha and beta — interact strongly with matter, so they ionise heavily and are stopped quickly: high ionising, low penetration. The neutral radiation — gamma — interacts weakly, so it ionises little but travels far: low ionising, high penetration. In order: for ionising power, alpha beats beta beats gamma. For penetration, it's the reverse — gamma beats beta beats alpha. That inversion is the mark.

NARRATOR: Here's your hook for the ordering. Think of it as the bouncer and the ghost. The alpha particle is the big, slow, heavily-charged bouncer — it grabs everyone at the door, so it never gets past the first wall: maximum ionising, minimum penetration. The gamma photon is the ghost — uncharged, untouchable, ignores everyone and walks straight through the building: minimum ionising, maximum penetration. Beta is the ordinary punter in between. Bouncer, punter, ghost — alpha, beta, gamma — and ionising runs down that list while penetration runs up it.

NARRATOR: One more property markers sometimes ask for: deflection in a magnetic field. Alpha is positive, so it curves one way. Beta is negative, so it curves the opposite way, and curves more sharply because it's so much lighter. Gamma is neutral, so it isn't deflected at all — it goes straight through the field undisturbed. That magnetic test was historically one of the ways the three were told apart.

QUESTION: So in an exam, if they say "compare alpha and gamma radiation," what do they actually want?

NARRATOR: They want point-by-point contrast with explicit contrast language — "whereas," "in contrast." Not two separate paragraphs. So: "Alpha radiation is a helium-4 nucleus with a charge of plus two and a relatively large mass, whereas gamma radiation is a chargeless, massless high-energy photon. Alpha is very highly ionising but has very low penetration, stopped by paper, in contrast to gamma, which is weakly ionising but extremely penetrating, requiring centimetres of lead. This reflects the inverse relationship between ionising power and penetration: the highly-charged alpha interacts strongly and is stopped quickly, whereas the neutral gamma interacts weakly and passes through." That structure — one property, both substances, a "whereas" between them — is what earns every mark.

NARRATOR: Now decay equations, and the conservation laws that govern them. Every nuclear equation must conserve three things at once. First, mass number — the top numbers on the left must equal the top numbers on the right. Second, atomic number, which is charge — the bottom numbers must balance, where we assign the emitted electron a bottom number of minus one and a positron plus one. Third, mass-energy — the total mass-energy is conserved, and any mass that goes missing reappears as kinetic energy of the products. That third one we'll cash out properly two episodes from now in mass defect and binding energy, where the missing mass times the speed of light squared gives the energy released. For now, just know it's conserved.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the four-row bookkeeping device — what each decay does to the mass number and the atomic number. This is a memorisable list, so here's the hook: "Alpha eats four and two, beta swaps a charge, gamma changes nothing." Let me unpack it.

NARRATOR: Alpha decay: the nucleus throws out a helium-4 nucleus, so the mass number drops by four and the atomic number drops by two. The parent becomes a different element, two places down the periodic table. "Alpha eats four and two."

NARRATOR: Beta-minus decay: a neutron inside the nucleus turns into a proton, emitting an electron and an antineutrino. The mass number doesn't change — a neutron and a proton both count as one nucleon, so the total is the same. But the atomic number goes up by one, because you've gained a proton. The element moves one place up the table. Here's the trap that costs people marks every single year: in beta-minus, Z increases. Students constantly write it as decreasing. Neutron to proton — you've made a proton — so the proton count goes up. "Beta swaps a charge," and beta-minus swaps it upward.

NARRATOR: Beta-plus decay: the mirror image. A proton turns into a neutron, emitting a positron and a neutrino. Mass number unchanged again, but now the atomic number goes down by one, because you've lost a proton. The element moves one place down. So beta-minus raises Z, beta-plus lowers Z, and both leave the mass number alone.

NARRATOR: Gamma decay: nothing changes — neither the mass number nor the atomic number. And this is another classic trap: gamma decay is not a transmutation. The element does not change. What's happening is that after an alpha or beta decay, the daughter nucleus is often left in an excited state — it has too much internal energy. It de-excites by shedding that excess energy as a single high-energy photon, the gamma ray. It's the exact nuclear analogue of the Bohr model, where an excited electron drops to a lower level and emits a photon — except here it's the whole nucleus dropping to its ground state. Same number of protons, same number of neutrons, just less energy. So in a gamma equation, you do not change the element. "Gamma changes nothing" — except the energy.

NARRATOR: Let's work a full numerical example, because this is examinable as a calculation and the method earns the marks. Take the alpha decay of uranium-238. Uranium-238, mass number two hundred and thirty-eight, atomic number ninety-two, decays by emitting an alpha particle. The full working is in Listing 1, but let me narrate every step.

NARRATOR: Step one, write and balance the equation. Uranium-238 goes to a daughter nucleus plus a helium-4 alpha particle. Conservation of mass number: the top numbers must give two hundred and thirty-eight, so the daughter's mass number is two hundred and thirty-eight minus four, which is two hundred and thirty-four. Conservation of charge: the bottom numbers must give ninety-two, so the daughter's atomic number is ninety-two minus two, which is ninety. Atomic number ninety is thorium. So uranium-238 alpha-decays to thorium-234 plus a helium-4 nucleus. Check it: two hundred and thirty-four plus four is two hundred and thirty-eight, good; ninety plus two is ninety-two, good. Always show that check — it's worth a mark on its own.

NARRATOR: Step two, find the mass defect — the mass that goes missing. The mass of the uranium-238 nucleus is two hundred and thirty-eight point zero five zero seven nine atomic mass units. The products: thorium-234 is two hundred and thirty-four point zero four three six three, and helium-4 is four point zero zero two six zero. Add those products: two hundred and thirty-eight point zero four six two three atomic mass units. The mass defect is the parent minus the products: two hundred and thirty-eight point zero five zero seven nine minus two hundred and thirty-eight point zero four six two three, which is zero point zero zero four five six atomic mass units. Mass has genuinely disappeared.

NARRATOR: Step three, convert that lost mass into energy. The shortcut every HSC student should memorise: one atomic mass unit of mass is equivalent to nine hundred and thirty-one point five mega-electron-volts of energy. That comes straight from Einstein's mass-energy equivalence — energy equals mass times the speed of light squared — and we'll derive it properly in the binding-energy episode. So the energy released is zero point zero zero four five six times nine hundred and thirty-one point five, which is about four point two five mega-electron-volts. That energy comes off as kinetic energy — mostly carried by the fast alpha particle, with a little recoil of the thorium nucleus. The full numbers are in Listing 1.

NARRATOR: A marker-pleasing remark to add: those quoted masses are atomic masses, so they include the electrons. In alpha decay the electron counts on both sides cancel out automatically, so you can use atomic masses directly with no correction. Mention that and you've shown you understand what the numbers are.

NARRATOR: Now — and this is a trap worth its own warning — that cancellation does not fully happen in beta decay. In beta-minus, the atomic number changes, so the number of atomic electrons doesn't match up, and you have to track the electron masses carefully. Listing 2 has a worked beta-minus example, the decay of lead-210 to bismuth-210, where keeping the electron masses straight changes the answer. If a beta-decay energy calculation comes up, that bookkeeping is exactly where the marks — and the mistakes — are.

NARRATOR: So what actually drives beta decay? This is the named dot-point on the weak force, and it's where today connects to the quarks from two episodes ago. Beta decay is not the strong force, and it is not electromagnetism. It is mediated by the third nuclear-scale force: the weak nuclear force, carried by the W boson — and its heavier cousin the Z boson. State that explicitly, because "beta decay is caused by the weak force, carried by the W boson" is itself a mark, and calling it strong-force or electromagnetic is a classic error.

NARRATOR: Here's the mechanism at the quark level, and it's beautiful. Remember a neutron is up-down-down and a proton is up-up-down. In beta-minus decay, the weak force reaches in and converts one down quark into an up quark. Change a down to an up, and up-down-down becomes up-up-down — the neutron has literally become a proton. To balance charge and the other conservation laws, that transformation emits a W-minus boson, which immediately turns into an electron — the beta particle — and an electron antineutrino. So at the particle level: a neutron becomes a proton plus an electron plus an antineutrino. The proton stays in the nucleus; the electron and antineutrino fly out.

NARRATOR: Beta-plus is the exact mirror. The weak force converts an up quark into a down quark, so up-up-down — a proton — becomes up-down-down — a neutron. This time it emits a positron — the antimatter twin of the electron, which we'll meet again in the PET-scan episode — and an electron neutrino. So a proton becomes a neutron plus a positron plus a neutrino. The neutron stays; the positron and neutrino leave.

NARRATOR: That's the answer to "describe the role of the weak force in beta decay." The weak force changes one quark's flavour — down to up in beta-minus, up to down in beta-plus — which converts a neutron into a proton or a proton into a neutron, emitting a beta particle and a neutrino or antineutrino. Name the force, name the quark change, name what's emitted. That's full marks.

QUESTION: But hang on — where did the neutrino come from? Why do we even say there's a neutrino, if it's basically invisible?

NARRATOR: Brilliant question, and the answer is one of the best detective stories in physics. Go back to that detail I flagged about beta particles: they come out with a range of energies, anything up to a maximum. Now compare that to alpha decay, where the alpha particles all come out with a single, sharp, discrete energy unique to the parent isotope. That makes sense — there are two products, the daughter and the alpha, and conservation of energy and momentum forces the energy split to be a fixed value. But beta decay, with apparently just two products as well — the daughter and the electron — should also give a single sharp energy. Instead it gave a smear, a continuous spectrum. Energy and momentum did not appear to balance. It looked like energy was vanishing.

NARRATOR: Physicists faced a choice: either abandon the conservation of energy — unthinkable — or accept that something invisible was carrying away the missing energy and momentum. In 1930, Wolfgang Pauli took the second option and proposed a third particle, emitted alongside the electron, sharing the energy. Because all the charge was already accounted for, it had to be neutral. Because it had never been detected, it had to have negligible mass and interact extraordinarily weakly. Enrico Fermi built it into his full theory of beta decay and gave it its name — neutrino, "little neutral one." Fermi's theory then correctly predicted the relationship between the maximum beta energy and the half-life, which is exactly the kind of confirmation that turns a guess into accepted physics. The neutrino itself wasn't directly detected until 1956 — twenty-six years after Pauli predicted it on pure conservation grounds.

NARRATOR: And here's a cross-link worth its weight in marks: this is the same logical move as the discovery of the neutron. Remember the Chadwick story — a mysterious, uncharged, hard-to-detect particle was proposed to rescue the conservation of energy and momentum in nuclear reactions, before anyone could see it directly. Pauli's neutrino is that exact reasoning again: don't throw away conservation laws — instead, predict the invisible particle that saves them. If an exam asks you to discuss how conservation laws drive discovery, you now have two parallel cases, the neutron and the neutrino, and citing both is a band-six move.

NARRATOR: Let me also forward-link where these decays are going. The rate at which all this happens — how fast a sample of uranium or lead actually decays — is governed by half-life, which is our very next episode. The energy we just calculated, that four point two five mega-electron-volts, comes from the mass defect via Einstein's equation, which is the episode after. And the positron from beta-plus decay is the seed of the PET scanner, near the end of the module. Today is the foundation that all three build on.

NARRATOR: Let's lock in today's takeaways the way you'd write them in an exam. The mass number A is the count of nucleons — protons plus neutrons; the atomic number Z is the proton count and sets the element. Nuclear stability is a tug-of-war: long-range Coulomb repulsion between all protons, versus short-range strong-force attraction between all nucleons — neutrons add glue with no extra repulsion, so big nuclei need extra neutrons, and past about Z eighty-three nothing is enough. Alpha is a helium nucleus, charge plus two, highly ionising, low penetration — the bouncer. Gamma is a photon, no charge, weakly ionising, high penetration — the ghost. Beta is a fast electron in between, and beta-minus raises the atomic number by one while beta-plus lowers it. Every decay equation conserves mass number, charge, and mass-energy. And beta decay is the weak force, carried by the W boson, flipping a quark's flavour and emitting a beta particle plus a neutrino — with the neutrino predicted by Pauli purely to save the conservation of energy and momentum. Write the strong force as Velcro-covered magnets, the radiations as bouncer-punter-ghost, and the bookkeeping as "alpha eats four and two, beta swaps a charge, gamma changes nothing."

QUESTION: Question three. Explain how the balance between the strong nuclear force and the electrostatic force determines whether a nucleus is stable. Pause the player, give a full extended response, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Inside a nucleus, all protons repel one another by the long-range electrostatic, or Coulomb, force, which grows stronger as they are brought closer and adds up over every pair of protons. Opposing this is the strong nuclear force, which attracts all nucleons — both protons and neutrons — but acts only over an extremely short range of about ten to the minus fifteen metres, a nearest-neighbour interaction, and even becomes repulsive at the very shortest separations. A nucleus is stable when the short-range strong attraction between close-packed neighbours outweighs the total long-range Coulomb repulsion; it is unstable, and will decay, when it does not. Because Coulomb repulsion accumulates rapidly as the number of protons grows, while neutrons add strong-force attraction without adding any repulsion, larger nuclei require progressively more neutrons than protons to remain stable. Beyond about eighty-three protons, no neutron excess can restore the balance, so all such nuclei are unstable and undergo radioactive decay toward the valley of stability. That answer names both forces, both ranges, the no-charge role of neutrons, and the consequence for large nuclei — that's the full mark allocation.

QUESTION: Question four. Describe the role of the weak nuclear force in beta-minus decay, and explain why the existence of the antineutrino was proposed. Pause, answer fully, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. In beta-minus decay, the weak nuclear force, carried by the W boson, converts a down quark into an up quark inside a neutron. This changes the neutron, which is up-down-down, into a proton, which is up-up-down, and emits an electron — the beta particle — together with an electron antineutrino. The proton remains in the nucleus, raising the atomic number by one while leaving the mass number unchanged. The antineutrino was proposed because beta particles are emitted with a continuous range of energies rather than a single discrete value; without a third particle, energy and momentum would not be conserved. Rather than abandon the conservation laws, Pauli proposed a neutral, near-massless particle to carry away the missing energy and momentum, which Fermi named the neutrino. Naming the weak force and W boson, the quark flavour change, the emitted particles, the Z change, and the conservation argument covers every mark.
