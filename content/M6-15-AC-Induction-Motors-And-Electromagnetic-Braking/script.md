NARRATOR: The stationary outer part of the motor is called the stator. NARRATOR: Here's the magic. Each pair of coils produces a magnetic field pointing in its own direction. NARRATOR: There's one feature of electromagnetic braking that's worth a mark on its own and that students constantly get wrong, so let me hit it hard. QUESTION: Third, four marks, the marquee one. "Explain how electromagnetic induction causes a torque on the armature of an AC induction motor." Pause, plan a structured answer with every link, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The three-phase stator coils, spaced a hundred and twenty degrees apart and fed by currents a hundred and twenty degrees out of phase, produce a magnetic field of constant magnitude that rotates around the stator at the supply frequency. As this rotating field sweeps past the bars of the squirrel-cage rotor, there is relative motion between the field and the bars, so the magnetic flux through the rotor circuits changes continuously. By Faraday's law, this changing flux induces an EMF in the bars, and because the end rings short-circuit the bars into closed loops, the EMF drives a large induced current. Each current-carrying bar in the stator field experiences a force by the motor effect. By Lenz's law, the induced current opposes the change producing it, the relative motion, so the force drags the rotor in the same direction as the rotating field, producing a torque. The rotor accelerates but always slips slightly behind the field, since matching the field's speed would remove the relative motion and the torque. Full marks need: rotating field, relative motion, changing flux, Faraday, induced current in the closed bars, motor-effect force, Lenz, torque in the field's direction, and slip.

QUESTION: Fourth, four marks. "Explain how a force opposing motion is generated during electromagnetic braking, and why such braking cannot bring a moving object to a sudden, complete stop on its own." Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. When a metal conductor, such as a disc or rail, moves through a magnetic field, the relative motion changes the magnetic flux through the metal. By Faraday's law this changing flux induces an EMF, which drives circulating eddy currents in the bulk of the conductor. By Lenz's law, a consequence of conservation of energy, these eddy currents flow in the direction that opposes the change producing them, which means they oppose the motion of the conductor, producing a retarding braking force. The conductor's kinetic energy is dissipated as heat, current-squared-times-resistance heating, in the metal, slowing it. Because the braking force is proportional to the conductor's speed, it is strongest at high speed and weakens as the object slows, approaching zero as the object approaches rest. It therefore tapers off smoothly and cannot deliver the force needed for a sudden, complete stop; a friction brake is usually added for the final stop. The marks: Faraday, eddy currents, Lenz opposing the motion, energy to heat, and the force-proportional-to-speed point.

QUESTION: Fifth and last, three marks, a compare question. "Compare an AC induction motor with a DC motor in terms of how current reaches the rotor, and explain why this makes the induction motor more reliable." Pause, use contrast language, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, and use "whereas" because it's a compare. In a DC motor, current is delivered to the rotor coil through brushes pressing against a split-ring commutator, which also reverses the current each half-turn; whereas in an AC induction motor no current is delivered to the rotor at all, the rotor current is induced by the rotating field, so there are no brushes, no commutator, and no slip rings touching the rotor. Because nothing physically rubs against the induction motor's rotor, there are no contacts to wear out, spark, or require maintenance, which is why the AC induction motor is more reliable, more robust, and the most widely used motor in the world. The marks: brushes-and-commutator versus induced rotor current, the explicit contrast, and reliability from the absence of contacts.
