NARRATOR: So where does the difference between AC and DC come from? Just one place. The device that connects the rotating coil to the stationary external terminals. That's it. That's the only structural difference between an AC and a DC generator. Let me give you both.

NARRATOR: The AC generator uses slip rings. Two of them. Each one is a continuous, complete conducting ring. Each end of the coil is permanently soldered to its own slip ring, and the rings rotate with the coil. The crucial feature: each coil end stays connected to the same ring, and therefore the same terminal, forever. The connection never swaps. NARRATOR: The DC generator uses a split-ring commutator instead. Same device as in the DC motor, the one we built four lessons ago. It's one ring split into two insulated half-segments. Each coil end connects to one half. The brushes are fixed in space. Now here's the action. Every half-revolution, the gap in the split ring rotates past the brushes, and the commutator swaps which coil-end each brush is touching. And it does this swap at exactly the moment the coil's EMF passes through zero and is about to reverse. So just as the coil's output is about to go negative, the commutator flips the external connection, which flips the sign back to positive. The result: the external circuit always receives current in one direction. Unidirectional.

QUESTION: So the commutator reverses the EMF inside the coil to keep it positive?

Both generators consist of a coil, the rotor, forced to rotate in the magnetic field of stationary stator magnets, with brushes pressing on a rotating contact to carry current to the external circuit. As the coil rotates, the angle between the field and the coil changes, so the magnetic flux, B A cosine theta, continuously changes, and by Faraday's law this induces a sinusoidal EMF in the coil, maximum when the coil's plane is parallel to the field and the flux is zero but changing fastest, and zero when the coil's plane is perpendicular to the field and the flux is maximum but not changing. This internal sinusoidal EMF is identical in both machines. The single structural difference is the connector. The AC generator uses two continuous slip rings, one per coil end, so the connection to the external circuit never reverses and the output is a true alternating current, a full sine wave reversing each half-revolution. Whereas the DC generator uses a split-ring commutator, two insulated half-rings, whose contact with the brushes swaps every half-revolution at the zero-EMF instant, reversing the external connection so the current stays unidirectional. Consequently the AC output is a full sinusoid swinging positive and negative, while the DC output is a pulsating, rectified sinusoid: every negative half is flipped positive, rising from zero to maximum and back twice per revolution, unidirectional but not flat. In summary, the mechanism and the internal EMF are identical, and only the slip-rings-versus-commutator difference distinguishes the alternating AC output from the pulsating DC output. That hits all six marks: shared structure and mechanism, the sinusoidal explanation, the precise connector difference with "whereas," and both output shapes named correctly.
