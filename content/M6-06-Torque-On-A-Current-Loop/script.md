NARRATOR: Before we get to today, let's walk back through the last few lessons, because everything in this episode is built directly on top of them. This is the part of the module where the threads start tying together, and if the earlier ideas are solid, today is going to feel almost inevitable.

NARRATOR: Two episodes ago, in the motor effect lesson, we established the single most important result we're going to use today. A current is just charges in motion. And a moving charge in a magnetic field feels a force. So a wire carrying a current, sitting in a magnetic field, feels a force too, because that force is just the sum of the forces on all the moving charges packed inside it. We gave it a name and a size. The force equals the magnetic field strength B, times the current I, times the length of wire in the field L, times the sine of the angle between the wire and the field. F equals B I L sine theta. Maximum force when the wire is at right angles to the field, sine of ninety degrees is one. Zero force when the wire lies along the field, sine of zero is nothing.

NARRATOR: And remember the mechanism, because the marker wants the mechanism, not the formula. The wire makes its own circular magnetic field, looping around it. That field adds to the external field on one side, making the combined field stronger there, and opposes it on the other side, making the combined field weaker. The wire gets pushed from the strong side toward the weak side. That two-field interaction is the why. The right-hand push rule, or the right-hand palm rule, is just the tool you use to find which way the push points. It is a direction-finder, never an explanation. Hold onto that distinction, because it matters again today.

NARRATOR: Now reach back a little further. Before the motor effect, we did charged particles in magnetic fields directly. The force on a single moving charge, q v B, is always perpendicular to its velocity, so it does no work, the speed never changes, only the direction. That gives uniform circular motion, radius equals m v over q B, and a period that doesn't depend on speed, which is the principle behind the cyclotron. And before that, charged particles in electric fields, where a uniform field gives a constant force, constant acceleration, and a parabolic path, exactly like projectile motion. We tied all three together in the great comparison episode, and then we saw them put to work in real devices, mass spectrometers and electron guns, using the mnemonic A S D, Accelerate, Select, Deflect.

NARRATOR: Here's the thread. Charges feel forces in fields. Moving charges in magnetic fields feel forces perpendicular to their motion. A current is moving charges, so a wire feels a force. That was the motor effect. Today we take that exact same force and we apply it not to a straight wire, but to a rectangular loop of wire that's free to rotate. And something new emerges. The two sides of the loop get pushed in opposite directions, and instead of the loop flying off across the field, it spins. That spinning turning effect is called torque, and understanding how torque behaves as the loop rotates is the whole game today. It's the bridge between a wire that twitches and an actual electric motor that turns.

NARRATOR: So here's where we're going. By the end of this episode you'll be able to do three things cleanly. One, explain, from first principles, why the two active sides of a coil form what's called a couple and produce rotation instead of being shoved sideways. Two, explain why the torque rises and falls as the coil turns — the single most examined idea in this lesson — and state exactly where it's maximum and where it's zero, with the reason for each. And three, calculate the torque using the formula torque equals n B I A cosine theta, while dodging the one trap that costs more marks here than anything else, the cosine-versus-sine confusion. Let's build it.

NARRATOR: Start with the setup, because the geometry is everything in this topic. Picture a single rectangular loop of wire. It's mounted on an axle, an axis of rotation, that runs straight down its middle, so the loop can spin like a page turning in a book. Now put that loop into a uniform magnetic field — imagine the field pointing horizontally, left to right across the page. The axis of rotation is lined up parallel to the field direction. Current flows around the loop: in one terminal, around all four sides, out the other.

NARRATOR: Look at the four sides separately, because they do not all do the same job. Two of the sides — the ones running across the loop, perpendicular to the field — carry their current at right angles to B. Those are your active sides. By the motor effect, each of them feels a force, F equals B I L, with sine of ninety degrees equal to one, so the full force, no reduction. The other two sides — the ones running along the loop, parallel to the field — carry their current in the same direction as B, or directly opposite to it. The current there is parallel to the field, sine of zero is zero, so those sides feel no useful force at all. Any tiny force on them points along the axle and can't turn anything. Plus, those sides are usually attached to the axis anyway. So: two sides drive the rotation, two sides are passengers. That asymmetry is the first key idea.

QUESTION: Hang on — why is the current on the two active sides going in opposite directions? Aren't they part of the same loop?

NARRATOR: Exactly because they're part of the same loop. It's one continuous wire. Trace the current with your finger: it comes up one active side, crosses the top, goes down the other active side, crosses the bottom, and back. So on one active side the current heads one way, and on the side directly opposite, it heads the other way. They're antiparallel. And that is the crucial detail, because the motor-effect force depends on current direction. Same external field, opposite currents, so by the right-hand rule the two forces come out equal in size but pointing in opposite directions. One side gets pushed, say, out of the page; the other side, into the page.

NARRATOR: Now here's the part the source material is very specific about, and the part students get backwards. In the standard textbook diagram, with sides often labelled, the force on one active side is into the page and the force on the other is out of the page. Into and out of the page — not up and down. If you ever find yourself saying one force is up and the other is down, stop, because you've likely confused the direction of the force with the direction the side will travel as it swings. The forces themselves, on this orientation, are into and out of the page. Get that right and the rest follows.

NARRATOR: So we have two equal forces, opposite in direction, acting on the two sides of the loop, which sit on opposite sides of the central axis. Two equal, opposite, parallel forces whose lines of action are offset from each other — that arrangement has a name in physics, and the HSC wants the name. It's a couple. The source describes it as a pair of moments. A couple is special because of what it does and does not do. It produces a net turning effect, a torque, but it produces zero net translational force. The two forces cancel as far as pushing the whole loop bodily across the field goes — one pushes out of the page, the other into the page, they sum to nothing — but because they're offset, they twist. The loop spins on its axle. It does not fly sideways.

NARRATOR: Let me say that again in exam language, because it's worth a mark and students miss it. Because the two motor-effect forces form a couple — equal, opposite, and acting at a separation from the axis — they produce rotation without translation. The coil turns on the spot rather than being pushed across the field. If you ever describe the coil "being pushed across" the field, that's wrong, and it tells the marker you don't understand what a couple is.

QUESTION: So that's why it rotates. But the question that always comes up is why the torque changes as it spins. Why isn't it just constant?

NARRATOR: That is the heart of this entire lesson, so let's slow right down. And the first thing I need you to lock in is what stays the same and what changes, because the most common wrong answer here gets that exactly backwards.

NARRATOR: First, the definition of torque, in plain terms. Torque is the turning effect of a force. It equals the force, times the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the line along which the force acts. That perpendicular distance has a few names — the lever arm, the moment arm, sometimes just d, the perpendicular distance. Torque equals force times that lever arm. More force, or more lever arm, more turning. And torque has its own unit: the newton metre. Not the joule. Write newton metre, written as N m. I'll come back to that, because writing joules there is a marked error even though the units are dimensionally identical.

NARRATOR: Now, the active sides of the coil. As the coil rotates, those two sides stay perpendicular to the field the whole way round — they're the sides crossing the field, and spinning the loop about its central axis doesn't change that. So the force on them, B I L, stays constant in magnitude through the entire rotation. The force does not get weaker as the coil turns. Burn that in. The force is constant.

NARRATOR: So if the force is constant, what makes the torque change? The lever arm. The perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of each force changes as the coil rotates. That's the whole mechanism. The torque varies because of geometry — because the leverage changes — not because the force weakens. Let me walk you through the two extreme positions, because those two positions are what the exam asks for by name.

NARRATOR: Position one. The plane of the coil is parallel to the field. The flat face of the loop lies along the field direction. In this orientation the two forces — one out of the page, one into the page — act at the very edges of the loop, at the full half-width out from the central axis on each side. Their lines of action are offset from the axis by the maximum possible perpendicular distance. Maximum lever arm, so maximum torque. This is the position of greatest turning effect. When the plane of the coil is parallel to the field, torque is at its maximum.

NARRATOR: Position two. Rotate the coil ninety degrees, so now the plane of the coil is perpendicular to the field. Equivalently, the normal to the coil — the line sticking straight out of its flat face — is now parallel to the field. The two forces still exist. They are still B I L, still full strength, nothing has changed about the force. But now look at where they point. They point straight outward, directly away from each other, along the line that passes right through the axis. The forces are now trying to pull the loop apart along its width, to stretch it. Their lines of action pass through the axis, so the perpendicular distance is zero. Zero lever arm. Zero torque. Even though the forces are full strength, there is no turning effect at all. The coil is not being turned at this instant.

NARRATOR: That contrast is the most important thing in this episode, so here it is one more time, as a pair. Plane parallel to the field: forces act at maximum distance from the axis, maximum torque. Plane perpendicular to the field: forces act along the axis, zero torque. And in between, as the coil rotates from one to the other, the torque falls smoothly from maximum to zero, following a cosine curve. Then if the coil keeps going, the lever arm opens back up and the torque builds again.

QUESTION: Before you go on — I want to test this. At the zero-torque position, is the torque zero because the force is zero? Pause the player, decide yes or no and why, then come back.

NARRATOR: No. And this is trap number one, so let's nail it. At the zero-torque position the force is not zero. The force is still B I L, full strength. The torque is zero because the forces act through the axis — zero perpendicular distance, zero lever arm — not because the forces vanished. If you write in an exam "the torque is zero because there is no force on the sides," that's wrong, and it's a wrong that markers specifically look for. The right sentence is: "the torque is zero because the forces, though still present, act along the axis of rotation, so their perpendicular distance is zero." Force present, leverage absent. Say it that way.

NARRATOR: While we're flagging the traps, here's the partner to that one. Some students say the force on the active sides decreases as the coil rotates. It does not. For sides that stay perpendicular to the field, the force is rock-steady at B I L the whole way round. What changes is the lever arm, the geometry, not the force. "The force decreases as it rotates" loses the mark. "The perpendicular distance, or lever arm, decreases as it rotates" earns it.

NARRATOR: Now let's turn all of that into the formula, because the formula is just a tidy package of everything we've said. For a coil of n turns, the torque equals the number of turns n, times the magnetic field strength B, times the current I, times the area of the coil A, times the cosine of the angle theta. Torque equals n B I A cosine theta. Let me unpack each piece. n is how many loops of wire are wound — more loops, more current-carrying sides stacked up, more torque. B is the field strength in tesla. I is the current in amps. A is the area enclosed by one turn — for a rectangle, length times width — in square metres. And theta, in this textbook convention, is the angle between the field and the plane of the coil.

NARRATOR: Check that the formula agrees with the physics we just built. Plane parallel to the field means theta is zero, cosine of zero is one, torque equals n B I A — its maximum value. Plane perpendicular to the field means theta is ninety degrees, cosine of ninety is zero, torque is zero. Perfect. The cosine factor is just the lever-arm story written in trigonometry. And notice the factors that increase torque are sitting right there in the formula: more turns n, stronger field B, larger current I, larger area A. There's your memory hook for "list the factors that increase torque" — you don't need a separate acronym, the formula itself is the list. n, B, I, A. If a question asks how to increase the torque on a coil, you read the four letters straight off the equation and explain each.

QUESTION: Okay, but I've seen the formula written with sine, not cosine. Which is right?

NARRATOR: Both. And this is the single biggest trap in the whole topic, so listen carefully, because getting this wrong is the number one way students drop marks here. The physics is identical; the only difference is how you define the angle theta.

NARRATOR: The textbook convention, the one we've been using, defines theta as the angle between the field and the plane of the coil. With that definition, torque equals n B I A cosine theta, and you get maximum torque when the plane is parallel to the field, theta equals zero. The NESA formula sheet writes the exact same physics differently. It defines theta as the angle between the field and the normal to the coil — the normal being the line sticking straight out of the coil's face, perpendicular to its plane. With that definition, torque equals n B I A sine theta, and you get maximum torque when the normal is perpendicular to the field.

NARRATOR: Both are correct because the two angles are complements — they add up to ninety degrees — and the cosine of one equals the sine of the other. Cosine of the plane angle equals sine of the normal angle. So they describe the identical curve. The practical rule, the one that saves your marks: when you read a question, find out which angle the diagram or the wording is giving you. Is theta measured to the plane of the coil, or to the normal? If it's to the plane, use cosine. If it's to the normal, use sine. Do not blindly copy "cosine" from your notes onto a formula-sheet question that's measuring to the normal, and don't blindly copy "sine" either. Read the angle, then pick the function. The number-one mark-loser in this topic is misreading which angle theta refers to and using the wrong trig function.

NARRATOR: Let's make it concrete and consistent: in this episode, theta is always the angle between the field and the plane of the coil, so we use cosine, and maximum torque is at theta equals zero, plane parallel to field. Good. Now let's work a full numerical example the way you'd write it for marks. QUESTION: This all explains why it rotates and why the torque changes. But if the torque drops to zero when the coil is perpendicular, what stops the motor right there? How does a real motor keep going?

NARRATOR: Brilliant question, and it's the perfect bridge to the next episode, so let me set it up but not solve it — solving it is M6-07's job. Here's the problem. Left as a bare loop, the coil spins up, but its own inertia carries it past the zero-torque position. The trouble is, the instant it goes past, the geometry flips, and the torque now acts to push the coil back the other way. So a plain DC loop doesn't spin continuously. It oscillates around the zero-torque position and stalls. A bare loop is a wobble, not a motor.

NARRATOR: The fix is a clever bit of switching called the split-ring commutator. It reverses the direction of the current through the coil at exactly the zero-torque position, so that after the coil passes through, the forces have flipped too, and the torque keeps pushing it the same way round. That converts oscillation into continuous one-direction rotation. We pull that apart fully next lesson. For now, the takeaway is just this: the torque on a bare loop varies as a cosine and hits zero twice per turn, and that zero is precisely the problem the commutator exists to solve. Do not, by the way, claim the bare loop spins continuously by itself — it doesn't, and saying so is a trap. Continuous rotation needs the commutator.
