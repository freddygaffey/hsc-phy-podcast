NARRATOR: Start one episode back, polarisation and Malus's law. The key idea: unpolarised light has its electric field oscillating in every plane perpendicular to the direction of travel, and a polarising filter transmits only the component of the electric field along its transmission axis, absorbing the rest. The first filter, hitting unpolarised light, always halves the intensity, regardless of its angle, because averaged over all those random directions, the mean of cosine-squared is one half. Then between two filters you use Malus's law: the transmitted intensity equals the maximum intensity times the cosine-squared of the angle between the filter axes. Crossed filters, ninety degrees apart, give cosine-squared of ninety, which is zero, so no light gets through at all. And the deep point, the one we said pays off today: only a transverse wave can be polarised. A longitudinal wave has nothing to filter, because its oscillation is along the direction of travel, not across it. Hold onto that. It's the clincher.

NARRATOR: First, the two rivals. In the late sixteen hundreds and into the seventeen hundreds, the dominant model of light was Isaac Newton's. Newton said light is a stream of tiny particles. NARRATOR: Now here's a subtlety that earns marks, and that weak answers miss completely. Both models could explain the everyday behaviour of light. A particle model bends light by imagining a force at the boundary; a wave model bends it by Huygens's principle. The point is, because both models account for reflection and refraction, those phenomena cannot be used to decide between them. This is a classic trap, so let me say it sharply.

QUESTION: Wait, so if I write in my evaluation that refraction proves light is a wave, that's wrong?

NARRATOR: It's worse than wrong, it shows you've missed the logic of the whole question. Refraction, on its own, proves nothing, because Newton's particle model explains it just as happily as Huygens's wave model does. That's the principle a strong evaluation runs on: the decisive evidence is the evidence only one model can explain. So reflection and ordinary refraction are off the table as deciders. Let's take the refraction disagreement first, because it's the cleanest knockout in the story.

NARRATOR: Here is the point of difference. When light passes from air into water, it bends towards the normal. Everyone agreed it bends towards the normal; that's just observation. The two models disagreed completely on why. Newton's particle model said: the water attracts the light particles as they cross the boundary, pulling them in, which speeds up the component of velocity perpendicular to the surface. For that geometry to bend the light towards the normal, the particle model requires light to travel faster in water than in air. Huygens's wave model said the opposite: the wavefront slows down on entering the water, so the part of the wavefront that enters first lags behind, and that lag swings the whole wavefront round towards the normal. For the wave model, light must travel slower in water than in air.

NARRATOR: Do you see how beautiful that is? Same observation, light bends towards the normal, but two flatly contradictory predictions about the speed. Particle model: faster in water. Wave model: slower in water. One of them has to be wrong, and a measurement can decide it. This is the kind of clean, falsifiable disagreement that science lives for.

NARRATOR: The measurement came in eighteen fifty. The French physicist Léon Foucault measured the speed of light in water and found it slower than in air. Now to the three pillars, the phenomena the particle model cannot explain at all.

NARRATOR: I'm going to give you a memory hook for the three pillars right now, because in the exam, under pressure, the danger isn't understanding them, it's blanking on one. The three pieces of evidence for the wave model are Diffraction, Interference, Polarisation. Take the first letters: D, I, P. Remember it as DIP. When you sit down to that evaluation question, the first thing you scrawl in the margin is DIP, and now you can't forget a pillar. We'll do each one as observation, then the killer line, why a particle cannot do it.

NARRATOR: Pillar one, diffraction. The observation: light bends and spreads around the edges of obstacles and through narrow gaps, when the gap or the obstacle is comparable in size to the wavelength, and it produces faint fringes at the very edge of a shadow. The wave explanation is Huygens's principle: every point on a wavefront acts as a source of tiny secondary spherical wavelets, and the new wavefront is the envelope, the surface that just touches all those wavelets. At an edge or a gap, those wavelets spread sideways into the region that should be in shadow. That's diffraction. Now the killer line, the contrast that earns the mark. Newton's particles, once they've been emitted, travel in straight lines until they hit something. A straight-line projectile has no mechanism whatsoever to bend around a corner into the shadow. So diffraction, light reaching into a region that straight-line particles could never reach, is evidence that light is a wave.

NARRATOR: There's a lovely historical wrinkle here, and it's worth knowing because it shows up in "analyse the evidence" questions. In Huygens's own time, the seventeenth century, the faintness of diffraction was actually used against the wave model. The argument went: if light were a wave, it should obviously bend round corners the way sound does, the way water waves do, and it clearly doesn't, shadows have sharp edges, so light can't be a wave. The flaw in that argument is that light's wavelength is minuscule, a few hundred nanometres, so the bending is tiny and you have to look hard to see it. The decisive, unmistakable demonstration didn't come until Thomas Young around eighteen-oh-two, and then Augustin-Jean Fresnel, who put the wave theory of diffraction on a full mathematical footing. So the honest history is: diffraction was always there as evidence, but it took a century and better experiments to read it correctly.

NARRATOR: Pillar two, interference, and this is the strongest single piece of evidence, so this is the paragraph to get right. The observation: in Young's double-slit experiment, performed around eighteen-oh-one to eighteen-oh-three, two coherent beams of light overlap and produce a pattern of alternating bright and dark bands on a screen. The wave explanation: light from the two slits travels slightly different distances to reach a given point, so there's a path difference. Where the path difference is a whole number of wavelengths, the waves arrive in phase, crest on crest, they reinforce, and you get a bright band, constructive interference. Where the path difference is an odd number of half-wavelengths, the waves arrive exactly out of phase, crest on trough, they cancel, and you get a dark band, destructive interference.

NARRATOR: Now here is the killer line, and it is the most important sentence in this entire episode, so I'm going to say it more than once. A particle model can explain the bright bands easily, just say more particles arrive there. But a particle model cannot explain the dark bands. Two streams of particles arriving at a point can only ever add up, more particles means brighter, fewer means dimmer; particles can never combine to give nothing. Yet at a dark fringe, light plus light produces darkness. Light arriving from two slits, cancelling to leave a black band. That is impossible for particles and completely natural for waves with a path difference. The production of darkness from two beams of light is the decisive evidence for the wave nature of light.

QUESTION: Why do you keep stressing the dark bands? Surely the bright bands are evidence too, two beams making a brighter spot?

NARRATOR: Because the bright bands, on their own, don't defeat the particle model, and that's exactly the trap. If all you say is "the two beams reinforce to make bright spots", a marker can reasonably reply: so what, two streams of particles piling up in the same place would also make a brighter spot. The bright bands are consistent with particles. It's the dark bands that are not. Particles cannot subtract; they cannot cancel; they cannot make darkness. So a weak answer talks only about waves adding up to bright spots and never mentions cancellation, and it caps its own marks. The strong answer goes straight for the throat: light plus light equals darkness, which particles cannot do. Emphasise the cancellation, every time. That's where the mark is.

NARRATOR: And one precision point while we're here, because markers test it: coherent. Young's two slits had to be coherent, meaning monochromatic, a single wavelength, and in a fixed phase relationship. Young achieved that by using a single primary source, one pinhole, whose wavefront he then split into two. Because both slits were lit by the same original wavefront, they stayed locked in phase. If you used two independent lamps instead, they'd be incoherent, their phase relationship random and constantly shifting, and the interference pattern would wash out into a blur. No coherence, no stable fringes. Don't let "coherent" decay into just "same colour" in your answer; it's same colour and constant phase.

NARRATOR: Pillar three, polarisation, and this one does two jobs, which is why it's the most powerful single observation of the three. The observation: pass light through a polarising filter and its oscillation is restricted to a single plane; put a second filter, an analyser, crossed at ninety degrees, and the light is extinguished completely. Malus's law captures it, the transmitted intensity is the maximum times cosine-squared of the angle, and at ninety degrees cosine-squared is zero, total darkness. Now the explanation. A transverse wave oscillates perpendicular to its direction of travel, so it has a definable plane of oscillation, and a filter can select one plane and block the rest. Job one: a particle stream has no plane of oscillation, nothing to filter, so the very fact that light can be polarised proves light is a wave.

NARRATOR: But here's job two, and this is the part that lifts polarisation above the other two pillars. Polarisation doesn't just prove light is a wave; it proves light is a transverse wave specifically. Because a longitudinal wave cannot be polarised. Think about it: in a longitudinal wave the oscillation is along the direction of travel, back and forth in the same line the wave moves. There's no sideways plane to select, so there's nothing for a filter to discriminate between. Only a transverse wave, oscillating across the direction of travel, has distinct planes a filter can pick from. So polarisation pins light down as transverse, and notice, this actually corrected Huygens. Huygens was right that light is a wave, but he wrongly thought it was longitudinal, like sound. Polarisation proved him half wrong: wave, yes, but transverse, not longitudinal.

NARRATOR: And this is a beautiful place to cash in Hertz. Remember the case of Heinrich Hertz verifying Maxwell, from the start of this module? Hertz generated radio waves with a spark-gap oscillator and detected them with a loop of wire. And one of the things he showed was that his radio waves could be reflected, refracted, and polarised, exactly like light. When he rotated his receiving loop, the signal died at ninety degrees, just like crossed polarising filters. That mattered enormously, because it confirmed that light is an electromagnetic transverse wave, the same kind of thing as Hertz's radio, only at a much shorter wavelength. So Hertz is the witness who ties polarisation to the full electromagnetic picture. In an evaluation, dropping Hertz's name as confirmation of the transverse, electromagnetic nature of light is a mark-earning move.

NARRATOR: So that's the case for the wave model: the refraction disagreement settled by Foucault, plus the three pillars, DIP, diffraction, interference, polarisation, each defeating the particle model in a way the particle model cannot answer. QUESTION: Question four, three marks. Explain why the polarisation of light provides more information about the nature of light than diffraction or interference alone. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Diffraction and interference show that light is a wave, but they do not reveal whether it is a transverse or a longitudinal wave. Polarisation does both: a particle stream has no plane of oscillation to filter, so polarisation confirms light is a wave, and because only a transverse wave has a plane of oscillation that a filter can select, while a longitudinal wave cannot be polarised, polarisation further proves that light is specifically a transverse wave. This corrected Huygens, who had wrongly proposed light was longitudinal. Three marks: confirms wave, proves transverse, and the contrast that longitudinal waves can't be polarised. That contrast is the whole point of the question.

QUESTION: Question five, the big one, seven marks. Evaluate the evidence supporting the wave model of light. Pause, plan it on DIP, write your full answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, and I'll give it in the structure you should use. Verdict first. The classical wave model of light is strongly supported by three phenomena, diffraction, interference and polarisation, each of which Newton's particle model cannot explain; however, it is ultimately shown to be incomplete by later quantum observations, so the evidence supports it decisively for classical optics while exposing its limits. The contest: both Newton's particle model and Huygens's wave model explained reflection and refraction, so those did not decide between them, but they made opposite predictions for the speed of light in water, particle faster, wave slower, and Foucault's eighteen-fifty measurement of light slower in water refuted the particle model. Diffraction: light spreads around edges and through gaps by Huygens's principle, whereas particles travel in straight lines and cannot bend around corners. Interference: Young's double slit produces bright and dark bands from coherent beams, whereas particles can only add to give brightness and can never cancel to give the dark bands, so darkness from light plus light is decisive for waves. Polarisation: a filter restricts light to one plane and a crossed filter extinguishes it, which is impossible for particles and for longitudinal waves, so polarisation proves light is a transverse wave, correcting Huygens, and Hertz confirmed electromagnetic waves polarise like light. Judgement and limit: the evidence from Young, Fresnel, Foucault and Hertz overwhelmingly supports the wave model for classical light, but it fails to explain black-body radiation and the photoelectric effect, which require light to behave as discrete photons, so the wave model is strongly supported but not a complete description of light. That answer has the verdict, the contest, three contrasted pillars, named evidence, and the limitation. That's seven marks.
