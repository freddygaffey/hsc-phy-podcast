NARRATOR: Welcome back to Module Three, Waves and Thermodynamics. We've built the wave model and then watched waves behave, reflect, refract, diffract, and superpose, and we finished last episode with standing waves and resonance. Today we take all of that and point it at one specific, wonderfully concrete wave: sound. Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave, and it's the perfect test case, because every idea from the last two episodes shows up in it. By the end you'll be able to describe sound as a pressure wave, relate pitch to frequency and loudness to intensity, explain how intensity falls with distance, work out the standing waves and harmonics that give a string or a pipe its musical note, calculate beats, and explain the Doppler effect. This is the physics of music and of every siren you've ever heard. But first, a recap.

NARRATOR: Last episode we said a standing wave forms when two identical waves travelling in opposite directions superpose, giving fixed nodes, always out of phase, zero displacement, and antinodes, always in phase, maximum displacement, and that this pattern stores energy rather than transporting it. We also said resonance occurs when a system is driven at its natural frequency, so energy transfers efficiently and the amplitude grows large. Hold both of those tightly, because a musical instrument is nothing more than a cleverly shaped object that resonates, building standing waves of sound at its natural frequencies. Today we make that quantitative. And recall from the very first episode that sound is the classic longitudinal mechanical wave, the air molecules oscillate along the direction of travel, and it needs a medium.

QUESTION: Quick check from last episode. In a standing wave, what is a node and what is an antinode, in terms of displacement? And what condition produces resonance? Pause the player, answer all three, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. A node is a point of permanently zero displacement, where the two waves are always out of phase and cancel. An antinode is a point of maximum displacement, where the two waves are always in phase and reinforce. And resonance occurs when the driving frequency equals the system's natural frequency, maximising energy transfer and amplitude. Nodes are zero, antinodes are maximum, resonance is driving frequency equals natural frequency. Lock those, because in a moment the fixed and open ends of strings and pipes will force nodes and antinodes into particular places, and that's what sets the musical note.

NARRATOR: Let's start with what sound actually is. Sound is a longitudinal wave: a vibrating source, a speaker cone, a guitar string, your vocal cords, pushes on the surrounding air, creating regions where the air molecules are squeezed together, compressions, high pressure, and regions where they're spread apart, rarefactions, low pressure. These pressure variations travel outward through the air as the wave, while each air molecule just oscillates back and forth a tiny distance about its rest position, it doesn't travel with the sound. So sound is really a travelling pattern of pressure, and you can describe it two equivalent ways: as the displacement of the air molecules, or as the pressure variation, and the two are a quarter-cycle out of step, where the displacement is zero the pressure swing is greatest. Because sound is mechanical, it needs a medium, there is no sound in the vacuum of space, and it travels faster in denser, stiffer media: roughly three hundred and forty metres per second in air, about fifteen hundred in water, and faster still in steel. The medium sets the speed, and notice it's the opposite ranking to what people expect, sound is slowest in air, the thin medium, and fastest in solids like steel, because the tightly bound, stiff particles of a solid pass the disturbance along far more quickly than the loosely spaced molecules of a gas. Temperature matters too: warmer air carries sound a little faster, because the molecules move faster and collide more often. The medium sets the speed. That's all in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now the two things your ear registers: pitch and loudness, and each maps onto a wave quantity. Pitch is how high or low a note sounds, and it's set by the frequency: a high frequency is a high pitch, a low frequency is a low pitch. A note at two hundred and fifty-six hertz is middle C; double the frequency and you go up an octave. Loudness is how loud the sound is, and it's set by the amplitude of the pressure variation, which we measure as intensity, the power carried by the wave per unit area. A bigger amplitude means a louder sound and more energy delivered. So pitch goes with frequency, loudness goes with amplitude and intensity, two independent properties, which is why you can play the same note, same pitch, loudly or softly, same frequency, different amplitude. Don't mix them up: frequency sets pitch, amplitude sets loudness. That mapping is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Loudness brings in an important quantitative idea: how intensity changes with distance. A point source radiates its sound energy outward in all directions, spreading it over the surface of an expanding sphere. Since the area of that sphere grows as the square of the distance, the same power is spread ever more thinly, and the intensity falls off as one over the distance squared. This is the inverse-square law: double your distance from the source and the intensity drops to a quarter; triple it and the intensity drops to a ninth. It's why a sound is so much quieter from across a field than up close, and the syllabus explicitly wants you to investigate the relationship between distance and intensity. The inverse-square behaviour is in Listing two, and notice it's a general feature of anything radiating from a point, it'll return for light intensity and for gravitation.

QUESTION: A quick application. You stand two metres from a small speaker and measure a certain sound intensity. You walk back to six metres away. By what factor has the intensity changed? Pause the player, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Intensity follows the inverse-square law, one over distance squared. You went from two metres to six metres, that's three times the distance. Intensity changes by one over three squared, one over nine. So the intensity is now one ninth of what it was at two metres. Three times the distance, one ninth the intensity. The trap is to think tripling the distance thirds the intensity, no, it's the square of the distance ratio, so a factor of nine, not three. That squaring is the whole point of the inverse-square law.

NARRATOR: Now the heart of the episode: how strings and pipes make musical notes, which is standing waves and resonance made concrete. Take a guitar string fixed at both ends. When you pluck it, waves travel along it and reflect off both fixed ends, and the reflections superpose to build standing waves, exactly the mechanism from last episode. But there's a constraint: the string is clamped at both ends, so each end must be a node, it can't move. Only certain wavelengths fit that condition, the ones that put a node exactly at each end. The simplest, longest-wavelength pattern has a node at each end and a single antinode in the middle, and for that one the string length equals half a wavelength. This lowest-frequency standing wave is called the fundamental, or first harmonic, and it's the dominant pitch you hear. Then there are patterns with more nodes, two antinodes, three antinodes, and so on, the higher harmonics, whole-number multiples of the fundamental frequency. The string length, its tension, and its mass per unit length set the fundamental, which is why a tighter or shorter or lighter string sounds higher. That's in Listing three.

NARRATOR: The same idea works in a pipe, a flute, an organ pipe, a bottle you blow across, but now the boundary conditions depend on whether the ends are open or closed. At a closed end the air can't move, so there must be a node; at an open end the air is free to move maximally, so there's an antinode. A pipe open at both ends has an antinode at each end, and its fundamental fits half a wavelength into the pipe, so the fundamental wavelength is twice the length, and it produces all the harmonics, every whole-number multiple. A pipe closed at one end has a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end, and its fundamental fits only a quarter of a wavelength into the pipe, so the fundamental wavelength is four times the length, and, crucially, a closed pipe produces only the odd harmonics, one, three, five times the fundamental. Two consequences worth memorising: a closed pipe of a given length sounds an octave lower than an open pipe of the same length, because its fundamental wavelength is four times the length rather than twice; and the closed pipe's missing even harmonics give it a distinctly different tone. The pipe rules are in Listing three, with worked frequencies in Listing four.

QUESTION: Let's use the pipe rule. A pipe is closed at one end and is zero point two metres long. The speed of sound is three hundred and forty metres per second. What is its fundamental wavelength, and its fundamental frequency? Pause the player, work both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's in Listing four. For a pipe closed at one end, the fundamental fits a quarter of a wavelength in the pipe, so the fundamental wavelength is four times the length, four times zero point two, zero point eight metres. Then the fundamental frequency is the speed of sound divided by the wavelength, three hundred and forty over zero point eight, which is four hundred and twenty-five hertz. So the wavelength is zero point eight metres and the frequency is four hundred and twenty-five hertz. The key step you had to know is the closed-pipe rule, fundamental wavelength equals four times the length, and then it's just the wave equation rearranged, frequency equals speed over wavelength.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "explain how a standing wave on a guitar string produces a note of definite pitch". The band-six version: when the string is plucked, waves travel along it and reflect off the two fixed ends; these reflected waves travel in opposite directions and superpose. Because the ends are fixed, each end must be a node, which means only standing waves of certain wavelengths can form on the string. The longest of these, the fundamental, has the lowest frequency and dominates the sound, giving the note its definite pitch; its frequency is set by the string's length, tension and mass per unit length. The marks are for: reflection at the fixed ends, superposition forming a standing wave, the fixed-end node condition selecting particular frequencies, and the fundamental setting the pitch. The weak answer, "the string vibrates and makes a sound", earns little because it never mentions reflection, superposition, the node condition, or the fundamental. Name the mechanism: reflect, superpose, nodes at the ends, fundamental sets the pitch.

NARRATOR: Here's why this matters for the sound you actually hear, the quality or timbre of a note. When a real string or pipe sounds, it doesn't produce just the fundamental; it produces the fundamental together with a mix of the higher harmonics, all at once, superposed. The fundamental fixes the pitch, but the particular blend of harmonics, how strong the second, third, fourth are relative to the fundamental, is what makes a violin and a flute playing the same note sound completely different. A flute is nearly a pure fundamental with weak harmonics, so it sounds smooth and clear; a violin is rich in harmonics, so it sounds bright and complex. And remember the closed pipe gives only odd harmonics, missing the evens, which is exactly why a clarinet, which behaves like a closed pipe, has its characteristic hollow, woody tone, different from a flute, which behaves like an open pipe with all the harmonics present. So the harmonic content explains timbre, and the boundary conditions, open or closed, control which harmonics are even allowed. That's the whole physics of why instruments have distinctive voices.

NARRATOR: Let me work one more pipe example, an open pipe this time, so you've seen both. Suppose an organ pipe open at both ends is zero point three four metres long and the speed of sound is three hundred and forty metres per second. The fundamental of an open pipe fits half a wavelength into the pipe, so the fundamental wavelength is twice the length, twice zero point three four, zero point six eight metres. The fundamental frequency is then the speed over the wavelength, three hundred and forty over zero point six eight, five hundred hertz. The second harmonic is twice that, one thousand hertz, the third harmonic three times, fifteen hundred hertz, and so on through all the whole-number multiples, because an open pipe permits every harmonic. Contrast that with the closed pipe we did earlier: same wave equation each time, but the boundary rule, twice the length for open, four times the length for closed, and all harmonics versus odd-only, is what you must remember. The working sits in Listing four.

NARRATOR: Now a phenomenon that comes straight out of superposition: beats. If you sound two notes whose frequencies are close but not equal, say two hundred and fifty-six hertz and two hundred and sixty hertz, they superpose, and because they drift in and out of phase with each other, the combined sound rises and falls in loudness, a slow throbbing called beats. The number of these loudness pulses per second is the beat frequency, and it equals the magnitude of the difference between the two frequencies. For our example, two hundred and sixty minus two hundred and fifty-six, that's four beats per second. Musicians use this to tune: as two strings approach the same frequency, the beats slow down, and when the beating stops entirely the notes are in tune. It also works the other way as a sensitive test, your ear can detect a beat of even one cycle per second easily, so beats let you hear a tiny frequency difference far smaller than you could ever judge by pitch alone, which is why piano tuners and orchestras rely on them. So the beat frequency is just the size of the difference of the two frequencies, and it's in Listing two. The cause is pure superposition, two close frequencies alternately reinforcing and cancelling.

QUESTION: A retrieval one. Two tuning forks, one at five hundred hertz and one at five hundred and three hertz, are sounded together. How many beats per second do you hear? And as you adjust the second fork so the beats slow down, is its frequency getting closer to or further from five hundred hertz? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The beat frequency is the magnitude of the difference, five hundred and three minus five hundred, three beats per second. And as the beats slow down, the two frequencies are getting closer together, because the beat frequency is the difference between them, so a smaller difference means slower beats; when the beats stop, the frequencies are equal. Three beats per second, and slowing beats mean the frequencies are converging. That's exactly how you tune by ear, listen for the beats to slow and vanish.

NARRATOR: The last idea is the Doppler effect, the change in the observed frequency of a wave because of relative motion between the source and the observer. You've heard it: a siren or a racing car sounds higher-pitched as it approaches and noticeably lower-pitched as it passes and recedes. Here's the cause, and getting it right is worth marks. When the source moves towards you, each successive wavefront is emitted from a little closer, so the wavefronts bunch up in front of the source, the wavelength you receive is shorter and the frequency you observe is higher, higher pitch. When the source moves away, the wavefronts spread out behind it, the wavelength you receive is longer and the observed frequency is lower, lower pitch. The crucial subtlety: the source itself is emitting at a constant frequency the whole time, what changes is the frequency you observe, because the relative motion compresses or stretches the wavefronts on their way to you. Approaching, higher; receding, lower. The Doppler relationship is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "explain, using the Doppler effect, why a siren's pitch drops as it passes you". The band-six version: as the siren approaches, its motion towards you compresses the sound wavefronts in front of it, shortening the wavelength you receive and so raising the frequency you observe above the emitted frequency, a higher pitch. As it passes and recedes, its motion away from you stretches the wavefronts, lengthening the received wavelength and lowering the observed frequency below the emitted frequency, a lower pitch. The pitch therefore drops as the siren goes from approaching to receding, even though the siren emits a constant frequency throughout. The marks are for: compression of wavefronts on approach giving a higher observed frequency, stretching on recession giving a lower one, and the key point that the emitted frequency is unchanged, it's the observed frequency that shifts. The weak answer, "the sound changes because the siren is moving", earns little. Name the compression and stretching of the wavefronts and the shift in observed frequency.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can study sound with a signal generator, a speaker, and a microphone feeding an oscilloscope: change the frequency and watch the pitch and the displayed waveform change; change the amplitude and watch the loudness and the height of the trace change. You can find the speed of sound with a resonance tube, a tube partly in water, by finding the lengths at which a held tuning fork makes the air column resonate loudly, those lengths mark the standing-wave pattern and give you the wavelength, then the speed from the wave equation. You can measure how intensity falls with distance using a sound-level meter at increasing distances and confirm the inverse-square trend. And you can hear beats directly by sounding two slightly mistuned forks. Each investigation is one of today's ideas made measurable, and describing one, the variable changed and what it showed, is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: saying pitch is loudness, no, pitch is frequency, loudness is amplitude and intensity. Trap two: thinking tripling the distance thirds the intensity, it's the inverse square, so a ninth. Trap three: forgetting that a closed pipe gives only odd harmonics and a fundamental wavelength of four times the length, while open pipes and strings give all harmonics with a fundamental of twice the length. Trap four: saying the source frequency changes in the Doppler effect, it doesn't, the observed frequency changes. Trap five: confusing beats, two close frequencies superposing, with the Doppler effect, relative motion, they're different phenomena. Avoid those five and sound is secure.

QUESTION: Question one. Explain why sound is described as a longitudinal wave, and state which property of the sound determines its pitch and which determines its loudness. Three marks. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Sound is longitudinal because the particles of the medium oscillate back and forth parallel to the direction in which the wave travels, creating compressions and rarefactions, regions of high and low pressure. QUESTION: Question three. A pipe open at both ends and a pipe closed at one end have the same length. Explain how their fundamental frequencies compare, and which harmonics each produces. Four marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. For a pipe open at both ends there is an antinode at each end, and the fundamental fits half a wavelength into the pipe, so the fundamental wavelength is twice the length. The open pipe produces all harmonics, whole-number multiples of its fundamental, whereas the closed pipe produces only the odd harmonics. QUESTION: Question four. Two guitar strings are sounded together. One is at three hundred and thirty hertz; the other is slightly out of tune, and four beats per second are heard. NARRATOR: Model answer. The beat frequency is the magnitude of the difference between the two frequencies, and it is four hertz, so the second string is four hertz away from three hundred and thirty hertz, meaning it is either three hundred and twenty-six hertz or three hundred and thirty-four hertz. As the guitarist tunes the second string closer to three hundred and thirty hertz, the difference between the frequencies decreases, so the beat frequency decreases and the beats slow down; when the string reaches three hundred and thirty hertz the beats stop altogether, indicating the strings are in tune. This tests beats as a tuning tool.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A car sounding its horn at a constant frequency drives past a stationary observer at speed. Describe and explain, using the Doppler effect, the change in the frequency the observer hears as the car approaches, passes, and recedes. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. As the car approaches, its motion towards the observer compresses the sound wavefronts ahead of it, shortening the wavelength that reaches the observer and so raising the observed frequency above the emitted frequency, the observer hears a higher pitch. While the car is approaching, this raised pitch stays roughly steady. As the car passes and begins to recede, its motion away from the observer stretches the wavefronts behind it, lengthening the received wavelength and lowering the observed frequency below the emitted frequency, the observer hears a lower pitch.
