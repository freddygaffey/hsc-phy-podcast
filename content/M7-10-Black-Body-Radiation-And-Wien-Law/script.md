NARRATOR: So, what is a black body? The definition the syllabus wants is precise: a black body is a perfect, or ideal, absorber, an object that absorbs all electromagnetic radiation that falls on it, at every wavelength, reflecting and transmitting none. And here is the deep point, the one the dot-point about using a model to explain is testing: a perfect absorber is necessarily also a perfect emitter. Those are the same object. Let me explain why, because that "why" is the mark.

NARRATOR: Think about thermal equilibrium. Imagine an object sitting at a fixed temperature inside an enclosure at that same temperature. Its temperature is steady, so by conservation of energy it must be radiating away energy at exactly the same rate it absorbs it. Now suppose this object is a perfect absorber: it soaks up every bit of radiation that lands on it. To keep its temperature constant, it must re-emit energy at that same total rate, otherwise it would heat up without limit, which doesn't happen. So a perfect absorber must also be the most efficient possible emitter at every wavelength. If it absorbs the most, it must emit the most, or the books don't balance. A good absorber is a good emitter; a perfect absorber is a perfect emitter. That equilibrium argument is exactly what "use models to explain why theoretical black bodies are perfect absorbers and emitters" is asking for.

NARRATOR: Now, no real material is a perfect absorber, so how do we build one? With a clever trick: a cavity with a tiny hole. Picture a hollow box, or a furnace, with thick walls and one very small hole drilled in the side. Any radiation that goes in through the hole bounces around inside, reflecting off the inner walls again and again, and at each bounce a little more is absorbed, until essentially none of it ever finds its way back out through the tiny hole. So the hole behaves as a near-perfect absorber: radiation goes in, nothing comes back. And by the equilibrium argument we just made, that means the hole is also a near-perfect emitter. Heat the walls of the cavity to some temperature, and the radiation that streams out of the hole is black-body radiation, characteristic only of the temperature of the cavity, not of what the walls are made of. That material-independence is the whole reason the black body is such a powerful standard. Every black body at the same temperature emits exactly the same spectrum.

NARRATOR: And here's the trap that catches students every year, so let me kill it now. A black body is not black. The name is a disaster. It's called black because at room temperature it absorbs all light and reflects none, so it would look black to your eye. But heat it up and it glows; at high temperature a black body is blindingly bright. The Sun is very nearly a black body, and it's the opposite of black. So if an exam answer says "a black body absorbs everything and emits nothing," that's wrong and it's costly. A black body absorbs all incident radiation and emits a full, characteristic spectrum that gets brighter and bluer as it heats. Absorbs everything, emits everything. Never "emits nothing."

NARRATOR: Right, here is the crisis, the heart of the episode, the ultraviolet catastrophe. By the end of the eighteen-hundreds the experimental black-body curves were measured beautifully: rise, peak, fall, a nice hump. The problem was explaining them with the physics of the day. Classical physics, Maxwell's wave theory of light plus the thermodynamics of the time, was used to predict the shape of that curve. The prediction is sometimes called the Rayleigh-Jeans result, and it was a disaster. Classical theory said that as the wavelength got shorter, the emitted intensity should rise, and keep rising, without limit, heading to infinity at the short-wavelength, ultraviolet end. Let that sink in. The theory predicted that every hot object, your cup of coffee, the walls of this room, should be pouring out infinite energy at short wavelengths. That's not just wrong, it's impossible: it violates the conservation of energy, you can't radiate infinite power. And it flatly contradicted the experiment, which showed the intensity peaking and then falling back toward zero at short wavelengths, not blowing up. The classical curve and the experimental curve agree nicely at long wavelengths, then peel catastrophically apart at the short end, where the classical curve shoots to infinity and the real one rolls over and drops. That short-wavelength disaster is the ultraviolet catastrophe. I've laid the two curves side by side, classical prediction versus experiment, in Listing five.

NARRATOR: This is the textbook case of a theory failing against data, and the syllabus loves it as exactly that. When the exam asks you to compare the classical and experimental curves, your answer is: they agree at long wavelengths, but the classical prediction rises without limit toward short wavelengths, predicting infinite intensity in the ultraviolet, whereas the experimental curve peaks and then falls. The disagreement is total at short wavelengths, and a prediction of infinite energy is physically impossible, so the classical theory had to be wrong. A theory must answer to experiment; this one didn't.

NARRATOR: Enter Max Planck, nineteen hundred. Planck found a mathematical fix, and the fix accidentally founded quantum physics. Here's the idea. Classical physics assumed the oscillating charges in the walls of the black body could emit and absorb energy in any amount whatsoever, continuously, like pouring water, any quantity, however small. Planck made one radical assumption: that these oscillators can only emit or absorb energy in discrete lumps, quanta, and the size of one lump is Planck's constant times the frequency, the very expression you met last episode. Energy comes in packets of h times f, nothing in between. Now watch how that one assumption kills the catastrophe. High-frequency radiation, the short-wavelength ultraviolet end, requires large energy quanta, because the quantum size is h times f and f is large. But at a given temperature, the particles only have so much energy to go around, set by that distribution we discussed. A large quantum is only rarely available, statistically, there just isn't usually enough energy in one place to make a big ultraviolet quantum. So the high-frequency, short-wavelength emission is suppressed rather than infinite. It's choked off. And that's exactly what bends the predicted curve back down at short wavelengths, so it peaks and falls, matching the experiment perfectly. The full logic is set out step by step in Listing six.

NARRATOR: Here's the band-six answer, in full. Classical wave theory predicted that the intensity of black-body radiation should increase without limit as the wavelength decreased, approaching infinity at short, ultraviolet wavelengths, the ultraviolet catastrophe. This contradicted both the experimental curve, which peaks and then falls, and the law of conservation of energy, since infinite emitted energy is impossible. Planck resolved this by proposing that the oscillators in the black body can only emit or absorb energy in discrete quanta, each of size equal to Planck's constant times the frequency, rather than continuously. Because high-frequency, short-wavelength radiation requires large energy quanta, and such large quanta are only rarely available at a given temperature, high-frequency emission is strongly suppressed. This produces a curve that rises, peaks, and falls, in exact agreement with experiment, eliminating the ultraviolet catastrophe. Notice every move there: state the classical prediction, name the catastrophe, give the two reasons it's wrong, conservation of energy and the experiment, then introduce quantisation with the equation, then explain the suppression mechanism, then conclude that it matches experiment.
