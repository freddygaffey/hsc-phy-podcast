NARRATOR: Now the apparatus, because you have to be able to describe it and, crucially, explain what it measures. Picture an evacuated glass tube, vacuum inside so the electrons don't bump into air molecules. Inside are two metal electrodes. One is the clean metal surface we shine light on, call it the cathode or the emitter. Light comes in through a window and strikes it, and photoelectrons are ejected. Facing it across the tube is the second electrode, the collector. The ejected electrons fly across the gap, land on the collector, and complete a circuit, so a small current flows, which we measure with a sensitive ammeter. That current is called the photocurrent, and it exists only while the light shines. More electrons arriving per second means a bigger photocurrent. So far, so straightforward. The clever part is how we measure the electrons' energy. The full setup is described in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Here's the trick, and it's the conceptual heart of the apparatus. We apply a voltage across the tube, but backwards, a reverse voltage that pushes against the electrons, making the collector negative so it repels the arriving electrons. This is called the stopping voltage, or the stopping potential. As we wind this reverse voltage up, only the electrons with enough kinetic energy can fight their way across to the collector; the slower ones get turned back. Crank it higher and higher, and you reach a particular voltage where even the fastest, most energetic electron is just barely turned back, and the photocurrent drops to exactly zero. That voltage is the stopping voltage, and it is a direct measure of the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons. Why? Pure energy bookkeeping, and this is a direct cash-in of Module six, the very first episode, charged particles in electric fields. There, the energy a charge gains or loses crossing a potential difference is the charge times the voltage. Here it runs in reverse: the fastest electron is stopped exactly when the electrical energy it has to climb, the electron's charge times the stopping voltage, equals the kinetic energy it started with. So the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons equals the charge on the electron times the stopping voltage. Measure the stopping voltage, and you've measured the maximum kinetic energy. That relationship, with a worked number, is in Listing four.

NARRATOR: D is for no Delay. When you shine light of a high enough frequency on the metal, electrons are emitted essentially instantly, with no measurable time delay, the moment the light arrives. NARRATOR: E is for Energy from frequency. The maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons, how fast they come off, depends only on the frequency of the light, the colour, and not at all on its brightness. Shine higher-frequency light, bluer, more ultraviolet, and the electrons come off with more energy, a bigger stopping voltage. Make the same-colour light brighter, and the electron energy does not change one bit. Energy of the electrons is set by frequency. E, energy from frequency.

NARRATOR: N is for Number from intensity. Now brightness does control something: it controls how many electrons come out per second, the photocurrent. Intensity sets the number, not the energy. N, number from intensity.

NARRATOR: And F is for threshold Frequency. This is the killer. For each metal there is a particular cut-off frequency, called the threshold frequency, below which no electrons are emitted at all.
