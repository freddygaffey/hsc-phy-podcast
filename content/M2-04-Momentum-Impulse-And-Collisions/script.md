NARRATOR: Last episode we met energy and the principle that it's conserved. Today we meet a second great conserved quantity: momentum. Momentum is what governs collisions and explosions, why a heavy truck is so hard to stop, why an airbag saves your life, and why a rocket works. By the end you'll be able to calculate momentum and impulse, apply the conservation of momentum to collisions, and distinguish elastic from inelastic collisions, which is the most common conceptual trap in the topic. But first, a quick recap, because today's conservation law sits right alongside last episode's.

NARRATOR: Last episode, work and energy: kinetic energy is one half m v squared, and in the absence of friction mechanical energy is conserved, energy transforms but the total is constant, and we drilled "transformed, not destroyed". And reaching back to the first episode of the module, Newton's third law: forces come in equal and opposite pairs acting on different objects. Hold that third law especially, because it's the reason momentum is conserved: in a collision the two objects push on each other with equal and opposite forces, and that's exactly what makes their total momentum stay constant. Energy and momentum are the two conserved quantities of this module, and a collision question often needs both, momentum to find the velocities, energy to classify the collision. Today we add momentum to the toolkit.

QUESTION: Quick check before we start. From episode one, what does Newton's third law say, and on how many objects do the two forces act? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Newton's third law says that if object A exerts a force on object B, then B exerts an equal and opposite force on A, and the two forces act on different objects, one on each. Hold that, because in a collision those two equal and opposite forces act for the same time, producing equal and opposite changes in momentum that cancel, which is precisely why total momentum is conserved. Let's define momentum first.

NARRATOR: Momentum is mass in motion, quantitatively the product of an object's mass and its velocity: momentum equals m v, measured in kilogram metres per second. And crucially, momentum is a vector, it has the same direction as the velocity, so in one dimension it carries a sign, and the sign discipline from Module One matters enormously here. A two-thousand-kilogram truck at ten metres per second has a momentum of twenty thousand kilogram metres per second; a one-kilogram ball at ten metres per second has just ten. That's why the truck is so much harder to stop, it has vastly more momentum, even at the same speed. Momentum measures how much "oomph" a moving object has, how hard it is to bring to rest, combining both how much mass is moving and how fast.

NARRATOR: Now impulse, which is the change in momentum. When a force acts on an object for a time, it changes the object's momentum, and the change in momentum equals the force times the time it acts: impulse equals force times time, written delta p equals F delta t, also in kilogram metres per second, or equivalently newton seconds. This is really Newton's second law in disguise: force equals mass times acceleration, and acceleration is the change in velocity over time, so force times time equals mass times change in velocity, which is the change in momentum. And there's a graphical version, just like work was the area under a force-displacement graph: impulse is the area under a force-time graph. The impulse relationship is in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Here's why impulse matters in the real world, and it's a guaranteed exam application. The change in momentum is fixed by the situation, a car crash brings you from full speed to zero, that's a set change in momentum. Since impulse equals force times time, for a fixed change in momentum, if you extend the time over which the change happens, you reduce the force. That's the entire principle behind safety design. An airbag, a crumple zone, a cricketer's follow-through catching a ball, a high-jumper's crash mat, all of them extend the time of the momentum change, which lowers the peak force, and it's the force that does the damage. The memory hook: extend the time, cut the force. Same change in momentum, spread over longer, means a gentler force. That's why you bend your knees when you land, you lengthen the stopping time and reduce the force on your legs.

QUESTION: Let me have you use impulse. A two-hundred-gram ball travelling at fifteen metres per second is caught and brought to rest. If the catch takes zero point three seconds, what is the average force on the ball? Pause the player, work it out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. First the change in momentum: the mass is zero point two kilograms, two hundred grams, and the velocity changes from fifteen metres per second to zero, so the change in momentum is mass times change in velocity, zero point two times fifteen, three kilogram metres per second. Impulse equals force times time and also equals the change in momentum, so force equals the change in momentum divided by the time, three divided by zero point three, ten newtons. So the average force is ten newtons. Now here's the impulse insight: if you caught it more slowly, drawing your hands back to make the catch take, say, zero point six seconds instead, the same three kilogram metres per second of momentum change spread over double the time would halve the force to five newtons. Extend the time, cut the force, that's why you give with the ball when you catch it. The working is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Now the big principle: conservation of momentum. In a closed system, one with no external net force, the total momentum before an interaction equals the total momentum after. In symbols, the sum of m v before equals the sum of m v after. This is the master tool for every collision and explosion problem. And it follows directly from Newton's third law, as we said: during the collision the two objects exert equal and opposite forces on each other for the same time, so they receive equal and opposite impulses, equal and opposite changes in momentum, which cancel, leaving the total unchanged. Because momentum is a vector, you must include directions, with signs in one dimension: momentum to the right positive, to the left negative. Conservation of momentum lets you find an unknown velocity after a collision purely from the masses and velocities before, no forces or times needed.

NARRATOR: Let me make the link to Newton's third law explicit, because explaining why momentum is conserved is examinable, not just using it. During a collision, object A pushes on object B, and by Newton's third law B pushes back on A with an equal and opposite force. The two objects are in contact for the same length of time, so each experiences that force for the same duration. Impulse is force times time, so A and B receive equal and opposite impulses, equal and opposite changes in momentum. Whatever momentum A gains, B loses, and vice versa, so the changes cancel exactly and the total momentum of the two-object system stays constant. That's the proof: conservation of momentum is a direct consequence of Newton's third law plus the equal contact time. When an exam asks you to explain why momentum is conserved, that's the answer, equal and opposite forces, equal time, equal and opposite impulses, changes cancel.

NARRATOR: And the real-world applications are worth banking, because the syllabus likes them. Rocket and jet propulsion: the engine throws exhaust gas backward, and to conserve momentum the rocket is pushed forward, it works even in the vacuum of space because it doesn't push against anything external, it's pure momentum conservation. Vehicle safety: airbags, crumple zones, and seatbelts all extend the time of the momentum change in a crash to reduce the force on the occupant, the impulse principle. Sport: a golfer or batter follows through to keep the club or bat in contact longer, increasing the impulse and so the ball's change in momentum and final speed. Even walking and swimming are momentum exchange, you push the ground or water backward, and it pushes you forward. Every one of these is either conservation of momentum or the impulse relationship in action, and naming the principle is what earns the mark.

NARRATOR: Let me work the classic collision, two objects that stick together, because it's the cleanest. The full working is in Listing two. A one-thousand-kilogram car moving at twenty metres per second runs into a stationary one-thousand-five-hundred-kilogram car, and they lock together and move off as one. Find their common velocity. Conservation of momentum: total momentum before equals total momentum after. Before: the moving car has momentum one thousand times twenty, twenty thousand kilogram metres per second, and the stationary car has zero, so the total before is twenty thousand. After: the two move together as a combined mass of two thousand five hundred kilograms at some velocity v, so the momentum after is two thousand five hundred times v. Set them equal: twenty thousand equals two thousand five hundred v, so v is eight metres per second, in the original direction. The wreckage moves off at eight metres per second. Notice we used only masses and velocities, momentum conservation did it all.

NARRATOR: Conservation of momentum works just as well in reverse, for explosions and recoil, and that's worth seeing. Take a stationary system that flies apart: a gun firing a bullet, a person stepping off a stationary boat, a rocket ejecting exhaust. Before, the total momentum is zero, nothing's moving. So after, the total momentum must still be zero, which means the two pieces fly apart with equal and opposite momenta. A two-kilogram rifle and a ten-gram bullet: if the bullet leaves at four hundred metres per second forward, its momentum is zero point zero one times four hundred, four kilogram metres per second forward, so the rifle must recoil with four kilogram metres per second backward, giving a recoil speed of four over two, two metres per second backward. The bullet and rifle have equal and opposite momenta, summing to the original zero. That's recoil, and it's why a rifle kicks, why a rocket is pushed forward as it throws gas backward, and why you drift backward stepping off a boat. Momentum conservation, starting from zero.

NARRATOR: A note on the force-time graph, because it's examinable and mirrors last episode. Impulse is the area under a force-time graph, just as work was the area under a force-displacement graph and displacement was the area under a velocity-time graph. So if you're given a graph of how the force during a collision varies with time, a spike that rises and falls, the area under that spike is the total impulse, the total change in momentum. For a constant force it's a simple rectangle, force times time; for a varying force you find the area of the shape. This is how real collisions are analysed, the force isn't constant during a crash, but its area over time still equals the change in momentum. Area under force-time equals impulse, the same "area under the graph" technique yet again.

NARRATOR: One more point on the vector nature, because collisions aren't always in a line. Momentum is conserved in every direction independently. In a two-dimensional collision, like two pucks glancing off each other on an air table, you conserve momentum separately in the horizontal direction and in the vertical direction, resolving each velocity into components, exactly the resolve-into-components method from Module One. At this level most problems are one-dimensional, where direction is just a sign, but know that the principle is fully vector: the total momentum vector is conserved, which means each component is conserved. So a stationary object struck off-centre, and the striker, share the momentum such that the sideways components cancel and the forward components add to the original, all bookkept as vectors.

NARRATOR: Now the distinction that costs the most marks in this topic: elastic versus inelastic collisions. In every collision, in a closed system, momentum is conserved, always, both kinds. The difference is about kinetic energy. In an elastic collision, kinetic energy is also conserved, the total kinetic energy after equals the total before; think of ideal billiard balls or atoms bouncing. In an inelastic collision, kinetic energy is not conserved, some of it is transformed into heat, sound, and deforming the objects, so the total kinetic energy after is less than before; the two cars sticking together is perfectly inelastic, the most kinetic energy is lost. The key exam statement: momentum is conserved in both elastic and inelastic collisions, but kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions. That one sentence is the heart of the topic, and the comparison is in Listing four.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. In the car collision we just did, where the cars stick together, is momentum conserved, and is kinetic energy conserved? Pause the player, answer both with reasons, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Momentum is conserved, because it's a closed system with no external net force, that's why we could use conservation of momentum to find the eight metres per second. But kinetic energy is not conserved, because the cars stick together, this is a perfectly inelastic collision, and a large amount of kinetic energy is transformed into heat, sound, and the work of crumpling and deforming the metal. If you check the numbers, the kinetic energy after is much less than before, the missing energy went into damage, which is exactly what a crumple zone is designed to do, absorb kinetic energy. So: momentum conserved, kinetic energy not, because it's inelastic. If you said both are conserved, that's the classic trap, kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions, and a sticking-together collision is the opposite of elastic. Momentum always, kinetic energy only if elastic.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "compare elastic and inelastic collisions". The band-six version: in both elastic and inelastic collisions, momentum is conserved, because in a closed system there is no external net force, and by Newton's third law the internal forces are equal and opposite. The difference lies in kinetic energy: in an elastic collision, total kinetic energy is also conserved; in an inelastic collision, kinetic energy is not conserved, as some is transformed into other forms such as heat, sound, and deformation. A perfectly inelastic collision, where the objects move off together, loses the maximum kinetic energy consistent with conserving momentum. The marks are for stating momentum is conserved in both, kinetic energy only in elastic, and explaining where the energy goes in an inelastic collision. State the similarity and the difference, that's what "compare" demands.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the gap. A weak answer says "in an elastic collision things bounce and in an inelastic collision they stick". That earns little: it doesn't mention momentum being conserved in both, doesn't say kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic, and doesn't explain where the energy goes. The strong answer names both conservation laws and which applies when. Momentum both, kinetic energy elastic only, energy transformed in inelastic. That's the difference between bands.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: thinking momentum isn't conserved in an inelastic collision, it is, it's conserved in every closed-system collision. Trap two: thinking kinetic energy is conserved in all collisions, it's conserved only in elastic ones. Trap three: dropping the vector nature of momentum, in one dimension assign signs for direction, especially when objects move toward each other. Trap four: confusing impulse with force, impulse is force times time, the change in momentum, not the force itself. Trap five: forgetting that "closed system, no external net force" is the condition for momentum conservation. Avoid those and collisions are routine.

NARRATOR: A word on strategy, because students mix up when to use momentum and when to use energy. For any collision or explosion, reach for conservation of momentum first, it always holds in a closed system and it directly gives you the unknown velocities, regardless of whether the collision is elastic or inelastic. Use energy second, and only carefully: kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions, so you can't assume it, instead you compute the kinetic energy before and after to classify the collision or to find how much energy was transformed. So the rule of thumb: momentum to find the velocities, kinetic energy to classify the collision or find the energy lost. Never start a collision problem by assuming kinetic energy is conserved unless you're told the collision is elastic, that assumption is the single most common error, because most real collisions are inelastic. Momentum is the reliable tool; kinetic energy is the diagnostic. And the two together, momentum to solve, energy to classify, are the full method for any collision question the exam can pose.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the threads together. Today's conservation of momentum follows from Newton's third law of episode one, and it stands beside conservation of energy from last episode, the two pillars of mechanics. A full collision analysis often uses both: momentum conservation to find the velocities after, then a kinetic energy comparison to classify the collision as elastic or inelastic. Impulse, force times time, is the time-integrated version of Newton's second law, just as the work-energy theorem was the distance-integrated version, a lovely symmetry. Forward, conservation of momentum becomes one of the deepest principles in Module Eight: it's why the two photons from matter-antimatter annihilation fly off in exactly opposite directions, to conserve the near-zero total momentum, and it's the logic Chadwick used to deduce the neutron and Pauli used to predict the neutrino. The conservation laws you build here run all the way to the subatomic world.

NARRATOR: Let me recap the takeaways, phrased for marks. One: momentum is mass times velocity, a vector in kilogram metres per second, so direction and sign matter. Two: impulse is the change in momentum, equal to force times time, delta p equals F delta t, and the area under a force-time graph; extend the time to cut the force, the principle of airbags and crumple zones. Three: in a closed system, total momentum is conserved, the sum of m v before equals the sum after, which follows from Newton's third law. Four: momentum is conserved in all collisions, but kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions; in inelastic collisions kinetic energy is transformed to heat, sound and deformation. Hold those and you can handle any collision problem.

QUESTION: Question one. Define momentum and impulse, and state the unit of each. Two marks. Pause the player, write your answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Momentum is the product of an object's mass and its velocity, a vector quantity, measured in kilogram metres per second. Impulse is the change in momentum of an object, equal to the force applied multiplied by the time for which it acts, measured in kilogram metres per second, or equivalently newton seconds. Two marks: momentum as m v with its unit; impulse as the change in momentum, force times time, with its unit. The trap is defining impulse as just "a force", it's force times time, the change in momentum.

QUESTION: Question two. A zero point five kilogram ball travelling at eight metres per second is struck and rebounds straight back at six metres per second. Calculate the impulse delivered to the ball. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing three. Momentum is a vector, so take the initial direction as positive. The initial momentum is zero point five times eight, four kilogram metres per second. The ball rebounds, so its final velocity is in the opposite direction, negative: zero point five times minus six, minus three kilogram metres per second. The impulse is the change in momentum, final minus initial, minus three minus four, which is minus seven kilogram metres per second, that is, seven kilogram metres per second in the direction of the rebound. Three marks: assigning opposite signs for the reversal; computing final minus initial; and the answer of seven kilogram metres per second. The trap is treating the rebound velocity as positive, that gives one instead of seven; the reversal of direction is the whole point, and it's why the impulse is larger than you'd expect.

QUESTION: Question three. A seventy-kilogram person jumps and lands, changing their momentum by four hundred and twenty kilogram metres per second as they stop. Explain, using impulse, why bending the knees on landing reduces the risk of injury. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The change in momentum on landing is fixed at four hundred and twenty kilogram metres per second regardless of how you land. Since impulse equals force times time and also equals the change in momentum, the force depends on the time over which the momentum change occurs: force equals the change in momentum divided by the time. Bending the knees increases the time taken to stop, so for the same change in momentum the average force is reduced, lowering the risk of injury. Three marks: the change in momentum is fixed; impulse equals force times time; increasing the time reduces the force. This is the airbag and crumple-zone principle, extend the time, cut the force.

QUESTION: Question four. A three-kilogram trolley moving at four metres per second collides with a one-kilogram stationary trolley, and they stick together. Calculate their common velocity after the collision, and state whether kinetic energy is conserved. Four marks. Pause, work it fully, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By conservation of momentum, total momentum before equals total momentum after. Before: the three-kilogram trolley has momentum three times four, twelve kilogram metres per second, and the one-kilogram trolley has zero, so the total is twelve. After: they move together as four kilograms at velocity v, so the momentum is four v. Set equal: twelve equals four v, so v is three metres per second in the original direction. Now kinetic energy: before, it's one half times three times four squared, twenty-four joules; after, one half times four times three squared, eighteen joules. Eighteen is less than twenty-four, so kinetic energy is not conserved, this is an inelastic collision, and the six joules difference was transformed into heat, sound and deformation. Four marks: momentum conservation set up; common velocity three metres per second; kinetic energy compared before and after; and the conclusion that it's not conserved because the collision is inelastic. The sticking-together is the giveaway that it's inelastic.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A sixty-kilogram skater moving at three metres per second collides with a stationary forty-kilogram skater. After the collision the sixty-kilogram skater continues forward at one metre per second. Calculate the velocity of the forty-kilogram skater, and determine whether the collision is elastic or inelastic. Five marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. By conservation of momentum, total before equals total after. Before: sixty times three plus forty times zero, one hundred and eighty kilogram metres per second. After: sixty times one, sixty, plus forty times the unknown velocity v. Set equal: one hundred and eighty equals sixty plus forty v, so forty v equals one hundred and twenty, v is three metres per second forward for the forty-kilogram skater. Now classify it: kinetic energy before is one half times sixty times three squared, two hundred and seventy joules. Kinetic energy after is one half times sixty times one squared, thirty joules, plus one half times forty times three squared, one hundred and eighty joules, totalling two hundred and ten joules. Two hundred and ten is less than two hundred and seventy, so kinetic energy is not conserved; the collision is inelastic, with sixty joules transformed into other forms. Five marks: momentum conservation set up with the stationary skater; the forty-kilogram skater's velocity of three metres per second; kinetic energy before; kinetic energy after; and the conclusion that it's inelastic because kinetic energy decreased. Notice the method: momentum conservation gives the unknown velocity, then comparing kinetic energy before and after classifies the collision. That two-step is the full collision analysis.
