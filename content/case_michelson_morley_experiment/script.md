NARRATOR: In the summer of eighteen eighty-seven, in the basement of a college dormitory in Cleveland, Ohio, two American scientists built the most precise measuring instrument that had ever existed. They floated a slab of sandstone weighing about a tonne and a half on a pool of liquid mercury, so it could turn without a tremor. They bounced a beam of light back and forth until it travelled eleven metres through the air. And they aimed all of that delicate machinery at a single, simple question. A question that everybody already knew the answer to. They expected to confirm something obvious — to measure a number that the best physics of the nineteenth century said simply had to be there. Instead, they got nothing. The number was zero. They had built a machine sensitive enough to catch one fortieth of the signal they were looking for, and it caught nothing at all.

NARRATOR: One of them, Albert Michelson, considered it the great failure of his career. He had wanted to detect something invisible that fills all of space — and he had failed. But that failure, that beautiful, stubborn, impossible zero, would crack open the foundations of physics. Within twenty years it would help a clerk in a Swiss patent office throw out an idea that had stood for two hundred years, and rewrite our understanding of space and time itself. This is the story of the most important failed experiment in the history of science.

QUESTION: So what was the thing they were looking for? What invisible substance did the whole of physics depend on?

NARRATOR: To answer that, we have to go back to a comfortable, confident idea that almost every physicist in the eighteen hundreds believed without question. It had to do with waves. Think about every wave you have ever met. Sound is a wave — and sound travels through air. Knock on a table and the wave travels through the wood. Drop a stone in a pond, and ripples spread across the water. In every single case, the wave is a disturbance moving through some material. The air, the wood, the water. Take the material away, and the wave cannot exist. There is no sound in the vacuum of space, because there is no air to carry it. A wave, it seemed, was always a wave in something.

NARRATOR: And then there was light. By the eighteen sixties, the Scottish physicist James Clerk Maxwell had done something magnificent. He had taken everything that was known about electricity and magnetism and compressed it into a handful of equations. And buried inside those equations was a number — the speed at which an electric and magnetic disturbance would ripple through space. When Maxwell calculated that number, it came out to about three hundred thousand kilometres per second. Which was exactly the measured speed of light. This was no coincidence. Light, Maxwell realised, was an electromagnetic wave. A ripple in the electric and magnetic fields, sailing through space.

NARRATOR: But that raised the obvious question. If light is a wave — a wave in what? Sound waves in air, water waves in water, light waves in . . . what? Physicists had an answer ready, and it had a beautiful name. The luminiferous aether. Luminiferous means light-bearing. The aether was imagined as a transparent, weightless, perfectly elastic substance filling every corner of the universe. It was in the empty space between the stars. It was in the gap between you and this very moment. It passed straight through solid walls and through your own body. You could not see it, weigh it, or feel it — but it had to be there, because light needed something to wave in. And here is the crucial part. The aether was assumed to be the one thing in the universe that was truly, absolutely at rest. It was the fixed stage on which everything else moved. The ultimate frame of reference. The still backdrop against which you could finally measure your true motion through the cosmos.

QUESTION: If the aether is sitting perfectly still, and the Earth is hurtling through it, shouldn't we be able to feel ourselves moving through it somehow?

NARRATOR: That is exactly the thought that made the whole thing testable. Picture yourself on a motorcycle on a perfectly still day, no wind at all. As you speed up, you feel a wind in your face — but it is not the air moving, it is you moving through the air. The faster you ride, the stronger that apparent wind. Now, the Earth is not standing still. It is racing around the Sun in its orbit at about thirty kilometres every second. That is a hundred thousand kilometres per hour. So if the aether is sitting still and the Earth is ploughing through it at thirty kilometres a second, there should be an aether wind — a steady stream of aether blowing past us, just as a rider feels a wind on a calm day. And that aether wind should have a measurable effect on light.

NARRATOR: Here is how. The speed of light, in this picture, is the speed of light relative to the aether. So if you shine a beam of light in the same direction the Earth is moving — downwind, so to speak — that light has to fight against the oncoming aether wind. Relative to you, standing on the Earth, it should travel a little slower, at the speed of light minus the speed of the Earth. And light sent the opposite way, with the wind at its back, should come back a little faster, at the speed of light plus the speed of the Earth. There should be a difference. A tiny difference, but a real one. And if you could measure that difference, you could measure the speed of the Earth through the aether. You could measure your absolute motion through the universe. That was the prize.

QUESTION: But the difference is tiny — the Earth's speed is only one ten-thousandth of the speed of light. How could anyone possibly measure something that small?

NARRATOR: That is the heart of the whole story, and it brings us to our hero. Albert Abraham Michelson was born in December eighteen fifty-two in Strelno, in Prussia — a town that is now Strzelno, in Poland. His family was Jewish, and when he was just two or three years old they emigrated to the United States, eventually settling out west during the gold rush years. Michelson was sharp, ambitious, and almost impossibly stubborn. He talked his way into the United States Naval Academy, where he trained from eighteen sixty-nine. And there he found his lifelong obsession — measuring the speed of light. Not roughly. Not approximately. With a precision that bordered on the fanatical. Michelson was the kind of man who could not leave a question alone. He once suffered a nervous collapse from the sheer intensity of his work and had to take time to recover. He was temperamental, difficult, brilliant — and utterly devoted to the idea that if you measured carefully enough, nature would tell you the truth.

NARRATOR: Michelson realised he did not need to measure the speed of light twice and compare the two numbers directly. That would never be precise enough. Instead, he would use the most sensitive trick known to physics — interference. He would let light interfere with itself. And to do that he invented an instrument so elegant that versions of it are still being used today, nearly a century and a half later. It is called the Michelson interferometer.

QUESTION: How does an interferometer actually work? How do you make light compete against itself?

NARRATOR: Let me walk you through it, because the idea is gorgeous. You start with a single beam of light. You send it at a special mirror called a beam splitter — it is a piece of glass, half-silvered, set at forty-five degrees. When the light hits it, half of the beam passes straight through, and half of it reflects off at a right angle. So now you have two beams, heading off in two perpendicular directions. Call them the two arms of the instrument. Each beam travels down its arm to a mirror at the far end, bounces off, and comes straight back to the beam splitter. And at the beam splitter, the two beams recombine and merge back into one, which travels on to a detector — in eighteen eighty-seven, the detector was simply Michelson's eye, looking through a small telescope.

NARRATOR: Now here is the magic. Light is a wave, with crests and troughs, like ripples on water. When the two beams recombine, their waves add together. If the two arms are exactly the same length, and light travels at exactly the same speed in both, then the two waves come back perfectly in step — crest lining up with crest. They reinforce each other, and you see a bright band of light. But if one beam takes even a tiny bit longer to make its round trip — even a fraction of a single wavelength longer — then the crests no longer line up. The waves fall out of step, and where they cancel out you see a dark band. The result is a pattern of light and dark stripes, called interference fringes. And the position of those fringes is exquisitely sensitive. Shift the timing of one beam by just half a wavelength of light — a distance of about two hundred and fifty billionths of a metre — and a bright fringe slides over to where a dark one used to be. This is a ruler that can measure a difference of a fraction of a wavelength of light. Nothing else in the nineteenth century came close.

NARRATOR: And now you can see the plan. Point one arm of the interferometer along the direction the Earth is moving — into the aether wind. Point the other arm sideways, across the wind. According to aether theory, the beam fighting up and down the wind and the beam crossing the wind should take slightly different times to complete their round trips. That difference would show up as a particular fringe pattern. Then — and this is the clever part — slowly rotate the entire instrument by ninety degrees. Now the arms swap places. The arm that was pointing into the wind is pointing across it, and vice versa. The time difference should reverse. And as it reverses, the fringes should visibly shift, sliding across the field of view. Watch the fringes move as you turn the apparatus, measure how far they move, and you have measured the aether wind. You have measured the motion of the Earth through the fabric of space.

QUESTION: And how big a shift were they expecting? How many fringes should move?

NARRATOR: This is where the numbers get beautiful, and the full calculation is in Listing one of the supplementary materials. With light of wavelength about five hundred nanometres, an effective arm length of eleven metres, the Earth moving at thirty kilometres a second, and light moving at three hundred thousand kilometres a second, the expected shift came out to about four tenths of a fringe. Nearly half a fringe should slide across the view as the instrument turned. Now, four tenths of a fringe is a small thing. But Michelson and Morley believed their instrument could detect a shift as small as one hundredth of a fringe — possibly even smaller. They were looking for a signal forty times larger than the smallest thing they could see. There was no way they could miss it. If the aether wind was there, it would announce itself loudly.

NARRATOR: But Michelson could not do this alone. The instrument needed to be bigger and steadier than anything he had built before, and he needed a partner — someone with a superb laboratory and the rare ability to actually understand what he was doing. He found that man in Edward Williams Morley.

NARRATOR: Morley was, on paper, the unlikeliest collaborator imaginable. He was born in eighteen thirty-seven into a New England family of ministers. He graduated top of his class from Williams College, trained at a theological seminary, and was ordained as a Congregational minister. For years he preached from a pulpit in Twinsburg, Ohio. But Morley had a second love — chemistry, and meticulous, obsessive measurement. By around eighteen eighty he had moved fully into science, taking a post at Western Reserve University in Cleveland, where he was said to be just about the only scientist for hundreds of miles capable of following Michelson's reasoning. The two men could not have been more different — a Polish-Jewish immigrant naval officer and a New England Protestant clergyman. They had met after both attended lectures by the great Lord Kelvin in Baltimore in the summer of eighteen eighty-four, and they discovered they shared a passion for getting the measurement exactly right, down to the last decimal place. They also shared a love of music. It was enough. The partnership was set.

QUESTION: So how do you build an instrument precise enough to catch four tenths of a fringe, when a passing truck or a footstep could ruin everything?

NARRATOR: With extraordinary, almost paranoid care. And fate gave them an unexpected hand. In October eighteen eighty-six, the main building of Michelson's own institution, the Case School of Applied Science, burned to the ground. The fire forced the experiment to relocate to the basement of Adelbert Hall, a dormitory at Morley's Western Reserve University. As it turned out, the basement was a gift — buried below ground level, it was far more isolated from vibration than any upper floor could have been.

NARRATOR: The centrepiece was a great square slab of sandstone, about a metre and a half on each side. On top of it they mounted the mirrors and the beam splitter. The whole stone floated in a circular trough filled with liquid mercury. Floating the apparatus on mercury did two things, both essential. First, it isolated the instrument from vibration almost perfectly — no footstep, no passing cart, no rumble of the city could shake it. Second, it meant one person could set the entire heavy stone arrangement turning with a single gentle push, and it would glide around smoothly and continuously, like a record on a turntable, taking minutes to complete a slow rotation. As it turned, the observer would walk slowly around with it, eye to the telescope, watching the fringes.

NARRATOR: And to get the eleven metres of light path, they used a trick. The stone slab was only a metre and a half across — you cannot fit eleven metres of straight beam on that. So they covered the slab with mirrors and folded the light path, bouncing each beam back and forth across the slab several times before sending it back to the centre. By folding the path, they multiplied the effective length, and the longer the path, the bigger the expected fringe shift. Every component was crafted by the finest makers in the region — the lenses and mirrors from John Brashear, a celebrated instrument-maker in Pittsburgh, the precision metalwork from Cleveland machinists. Some of the funding came from a research grant from the American Association for the Advancement of Science. This was the cutting edge of nineteenth-century engineering, all aimed at a single number.

QUESTION: So in the summer of eighteen eighty-seven, they finally switched it on. What happened?

NARRATOR: In July of eighteen eighty-seven, they ran the experiment. The sessions were concentrated over just a few days — the eighth, ninth, eleventh and twelfth of July. They observed near noon and again near six in the evening, to catch the aether wind blowing from different directions as the Earth turned. They made thirty-six complete rotations of the apparatus in total. Two observers took readings independently to guard against any one person fooling themselves. Michelson would press the stone, set it slowly turning, and watch through the telescope as the fringes drifted across his field of view. Again and again, around and around.

NARRATOR: And the fringes . . . barely moved. They expected four tenths of a fringe. What they saw was a flicker — a trembling at the level of the instrument's own noise. The fringes wandered by perhaps two hundredths of a fringe at the very most, and on average far less than that. Where there should have been a clear, marching, unmistakable shift of nearly half a fringe as the instrument turned through the aether wind — there was essentially nothing. The signal they had built this magnificent machine to detect simply was not there.

NARRATOR: In their paper, published in November eighteen eighty-seven in the American Journal of Science, they reported it with careful, devastating precision. The actual displacement, they wrote, was certainly less than the twentieth part of the expected amount, and probably less than the fortieth part. Certainly less than a twentieth. Probably less than a fortieth. They had a ruler that could read down to a hundredth of a fringe, and pointed at the aether wind, it read zero.

NARRATOR: Michelson's own words from that summer survive, in a letter he wrote to the great English physicist Lord Rayleigh in August of eighteen eighty-seven. The experiments on the relative motion of the Earth and aether, he wrote, have been completed, and the result is decidedly negative. Decidedly negative. There was no triumph in it. Michelson had set out to detect the aether, to measure the Earth's motion through it — and he had found nothing. To him, this felt like failure. He had built the most sensitive instrument in the world and it had refused to give him the answer he wanted. The paper ended with a plan to repeat the experiment every three months, to be absolutely sure no uncertainty remained. They never systematically did. Within two years the partnership had dissolved.

QUESTION: So what does it mean? If the aether wind isn't there, where did it go?

NARRATOR: This is the moment to slow down, because the result is more subtle than people often think. The null result did not, by itself, prove that the aether does not exist. What it proved was narrower and stranger. It proved that the Earth is not moving through the aether at its orbital speed. And that is genuinely bizarre. The Earth is unquestionably moving — it is racing around the Sun. So if there is a stationary aether out there, the Earth must be ploughing through it. The experiment was sensitive enough to catch one fortieth of the expected effect. It caught nothing. It was as though the aether wind simply did not exist. As though, no matter which way you pointed your light beam, no matter how the Earth moved, light always travelled at exactly the same speed. And that should have been impossible.

NARRATOR: The physics community did not immediately leap to the radical conclusion. Most physicists assumed something more comfortable. Perhaps the apparatus was somehow flawed. Perhaps the Earth dragged a little blanket of aether along with it, so that near the ground there was no wind. The theory of the aether was two hundred years in the making — you do not abandon it because of one strange result. You patch it. And the most ingenious patch came from an Irish physicist named George FitzGerald.

QUESTION: How do you explain away a result this clean? How do you save the aether?

NARRATOR: FitzGerald, working at Trinity College Dublin, published a brief letter in the journal Science in May of eighteen eighty-nine. It was just a paragraph long, but it contained a startling idea. What if, FitzGerald suggested, any object moving through the aether physically shrinks in the direction of its motion? What if the aether wind itself squeezes matter, compressing it ever so slightly along the line of travel? Now think about what that would do to the interferometer. The arm pointing into the aether wind — the one where light should have taken longer — gets physically shortened by exactly the right amount. The shorter arm cancels out the slower light. The two effects balance perfectly, the round trips come out equal, and the fringes do not shift. The null result is explained. And the contraction is undetectable, because your measuring rulers shrink right along with everything else — you can never catch the contraction by measuring it, since your ruler is shrinking too.

NARRATOR: A Dutch physicist named Hendrik Lorentz arrived at the very same idea independently, a few years later, and he was a far more thorough mathematician than FitzGerald. Lorentz worked the idea out in detail. He found the exact contraction factor needed — it depends on the ratio of your speed to the speed of light, through a quantity we now call gamma, the Lorentz factor. The full expression is in Listing three of the supplementary materials. Over the eighteen nineties and into the nineteen hundreds, Lorentz built an increasingly elaborate mathematical machine to make the aether survive. He introduced something he called local time — a kind of mathematical bookkeeping time for moving observers. By nineteen oh four he had a complete set of equations, the Lorentz transformations, that described how measurements of space and time change between a stationary frame and a moving one, in just the right way to keep the speed of light looking constant and to keep Maxwell's equations intact.

QUESTION: So Lorentz basically had the right equations. Did he solve it? Did he understand what was happening?

NARRATOR: He had the equations. But he did not have the meaning. And this is one of the most fascinating turns in the whole history of physics. For Lorentz, the aether was still real. The contraction was a real, physical squeezing of matter, caused by the aether pressing on it as it moved. The local time was a mathematical fiction — a convenient trick. Only the aether's time, he believed, was the true time. Lorentz had assembled all the right machinery, the exact transformation equations that special relativity uses to this day, but he had bolted them onto the old picture. He was holding the key in his hand and using it to prop the old door shut. It took someone willing to throw the whole picture away to see what the equations were really saying.

NARRATOR: That someone was a twenty-six-year-old patent clerk in Bern, Switzerland, named Albert Einstein.

QUESTION: What did Einstein do that Lorentz and FitzGerald couldn't?

NARRATOR: In nineteen oh five — his miracle year, in which he published four papers that each could have made him famous — Einstein produced a paper titled On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies. And in it he did something audacious. He did not try to explain the null result. He did not invent a force to shrink rulers or a fiction to fix the clocks. Instead, he started over from two simple statements, and demanded the universe obey them. These are Einstein's two postulates, and they are written out in Listing four. The first postulate is the principle of relativity — the laws of physics are exactly the same in every inertial frame of reference. That is, for anyone moving at constant velocity, the laws are identical. There is no experiment you can do, sealed inside a smoothly moving train or ship, that tells you whether you are moving or at rest. The second postulate is the radical one. The speed of light in a vacuum is the same for every observer, no matter how fast the source is moving, and no matter how fast the observer is moving.

NARRATOR: Sit with that second postulate for a moment, because it defies all common sense. If I am standing still and I measure a light beam, I get three hundred thousand kilometres a second. If you are flying toward that same light beam at half the speed of light, common sense says you should measure it coming at you faster. You do not. You measure exactly the same three hundred thousand kilometres a second. The speed of light refuses to add or subtract the way ordinary velocities do. It is simply a fixed feature of the universe, the same for everyone.

NARRATOR: And here is the breathtaking part. If you accept those two postulates, the Lorentz transformations — the exact same equations Lorentz had laboured over — fall out automatically, as a matter of pure logic. Length contraction comes out of it. Time dilation comes out of it. The constancy of the speed of light comes out of it. But now they mean something completely different. For Einstein, there is no aether. There is no fixed stage, no absolute rest, no special frame. Length contraction is not the aether squeezing matter — it is a consequence of the geometry of space and time itself, and it is symmetric, so each observer sees the other's ruler as shortened. And the Michelson-Morley null result is no longer a mystery to be explained away. It is the most natural thing in the world. Of course the fringes did not shift. Of course there was no aether wind. The speed of light is the same in every direction for every observer — that is the second postulate. And here the first postulate does its quiet work too. Because all inertial frames of reference are equivalent, there is no privileged frame, no aether at rest to move against — so it simply cannot matter which way you point the apparatus. Turn the instrument however you like, and the light keeps the same speed in every arm. The two postulates together guarantee that no fringe shift can ever appear. The experiment was not a failure. It was a glimpse of one of the deepest truths about reality.

QUESTION: Did Einstein actually base his theory on the Michelson-Morley experiment? Was that the spark?

NARRATOR: This is one of the great myths of physics, and it is worth setting straight. Generations of textbooks have told a tidy story — Michelson and Morley find the null result, Einstein reads about it, and is inspired to invent relativity. A straight line from one to the other. But historians of science no longer accept that line. Einstein's nineteen oh five paper does not even cite the Michelson-Morley experiment. He arrived at relativity mainly through theory — through a deep discomfort with the way Maxwell's electromagnetism and Newton's mechanics refused to fit together. He thought about what it would mean to chase a beam of light, to ride alongside it. He pointed to experiments on light moving through flowing water, and to the way starlight appears to shift as the Earth moves, as his supporting evidence — not to Michelson and Morley. Whether he even knew of the experiment clearly before nineteen oh five is genuinely debated; he may have absorbed it indirectly through Lorentz's writings, but later in life he could not clearly remember when he first learned of it. The connection between the experiment and the theory was, to a large degree, stitched together afterwards, by people teaching the story. The truth is messier, and more interesting. The experiment did not cause the theory. But it stands as the single most powerful piece of evidence that the theory is right.

QUESTION: So what happened to Michelson? Did he ever come to see his failure as a triumph?

NARRATOR: The aftermath has a bittersweet symmetry to it. The partnership with Morley broke apart almost immediately. Michelson was in bitter conflict with the trustees of Case School over his research budget — he complained that they had spent the school's money on land and buildings rather than on professors and research. By eighteen eighty-nine he had left for Clark University, and the famous collaboration was over. Morley, offered a job elsewhere, was persuaded to stay in Cleveland.

NARRATOR: Michelson went on to a brilliant career, becoming the first head of physics at the new University of Chicago, and spending decades doing what he loved most — measuring the speed of light with ever-greater precision. In nineteen oh seven he became the first American ever to win a Nobel Prize in the sciences. But here is the irony. He won it for his precision optical instruments and his measurements, not specifically for the famous aether experiment. And by all accounts, Michelson remained uncomfortable that the great null result had come to define his legacy. He had wanted to find the aether. He went to his grave in nineteen thirty-one — his very last measurements of the speed of light, completed just before he died, landed within about one kilometre per second of the modern accepted value — and he is said to have never fully embraced the revolution his failure had launched.

NARRATOR: There is a strange coda, too. After Morley retired, another physicist, Dayton Miller, kept running interferometer experiments for decades, up on Mount Wilson in California. And Miller claimed — repeatedly, and seriously — that he could see a small aether-drift signal after all. For a time, in the nineteen twenties, this caused real alarm. Einstein himself reportedly remarked that if Miller's results were correct, then his own theory was wrong. But careful re-analysis, completed decades later, showed that Miller's tiny signal was an illusion — caused by minute differences in temperature across his apparatus, which made one arm expand a hair more than the other and mimicked a faint fringe shift. It is a humbling lesson in just how hard it is to chase a systematic error out of a precision experiment. The null result held.

NARRATOR: And the legacy of that null result is staggering. The Lorentz transformations, which Einstein reinterpreted, became the bedrock of all of modern physics — every relativistic calculation, the equivalence of mass and energy, the behaviour of particles in accelerators, all of it rests on that foundation. Modern versions of the Michelson-Morley experiment, using lasers and resonant cavities, have now confirmed that the speed of light is the same in all directions to a precision of better than one part in a hundred thousand trillion. And Michelson's beautiful interferometer design — the beam splitter, the two arms, the recombining beams, the interference fringes — did not fade into history. It became the heart of LIGO, the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory, with arms four kilometres long. In twenty fifteen, that descendant of Michelson's instrument detected ripples in space-time itself — gravitational waves from two black holes colliding over a billion light-years away. The instrument built to find the aether and failed went on, a century later, to hear the universe ringing.

NARRATOR: That is the deepest lesson of this story. Michelson and Morley set out to measure something everyone was sure was there, and they found nothing — a clean, undeniable nothing. And in science, a sufficiently precise nothing can be worth more than a thousand confirmations. Their zero did not fail to answer the question. It answered a far bigger question than they ever thought to ask. It told us there is no aether, no absolute rest, no fixed stage in the cosmos. The speed of light is woven into the structure of space and time itself. A failure, yes. The most important failure in the history of physics.

NARRATOR: For your New South Wales Higher School Certificate Physics course, this story sits at the heart of the nature of light, in the section on light and special relativity. The syllabus asks you to outline Einstein's first and second postulates of special relativity. State them in the exact words the markers expect. One postulate is that all inertial frames of reference are equivalent. The other is that the speed of light in a vacuum is an absolute constant. Different textbooks number these two in opposite orders, so what matters in the exam is that you state both precisely, not which one you call first. The syllabus also asks you to analyse the evidence supporting the theory of relativity, and the Michelson-Morley experiment is a centrepiece of that evidence, alongside the muon lifetime measurements and atomic clocks carried on aircraft. The null result is consistent with, and serves as powerful support for, the constancy of the speed of light in all inertial frames. And here is the exam-ready point worth memorising. Einstein's first postulate is what makes sense of the null result on rotation. Because every inertial frame is equivalent, there is no aether to move through, so it cannot matter which direction you point the apparatus, and no change in the interference pattern should appear when you turn it. Just remember the careful framing. The experiment was designed to detect the aether, not to test special relativity, which did not yet exist. It did not prove Einstein right in eighteen eighty-seven. But its stubborn, beautiful zero is exactly the result Einstein's postulates demand — and that is why, more than a century later, we are still telling the story of the experiment that failed, and changed everything.
