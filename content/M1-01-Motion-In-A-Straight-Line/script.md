NARRATOR: Welcome to Module One, Kinematics, and the very first episode of the course. Kinematics is the physics of describing motion, how things move, without yet worrying about what causes the motion, that's next module, Dynamics, with forces. Today we lay the foundation the entire course is built on, and it's deceptively simple: the four quantities we use to describe straight-line motion, and one discipline that separates the students who lose marks from the ones who don't. The discipline is the difference between a scalar and a vector. By the end of today you'll be able to distinguish distance from displacement and speed from velocity, explain the difference between average and instantaneous, define acceleration properly, and handle the sign conventions that make one-dimensional motion behave. This is the episode that, if you nail it, makes everything from motion graphs to projectile motion click later. Since this is the first lesson, there's nothing to recap yet, so let's just begin.

NARRATOR: Here's the single most important idea in the whole module, so I'm putting it first. Every quantity in physics is one of two types: a scalar or a vector. A scalar has only a size, a magnitude, a number with a unit. A vector has a magnitude and a direction. That's the whole distinction: a vector tells you how much and which way; a scalar only tells you how much. Mass is a scalar, five kilograms, no direction. Time is a scalar. But the quantities of motion split across the line, and getting that split right is worth real marks, because markers specifically check that you treat vectors as vectors. Here's a memory hook to lock the vector quantities of motion: the vector trio is Displacement, Velocity, Acceleration, call it D-V-A, and every one of them must carry a direction. Their scalar shadows, the size-only versions, are distance and speed. So when you give a vector answer, you always state the direction. Drop the direction off a vector and you've given a scalar answer to a vector question, and that's a lost mark. The full set of these quantities, with their units and whether each is a scalar or a vector, is collected in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Let's make that concrete with the first pair: distance and displacement. Distance is a scalar, it's the total length of the path you actually travelled, the number the odometer in a car racks up. Displacement is a vector, it's the straight-line change in your position from start to finish, with a direction, an arrow from where you started to where you ended. Here's the classic example that the exam loves, and you should have it ready. Imagine you run one full lap of a four-hundred-metre running track and stop exactly where you started. The distance you travelled is four hundred metres, the whole way around. But your displacement is zero, because you finished in the same place you began, there's no straight-line change in position at all. Distance four hundred metres, displacement zero. That single example proves they are different quantities, not just two words for the same thing. Distance can never be negative and never decreases; displacement has direction and can be positive, negative, or zero.

QUESTION: So when would distance and displacement actually be equal?

NARRATOR: Good question, and it's a clean test of whether you've understood it. Distance and displacement are equal in magnitude only when the motion is in a straight line without reversing direction, when you travel straight from A to B and never double back. The moment the path curves, or you turn around and come back on yourself, the distance, the total path, becomes larger than the displacement, the straight-line gap between the ends. Walk three metres east, then one metre back west, and your distance is four metres, three plus one, but your displacement is two metres east, the net result. So they coincide only for straight-line, one-direction motion, and they part company the instant the path bends or reverses. There's a worked version of that there-and-back journey in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Now the second pair, speed and velocity, and it's the exact same scalar-versus-vector split, applied to how fast you're moving. Speed is a scalar, how fast, no direction, sixty kilometres per hour. Velocity is a vector, how fast and in which direction, sixty kilometres per hour north. The relationship to the first pair is direct: speed is the rate of covering distance, and velocity is the rate of change of displacement. So speed is distance over time, and velocity is displacement over time. And because displacement can be zero while distance isn't, you get the result that catches people: run that full lap in, say, eighty seconds, and your average speed is four hundred metres over eighty seconds, five metres per second, a real number. But your average velocity is your displacement, zero, over eighty seconds, which is zero. Same trip, average speed five metres per second, average velocity zero. They are not the same quantity, and the average speed is not just the size of the average velocity. Burn that in.

NARRATOR: That brings us to a distinction inside velocity itself: average versus instantaneous. Average velocity is the total displacement divided by the total time for a whole trip, it smooths over everything that happened in between. Instantaneous velocity is your velocity at one single instant, at one moment in time, the reading on the speedometer right now, with a direction attached. Think of a car trip across the city: your average velocity might be thirty kilometres per hour in some direction over the whole journey, but at any given instant you could be doing zero at a red light or eighty on a clear stretch. The average doesn't tell you what happened at each moment; the instantaneous value does. The same split applies to speed: average speed over the trip versus the instantaneous speed your speedometer shows. We'll make this visual next episode with motion graphs, where instantaneous velocity becomes the gradient of a line at a single point, but for now hold the idea: average is over an interval, instantaneous is at an instant.

NARRATOR: Let me make that split land with numbers. Suppose you drive twelve thousand metres across town and it takes one thousand seconds, including a long wait at a level crossing. Your average velocity is twelve thousand metres over one thousand seconds, twelve metres per second, in the direction of the trip. But during that level-crossing wait your instantaneous velocity was zero, and on the open road it might have hit twenty-five. The average is a single summary of the whole journey; it deliberately hides the moment-to-moment story. The syllabus actually asks you to measure both in a practical investigation, so know how it's done: you track a moving object's position over time and analyse the data. The classic tools are a ticker-timer, which punches dots on a tape at a fixed rate so the dot spacing reveals the speed, and, more commonly now, a motion sensor or light gates feeding a data logger. Average velocity comes from the overall displacement and total time; instantaneous velocity comes from the displacement over a very short interval around the instant you care about, the shorter the interval, the closer you get to the true instantaneous value. That "shrink the interval" idea is exactly what becomes the gradient of a tangent next episode.

NARRATOR: And notice the pattern quietly forming, because it's the backbone of kinematics: each quantity is the rate of change of the one before it. Velocity is the rate of change of displacement, how fast your position is changing. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, how fast your velocity is changing. It's a ladder, position, then velocity, then acceleration, each step the rate of change of the step below. That single idea, rate of change, is what every motion graph and every equation of motion in this module is built on, and it's why the gradient of a graph, which is exactly a rate of change, is about to become so powerful.

QUESTION: Before we go on, here's one to actually stop for. A swimmer swims fifty metres to the far wall of a pool and then fifty metres back, taking one hundred seconds in total. What is the swimmer's average speed, and what is the swimmer's average velocity? Pause the player, work out both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The average speed is the total distance over the total time. The swimmer covered fifty metres there and fifty metres back, a total distance of one hundred metres, in one hundred seconds, so the average speed is one hundred over one hundred, one metre per second. The average velocity is the total displacement over the total time. The swimmer finished back at the starting wall, so the displacement is zero, and the average velocity is zero over one hundred seconds, which is zero. Average speed one metre per second, average velocity zero. If you got both, and especially if you said the velocity was zero, you've understood the single most tested idea in this episode. If you said the average velocity was one metre per second, that's the trap, you used distance where velocity demands displacement. The full working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Now the third member of the vector trio, acceleration, and this is where careful definitions earn marks. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. Not the rate of change of speed, the rate of change of velocity, and that distinction matters because velocity is a vector. So acceleration is itself a vector: it's how much the velocity changes, divided by the time it takes, and it points in the direction of the change in velocity. In symbols you'd write acceleration equals the change in velocity divided by the change in time, that is, the final velocity minus the initial velocity, over the time taken. The change in velocity is final minus initial, always that order. The unit is metres per second, per second, because you're measuring how many metres per second your velocity changes by, each second. We say "metres per second squared", but it means "metres per second, every second".

NARRATOR: Here's the subtlety that the exam uses to separate students, and it's worth a mark every time. A negative acceleration does not always mean "slowing down". Acceleration is a vector, so its sign just tells you its direction relative to whichever direction you chose as positive. If an object is moving in the positive direction and has a negative acceleration, then yes, it's slowing down. But if an object is already moving in the negative direction and has a negative acceleration, it's actually speeding up, going faster and faster in the negative direction. The everyday word "deceleration" just means the acceleration opposes the motion, whatever direction that is. So don't read "negative acceleration" as "braking" automatically; read it as "acceleration pointing in the negative direction" and then check which way the object is moving. That care is exactly what a marker is looking for.

NARRATOR: Which brings us to the tool that makes all of one-dimensional motion manageable: the sign convention. In a straight line, a vector only has two possible directions, so we don't need arrows and angles, we just need a plus or a minus. You choose one direction to be positive, say, to the right, or upward, and then everything in that direction is positive and everything in the opposite direction is negative. A velocity of minus five metres per second simply means five metres per second in the negative direction. The golden rule: choose your positive direction at the start of the problem, state it, and stay consistent the whole way through. Most sign errors in kinematics come from quietly switching conventions halfway. Pick a direction, commit to it, and the vectors of one-dimensional motion become simple signed numbers. This is the trick that lets us treat vertical motion under gravity, where down is usually negative, as straightforward arithmetic, which you'll use heavily in the equations-of-motion episode and again in projectile motion next year.

NARRATOR: Let me make acceleration concrete with numbers, because the definition only really lands once you've computed one, and the sign is where people slip. Take east as positive. A car is travelling east at twenty metres per second and, over four seconds, slows to twelve metres per second, still heading east. The acceleration is the change in velocity over the time: the final velocity minus the initial velocity, twelve minus twenty, is minus eight metres per second, and dividing by four seconds gives minus two metres per second per second. Negative, and because the car is moving in the positive direction, the negative acceleration opposes the motion, so the car is slowing down. Now the contrasting case that exposes the trap. A ball is rolling west, in the negative direction, at three metres per second, and one second later it's moving west at seven metres per second, faster. Its initial velocity is minus three, its final velocity is minus seven, so the change in velocity is minus seven minus minus three, which is minus four metres per second, over one second, minus four metres per second per second. The acceleration is negative again, but this time the ball is speeding up, because the acceleration points the same way as the motion, both negative, both west. Same negative sign on the acceleration, opposite effect on the speed, entirely because of the direction of travel. Both calculations are laid out in Listing five. That is precisely why "negative acceleration" must be read as "acceleration in the negative direction", never automatically as "braking".

NARRATOR: One quick word on the numbers themselves, because it carries through every calculation in this course. Physics answers are measurements, so they come with units and with a sensible number of significant figures. Always write the unit, metres, metres per second, metres per second per second, an answer without a unit is incomplete and can be marked wrong. And don't quote ten digits off your calculator: match the precision of the data you were given, usually two or three significant figures at this level. A velocity of "four metres per second east" is a complete physical answer; "4.0" on its own is not. That discipline, units and significant figures, comes straight from the working-scientifically skills, and the marker applies it everywhere.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the headline extended-response answer in full, because "distinguish between distance and displacement" or "between speed and velocity" is about as predictable as exam questions get. Here's the strong, full-mark version for distance and displacement. Distance is a scalar quantity that measures the total length of the path travelled by an object, and it has magnitude only. Displacement is a vector quantity that measures the change in position of an object from its starting point to its finishing point, and it has both magnitude and direction. For example, an athlete who runs one complete lap of a four-hundred-metre track travels a distance of four hundred metres but has a displacement of zero, because they finish at their starting position. Hear everything that answer does: it names each as scalar or vector, it defines each precisely, it states that displacement has direction, and it clinches the difference with a worked example. That is the full mark.

NARRATOR: Now the weak answer, so you can hear exactly where the marks leak away. A weak answer says: "Distance is how far something travels and displacement is how far it is from where it started." That sentence isn't wrong, but it earns maybe one mark out of three, because it never uses the words scalar and vector, it never mentions that displacement has a direction, and it gives no example to show they can differ. The marks live in naming the scalar-vector distinction, stating the direction, and proving the difference with the lap example. Same topic, a third of the marks. Define the type, state the direction, give the example.

NARRATOR: Let me do one clean calculation in full, the kind that opens a kinematics question, because the method is what's marked. Suppose a cyclist travels two hundred metres east in twenty seconds, then turns around and travels eighty metres west in ten seconds. Find the average speed and the average velocity for the whole trip. Take east as positive, state that first. The total distance is two hundred plus eighty, two hundred and eighty metres, distance doesn't care about direction. The total displacement is two hundred east minus eighty west, that's plus two hundred minus eighty, a hundred and twenty metres east. The total time is twenty plus ten, thirty seconds. So the average speed is two hundred and eighty over thirty, about nine point three metres per second, a scalar, no direction. And the average velocity is a hundred and twenty over thirty, four metres per second, east, a vector, with the direction stated. Notice the average speed and the size of the average velocity are different numbers, nine point three versus four, exactly because the cyclist doubled back. The full working, with the sign convention laid out, is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: It's worth pausing on what those two numbers actually mean, because the contrast is the whole point. The average speed, nine point three metres per second, tells you how hard the cyclist was working, how much ground they covered per second, regardless of direction. The average velocity, four metres per second east, tells you the net effect of the trip, how their position changed overall per second. A delivery rider weaving all over a suburb might have a high average speed but a tiny average velocity, because they end up almost back where they started. So the gap between the two numbers is itself information: it measures how much the journey doubled back on itself. When the average speed and the magnitude of the average velocity are equal, you know the motion was a straight shot in one direction; when they differ, the path must have curved or reversed. That's the kind of interpretation an "explain" question rewards, not just the numbers, but what they tell you about the motion.

NARRATOR: Before the recap, let me bank the traps in one place, because in kinematics the marks are lost in the same handful of spots every year. Trap one: giving a vector answer with no direction, "four metres per second" when the question wanted velocity, which needs "four metres per second, east". Trap two: using distance where the question asked for displacement, the lap is the warning, four hundred metres of distance but zero displacement. Trap three: assuming average speed equals the magnitude of average velocity, it only does so for straight-line, non-reversing motion. Trap four: reading "negative acceleration" as "slowing down" without checking the direction of motion. Trap five: switching your positive-direction convention partway through a problem, the source of most sign errors. And trap six: dropping units or quoting a calculator's full string of digits instead of a sensible two or three significant figures. Every one of those is a definitional or discipline error, not a hard physics error, which means every one is a free mark once you know to look for it.

NARRATOR: Let me recap the takeaways, phrased the way you'd write them for marks. One: a scalar has magnitude only; a vector has magnitude and direction, and the vector trio of motion is displacement, velocity, and acceleration, D-V-A, each needing a direction. Two: distance is the scalar total path; displacement is the vector straight-line change in position, and they differ whenever the path bends or reverses, the lap gives distance four hundred metres but displacement zero. Three: speed is distance over time, a scalar; velocity is displacement over time, a vector; and average speed is not the magnitude of average velocity. Four: average is over an interval, instantaneous is at an instant. Five: acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, a vector, in metres per second per second, and a negative acceleration means "in the negative direction", not automatically "slowing down". And the method spine: choose a positive direction, state it, and stay consistent. Hold those and you own the foundation of kinematics.

QUESTION: Question two. A car travels six hundred metres north, then two hundred metres south. Calculate the total distance travelled and the magnitude and direction of the displacement. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, working in Listing four. Take north as positive. The total distance is six hundred plus two hundred, eight hundred metres, a scalar. The displacement is six hundred north minus two hundred south, that is plus six hundred minus two hundred, four hundred metres, and since it's positive it's directed north. So the distance is eight hundred metres and the displacement is four hundred metres north. Three marks: the distance, the displacement magnitude, and the direction. QUESTION: Question three. Explain the difference between average velocity and instantaneous velocity. Two marks. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Average velocity is the total displacement of an object divided by the total time taken over an interval, giving a single overall value for the whole motion. Instantaneous velocity is the velocity of the object at one particular instant in time, such as the reading shown by a speedometer at that moment, with its direction. Two marks: one for average velocity as displacement over total time across an interval, one for instantaneous velocity as the velocity at a single instant. The distinction the marker wants is "over an interval" versus "at an instant".

QUESTION: Question four. A runner completes one full lap of a four-hundred-metre circular track in eighty seconds. Calculate the average speed and the average velocity. Justify the value of the average velocity. Three marks. Pause, answer fully, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The average speed is the total distance over the total time: four hundred metres over eighty seconds, which is five metres per second. The average velocity is the total displacement over the total time. Because the runner finishes at the same point they started, the displacement is zero, so the average velocity is zero divided by eighty seconds, which is zero. The justification: average velocity depends on displacement, not distance, and the displacement for a complete lap is zero because the start and finish positions are identical. Three marks: the average speed, the average velocity of zero, and the justification tying it to zero displacement. This is the headline example of the whole episode, distance and displacement are different, and so are speed and velocity.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. An object moving in the positive direction has a negative acceleration. A student claims this means the object must be slowing down. Evaluate this claim. Three marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The claim is correct in this specific case but not as a general rule. Acceleration is a vector equal to the rate of change of velocity, and its sign indicates its direction relative to the chosen positive direction, not whether the object is speeding up or slowing down. If an object is moving in the positive direction and its acceleration is negative, the acceleration opposes the motion, so the object is indeed slowing down, and the student is right here. However, if an object were moving in the negative direction with a negative acceleration, the acceleration would be in the same direction as the motion, so the object would be speeding up. Therefore the claim is true only because the object happens to be moving in the positive direction; in general, a negative acceleration means the acceleration points in the negative direction, and whether the object speeds up or slows down depends on the direction of its velocity. Three marks: identify the claim as true only for this case; explain that the sign of acceleration is a direction, not "slowing"; and give the counter-case of negative-direction motion speeding up. Notice the verb "evaluate" wants a judgement, the claim is conditionally true, plus the reasoning, that's the band-six move.
