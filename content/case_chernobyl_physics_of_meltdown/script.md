NARRATOR: It is twenty-three minutes and forty seconds past one in the morning, on the twenty-sixth of April, nineteen eighty-six. In the control room of Reactor Number Four at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant, in what was then the Soviet Union, a twenty-five-year-old engineer named Leonid Toptunov reaches out and presses a button. The button is coloured to stand out from everything around it, and it has a name. It is called the AZ-5 button. It is the emergency shutdown. Press it, and a hundred and seventy control rods drop into the heart of the reactor, soaking up neutrons, smothering the nuclear chain reaction, bringing everything to a safe and silent halt. It is the button you press when you want the reactor to stop. Toptunov presses it. And the power, instead of falling to zero, begins to climb. In three seconds it rises to something on the order of thirty thousand megawatts. That is roughly ten times the reactor's maximum rated output. The shutdown button has set off the explosion.

NARRATOR: How is that possible? How can the button designed to stop a nuclear reactor be the very thing that blows it apart? The answer is not a story about a careless operator pressing the wrong switch. The answer is physics. Specific, knowable, predictable physics, built into the design of that reactor, that turned a safety system into a detonator. And here is the part that should make the hair stand up on the back of your neck. That physics had been known to a handful of Soviet scientists for years. There were documents. There were warnings. And the men in that control room, the men who would die in agony over the following days, had never been told.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how a nuclear reactor works, and of one specific reactor that was built with a flaw hidden inside its physics like a splinter under the skin. It is a story about fission and feedback loops, about a poison that builds up inside a reactor core, about a safety test that became the worst nuclear accident in history, and about an isotope of caesium with a thirty-year half-life that, even now, keeps a region the size of a small country closed to human beings. To understand what happened in those three seconds, we have to start with the most basic question of all. What is a nuclear reactor, and how does it make heat?

QUESTION: So let's start there. What actually happens inside a nuclear reactor?

NARRATOR: At its heart, a nuclear reactor runs on a single physical process called fission. Take a nucleus of uranium-235, a heavy, slightly unstable lump of ninety-two protons and a hundred and forty-three neutrons. Fire a neutron into it, and the nucleus absorbs that neutron to become uranium-236 for the briefest instant, a fat, jittering nucleus that wobbles, stretches, and splits into two smaller nuclei, often something like barium and krypton. When it splits, it releases an enormous amount of energy, around two hundred million electronvolts per fission, mostly as the kinetic energy of those two fragments flying apart. And the deep reason that energy comes out is something you will meet in the syllabus as binding energy per nucleon. The two medium-sized fragments are more tightly bound, more stable, than the heavy uranium they came from, and that gain in stability is released as energy. To put that in perspective, splitting a single uranium nucleus releases something like twenty million times more energy than burning a single atom of carbon in coal, where each carbon atom yields only about ten electronvolts. That is the prize. That is why we build reactors. And it is worth remembering the people who first understood it. In nineteen thirty-eight, Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann split uranium in Berlin and were baffled to find barium in the debris, and it was Lise Meitner and her nephew Otto Frisch who worked out what had happened and gave the process its name, fission. Within a year Frederic Joliot and his team had confirmed that each fission released two or three further neutrons, which is what makes everything that follows possible.

NARRATOR: But the truly important thing is what else comes out of the split. Along with the two fragments and the energy, each fission throws out two or three fresh neutrons. And those neutrons can go and find other uranium nuclei, and split them, releasing more energy and yet more neutrons, which split more nuclei, and so on. This is a chain reaction. One fission triggers more fissions, which trigger still more. And here is the distinction the whole of nuclear engineering turns on, the one your syllabus asks you to compare directly. In a controlled chain reaction, on average exactly one neutron from each fission survives to trigger exactly one more fission. The numbers balance, the reaction holds steady, and you have a reactor producing a constant, useful flow of heat. In an uncontrolled chain reaction, more than one neutron from each fission goes on to cause another, so the number of fissions multiplies, two becomes four becomes eight, doubling and redoubling, and the power climbs without limit. A power reactor is engineered to sit forever on the knife edge of exactly one. A bomb is engineered to do the opposite. The art of running a reactor is keeping that average pinned at one, and you do it by managing the neutrons.

QUESTION: You said fire a neutron into the uranium. Does the speed of the neutron matter?

NARRATOR: It matters enormously, and this is one of the most counterintuitive facts in all of nuclear physics. You might think that to smash a nucleus apart, you would want to hit it with a fast, energetic neutron. The opposite is true. Uranium-235 is far more likely to capture a slow neutron than a fast one. A fast neutron, fresh out of a fission, tends to glance off or recoil away. A slow neutron, dawdling along, is much more readily absorbed, and absorption is what triggers the split. The physicists describe this in terms of something called the reaction cross-section, which you can think of as the effective size of the target the nucleus presents. For a slow neutron, the uranium nucleus looks like a big fat target. For a fast neutron, it looks tiny.

NARRATOR: So here is the problem. The neutrons that come flying out of a fission are fast. Very fast. If we want them to go on and trigger more fissions efficiently, we need to slow them down first. And that job, slowing the neutrons, is done by a part of the reactor called the moderator.

QUESTION: The moderator slows the neutrons down. How does it do that?

NARRATOR: By getting them to bounce off light atoms. Picture a fast neutron crashing through a material made of small, light nuclei. Every time the neutron collides with one of those nuclei, it loses a little energy, the way a billiard ball loses speed when it knocks into another ball of similar size. After many collisions, the neutron has slowed from a fast neutron to what physicists call a thermal neutron, slow enough to be readily captured by uranium. The material that does this slowing is the moderator. In many reactors the moderator is ordinary water. In the reactor at Chernobyl, it was something different, and that difference is at the very centre of our story. The moderator at Chernobyl was solid graphite. Blocks of it. One thousand seven hundred tonnes of graphite, stacked into a great cylinder eleven point eight metres across and seven metres tall, riddled with channels. And there is history in that choice. The very first controlled nuclear chain reaction on Earth, achieved by Enrico Fermi and his team under a squash court in Chicago in nineteen forty-two, was built exactly this way, a pile of graphite bricks laced with uranium, its rate held in check by cadmium control rods. Graphite moderation is old, proven physics. The catch is that solid graphite behaves very differently from water when things start to go wrong, and that difference is the trap waiting at the heart of this story.

QUESTION: Okay, so we have fuel, and we have a moderator to slow the neutrons. What else does a reactor need?

NARRATOR: Two more essential parts, and you must hold all four in your head, because the whole disaster turns on how these four pieces interact. The third part is the control rods. These are rods made of a material that is hungry for neutrons, that absorbs them greedily without splitting, materials like boron carbide. Here is the crucial distinction that trips up so many students. The moderator slows neutrons down so they can cause fissions. The control rods absorb neutrons so they cannot cause fissions. Slowing and absorbing are completely different jobs. Push the control rods deep into the core, and they drink up neutrons, starving the chain reaction, and the power falls. Pull them out, and more neutrons survive to cause fissions, and the power rises. The control rods are the throttle and the brake. The hundred and seventy control rods, and that emergency button Toptunov pressed, were the means by which the operators steered the reactor.

NARRATOR: And the fourth part is the coolant. The whole point of a reactor is to make heat, and that heat has to be carried away to do useful work, to boil water into steam, to spin a turbine, to generate electricity. At Chernobyl, the coolant was ordinary water, light water, pumped up through those vertical channels in the graphite, flowing past the fuel, carrying the heat away, and in the process boiling into steam that drove the turbines directly. So there it is. Four parts. Fuel, where the fission happens. Moderator, which slows the neutrons. Control rods, which absorb neutrons to control the rate. And coolant, which carries the heat away. In a well-designed reactor, these four work together in a stable, self-correcting balance. In the RBMK reactor at Chernobyl, they did not.

QUESTION: RBMK. What does that stand for, and why does it matter?

NARRATOR: RBMK is a Russian abbreviation, and it translates roughly as high-power channel-type reactor. It was a Soviet design, big, cheap to build, and it had one feature that set it apart from almost every Western reactor, a feature that sounds innocent and turns out to be lethal. In most Western reactors, a pressurised water reactor for example, the water plays two roles at once. It is both the moderator and the coolant. The very same water that slows the neutrons also carries away the heat. In the RBMK, those two jobs were split apart and given to two different substances. The graphite was the moderator. The water was only the coolant. And this separation, this single design choice, created a monster hiding in the physics. It is called the positive void coefficient. To understand the accident, you have to understand this one idea above all others. So let me build it for you carefully.

QUESTION: All right. What is a void coefficient?

NARRATOR: A void, in this context, is a bubble of steam. When the coolant water flowing past the hot fuel boils, it turns from liquid water into steam, and where there was once dense liquid there is now a void, a region of gas. The void coefficient is a number that tells you what happens to the reactor's power when those steam voids form. Does the power go up, or does it go down? If forming steam makes the power go down, the coefficient is negative, and that is a safe, self-correcting situation. If forming steam makes the power go up, the coefficient is positive, and that is dangerous, because it means the reactor can feed on its own heat. Now, here is the beautiful and terrible logic of why these two reactor designs behave in opposite ways.

NARRATOR: Think first about a Western water-moderated reactor. The water is doing both jobs. It is slowing the neutrons, and it is also, to some degree, absorbing them. Now suppose the water starts to boil and steam voids appear. Steam is far less dense than liquid water, about a thousand times less dense. So when the water boils, you lose the moderator. The neutrons are no longer being slowed down. And in a reactor that relies on water to slow its neutrons, losing the moderator means the chain reaction falters. Fewer neutrons get slowed to the right speed, fewer fissions happen, and the power drops. So the boiling is self-limiting. The reactor naturally calms itself down. That is a negative void coefficient, and it is a profoundly safe property to have. The reactor pushes back against its own overheating.

QUESTION: And in the RBMK, with the graphite, it was different?

NARRATOR: Completely, fatally different. In the RBMK, the moderator was the graphite, and the graphite does not boil. It just sits there, a solid block, slowing neutrons no matter what the water does. The water in an RBMK was only the coolant, and water, as well as carrying heat, also absorbs some neutrons. It is a mild neutron absorber, a mild parasite on the chain reaction. So now ask, what happens when the water in an RBMK boils into steam? You lose a neutron absorber. The moderation keeps going, because the graphite is untouched, still patiently slowing neutrons exactly as before. But the water that used to soak up a fraction of those neutrons has turned to steam, which absorbs almost nothing. So suddenly there are more neutrons available to cause fissions. More fissions means more power. More power means more heat. More heat means more boiling, more steam voids, even fewer neutrons absorbed, even more fissions. Do you see the loop? Boiling leads to more power, and more power leads to more boiling.

NARRATOR: That is the positive void coefficient. It is a feedback loop that runs in exactly the wrong direction. In a safe reactor, overheating triggers a response that cools things down. In the RBMK, overheating triggered a response that heated things up further, which heated them up further still. And the RBMK's positive void coefficient was not just positive, it was the largest of any commercial reactor ever built. At Chernobyl it had a value engineers wrote as plus four point five beta, where beta is a technical unit measuring how strongly a change pushes the reactor toward running away. The crucial thing for you to hold onto is simply this. In that reactor, steam bubbles in the coolant did not calm the reaction. They accelerated it. The reactor was, in a very real sense, holding a loaded gun pointed at itself.

QUESTION: If the design was this dangerous, why was it built? And why was it allowed to run?

NARRATOR: Because the positive void coefficient is mostly a problem at low power. At full power, the RBMK had other stabilising effects that kept it in check, and the operators were trained to run it up at full power where it was relatively well-behaved. The danger lived at low power, where the coolant was already close to boiling and those competing stabilising effects were weak. And there was a deeper reason it was tolerated. The RBMK was a point of Soviet engineering pride. It was cheaper than Western designs, it could be refuelled without shutting down, and admitting that it had a fundamental design flaw was, in the political climate of the time, very nearly unthinkable. The flaw was known to the design institutes. It had even been written into a classified report. But it was not shared with the people who ran the reactors night after night. The operators believed they were driving a safe machine. They were not.

QUESTION: So what was the test? You mentioned a safety test. What were they trying to do that night?

NARRATOR: Here is the bitter irony at the centre of the whole tragedy. The accident happened during a safety test. They were trying to make the reactor safer. The question the test was meant to answer was a genuine and sensible one. Imagine the plant suddenly loses its external electricity supply. The reactor still needs power to run its enormous coolant pumps, because if the pumps stop, the core overheats. There were backup diesel generators, but they took somewhere between sixty and seventy-five seconds to spin up to full power. So there was a dangerous gap of about a minute with no guaranteed power to the pumps. The idea behind the test was this. When the external power fails, the reactor's own steam turbine keeps spinning for a while as it coasts down, like a flywheel. Could that coasting turbine, in its dying moments, generate enough electricity to keep the pumps running during that sixty-second gap? It was a reasonable thing to want to know. The test had even been tried before, on three earlier occasions, and had not worked. This was another attempt.

NARRATOR: The plan was to run the test during the day shift on the twenty-fifth of April, with the most experienced operators present and the reactor brought down to a moderate power. And early in the day, it began. At one in the afternoon, power was already being reduced, and the emergency core cooling system was switched off as part of the preparation, and, fatefully, never switched back on. But then at two in the afternoon came a phone call that changed everything. It was the electricity grid controller in Kiev. The grid needed the power. There was demand in the network, and a major plant could not simply drop offline. So the controller refused permission to shut down. And the reactor was held, half-throttled, at about sixteen hundred megawatts, for nine long hours, with experienced day-shift staff going home and a fresh, far less prepared night shift coming on.

QUESTION: So by the time the test could finally run, it was the middle of the night and a different crew was in charge?

NARRATOR: Exactly. It was approaching midnight when the grid finally released the plant to continue the shutdown. The night shift had taken over. The shift supervisor was Aleksandr Akimov. At the controls, reducing the power, sat Leonid Toptunov, twenty-five years old, with only a couple of months of experience as a senior reactor control engineer. This was the first time he had ever taken a reactor through a shutdown himself. And overseeing the whole operation, the most senior man in the room, was the deputy chief engineer, Anatoly Dyatlov, a hard, intimidating figure who had decided this test would be completed on his watch. These were the people now steering a reactor with a hidden flaw, in the dark, at low power, in exactly the regime where that flaw was most dangerous, and none of them knew it was there.

QUESTION: And something went wrong with the power, didn't it?

NARRATOR: Something went very wrong. The plan was to stabilise the reactor at around seven hundred to a thousand megawatts of thermal power for the test. But as Toptunov reduced the power and switched the control system over to a different automatic mode, the power did not gently settle. It plunged. It crashed all the way down to about thirty megawatts. Thirty. The reactor had very nearly shut itself off completely. And to understand why that crash was so catastrophic, and why pulling out of it was so dangerous, I have to introduce you to the second great villain of this story. A poison. A poison that builds up inside a reactor when you slow it down. Its name is xenon.

QUESTION: A poison inside the reactor? What do you mean by poison?

NARRATOR: In nuclear engineering, a poison is any substance that absorbs neutrons and chokes the chain reaction, the way a real poison chokes a living thing. And it turns out that when uranium fissions, some of the fragments it produces are themselves neutron-hungry. The worst of all is an isotope called xenon-135. Xenon-135 has, by an enormous margin, the largest appetite for neutrons of any nucleus known. Its cross-section, that effective target size we spoke about, is millions of times larger than uranium's. A single xenon-135 nucleus will swallow a neutron the instant one comes near. It is a black hole for neutrons. And here is the subtle, deadly part. Xenon-135 is partly produced directly in fission, but mostly it is produced indirectly. Fission produces another isotope, iodine-135, and that iodine slowly decays, with a half-life of about six and a half hours, into xenon-135.

QUESTION: So why does slowing the reactor down cause the xenon to build up?

NARRATOR: Because of a delicate balance between how xenon is created and how it is destroyed. When the reactor is running at full power, with neutrons flooding the core, the xenon-135 is destroyed almost as fast as it forms. The neutrons crash into the xenon and burn it away. Production and destruction sit in balance, and the xenon level stays low and steady. But now drop the power. Suddenly the flood of neutrons dries up. The xenon is no longer being burned away, because there are far fewer neutrons to burn it. But, and this is the crucial point, the iodine-135 that built up during the earlier hours of high-power running is still sitting in the core, and it keeps right on decaying into fresh xenon-135 at its own steady pace, indifferent to what the reactor is doing now. So you have xenon pouring in from the decaying iodine, and almost nothing destroying it. The xenon level surges upward. The core fills with neutron poison. Engineers call this the xenon pit, or xenon poisoning, and it can make a reactor temporarily impossible to bring back up to power.

NARRATOR: And remember that nine-hour delay, when the reactor was held at half power waiting for the grid? That had already loaded the core with iodine. So when the power crashed to thirty megawatts, the xenon came flooding in on top of an already poisoned core. The reactor was being smothered. By every rule of good practice, by the reactor's own operating manual, there was only one correct decision at that moment. Shut the reactor down. Walk away. Wait a day or so for the xenon to decay and the iodine to clear. Try the test another time. That was the safe path, and the manual demanded it.

QUESTION: But they didn't shut it down.

NARRATOR: They did not. Under pressure, with Dyatlov pushing to complete the test rather than abandon it and start over, the crew decided to drag the reactor back up out of the pit. And the only way to fight the xenon poison, the only way to coax the chain reaction back to life against all that neutron-absorbing xenon, was to pull out control rods. So they pulled them. One after another. They withdrew rod after rod after rod, removing the very neutron absorbers that gave them control, just to claw the power back up. And even with this enormous effort, they could only get the reactor up to about two hundred megawatts, far below the seven hundred minimum the test was supposed to use, and far below where the reactor was stable.

QUESTION: How many control rods did they end up pulling out?

NARRATOR: This is measured by something called the operating reactivity margin, which is essentially the number of control rods left inserted in the core, the reserve of braking power the operators still had in hand. The rules said this margin must never fall below the equivalent of about twenty-six to thirty rods. That was a hard safety limit. When they finally calculated their margin, at about twenty-two minutes past one in the morning, less than two minutes before the disaster, it came out at the equivalent of around eight rods. Eight. Less than a third of the absolute minimum. The reactor was running with almost all of its brakes pulled out, balanced on a knife edge, choked with xenon, at a low power where its positive void coefficient was at its most vicious. And to make it worse, the operators did not even have a clear real-time display of that margin. They had to work it out by hand from a printout, which is why they did not realise how dangerous their position was until it was almost too late. Every single safety margin had been eroded. The reactor was a bomb, and the crew was about to light the fuse, believing they were about to finish a routine test.

QUESTION: So at this point they actually start the test?

NARRATOR: At four seconds past one twenty-three in the morning, yes, they begin the test. They close the valves to the turbine, and it starts to coast down, exactly as planned. But as the turbine slows, the coolant pumps connected to it slow as well. Less water is being driven through the core. And less water flow, in a reactor already barely holding together, means the water lingers longer against the hot fuel, and it begins to boil. Steam voids form in the channels. And now the monster wakes up. Remember the positive void coefficient. Steam voids form, the power creeps up, more steam forms, the power climbs further. The feedback loop, dormant until this moment, begins to turn. Slowly at first, the reactor power starts to rise. The crew can see it climbing. And at thirty-six seconds past, after the test had run for a little over half a minute, one of them reaches for the emergency shutdown. At forty seconds past one twenty-three, the AZ-5 button is pressed.

QUESTION: And here is the part I still can't believe. The shutdown button made it worse. How?

NARRATOR: This is the final, almost unbelievable twist, and it is pure physics. It comes down to the design of the control rods themselves. The RBMK control rods had a flaw so perverse it sounds invented. Each rod was not a simple solid column of neutron absorber. Each rod had, attached to its lower end, a long section, four and a half metres of it, made of graphite. Graphite. The moderator. And below that graphite tip, when the rod was fully withdrawn, sat a column of water. Now think about what happens when you press the button and a fully withdrawn rod begins to descend into the core. The first thing to enter the core is not the neutron-absorbing part of the rod. The first thing to enter is that graphite tip. And graphite, as we know, is a moderator. It displaces the water that was sitting there, and water, you will recall, is a neutron absorber.

NARRATOR: So in the first instant of insertion, in the bottom region of the core, you are replacing a neutron absorber, water, with a neutron moderator, graphite. You are, for those first few seconds, not slowing the reaction but speeding it up. The control rod, in the very act of beginning to shut the reactor down, first adds reactivity. Engineers call it, with grim understatement, the positive scram effect. Under normal conditions it was a small, brief blip, swiftly overwhelmed as the absorbing part of the rods drove home. But these were not normal conditions. The reactor was already supercritical, already surging on its positive void coefficient, already stripped of nearly all its control rods so that there was an unusually long way for them to travel. When Toptunov pressed AZ-5, all those graphite tips plunged into the bottom of the core at once, and into a reactor poised on the very edge of running away, they delivered one final, fatal shove of reactivity.

QUESTION: And that's the power spike. Thirty thousand megawatts in three seconds.

NARRATOR: That is the spike. Three seconds after the button was pressed, the power excursion alarm screamed and the power was already soaring through hundreds of megawatts and climbing vertically. The estimates of the peak are staggering, on the order of thirty thousand megawatts, roughly ten times the reactor's maximum design output, reached in about three seconds. And then physics took over from engineering entirely. The fuel itself, heated to an impossible temperature in an instant, shattered. The control rods, descending into channels that were already deforming from the heat, jammed partway down. The operating log records the moment in cold, fragmentary words. Severe shocks. The rods stopped moving before reaching the lower limit. The reactor could no longer be shut down by any means. It was beyond human control.

NARRATOR: Then it exploded. At about fifty-eight seconds past one twenty-three, the first explosion. Almost certainly a steam explosion, as the superheated fuel flashed the remaining water into steam with such violence that it blew the core apart. The thousand-tonne steel and concrete lid sitting on top of the reactor was thrown upward and came back down at an angle, like the lid flipped off a pot. Seconds later, a second explosion, more powerful still, estimated at the energy of around two hundred and twenty-five tonnes of TNT, tore the reactor open to the sky. Whether the second blast was hydrogen igniting, or a brief burst of runaway fission, a so-called nuclear fizzle, is debated to this day. But the result was not in doubt. The core of Reactor Number Four was ripped open. Glowing chunks of graphite and fragments of nuclear fuel were scattered across the plant grounds and the roof. The reactor that could never explode had exploded.

QUESTION: What happened to the people in those first minutes and hours?

NARRATOR: They walked into something they could not see, could not smell, and could not comprehend. The firefighters arrived within minutes, around half past one in the morning, with no special protection, no idea that the chunks of black rock scattered across the ground were pieces of a reactor core. Some picked up graphite with their bare hands. The radiation levels on the wreckage of the reactor floor were measured later at over five roentgens per second, which works out to something like twenty thousand roentgens per hour. A dose that kills half the people exposed to it is somewhere around four hundred to five hundred roentgens. Do the arithmetic. In that environment, a man could absorb a lethal dose in under a minute. And there is one detail that captures the whole human tragedy. Dyatlov, even after the explosions, refused at first to believe the reactor itself was destroyed. He ordered Akimov and Toptunov to manually open valves and pump water into the core, to cool a reactor that no longer existed. The two men went and did it, working for hours in lethal radiation, flooding a hole in the ground. Both of them died within weeks. Toptunov, the young man who had pressed the button on the orders of others, died on the fourteenth of May, less than three weeks later, of acute radiation poisoning. He was twenty-five years old.

QUESTION: How many people died, in the end?

NARRATOR: This is where I have to be very careful, because the truth has been distorted in both directions, exaggerated by some and minimised by others. The number of direct, immediate deaths is small and well established. Two people were killed in the explosion and its immediate aftermath. Twenty-eight more died within three months from acute radiation syndrome, the firefighters and operators who absorbed massive doses in those first hours. So thirty direct deaths. But that is not the whole story. There were the longer-term cancers, above all thyroid cancer in children, caused by another radioactive isotope released that night, iodine-131. Iodine-131 has a half-life of just over eight days, so it is intensely radioactive but short-lived. It contaminated the grass, which was eaten by cows, which produced milk, which was drunk by children. The iodine concentrates in the thyroid gland, and several thousand children developed thyroid cancer in the years that followed. Most were treatable, but it was a vast and preventable wave of harm. The total eventual death toll from cancers is genuinely uncertain and fiercely contested, with serious scientific estimates ranging from several thousand up to figures many times higher, depending on the methods used.

QUESTION: You keep mentioning these isotopes by name. Iodine-131, and earlier caesium. Why do the half-lives matter so much?

NARRATOR: Because the half-life is the single most important number for understanding how long a place stays dangerous. Let me remind you what a half-life is. Radioactive nuclei are unstable, and they decay at random, but for any given isotope there is a fixed time over which, on average, half of the nuclei present will have decayed away. That fixed time is the half-life. After one half-life, half remains. After two half-lives, a quarter remains. After three, an eighth. The decay follows a smooth exponential curve. And the half-life is a fingerprint, utterly different from one isotope to the next. For iodine-131, the half-life is about eight days, which is why, ferociously dangerous as it was, the iodine threat had largely faded within a couple of months. Eight days, sixteen days, twenty-four days, and after ten or so half-lives, a few months, it is essentially gone.

QUESTION: And the caesium is the one that lasts?

NARRATOR: Caesium-137 is the one that lasts. Its half-life is just over thirty years, and it is the reason the exclusion zone around Chernobyl is still, today, a forbidden place. Caesium-137 was spread across the landscape, eighty-five petabecquerels of it, a colossal quantity, drifting on the wind across Europe and settling into the soil. It decays by emitting a beta particle, transforming into a short-lived form of barium, which then emits a gamma ray of about six hundred and sixty thousand electronvolts, an energetic, penetrating gamma ray that makes the contamination both detectable and hazardous. Now think about that thirty-year half-life. After thirty years, half the caesium is gone. After sixty years, a quarter. To fall to about a thousandth of its original level, you need roughly ten half-lives, which is about three hundred years. That is why the most contaminated land around Chernobyl will not be safe for human habitation until somewhere in the twenty-third century. The half-life of one isotope, written into the physics of a single nucleus, is what sets the timescale for a human exclusion zone three centuries long. Alongside the caesium sits strontium-90, with a similar half-life of about twenty-nine years, a bone-seeking poison that compounds the problem.

QUESTION: Who cleaned it up, and what did it take?

NARRATOR: An army of people the Soviets called liquidators, because their job was to liquidate the consequences of the accident. Eventually around six hundred thousand people would receive that status, with roughly two hundred thousand doing the most intense work in nineteen eighty-six and eighty-seven. Helicopter pilots flew over the open, burning core, dropping thousands of tonnes of sand, clay, lead, and boron, the boron to absorb neutrons, into the gaping wound. Miners dug a tunnel beneath the reactor in brutal heat to stop molten core material reaching the groundwater. Soldiers, sent onto the roof to shovel radioactive graphite back into the core by hand, could work for only forty or ninety seconds each because the radiation was so intense that machines failed but men, briefly, could function. Many of their dosimeters had simply pinned at maximum, so their true doses were never known. And over the ruined reactor, by December of that same year, they built a vast concrete and steel tomb, the sarcophagus, to seal the wreckage away. Decades later, in twenty sixteen, an even larger structure, the New Safe Confinement, the largest movable land-based structure ever built, was slid over the top of the crumbling original.

QUESTION: And the towns? The people who lived there?

NARRATOR: The nearby city of Pripyat, home to nearly fifty thousand people, most of them families of the plant workers, was evacuated, but not until well over a day after the explosion, after people had already spent hours outdoors under a sky that some described as glowing with an odd colour. Eventually a zone of thirty kilometres radius around the reactor was cleared, and in total, over the following years, something like three hundred and fifty thousand people were resettled, their towns and farms and homes abandoned to the forest. The exclusion zone remains, to this day, one of the largest uninhabited areas in Europe. And here is a strange and haunting coda. With the humans gone, the wildlife has returned in force. Wolves, boar, deer, even rare horses now roam through the empty streets of Pripyat and the contaminated forests. The radiation harms them, but the absence of human beings has, on balance, been better for the wild populations than the radiation has been bad. Nature, it turns out, fears us more than it fears our poisons.

QUESTION: Before we finish, I have to ask. Was this a nuclear explosion? Like a bomb?

NARRATOR: No, and this is an important misconception to clear away. Chernobyl was not a nuclear bomb, and it could not have been. A nuclear weapon requires uranium enriched to over ninety percent in the fissile isotope, assembled into a critical mass in a few millionths of a second by precision explosives. The fuel at Chernobyl was barely two percent enriched, and the geometry was nothing like a weapon. The explosions that destroyed the reactor were, at their core, the explosions of a colossal steam boiler tearing itself apart, possibly with hydrogen and a brief flicker of runaway fission contributing. The energy released was a few hundred tonnes of TNT equivalent, enormous by the standards of an industrial accident, but thousands of times smaller than even a small nuclear weapon. The reactor destroyed itself, scattered its radioactive contents, and caught fire. It did not, and physically could not, detonate like a bomb. The danger of Chernobyl was never a bomb-like blast. The danger was the radioactive material flung into the air and spread across a continent, with half-lives measured in decades.

QUESTION: And the men who were blamed? What happened to them?

NARRATOR: The Soviet state, at first, blamed the operators almost entirely. Dyatlov, the plant director, and the chief engineer were tried and convicted in nineteen eighty-seven. Dyatlov was sentenced to ten years. But the truth, established by later international investigations, is more disturbing and more honest. Yes, the operators broke rules. They ran below the minimum reactivity margin, they proceeded at a prohibited power level, they overrode safety systems. But they did so partly under the command of a domineering superior, and far more importantly, they did so in ignorance of the fatal flaws designed into their reactor, the extreme positive void coefficient at low power, and the graphite-tipped control rods that could add reactivity in the first seconds of a shutdown. That knowledge had existed in the Soviet nuclear establishment, on paper, for years. It had been deliberately kept secret. The operators were handed a machine with a hidden trap and then blamed for falling into it. The man who led the Soviet scientific delegation that finally explained the accident to the world, the physicist Valery Legasov, became tormented by the cover-up and the failures of the system. He took his own life two years to the day after the explosion.

QUESTION: So what changed, after all of it?

NARRATOR: Everything, slowly. Every remaining RBMK reactor in the Soviet Union was modified to fix the flaws that had been hidden for so long. The fuel enrichment was raised, which cut that monstrous void coefficient down by a factor of six. Dozens of extra fixed neutron absorbers were installed in each core. The minimum number of control rods that had to stay in the reactor was raised. The shutdown system was made faster. The graphite tips were shortened to eliminate the positive scram effect. And operators were finally given real-time displays of the very safety margins they had been forced to calculate by hand. Beyond the hardware, the world's nuclear operators created an organisation to share safety information openly across borders, recognising that secrecy itself had been a cause of the disaster. The lesson Chernobyl burned into the world was not only about a void coefficient or a graphite tip. It was that a reactor is only as safe as the honesty of the people who design it and the knowledge given to the people who run it.

NARRATOR: For your HSC Physics course, this story is the evidence behind several of the most important ideas in Module eight, From the Universe to the Atom, specifically the dot-points on nuclear fission and the structure of a fission reactor. When the syllabus asks you to explain the process of nuclear fission, and to compare controlled and uncontrolled chain reactions, Chernobyl is the ultimate case study of both. Remember that the energy of fission comes from binding energy, the two medium-sized fragments being more tightly bound and more stable than the heavy uranium they split from, with the gain in stability released mostly as the kinetic energy of those fragments. A controlled chain reaction is one where, on average, exactly one neutron from each fission goes on to cause the next fission, holding the reactor at steady power. An uncontrolled chain reaction is one where each fission triggers more than one further fission, so the rate climbs without limit, and that is precisely what the positive void coefficient and the graphite tips unleashed in those three seconds. And when the syllabus asks you to compare the energy of nuclear reactions with fossil fuels and conventional explosives, hold onto the two anchors from this episode, that a single uranium fission releases some twenty million times the energy of burning one carbon atom, yet the whole blast that tore the core open was only a few hundred tonnes of TNT, thousands of times weaker than a real nuclear weapon. When you are asked to explain the structure and function of the four key components of a fission reactor, remember them through this story. The fuel, the slightly enriched uranium where fission occurs and energy is released. The moderator, the graphite that slowed the neutrons so they could be captured. The control rods, made of neutron-absorbing material, the throttle and the brake on the reaction rate. And the coolant, the water that carried the heat away to drive the turbine and, when it boiled, set the whole catastrophe in motion. And when you study radioactive decay and half-life, remember caesium-137, with its half-life of thirty years, the single number that explains why a zone the size of a small country must stay closed to human beings for three hundred years. The physics that destroyed Reactor Number Four had been known for years. The deepest lesson of Chernobyl is that knowing the physics is not enough.
