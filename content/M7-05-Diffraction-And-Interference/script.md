NARRATOR: Link two, Coherence, and to get there I need to bring in Huygens. Christiaan Huygens, a seventeenth-century Dutch physicist, gave us Huygens's Principle, which is the engine under diffraction. It says: every point on a wavefront acts as a source of tiny secondary circular wavelets that spread out in the direction of travel, and the new wavefront a moment later is the surface that touches the leading edge of all those wavelets, their envelope. That sounds abstract, but it does real work here. When a single flat wavefront of monochromatic light arrives at the barrier and reaches the two slits, each slit becomes a fresh point source of these spreading wavelets. The slit doesn't create the light, and this is a trap I'll hammer again later; the slit just becomes a new source of the light that was already there, re-radiating it as a spreading wave.

NARRATOR: Now the key consequence. Because both slits are lit by the same original wavefront, the two emerging waves are coherent. Coherent means two things together: same wavelength, and a constant, fixed phase relationship, the two waves keep in lockstep, never drifting relative to each other. That constant phase relationship is what makes a stable, sittable, photographable pattern possible. If the two sources drifted randomly in phase, the bright and dark bands would jitter around and smear into uniform grey faster than your eye could see. This is exactly why Young's genius was to take one source and split it into two with the slits, rather than using two separate lamps. Two separate lamps are incoherent, their phases are random and unrelated, and they produce no stable pattern whatsoever. One source, split in two, gives you two coherent sources for free. Say it in an answer: the two slits act as two coherent sources because they are illuminated by the same wavefront from a single monochromatic source.

NARRATOR: Links four and five, Constructive and Destructive, are where the pattern actually comes from, and the single idea that controls both is path difference. NARRATOR: Here is the rule for a bright band, constructive interference. NARRATOR: And here is the rule for a dark band, destructive interference.
