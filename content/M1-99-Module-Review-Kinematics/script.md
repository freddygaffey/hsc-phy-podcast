NARRATOR: This is the module review for Kinematics, Module One, and the job today is different from a normal lesson. We're not learning anything new. We're stepping back to see the four episodes as one connected whole, because the exam doesn't ask you about "displacement" in isolation, it hands you a scenario and expects you to pull graphs, equations and vectors together in a single answer. So today is about the connections and the integrated questions, and I'll lean on you to retrieve, not just listen. Here's the one-sentence spine of the whole module: kinematics is describing motion precisely, with the right quantities, and then predicting it, with graphs, equations and vectors. Four episodes, one spine. Let's reassemble them.

NARRATOR: Episode one gave us the language. A scalar has magnitude only; a vector has magnitude and direction. The vector trio of motion is D-V-A, displacement, velocity, acceleration, each needing a direction. Distance is the scalar total path; displacement is the vector net change in position, zero for a closed loop. Speed is distance over time; velocity is displacement over time; and average speed is not the magnitude of average velocity when the path reverses. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity, a vector, and a negative acceleration is a direction, not automatically "slowing down". And the discipline that runs through everything: choose a positive direction, state it, stay consistent.

NARRATOR: Episode two put motion onto graphs. The three rules, and the hook is "gradient climbs the ladder, area descends it": the gradient of a displacement-time graph is the velocity, the gradient of a velocity-time graph is the acceleration, and the area under a velocity-time graph is the displacement. A horizontal line means at rest on a displacement-time graph but constant velocity on a velocity-time graph, the most tested distinction in the topic. And on a velocity-time graph, area above the time axis is positive displacement, below is negative, so the net of the areas is displacement while the total is distance, episode one's distinction reappearing.

NARRATOR: Episode three gave us the equations of uniformly accelerated motion, the suvat equations, and the method: write the five quantities, spot the one that's missing, and choose the equation that omits it. V equals u plus a t has no displacement; s equals u t plus half a t squared has no final velocity; v squared equals u squared plus two a s has no time; s equals half, u plus v, times t has no acceleration. They work only for constant acceleration, and the headline case is motion under gravity, where the acceleration is g, about nine point eight metres per second per second downward, with the velocity zero but the acceleration still g at the top of a throw.

NARRATOR: And episode four lifted us into two dimensions. Resolve a vector into perpendicular components, from the horizontal the horizontal part is magnitude times cosine and the vertical is magnitude times sine, remembering adjacent is cosine and opposite is sine. Recombine with Pythagoras for the magnitude and the inverse tangent for the direction, never adding magnitudes as scalars. Perpendicular components are independent, which splits any plane problem into two. And the velocity of A relative to B is the velocity of A minus the velocity of B, a vector subtraction, built as a triangle in the boat, plane and two-car scenarios. The full toolkit is collected in Listing one, and the mnemonics in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Here they are. V equals u plus a t, no displacement. S equals u t plus half a t squared, no final velocity. V squared equals u squared plus two a s, no time. S equals half, u plus v, times t, no acceleration. If you had all four with their missing variables, your equation selection is solid, because in the exam you identify what's missing and the equation chooses itself. If you stumbled, that's the thing to drill tonight, the equations plus their gaps.

NARRATOR: Now let's see how the module connects, because that's what the integrated questions test. The deepest connection is that graphs and equations are the same physics. The suvat equations come straight out of the velocity-time graph: the gradient gives v equals u plus a t, and the area, the trapezium, gives s equals half, u plus v, times t and s equals u t plus half a t squared. So when a question hands you a velocity-time graph, you can either read it geometrically, gradient and area, or write the suvat equations, and they must agree. A strong student moves freely between the two. And the rate-of-change ladder ties episodes one, two and three together: velocity is the rate of change of displacement, acceleration the rate of change of velocity, and "rate of change" is exactly the gradient, which is why the graphs work.

QUESTION: So how would an exam actually combine these? Give me an example of a question that needs more than one episode.

NARRATOR: Here's the classic, and we'll work it fully, it's in Listing two. A ball is thrown horizontally, or a car accelerates and you're given a velocity-time graph and asked for both the acceleration and the distance, then asked to sketch the displacement-time graph, then given a second object and asked for the relative velocity. One scenario, four episodes. Let me do a concrete integrated one. A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly, reaching twenty-four metres per second after eight seconds, then travels at that constant velocity for a further ten seconds. Part one: find the acceleration during the first phase. Part two: find the total distance travelled. Part three: describe what the displacement-time graph looks like.

NARRATOR: Part one, the acceleration. This is the gradient of the velocity-time graph in the first phase, or equivalently suvat with u zero, v twenty-four, t eight, find a, displacement missing, so v equals u plus a t: twenty-four equals zero plus a times eight, so a is three metres per second per second. Graph and equation agree, the gradient is twenty-four over eight, three. Part two, the total distance, the area under the velocity-time graph. First phase is a triangle: half times eight times twenty-four, ninety-six metres. Second phase is a rectangle: ten times twenty-four, two hundred and forty metres. Total, three hundred and thirty-six metres. Part three, the displacement-time graph: in the first phase, constant acceleration, so the displacement-time graph is an upward curve getting steeper, a parabola; in the second phase, constant velocity, so it's a straight line sloping upward at a constant gradient of twenty-four. See how parts one and two used episodes two and three interchangeably, and part three used the graph-shape correspondence? That's an integrated question, and the marks reward moving between the representations.

QUESTION: This is a flagship integrated one, so pause the player and really attempt it. A projectile is launched at twenty-five metres per second at an angle of thirty degrees above the horizontal. Find the vertical component of the launch velocity, and then the time taken to reach the highest point. Take g as nine point eight. Pause now, work both parts, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the worked answer, and notice it's episode four then episode three, back to back. First, resolve the launch velocity into components, episode four. The vertical component is the magnitude times the sine of the angle, twenty-five times the sine of thirty degrees, and sine of thirty is zero point five, so the vertical component is twelve point five metres per second upward. Now the time to the highest point, episode three. Take up as positive. Vertically, the initial velocity is twelve point five, at the top the velocity is zero, the acceleration is minus nine point eight. Final velocity zero, displacement missing, use v equals u plus a t: zero equals twelve point five plus minus nine point eight times t, so nine point eight t equals twelve point five, t is about one point three seconds. So you resolved with episode four to get the vertical component, then fed it into episode three's suvat under gravity. That two-step, resolve then apply suvat to the vertical, is the whole of projectile motion next year, and it's why these two episodes are joined at the hip. The working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Let me do one more integrated example, because it ties graphs to the distance-versus-displacement distinction and it's a favourite. The full working is in Listing four. An object's velocity-time graph rises from zero to ten metres per second over two seconds, stays at ten for three seconds, then falls from ten down through zero to minus six metres per second over the next four seconds, the object has reversed direction. Find the total distance travelled and the net displacement. Break the area into pieces, watching the sign. From zero to five seconds the object moves in the positive direction: a triangle of half times two times ten, ten metres, plus a rectangle of three times ten, thirty metres, so forty metres positive. In the falling section, the line crosses zero somewhere; the part still above the axis adds positive area, and the part below the axis is negative displacement. Suppose the area above in that last section is about six metres and the area below is about nine metres. Then the displacement is forty plus six minus nine, thirty-seven metres in the positive direction, the net. But the distance is forty plus six plus nine, fifty-five metres, the total path, because distance ignores direction and just adds. Same graph: displacement thirty-seven metres, distance fifty-five metres. That single example exercises episode two's areas and episode one's distance-versus-displacement together, and the verb in the question, "distance" or "displacement", tells you whether to add or to net the areas.

NARRATOR: A word on exam technique for this module, because the same habits earn marks across every question. First, always write down the suvat list or sketch the graph before you compute, examiners award method marks for a clear setup even if the arithmetic slips. Second, state your positive direction at the start of any motion-under-gravity or relative-velocity problem, and sign every quantity by it. Third, give vector answers with a direction, never a bare number. Fourth, carry units through and round to a sensible two or three significant figures, an answer without units can be marked wrong. Fifth, when a graph is involved, decide whether you need a gradient or an area, and let the units confirm it, a gradient on a velocity-time graph gives an acceleration, an area gives a displacement. Those five habits are worth as much as knowing the physics, because in kinematics most lost marks are setup and discipline, not deep misunderstanding.

NARRATOR: Let me flag the traps that span the module in one pass, because a review is where you inoculate yourself. Trap one: a vector answer with no direction, displacement, velocity, acceleration all need a direction stated. Trap two: confusing distance with displacement, total versus net, including the area-above-versus-below on a velocity-time graph. Trap three: confusing the two horizontal-line meanings, at rest on a displacement-time graph, constant velocity on a velocity-time graph. Trap four: confusing the gradient with the area on a velocity-time graph, gradient is acceleration, area is displacement. Trap five: using suvat when the acceleration isn't constant. Trap six: sign errors under gravity, decide up or down as positive and apply it to g and every quantity. Trap seven: in resolution, swapping sine and cosine, adjacent is cosine from the reference angle. Trap eight: in relative velocity, adding when you must subtract, the rule is A minus B. Eight traps, and every one is a definitional or discipline slip, which means every one is a free mark once you watch for it.

NARRATOR: Now let me give you a worked weak-versus-strong on the most common integrated question type, "describe and analyse the motion shown in this velocity-time graph", because seeing the marking boundary is worth more than another perfect example. Suppose the graph rises from rest to a peak velocity, holds steady, then falls back to zero. A weak, band-four answer says: "The object speeds up, stays the same, then slows down and stops." That's a plain-English description of the picture and earns little. The strong answer translates every feature into physics: "In the first phase the velocity increases uniformly from rest, shown by the positive constant gradient, so the object has a constant positive acceleration. In the second phase the velocity is constant, shown by the horizontal line, so the acceleration is zero. In the third phase the velocity decreases uniformly to zero, a negative gradient, so the object decelerates. The total displacement is the area under the graph." Hear the difference: the strong answer names acceleration from the gradient, names constant velocity from the horizontal section, and names displacement from the area, the physics behind every feature. Feature of the graph, then the physical quantity it represents. That's the band-six move.

QUESTION: Retrieval before the final questions. State the relative-velocity rule, and use it for this: a car drives north at fifteen metres per second, a truck drives south at ten metres per second. What is the velocity of the car relative to the truck? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: The rule is: the velocity of A relative to B is the velocity of A minus the velocity of B. Here A is the car, fifteen north; B is the truck, ten south, which is minus ten north. So the velocity of the car relative to the truck is fifteen north minus, in brackets, ten south, and subtracting a southward ten is the same as adding a northward ten, giving twenty-five metres per second north. To someone in the truck, the car appears to approach, or recede, at twenty-five metres per second. That's why head-on closing speeds are so large, the velocities subtract, and subtracting an opposite velocity adds the magnitudes. The rule did it: A minus B.

QUESTION: Question one. A velocity-time graph shows an object accelerating uniformly from rest to twelve metres per second in four seconds, then holding twelve metres per second for six seconds. Calculate the acceleration in the first phase and the total displacement. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. The acceleration is the gradient of the first phase: twelve over four, three metres per second per second. The total displacement is the area: a triangle, half times four times twelve, twenty-four metres, plus a rectangle, six times twelve, seventy-two metres, giving ninety-six metres total. Three marks: acceleration from the gradient, the triangular area, and the rectangular area summed. The trap is forgetting the half on the triangle for the accelerating phase.

QUESTION: Question two. Distinguish between distance and displacement, and between speed and velocity, using the example of an athlete running one lap of a four-hundred-metre track. Three marks. Pause, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Distance and speed are scalars; displacement and velocity are vectors, with both magnitude and direction. For one complete lap, the distance travelled is four hundred metres, the total path length, but the displacement is zero, because the athlete finishes at the starting point. Consequently the average speed is non-zero, four hundred metres over the time taken, while the average velocity is zero, because displacement is zero. Three marks: scalar-versus-vector named; distance four hundred metres with displacement zero; and average speed non-zero with average velocity zero. This braids episode one's two key distinctions into one answer.

QUESTION: Question three. A ball is thrown vertically upward at nineteen point six metres per second. Taking up as positive and g as nine point eight, calculate the time to reach maximum height and the maximum height. Four marks. Pause, work both, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. Take up as positive, a is minus nine point eight, u is nineteen point six. At maximum height v is zero. Time: v equals u plus a t, zero equals nineteen point six minus nine point eight t, so t is two seconds. Maximum height: v squared equals u squared plus two a s, zero equals nineteen point six squared plus two times minus nine point eight times s, that's zero equals three hundred and eighty-four point two minus nineteen point six s, so s is about nineteen point six metres. Four marks: v zero at the top; time two seconds; correct equation for the height; and the height of about nineteen point six metres. The trap is a sign error on g, with up positive, g is negative.

QUESTION: Question four. A boat travels at five metres per second relative to the water, pointed straight across a river that flows at twelve metres per second. Wait, let me correct that to a cleaner set: the boat travels at five metres per second across, the river flows at twelve metres per second. Calculate the resultant speed of the boat relative to the bank and its direction. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. The across velocity, five, and the downstream velocity, twelve, are perpendicular, so the resultant is the hypotenuse: the square root of five squared plus twelve squared, the square root of twenty-five plus one hundred and forty-four, the square root of one hundred and sixty-nine, which is thirteen metres per second. The direction, downstream from straight across, has a tangent of twelve over five, about sixty-seven degrees downstream. So the boat moves at thirteen metres per second, about sixty-seven degrees downstream of straight across. Three marks: perpendicular vectors recognised, Pythagoras for thirteen, and the direction. Five, twelve, thirteen is a Pythagorean triple, a favourite of examiners. The trap is adding five and twelve to get seventeen.

QUESTION: Question five, the big integrated one. A stone is thrown horizontally from the top of a cliff at fifteen metres per second. It lands three seconds later. Taking g as nine point eight, calculate the height of the cliff and the horizontal distance from the base where it lands. Then state why the horizontal and vertical motions can be treated separately. Five marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, and this is projectile motion in miniature, episodes three and four together. Treat the vertical and horizontal motions separately. Vertically, take down as positive: the initial vertical velocity is zero, because the throw is horizontal, the acceleration is nine point eight, and the time is three seconds. The height is the vertical displacement, s equals u t plus half a t squared, zero plus half times nine point eight times three squared, half times nine point eight times nine, about forty-four metres. Horizontally, there's no acceleration, so the motion is at constant velocity: horizontal distance equals horizontal velocity times time, fifteen times three, forty-five metres. The horizontal and vertical motions can be treated separately because they are perpendicular, and perpendicular components of motion are independent, the horizontal velocity does not affect the vertical motion and vice versa, so gravity governs the vertical fall while the horizontal velocity stays constant. Five marks: separating the two directions; the cliff height of about forty-four metres from the vertical suvat; the horizontal distance of forty-five metres from constant-velocity motion; the initial vertical velocity being zero; and the statement that perpendicular components are independent. That last point, the independence, is the conceptual heart, and it's exactly the boat-river insight from episode four. Hold this question in mind, because next year in Advanced Mechanics, every projectile problem is this one.

QUESTION: Question six, a synthesis one. A car accelerates uniformly from ten metres per second to thirty metres per second over a distance of two hundred metres. Calculate the acceleration and the time taken, and explain how you could find the same distance from a velocity-time graph. Four marks. Pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. For the acceleration, time is missing, so use v squared equals u squared plus two a s: thirty squared equals ten squared plus two times a times two hundred, that's nine hundred equals one hundred plus four hundred a, so four hundred a equals eight hundred, a is two metres per second per second. For the time, now use v equals u plus a t: thirty equals ten plus two t, so two t equals twenty, t is ten seconds. From a velocity-time graph, you would plot velocity against time, with a straight line rising from ten to thirty metres per second over the ten seconds, and the distance would be the area under that line, a trapezium of average height twenty metres per second times width ten seconds, which is two hundred metres, matching the given distance. Four marks: acceleration two metres per second per second; time ten seconds; describing the graph; and identifying the distance as the trapezium area that equals two hundred metres. Notice the answer used episode three's equations and then confirmed the distance with episode two's area, the two representations agreeing, exactly the integration this module is built on.
