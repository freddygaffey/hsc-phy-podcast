NARRATOR: By the year nineteen thirty, physicists thought they understood what an atom was made of. There were electrons, discovered by J.J. Thomson back in eighteen ninety-seven. And there were protons, the heavy positive particles at the centre, identified by Ernest Rutherford in nineteen nineteen. Two ingredients. Negative electrons on the outside, positive protons in the core. It seemed like the recipe was complete.

NARRATOR: But there was a problem. A quiet, stubborn, embarrassing problem that physicists had been stepping around for years. And it was hiding in plain sight, in the simplest comparison you could possibly make. Take hydrogen, the lightest atom. It has one proton in its nucleus. Now take helium, the next one up. Helium has two protons. Two protons should weigh twice as much as one proton. So helium should be twice as heavy as hydrogen.

NARRATOR: It isn't. Helium is four times as heavy as hydrogen. Not two times. Four. There is twice as much mass in that helium nucleus as the protons can account for. Something is in there. Something heavy. Something that adds weight but adds no charge, because the chemistry says helium carries exactly two units of positive charge, no more. Whatever this extra stuff was, it was invisible to every instrument they had. It left no track in a cloud chamber. It bent in no magnetic field. It triggered no detector. For more than a decade, the heart of the atom kept a secret that nobody could see.

NARRATOR: This is the story of how one man, working in a cold Cambridge laboratory over a single feverish fortnight in the winter of nineteen thirty-two, finally caught the thing that wasn't there. He didn't see it. Nobody could see it. He proved it existed using nothing but a block of wax, a stream of mystery radiation, and two of the most reliable laws in all of physics. The law of conservation of energy. And the law of conservation of momentum.

[pause]

NARRATOR: Let me set the stage properly, because to understand why this was so hard, you have to understand how blind everyone was. In the nineteen twenties, the way you detected a particle was by its charge. A charged particle moving through gas rips electrons off the atoms it passes, leaving a trail of ionisation. In a cloud chamber, that trail shows up as a visible line of tiny droplets. Put the chamber in a magnetic field, and the trail curves. From the curve you read off the charge and the momentum. This was the entire toolkit. Charge was everything. Charge was how you saw.

NARRATOR: So imagine trying to find a particle that has no charge at all. It rips no electrons loose. It leaves no trail. It curves in no field. It glides through the chamber like a ghost through a wall, and your beautiful detector sees absolutely nothing. A neutral particle is, almost by definition, an invisible particle. This is why the mass problem festered for so long. Everyone could feel the missing weight in the arithmetic, but nobody could devise an experiment to catch the culprit.

NARRATOR: There was, at the time, an answer that a lot of clever people believed. Maybe the nucleus contained extra protons, and to cancel out their charge, it also contained extra electrons buried inside it. A proton and an electron stuck tightly together would have roughly the mass of a proton and a charge of nearly zero. On paper, that fixes the helium problem. You just say the helium nucleus is four protons and two electrons, all packed together. The mass works out to four, the net charge works out to two. Problem solved.

NARRATOR: Except it wasn't solved. It was a fudge, and the deepest thinkers knew it. There were terrible difficulties with the idea of an electron trapped inside the nucleus. The new quantum mechanics, which was being built in those very years, said that to confine an electron into a space as small as a nucleus, only a few femtometres across, you would need to give it an enormous energy. The electron's quantum wavelength was simply too large to fit. It was like trying to keep an ocean wave inside a teacup. The mathematics rebelled. The nuclear electron was a patch that papered over the crack without fixing the wall behind it.

NARRATOR: One man had already guessed the real answer, and he had guessed it years before anyone could prove it. Ernest Rutherford. In nineteen twenty, in a grand address to the Royal Society called the Bakerian Lecture, Rutherford floated an idea that sounded almost like science fiction. He suggested there might exist a particle with a mass close to that of the proton, but carrying no electric charge whatsoever. A neutral twin of the proton. He even gave it a name. The neutron.

NARRATOR: Rutherford imagined it might be a proton and an electron bound together so intimately that from the outside it looked like a single neutral object. He didn't have the modern picture, but the instinct was dead right. A heavy, neutral particle in the nucleus would explain the missing mass at a stroke. And because it had no charge, it would explain why nobody could detect it. The trouble was, an idea is not a discovery. Rutherford could propose the neutron. He could not find it. And neither could his loyal younger colleague, a quiet Englishman named James Chadwick, who spent much of the nineteen twenties hunting for the particle in the basement of the Cavendish Laboratory and coming up empty, year after year after year.

[pause]

NARRATOR: Now the scene shifts to Germany, to the year nineteen thirty. Two physicists, Walther Bothe and his student Herbert Becker, were doing what physicists of that era loved to do. They were taking a radioactive source that spat out alpha particles, and using those alpha particles as tiny bullets to bombard light elements, to see what came flying out. Alpha particles, remember, are helium nuclei. Two protons and two neutrons, though of course nobody yet knew about the neutrons. They are fast, heavy, positively charged projectiles. Fire them at a target and sometimes they smash into a nucleus and knock something loose.

NARRATOR: Bothe and Becker fired their alpha particles at a sample of beryllium, the light metal that sits at element number four. And beryllium did something strange. It gave off a new kind of radiation. A radiation that was astonishingly penetrating. It would pass clean through several centimetres of lead, the kind of barrier that stops most things cold. Whatever this radiation was, it carried no charge, because it wasn't deflected and it ploughed through matter like the matter wasn't there.

NARRATOR: Bothe and Becker reached for the only neutral radiation they knew. Gamma rays. High-energy photons, packets of pure electromagnetic energy, the most penetrating radiation in the textbooks. They concluded the beryllium was emitting unusually energetic gamma rays, and they estimated the energy at around ten million electron volts. That is a colossal energy for a gamma ray. But gamma rays were neutral, gamma rays were penetrating, and gamma rays were familiar. So gamma rays it was. They published, and they moved on. They had the discovery in their hands and they put the wrong label on it.

NARRATOR: I want to pause on that, because it's the heart of how science actually works. Bothe and Becker were excellent experimentalists. They saw the effect clearly. Their measurements were sound. Their mistake was one of interpretation, of reaching for the explanation that already existed instead of inventing the one that didn't. The beryllium radiation was real. The penetration was real. The neutrality was real. But the word gamma was wrong, and that single wrong word would send the next group of physicists chasing a contradiction they could not resolve.

[pause]

NARRATOR: That next group was in Paris, and they were physics royalty. Irène Joliot-Curie, the daughter of Marie Curie, who had grown up in the most famous laboratory in the history of radioactivity. And her husband, Frédéric Joliot. Together, the Joliot-Curies were among the finest experimental physicists in the world, and they decided to investigate this mysterious beryllium radiation more closely. It was early nineteen thirty-two.

NARRATOR: They took the penetrating radiation coming off the beryllium, and they let it strike a block of paraffin wax. Now, why wax? Because paraffin is a hydrocarbon. It is absolutely packed with hydrogen atoms, and a hydrogen nucleus is simply a single proton. So paraffin wax is, in effect, a brick full of loose protons, sitting there waiting to be knocked out. And knocked out they were. When the beryllium radiation hit the wax, protons came flying out of the front of it. Fast protons. The Joliot-Curies measured their speed, and the protons were screaming out of that wax at enormous velocity, carrying an energy of several million electron volts.

NARRATOR: The Joliot-Curies, like Bothe and Becker before them, looked at this and reached for the familiar answer. The radiation was gamma rays, they said, and somehow these gamma rays were knocking the protons loose. They were thinking of something like the Compton effect, where a photon bounces off a charged particle and gives it a kick. So they published their result in the spring of nineteen thirty-two. Mysterious neutral radiation from beryllium ejects high-speed protons from paraffin. Probably gamma rays.

NARRATOR: And here is where one of the great moments in the history of physics takes place. Because across the English Channel, in Cambridge, that paper landed on the desk of James Chadwick. And Chadwick read it on a Friday morning, and Chadwick did not believe it for one second.

[pause]

NARRATOR: To understand Chadwick's reaction, you have to understand the man and his obsession. James Chadwick had been hunting the neutron for more than a decade. He had absorbed Rutherford's nineteen twenty prediction directly from Rutherford himself. He had searched for the particle and failed and searched again. The neutron lived in his mind as a real thing waiting to be found. So when he read the Joliot-Curie paper describing a neutral radiation from beryllium that punched protons out of wax, he didn't see gamma rays. He saw his ghost. He saw the particle he had been chasing his entire career, mislabelled by people who weren't looking for it.

NARRATOR: But Chadwick was a careful scientist, and feeling something is not proving it. He had a specific, devastating physical objection to the gamma-ray explanation, and it came straight from the most basic mechanics. Think about what it takes to knock a proton forward at high speed. A proton is a heavy particle. To give it a violent shove, whatever hits it must carry real momentum, and it must transfer a serious chunk of that momentum in the collision.

NARRATOR: Now, a gamma ray is a photon. It has energy, and it carries momentum, but for a given energy a photon carries very little momentum, because its momentum is just its energy divided by the speed of light, and the speed of light is an enormous number. A photon is, in a sense, momentum-poor. It is like trying to knock over a bowling ball by throwing a ping-pong ball at it. To move the bowling ball at all, you would have to throw the ping-pong ball impossibly hard.

NARRATOR: So Chadwick did the calculation. He asked: if the radiation really is gamma rays, how much energy would each photon need to carry, in order to knock a proton out of that wax at the measured speed? And the answer was staggering. To slam a proton forward at the velocity the Joliot-Curies observed, a gamma-ray photon would need an energy of around fifty million electron volts. Fifty. That is an absurdly large energy for a gamma ray, far beyond anything seen before.

NARRATOR: And then came the killing blow. Where would that fifty million electron volts even come from? The only energy source in the whole experiment was the alpha particles striking the beryllium, and those alpha particles carried an energy of only about five million electron volts. The cupboard contained five million electron volts of energy. But the gamma-ray story demanded fifty. You cannot pour fifty units of energy out of a jug that holds five. That is a tenfold increase, energy appearing from nowhere. It is not a small discrepancy you could blame on a sloppy measurement. It is a flat violation of the conservation of energy. The gamma-ray explanation didn't just look unlikely. It was impossible. It broke the most sacred law in physics.

[pause]

NARRATOR: This is the moment the case study turns. The Joliot-Curies had been willing to swallow that impossibility because the alternative hadn't occurred to them. Chadwick refused to swallow it, because for him the alternative was the whole point of his life. So he asked the question the other way around. Forget what the radiation is supposed to be. Look at what it does, and ask what it would have to be to do that.

NARRATOR: What kind of particle knocks a proton forward at exactly the right speed without needing absurd amounts of energy? The answer, from simple mechanics, is a particle with roughly the same mass as the proton itself. Think about it on a billiard table. When the cue ball strikes another ball head-on, and the two balls have equal mass, the cue ball can stop dead and hand over almost all its speed to the ball it hit. The transfer is beautifully efficient. A heavy thing hitting a light thing barely shoves it; a light thing hitting a heavy thing bounces off; but two equal masses pass momentum between them cleanly and completely.

NARRATOR: So if the beryllium radiation were a stream of neutral particles, each one weighing about as much as a proton, then a head-on collision would transfer almost all of the particle's speed to the proton. No miracle energy required. The neutral particle just needs to be moving at the speed you'd expect from the modest few million electron volts the reaction actually supplies, and conservation of momentum does the rest. The numbers stop screaming. The contradiction dissolves. Everything balances.

NARRATOR: This neutral particle, with the mass of a proton and no charge, was exactly the neutron that Rutherford had dreamed up twelve years earlier. The radiation from beryllium was never gamma rays at all. It was a beam of free neutrons, the first ever produced by human hands, and three separate teams of brilliant physicists had held them and failed to recognise what they were holding.

[pause]

QUESTION: Here is the kind of reasoning the examiner wants you to reproduce. Explain why the high speed of the protons ejected from the paraffin wax was evidence against the gamma-ray hypothesis, and how it pointed instead to a neutral particle with mass similar to that of a proton.

NARRATOR: Work through it the way Chadwick did. First, the energy argument. To eject the protons at their measured speed using gamma rays, each photon would need an energy of about fifty million electron volts. But the incident alpha particles supply only about five million electron volts. The gamma-ray hypothesis therefore demands roughly ten times more energy than the reaction can supply, which violates the conservation of energy. That alone rules photons out.

NARRATOR: Second, the momentum argument. A photon carries very little momentum for its energy, so it is extraordinarily inefficient at shoving a heavy proton forward. But a massive neutral particle, with a mass close to the proton's, transfers momentum to a proton with great efficiency, just like two equal billiard balls in a head-on collision. So the observed protons fit a massive neutral projectile perfectly, while breaking the photon picture. Conservation of energy and conservation of momentum, applied together, do not merely suggest the neutron. They force it.

[pause]

NARRATOR: Now let me take you into the actual experiment, because Chadwick did not just sit and think. The week he spent in February nineteen thirty-two has become legendary in physics, the famous fortnight in which one man nailed down a particle that had eluded everyone for a generation. He read the Joliot-Curie paper, and within days he had commandeered the apparatus and begun his own version, refined and aimed precisely at the question of mass.

NARRATOR: Chadwick built his source from polonium, an intensely radioactive element that pours out alpha particles. He placed the polonium next to a disc of beryllium, so the alpha particles would strike the beryllium and generate the mysterious radiation. Then he let that radiation strike his paraffin wax and measured the protons it ejected, finding they came off at a speed of about three point three times ten to the seven metres per second. That is over thirty million metres per second, around a tenth of the speed of light. Genuinely fast protons.

NARRATOR: But Chadwick did something cleverer than just measuring the protons from wax. He replaced the wax with other targets. He let the radiation strike nitrogen, and measured how fast it knocked nitrogen nuclei forward. He used different elements with different known masses, and measured the recoil speed of each one. And this is the masterstroke, because now he had a set of equations he could actually solve.

NARRATOR: Here is the logic, and it is pure conservation of momentum and conservation of energy applied to an elastic collision. Imagine an unknown neutral particle, with an unknown mass and an unknown speed, striking a stationary proton head-on. Conservation of momentum says the total momentum before equals the total momentum after. Conservation of kinetic energy, for an elastic collision, says the total kinetic energy before equals the total after. With those two laws you can write down exactly how fast the proton will recoil, in terms of the unknown particle's mass and speed. Then do the same for nitrogen, which has a known and much larger mass. Now you have two equations describing the same mystery particle hitting two different known targets, and you measure both recoil speeds.

NARRATOR: Two equations, two unknowns. The unknown speed of the neutron cancels out of the ratio, and what survives is the mass of the neutron itself, written purely in terms of things Chadwick had measured. He turned the crank. He compared the recoil of the hydrogen nuclei with the recoil of the nitrogen nuclei, and out of the algebra dropped a number for the mass of the neutral particle. The full working is laid out step by step in Listing one, and the masses and energies he was juggling are collected in Listing two.

NARRATOR: The number Chadwick got was that the neutron has a mass of about one point one five times the mass of a proton. The modern, precise value is that the neutron is one point zero zero one four times the mass of the proton. The neutron is, to extraordinary accuracy, just a hair heavier than the proton. Chadwick, working with crude detectors and a fortnight of frantic effort, landed within fifteen per cent of the truth, and crucially he proved the essential point beyond argument: this particle has mass comparable to a proton, and it has no charge. It was the neutron. After twelve years of prediction and a decade of failed searches, James Chadwick had found it.

NARRATOR: He wrote it up at once. His first short paper, with the deliberately cautious title Possible Existence of a Neutron, was submitted to the journal Nature on the seventeenth of February, nineteen thirty-two. He had read the Joliot-Curie paper only days before. The legend, encouraged by Chadwick himself, is that he cracked it in a single week, and the documented timeline is barely longer. From reading the clue to submitting the discovery, just over a fortnight. He had been preparing for that fortnight, though, for the better part of twelve years.

NARRATOR: And notice the modesty in that title. Possible existence. Chadwick knew that his whole argument rested on the conservation of energy and the conservation of momentum, and he knew that at that very moment, in nineteen thirty-two, some of the greatest physicists alive were openly questioning whether those classical conservation laws even applied inside the nucleus. Niels Bohr himself, the towering figure of quantum theory, suspected that energy might not be perfectly conserved in nuclear processes. So Chadwick ended his paper with a scientist's honest caveat. All the evidence, he wrote, points to the neutron, unless the conservation of energy and momentum should fail at some point inside the nucleus. He was staking his discovery on the two laws, and saying so out loud. The laws held. Bohr was eventually proven wrong, and the neutron was real.

[pause]

NARRATOR: So what did it mean? Why does this quiet, careful piece of detective work rank among the most consequential experiments of the twentieth century? Because the neutron was the missing key to the entire nucleus, and once you had it, the whole subject of nuclear physics burst open.

NARRATOR: First, the mass problem vanished instantly. Helium is four times as heavy as hydrogen because its nucleus contains two protons and two neutrons. Four particles, each with about the mass of a proton, two of them charged and two of them neutral. The charge comes out as two, the mass comes out as four, and the embarrassing arithmetic that had haunted physics for a decade simply resolved itself. Every element fell into place. The number of protons gives you the charge and the chemistry. The protons plus the neutrons give you the mass. Isotopes, those puzzling variants of an element with the same chemistry but different weights, were suddenly obvious. They were just atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

NARRATOR: But the deeper consequence was about what the neutron could do. Remember the original problem of detecting charged particles. A charged particle trying to enter a nucleus is violently repelled, because the nucleus is positively charged and like charges push apart. To force a proton or an alpha particle into a nucleus, you have to fire it incredibly hard to overcome that electrical wall. But the neutron has no charge. The electrical wall does not exist for it. A neutron can drift right up to a nucleus, however gently, and slip inside without any resistance at all.

NARRATOR: This made the neutron the perfect tool for reaching into the atomic nucleus and rearranging it. Within just a few years, physicists were firing neutrons at heavy elements and watching nuclei transform. In nineteen thirty-eight, Otto Hahn, Lise Meitner and Fritz Strassmann discovered that when a neutron strikes a uranium nucleus, the nucleus can split roughly in two, releasing an enormous burst of energy and, critically, releasing more neutrons. Those new neutrons can split more uranium nuclei, which release still more neutrons, and you have a chain reaction. That discovery is nuclear fission, and it leads directly to nuclear reactors and to the atomic bomb. None of it is possible without the neutron, and the neutron is not possible without Chadwick's fortnight.

NARRATOR: James Chadwick received the Nobel Prize in Physics in nineteen thirty-five, just three years after the discovery, an almost unheard-of speed that tells you how immediately the importance was recognised. He went on to lead the British contribution to the Manhattan Project during the Second World War, his neutron having become the trigger of the nuclear age. The Joliot-Curies, for their part, were not left empty-handed. They turned to other reactions and discovered artificial radioactivity, the creation of new radioactive isotopes, and won their own Nobel Prize, in Chemistry, in that same year of nineteen thirty-five. Frédéric Joliot reflected ruefully that if only he had read Rutherford's old lecture about the neutron, he and Irène might well have identified the particle before Chadwick. They had it in their hands. They just called it by the wrong name.

[pause]

NARRATOR: There is a lesson buried in this story that goes beyond the physics, and it is worth saying plainly. Three teams of first-rate scientists produced free neutrons in their laboratories. Bothe and Becker in Germany. The Joliot-Curies in Paris. And each of them looked at the evidence and reached for the explanation that was already lying around, the familiar gamma ray. Chadwick's genius was not that he had better equipment. It was that he took the data seriously enough to follow it where it led, even when where it led was uncomfortable and strange. He trusted conservation of energy and conservation of momentum more than he trusted the comfortable story, and when the comfortable story violated those laws, he threw out the story, not the laws.

NARRATOR: And that brings us to where this sits in your course. In the New South Wales H S C Physics syllabus, within the module Matter, Energy and the Cosmos, you are asked to analyse how the law of conservation of energy and the law of conservation of momentum were applied in experiments to determine the existence and properties of the neutron. That syllabus point is, almost word for word, the story you have just heard. The existence of the neutron was established because gamma rays would have violated conservation of energy, needing fifty million electron volts from a reaction whose alpha particles could supply only about five. The properties of the neutron, its mass of about one point one five proton masses and its lack of charge, were pinned down by applying conservation of momentum and conservation of energy to elastic collisions with protons and nitrogen nuclei. When you sit the exam and you are asked about Chadwick, this is what you tell them. A neutral particle, invisible to every detector, caught not by being seen but by being reasoned into existence with two laws that refused to be broken.
