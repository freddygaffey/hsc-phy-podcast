NARRATOR: Welcome back to Module Four, Electricity and Magnetism, and to the last new topic of Year Eleven Physics: magnetism. We've built electric charge, the force between charges, the electric field, and then current and circuits. Today we meet magnetism, which feels at first like a separate phenomenon, fridge magnets and compass needles, but which turns out to be intimately linked to electricity, because moving charges, electric currents, produce magnetic fields. That link is the seed of the entire Module Six next year, electromagnetism. By the end of this episode you'll be able to describe magnetic poles and fields, explain how materials become magnetised, work out the magnetic field around a current-carrying wire and inside a solenoid with the right-hand rule, and see how an electromagnet works. This is the bridge from Year Eleven straight into Year Twelve. But first, a recap, because the field idea carries directly across.

NARRATOR: Earlier this module, in the electrostatics episode, we built the idea of a field: a region of space where an object feels a force, represented by field lines that show direction and strength, lines closer together where the field is stronger, lines that never cross. We had electric field lines running from positive to negative charges. Today we reuse that whole apparatus for magnetism: magnetic poles instead of charges, magnetic field lines instead of electric ones, but the same rules of reading direction and strength from the lines. Hold the field-line method from electrostatics, because today it just gets a magnetic version. And hold one more thing, from last episode: an electric current is moving charge, because the headline of today is that moving charge creates magnetism.

QUESTION: Quick check from the electrostatics episode. In a field-line diagram, what does the spacing of the lines tell you, and can field lines ever cross? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The spacing of field lines shows the strength of the field: lines close together mean a strong field, lines far apart mean a weak field. And no, field lines can never cross, because the field has only one direction at each point, and a crossing would mean two directions at once. Spacing shows strength, and lines never cross. Those exact rules carry over to magnetic field lines today, so you already know how to read them. Now let's meet magnetic poles.

NARRATOR: Every magnet has two poles, called north and south, and the fundamental rule mirrors the one for charge: like poles repel, and unlike poles attract. Two north poles push apart, two souths push apart, but a north and a south pull together. So far it looks just like electric charge, but there's a crucial difference, and the syllabus wants it: magnetic poles always come in pairs. You can have an isolated positive or negative charge, but you cannot have an isolated north or south pole. If you cut a bar magnet in half hoping to separate the poles, you don't get a lone north and a lone south, you get two smaller, complete magnets, each with its own north and south. No matter how finely you divide it, every piece has both poles. A single, isolated pole, called a magnetic monopole, has never been found. So: like poles repel, unlike attract, and poles are inseparable, always in north-south pairs. Those rules are in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Now magnetic materials, because not everything responds to a magnet. The materials that can be strongly magnetised and are attracted to magnets are called ferromagnetic, and there are only a few common ones: iron, nickel, cobalt, and some of their alloys, like steel. Most materials, wood, plastic, copper, aluminium, are not magnetic in this way. What makes a material ferromagnetic is its internal structure, which the syllabus describes in terms of domains. Inside a ferromagnetic material there are tiny regions called magnetic domains, each of which is itself like a miniature magnet with a north and south, because the magnetic effects of its atoms are aligned. In an unmagnetised piece of iron, these domains point in random directions, so their magnetic effects cancel out and the iron has no overall magnetism. When you place the iron in an external magnetic field, the domains rotate to line up with that field, and once they're aligned their individual magnetic fields add together, giving the whole piece a strong net magnetism, it has become a magnet. That domain-alignment picture is in Listing one, and explaining it is a guaranteed exam item.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "explain, in terms of domains, how a ferromagnetic material becomes magnetised". The band-six version: a ferromagnetic material contains many tiny regions called domains, each of which acts as a small magnet because the magnetic fields of its atoms are aligned within it. In an unmagnetised sample, the domains are oriented randomly, so their magnetic fields point in all directions and cancel out, giving no overall magnetism. When the sample is placed in an external magnetic field, the domains rotate and align so that they point in the direction of the applied field; their now-aligned magnetic fields add together, producing a strong net magnetic field, so the material has become magnetised. The marks are for: domains as small aligned-atom magnets, random orientation cancelling in the unmagnetised state, and alignment with an external field adding up to give net magnetisation. The weak answer, "the iron lines up with the magnet", earns little because it never mentions domains or the random-to-aligned change. Name the domains and the random-to-aligned transition.

QUESTION: A retrieval one. Name the main ferromagnetic elements. And what happens to the domains in a piece of iron, first when it is unmagnetised, and then when it becomes magnetised? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The main ferromagnetic elements are iron, nickel and cobalt, plus alloys like steel. In an unmagnetised piece of iron, the magnetic domains point in random directions, so their magnetic fields cancel and there is no overall magnetism. When the iron becomes magnetised, the domains align, rotating to point in the same direction, usually along an applied field, so their fields add to give a strong net magnetisation. Iron, nickel, cobalt; random domains cancel, aligned domains add. If a magnet is heated or hammered hard, by the way, the domains can be jostled back out of alignment, which is why magnets can lose their magnetism, a nice consequence of the domain picture.

NARRATOR: The domain idea also explains a useful distinction between two kinds of magnetic material, soft and hard, which matters for choosing the right material for a job. A magnetically soft material, like pure iron, magnetises easily when a field is applied but also loses its magnetism easily when the field is removed, because its domains align readily but slip back to random just as readily. A magnetically hard material, like steel, is harder to magnetise but holds its magnetism once magnetised, because its domains, once aligned, resist going back. So you make a permanent magnet, like a fridge magnet or a compass needle, from a magnetically hard material that stays magnetised; but you make the core of an electromagnet, which you want to switch on and off cleanly, from a magnetically soft material like iron, so that it becomes strongly magnetic when the current is on and loses its magnetism the instant the current is switched off. Choosing soft iron for a switchable electromagnet and hard steel for a permanent magnet is a direct, examinable application of the domain picture.

NARRATOR: Now the magnetic field itself. A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where a magnetic force would be felt, and we represent it with magnetic field lines, just like the electric case. The rules: outside a magnet, the field lines run from the north pole to the south pole, that's the defined direction, the direction the north end of a tiny compass would point; the lines are closer together where the field is stronger, near the poles; and they never cross. Around a bar magnet, the lines curve out of the north pole, loop around through the surrounding space, and come back into the south pole, forming closed loops. The direction is conventionally taken as the direction of the force on an imaginary tiny north pole. So a compass needle, which is itself a small magnet, lines up along the field, its north end pointing the way the field points, which is exactly how a compass reveals a field's direction. Field lines from north to south, density showing strength, are in Listing two.

NARRATOR: A beautiful example is the Earth itself, which behaves like a giant bar magnet, with a magnetic field that a compass responds to. Here's a subtlety worth a mark: the Earth's geographic North Pole is actually near a magnetic south pole. Why? Because the north end of a compass needle is attracted to it, and unlike poles attract, so the pole up near the geographic north must be a magnetic south. We just call the compass end that points north its "north-seeking pole". The Earth's field is what makes compasses work for navigation, and it also shields us from charged particles streaming from the Sun, which spiral down the field lines near the poles and create the auroras, the northern and southern lights. The Earth's field is noted in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Now the heart of today, the deep link between electricity and magnetism: a moving charge, an electric current, produces a magnetic field. This was discovered in 1820 when the Danish physicist Hans Christian Oersted noticed, during a lecture, that a compass needle deflected whenever he switched on a current in a nearby wire, and swung back when he switched it off. It was a landmark moment: for the first time, electricity and magnetism, long thought to be entirely separate forces, were shown to be linked, two faces of a single phenomenon. That unification is one of the great threads of nineteenth-century physics, and it's the very link you'll build on throughout Module Six. For a long straight current-carrying wire, the magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire, circular loops centred on the wire, lying in planes perpendicular to it. The direction of these circles is given by the right-hand grip rule: if you grip the wire with your right hand, with your thumb pointing in the direction of the conventional current, your curled fingers point in the direction of the magnetic field around the wire. The field gets weaker with distance from the wire. Quantitatively, the field strength equals the permeability of free space times the current, divided by two pi times the distance from the wire, B equals mu-nought I over two pi r. So the field is proportional to the current, double the current and you double the field, and inversely proportional to the distance, twice as far means half the field. The wire's circular field, the grip rule, and that equation are in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Let me put a number on the wire's field so you've seen the substitution, it's in Listing three. Suppose a long straight wire carries a current of ten amperes, and you want the field strength at a distance of five centimetres, that's zero point zero five metres, from it. The field is B equals mu-nought I over two pi r, with mu-nought, the permeability of free space, equal to four pi times ten to the minus seven. Substitute: four pi times ten to the minus seven, times ten, divided by two pi times zero point zero five. The pi cancels top and bottom, leaving roughly four times ten to the minus five tesla, that's about forty microtesla, comparable to the Earth's own field. Notice how small everyday magnetic fields from wires are, and notice the structure: ten times the current would give ten times the field, while ten times the distance would give a tenth of the field. The unit of magnetic field strength, by the way, is the tesla.

QUESTION: Let's use the right-hand grip rule. A vertical wire carries conventional current straight upward. Using the grip rule, which way does the magnetic field circulate around it, as seen looking down from above, clockwise or anticlockwise? Pause the player, work it out with your right hand, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Point the thumb of your right hand straight up, in the direction of the current; your fingers curl anticlockwise as seen from above. So the magnetic field circulates anticlockwise around the wire when viewed from above, looking down. If you found it clockwise, you probably used your left hand or pointed your thumb down, it's the right hand, thumb along the conventional current. Getting this direction right is a recurring exam mark, and it's absolutely essential next year for the motor effect, so practise it now with an actual wire and your actual right hand. The grip rule is in Listing three.

NARRATOR: Now wind that wire into a coil, and you get a solenoid, one of the most important arrangements in physics. A solenoid is a long coil of wire; when current flows through it, the circular fields of all the individual turns combine and reinforce, producing a magnetic field that, remarkably, looks just like that of a bar magnet, with a north pole at one end and a south pole at the other, and a strong, nearly uniform field running straight down the middle inside the coil. You find which end is north using a right-hand rule for the solenoid: curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction the current flows around the coils, and your thumb points to the north end. The field strength inside a solenoid is the permeability of free space times the number of turns times the current, divided by the length, B equals mu-nought N I over L. So you make the field stronger by increasing the current, adding more turns, or packing the turns closer, more turns per metre. Notice it's the number of turns per unit length that matters, the turns divided by the length, so a short coil wound with many tight turns gives a stronger field than the same number of turns spread thinly over a long coil. The solenoid's bar-magnet-like field, its right-hand rule, and that equation are in Listing three, with a worked example.

QUESTION: A retrieval check on the two fields. Describe the shape of the magnetic field around a long straight wire, and the shape of the field produced by a solenoid. How do they differ? Pause the player, describe both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. Around a long straight wire, the magnetic field forms concentric circles centred on the wire, lying in planes at right angles to it, with the direction given by the right-hand grip rule and the strength falling off with distance. A solenoid, a coil of wire, produces a field that looks like a bar magnet's: field lines emerge from one end, the north, loop around the outside, and return to the other end, the south, with a strong, nearly uniform field down the central axis inside the coil. So they differ in shape: the straight wire gives circles around it, while the solenoid gives a bar-magnet pattern with distinct poles and a uniform interior field. Circles for the wire, bar-magnet pattern for the solenoid, don't confuse the two, that's a classic trap.

NARRATOR: The solenoid leads straight to the electromagnet, a hugely useful device and the bridge into Module Six. If you place a piece of iron, a ferromagnetic core, inside a solenoid, the iron's domains align with the coil's field and add their own magnetism to it, making the overall magnetic field far stronger, often hundreds of times stronger, than the coil alone. That's an electromagnet: a solenoid with an iron core. Its great advantage over a permanent magnet is control, it's a magnet only while the current flows, so you can switch it on and off, reverse its poles by reversing the current, and adjust its strength by adjusting the current. This is why electromagnets lift cars in scrapyards and then drop them by switching off, why they sort scrap steel from aluminium, why they slam shut the door of a security lock when the power is on, and it's the working principle inside electric motors, generators, transformers, loudspeakers and maglev trains, all of which you'll study next year. The electromagnet combines today's two big ideas, the current's magnetic field and the magnetisation of iron, and it's in Listing two.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants investigations. You can map a bar magnet's field by sprinkling iron filings on paper over it, they line up along the field lines, or by moving a small compass around it and marking the needle's direction. You can show the field around a current-carrying wire by passing the wire vertically through a horizontal card with iron filings or small compasses on it, switching on the current, and seeing the circular pattern, then reversing the current to see the circles reverse, confirming the grip rule. You can build a simple electromagnet by winding insulated wire around an iron nail and connecting a battery, then count how many paperclips it lifts as you vary the current or the number of turns, testing B equals mu-nought N I over L qualitatively. A key examinable point is that the field, and the lifting power, increase with both the current and the number of turns. Describing such an investigation and its result is examinable.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: thinking you can isolate a single magnetic pole, you can't, cutting a magnet just makes two smaller complete magnets; poles always come in pairs. Trap two: using the left hand, or the wrong thumb direction, for the grip rule, it's the right hand, thumb along the conventional current, fingers curling the way of the field. Trap three: confusing the wire's field, concentric circles, with the solenoid's field, bar-magnet-like with poles, they're different shapes. Trap four: forgetting that the field depends on the current, so no current means no magnetic field from a wire or solenoid, and more current means more field. Trap five: getting the Earth muddled, the geographic north is a magnetic south, because it attracts the north end of a compass. Avoid those five and magnetism is secure.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A student winds a coil of wire around an iron nail and connects it to a battery, making an electromagnet.
