NARRATOR: Welcome back to Module Three, Waves and Thermodynamics. Last episode we built the wave vocabulary: a wave transfers energy without transferring matter, we met transverse and longitudinal, mechanical and electromagnetic, the wave quantities, and the wave equation, speed equals frequency times wavelength. Today we put waves to work. We ask what waves actually do, how they behave when they hit a boundary, cross into a new medium, squeeze through a gap, and, most importantly, what happens when two waves meet in the same place at the same time. By the end you'll be able to describe reflection, refraction and diffraction, apply the principle of superposition, explain interference and standing waves, and describe resonance. These behaviours are the heart of the whole topic, and they return next year as the wave model of light. But first, a quick recap.

NARRATOR: Last episode's headline was that a wave is energy on the move through a medium, with the particles only oscillating about fixed points. We had the wave equation, speed equals frequency times wavelength, and the crucial fact that the wave speed is set by the medium, not by the source, so if the frequency changes the wavelength adjusts to keep the speed right, and if the medium changes the speed changes. Hold that last point especially, because it's the engine of refraction today. And hold the idea of phase, where a particle is in its cycle, crest, trough, or in between, because today, when two waves meet, whether they're in phase or out of phase decides everything.

QUESTION: Quick check from last episode. The wave speed is set by what, the source or the medium? And if a wave keeps the same frequency but moves into a medium where it travels faster, what happens to its wavelength? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: The wave speed is set by the medium, not the source. And if the frequency stays the same, because frequency is fixed by the source, while the speed increases in the new medium, then since speed equals frequency times wavelength, the wavelength must increase. Frequency fixed, speed up, wavelength up. Lock that, because it is exactly what happens in refraction, which is our second behaviour today. Now let's take the four behaviours one at a time, and they're listed for you in Listing one: reflection, refraction, diffraction, and superposition.

NARRATOR: First, reflection. Reflection is the bouncing of a wave back into its original medium when it meets a boundary. When a wave hits a barrier it can't cross, it returns into the medium it came from. The rule is the law of reflection: the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, where both angles are measured from the normal, the line perpendicular to the surface. An echo is the reflection of a sound wave off a distant surface; your image in a mirror is the reflection of light. One subtlety the syllabus likes: when a wave on a string reflects off a fixed end, a crest comes back as a trough, it's inverted, flipped, because the fixed end can't move and pushes back; but when it reflects off a free end, a crest comes back as a crest, not inverted. That inversion at a fixed boundary matters in a moment when we build standing waves. So reflection: the wave bounces back, angle in equals angle out, and a fixed-end reflection flips the wave over.

NARRATOR: Second, refraction. Refraction is the change in direction of a wave as it passes from one medium into another, caused by a change in the wave's speed. Here's the chain of logic, and it's worth getting exactly right because it's a guaranteed exam item. When a wave crosses into a new medium, its speed changes, that's the cause. Its frequency does not change, frequency is fixed by the source. So, from the wave equation, the wavelength changes to match the new speed. And if the wave meets the boundary at an angle, that change in speed makes it change direction, it bends. The classic picture: a wave slowing down as it enters a denser medium bends towards the normal, and speeding up as it enters a less dense medium bends away from the normal. A real-world example: light bends as it enters water, which is why a straw in a glass looks bent, and why a pool looks shallower than it is. Water waves give a cleaner picture still: where the water gets shallower the waves slow down, and if they approach the shallow patch at an angle the slowing edge lags behind, swinging the whole wavefront round towards the normal, which is exactly why ocean waves tend to line up parallel to a beach no matter which way they started, the shallowing water near the shore refracts them around until they arrive almost head-on. The bending always traces back to one part of the wavefront travelling slower than another. The key teaching point, the one that earns marks, is that refraction is caused by a change in speed, with the frequency staying constant and the wavelength changing. That's in Listing one.

QUESTION: A retrieval one. When a wave refracts into a new medium and slows down, which of the three quantities, frequency, wavelength, speed, stay the same and which change? Pause the player, sort all three, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The speed changes, it decreases, that's the cause of refraction. The frequency stays the same, it's fixed by the source and doesn't change at a boundary. And therefore the wavelength changes, it decreases too, because speed equals frequency times wavelength and the frequency is constant, so wavelength tracks the speed. Frequency constant, speed and wavelength both change together. If you said frequency changes, that's the single most common error in this topic, fix it now: at a boundary, frequency is the quantity that stays put. The bending of direction follows from the speed change when the wave hits at an angle.

NARRATOR: Third, diffraction. Diffraction is the spreading out, or bending, of a wave as it passes through a gap or around the edge of an obstacle. Waves don't only travel in straight lines; when they pass an edge or squeeze through an opening, they fan out into the region beyond. The crucial rule, and this is the examinable part, is that diffraction is most pronounced when the size of the gap is comparable to the wavelength of the wave. A wide gap, much bigger than the wavelength, lets the wave through almost straight, with only a little spreading at the edges; but a narrow gap, about the same size as the wavelength, makes the wave spread out dramatically on the far side, almost like a new circular wave radiating from the gap. This is why you can hear someone around a corner even when you can't see them: sound has a wavelength of around a metre, comparable to the size of a doorway, so it diffracts and spreads through and around, whereas light has a wavelength far smaller than a doorway, so it barely diffracts and travels essentially straight. Same doorway, different wavelengths, very different spreading. Diffraction is in Listing one, and it previews the slit experiments you'll meet in Module Seven on the nature of light.

NARRATOR: Now the big one, the idea that ties the rest of the episode together: superposition. The principle of superposition says that when two or more waves overlap at a point, the resultant displacement at that point is the sum of the displacements each wave would produce on its own. You simply add the displacements, taking direction, the sign, into account. Two crests meeting make a bigger crest; a crest meeting a trough partly or wholly cancel. And here's a beautiful feature of waves: after they overlap and add, they pass straight through each other and carry on unchanged, as if nothing happened. The adding only happens while they occupy the same place at the same time. Superposition is just addition of displacements, but it's the foundation of interference, standing waves, and resonance, everything in the rest of this episode. It's in Listing one, and a worked superposition is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Superposition leads straight to interference, the pattern you get when two waves overlap consistently. There are two cases, and they're set by phase. When two waves arrive in phase, crest lining up with crest and trough with trough, they reinforce: the displacements add to give a larger amplitude. That's constructive interference. When two waves arrive out of phase, crest lining up with trough, they oppose: the displacements subtract, and if the amplitudes are equal they cancel completely to give zero. That's destructive interference. So constructive means in phase, reinforce, bigger; destructive means out of phase, cancel, smaller or zero. A familiar example is sound: in a hall there are spots where reflections arrive in phase and the sound is loud, constructive, and dead spots where they arrive out of phase and the sound is quiet, destructive. The in-phase-versus-out-of-phase distinction is the key, and it's in Listing three. Whether interference is constructive or destructive at a point depends entirely on whether the waves arrive in phase or out of phase there.

NARRATOR: There's a neat way to predict where interference is constructive and where it's destructive, using path difference, and it's worth a mark. Suppose two sources send out identical waves, in step with each other, and you stand at some point receiving both. The path difference is the difference in the distances the two waves travelled to reach you. If that path difference is a whole number of wavelengths, zero, one, two wavelengths, and so on, the two waves arrive crest-on-crest, in phase, and you get constructive interference, a loud spot or a bright spot. If the path difference is an odd number of half-wavelengths, a half, one and a half, two and a half wavelengths, the waves arrive crest-on-trough, out of phase, and you get destructive interference, a quiet spot or a dark spot. So whole wavelengths means constructive, extra half-wavelengths means destructive. That single rule explains the pattern of loud and quiet spots you'd map in a hall, or the bright and dark bands you'll meet next year when light passes through two slits, it's the same path-difference logic, and there's a worked path-difference example in Listing two.

QUESTION: Let's check it. Two waves of equal amplitude overlap at a point. In case A they arrive in phase, crest on crest. In case B they arrive exactly out of phase, crest on trough. Describe the resultant amplitude in each case. Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. In case A, in phase, the waves interfere constructively: the displacements add, so the resultant amplitude is the sum of the two, double a single wave's amplitude if they're equal. In case B, exactly out of phase, the waves interfere destructively: the displacements are equal and opposite, so they cancel, and the resultant amplitude is zero. In phase gives reinforcement, out of phase gives cancellation. That's superposition applied: you literally add the displacements, and the phase relationship decides whether the sum is large or zero. Constructive, in phase, add; destructive, out of phase, cancel.

NARRATOR: Now we use superposition to build something the syllabus singles out: the standing wave, also called a stationary wave. Here's how it forms. Take two identical waves, same frequency, same amplitude, travelling in opposite directions through the same medium, for instance a wave on a string travelling along it and its own reflection coming back off the fixed end. These two waves superpose everywhere, all the time. At certain fixed points the two waves are always exactly out of phase, so they permanently cancel, and the medium there never moves: these points are called nodes, points of zero displacement. Halfway between the nodes, the two waves are always in phase, so they reinforce and the medium oscillates with maximum amplitude: these points are called antinodes. The result is a wave pattern that appears to stand still, the nodes and antinodes stay in fixed positions, which is why it's called a standing wave. The standing wave is in Listing four, and notice the geometry: the distance between adjacent nodes is half a wavelength of the original waves. That geometry is the whole reason standing waves matter for music. On a string of fixed length, only certain wavelengths fit neatly with a node at each fixed end, the longest is the one where the string holds a single antinode, that's the fundamental, and then patterns with two, three, four antinodes give the higher harmonics. So the fixed length of the string selects a discrete set of allowed standing waves, and that discrete set of frequencies is exactly what you hear as the pitch and timbre of a plucked string. We'll make that quantitative next episode with sound; for now just hold the link, fixed boundaries plus half-wavelength node spacing equals a discrete ladder of allowed standing waves.

NARRATOR: The crucial conceptual contrast, and a favourite exam question, is between a standing wave and a normal travelling wave, which we also call a progressive wave. A progressive wave transports energy from one place to another, the energy travels along with the wave; that's everything we said last episode. A standing wave does not transport energy along the medium; the energy stays put, sloshing back and forth between the nodes, stored in the oscillation rather than carried away. So a progressive wave moves energy through space, a standing wave stores energy in a fixed pattern of nodes and antinodes. Don't confuse them: if a question describes fixed points that never move, nodes, and a pattern that doesn't travel, it's a standing wave, energy stored; if it describes energy being carried from a source to somewhere else, it's a progressive wave. That distinction is in Listing four, and being able to explain, in terms of superposition, how a standing wave forms, is a classic extended-response item.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for "explain, in terms of superposition, how a standing wave forms". The band-six version: a standing wave forms when two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travel in opposite directions through the same medium, for example an incident wave and its reflection, and superpose. By the principle of superposition their displacements add at every point. At points where the two waves are always out of phase, the displacements always cancel, producing nodes, points of permanently zero displacement; at points where the two waves are always in phase, the displacements always reinforce, producing antinodes, points of maximum oscillation. Because these nodes and antinodes stay in fixed positions, the pattern appears to stand still. The marks are for: two identical waves in opposite directions, the appeal to superposition, the out-of-phase cancellation giving nodes, the in-phase reinforcement giving antinodes. The weak answer is just "the waves add up", which says nothing about opposite directions or the in-phase and out-of-phase conditions that produce the fixed nodes and antinodes. Name the phase conditions and the node-antinode result.

NARRATOR: Standing waves lead to the last idea of the episode: resonance. Every physical object that can oscillate has a natural frequency, the frequency at which it naturally tends to vibrate when disturbed, set by its own properties, its size, tension, mass, stiffness. A guitar string plucked vibrates at its natural frequency; a wine glass rings at its natural frequency. Resonance occurs when you drive an oscillating system with a periodic force whose frequency equals the system's natural frequency. When the driving frequency matches the natural frequency, energy is transferred into the system extremely efficiently on every cycle, and the amplitude of the oscillation grows large. Think of pushing a child on a swing: push in time with the swing's natural rhythm and small pushes build a big swing; push out of time and you fight it. So the relationships the syllabus wants are these: when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency, energy transfer is maximised and the amplitude becomes large; when the driving frequency is well away from the natural frequency, energy transfer is poor and the amplitude stays small. Resonance is in Listing three. It's how a tuning fork sets another identical fork ringing across the room, and, pushed to extremes, how a bridge or a wine glass can be shaken apart, a periodic driving force at the natural frequency, building the amplitude until something gives.

QUESTION: A retrieval one on resonance. To make a system oscillate with a large amplitude using only small driving forces, what must be true about the driving frequency compared with the system's natural frequency? And in one phrase, what is being transferred efficiently when that condition is met? Pause the player, answer both, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. To build a large amplitude with small driving forces, the driving frequency must equal the system's natural frequency, that's the condition for resonance. And what's being transferred efficiently is energy: at resonance, energy is fed into the oscillating system on every cycle and accumulates, so the amplitude grows. Driving frequency equals natural frequency, energy transfer is maximised, amplitude grows large. The swing analogy captures it: time your pushes to the natural rhythm and the energy you add each cycle builds up. Off resonance, your pushes sometimes add and sometimes oppose, and the amplitude stays small.

NARRATOR: A practical note, since the syllabus wants you to investigate these behaviours. You can show reflection, refraction and diffraction beautifully in a ripple tank: a barrier shows reflection, a shallow region slows the waves and shows refraction, and a gap of adjustable width shows diffraction, narrow the gap towards the wavelength and watch the spreading increase. You distinguish progressive from standing waves on a string driven by a vibrator: at most frequencies you get a messy travelling disturbance, but at special frequencies a clean standing wave locks in, with stationary nodes you can mark. And you explore resonance by driving a mass on a spring, or a string, and sweeping the driving frequency: the amplitude peaks sharply as you pass through the natural frequency. In each case you're seeing today's four behaviours, plus superposition, made visible. Being able to describe such an investigation and what it shows is examinable, and a good answer always names the variable you changed, the gap width, the medium depth, or the driving frequency, and the behaviour it produced, more spreading, a bent path, or a peak in amplitude.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: saying frequency changes during refraction, it does not, frequency is fixed by the source and stays constant across a boundary; it's the speed and wavelength that change. Trap two: thinking diffraction is the same for all waves through a gap, it depends on the gap-to-wavelength ratio, so the same gap diffracts a long-wavelength wave strongly and a short-wavelength wave hardly at all. Trap three: confusing constructive and destructive interference, constructive is in phase and reinforces, destructive is out of phase and cancels, get the phase condition the right way round. Trap four: calling a standing wave a wave that travels, it doesn't, its nodes and antinodes are fixed and it stores energy rather than transporting it; that's the opposite of a progressive wave. Trap five: thinking resonance needs a big driving force, it doesn't, the point of resonance is that small forces at the natural frequency build a large amplitude because the energy accumulates cycle after cycle. Avoid those five and the whole topic is secure.

QUESTION: Question three. Explain why you can hear a sound around a corner but cannot see around the corner, in terms of diffraction. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. Diffraction, the spreading of a wave through a gap or around an edge, is most pronounced when the gap or obstacle is comparable in size to the wavelength. QUESTION: Question four. Two identical waves travelling in opposite directions on a string superpose. Explain, in terms of superposition, how this produces a standing wave, and define a node and an antinode. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing four. By the principle of superposition, the displacements of the two waves add at every point of the string. At points where the two waves are always out of phase, their displacements always cancel, so the string never moves there; these points of permanently zero displacement are nodes. At points where the two waves are always in phase, their displacements always reinforce, so the string oscillates with maximum amplitude there; these points are antinodes. Because the nodes and antinodes stay in fixed positions, the pattern appears stationary, a standing wave. Four marks: superposition adds the displacements; out-of-phase points cancel to give nodes, defined as zero displacement; in-phase points reinforce to give antinodes, defined as maximum displacement; and the pattern is fixed, hence standing. The trap is the weak answer "the waves add up", which omits the opposite-direction setup and the in-phase and out-of-phase conditions.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. A mass on a spring has a natural frequency of two hertz. It is driven by a small periodic force. Describe and explain what happens to the amplitude of the oscillation as the driving frequency is slowly increased from one hertz, through two hertz, to three hertz. Four marks. Pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. As the driving frequency increases from one hertz towards two hertz, it approaches the natural frequency, so energy is transferred into the system more and more efficiently each cycle and the amplitude grows. At two hertz the driving frequency equals the natural frequency: this is resonance, energy transfer is maximised, and the amplitude reaches its largest value, a sharp peak. As the driving frequency increases beyond two hertz towards three hertz, it moves away from the natural frequency, energy transfer becomes inefficient again, and the amplitude falls back to a small value. So the amplitude rises to a sharp maximum at the natural frequency of two hertz and falls away on either side. Four marks: amplitude increases as the driving frequency approaches two hertz; a maximum at two hertz identified as resonance with the explanation that driving frequency equals natural frequency and energy transfer is maximised; amplitude decreases beyond; and the overall shape, a sharp peak at the natural frequency. The key idea is that resonance, and the large amplitude, happens precisely when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency.
