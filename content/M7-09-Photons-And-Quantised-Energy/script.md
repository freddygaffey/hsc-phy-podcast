QUESTION: So is this where Planck's constant comes from? Did Einstein invent the photon?

NARRATOR: Careful here, because the HSC tests the attribution and students get it backwards. The order is Planck first, then Einstein. In nineteen hundred, Max Planck was trying to fix a disaster in classical physics called the ultraviolet catastrophe, which is the topic of our very next episode. To make the maths come out right, he had to assume that energy could only be exchanged in discrete chunks of size Planck's constant times frequency. He introduced the constant that now bears his name. But, and this matters, Planck thought it was just a mathematical trick, a bookkeeping fiddle to make the equation fit the data. He did not believe energy was really lumpy. He was deeply uncomfortable with it. Then in nineteen-oh-five, Albert Einstein took the quanta seriously and said no, these lumps are physically real, light genuinely travels as discrete particles, photons. Einstein applied that to the photoelectric effect, which is two episodes away, and won the nineteen twenty-one Nobel Prize for it. So in the exam: Planck introduced the energy-equals-Planck's-constant-times-frequency relation, in nineteen hundred, to fix black-body radiation, and regarded it as a trick. Einstein, in nineteen-oh-five, gave the photon physical reality. Don't credit Einstein with the equation, and don't claim Planck embraced quantum theory. He didn't, not at first.

NARRATOR: That's a real cross-link, so let me make it explicit, forward and back. NARRATOR: Now let's do the central new idea, the one the extended-response dot-point is built on. Energy versus intensity. This is the heart of the episode, so I'm going to say it more than once, in different words, because at high speed a single pass slips by. Here it is plainly. The energy of each individual photon depends only on the frequency. It does not depend on how bright the beam is. The brightness, the intensity, of the beam depends on something completely separate: the number of photons arriving every second. Two separate knobs. Frequency sets the energy of each photon. Number of photons sets the brightness. They do not touch each other.

NARRATOR: Let me make that concrete. Take a red laser pointer. Turn up its power so it's brighter. What changed? You did not make each red photon more energetic. Each red photon still carries exactly the same energy it always did, set by red's frequency. What you changed is the number of red photons leaving the laser every second. Brighter, same colour, means more photons of the same energy. Now instead, swap the red laser for a blue one of the same brightness. Now you've changed the frequency. Blue is a higher frequency than red, so each blue photon carries more energy than each red photon. The colour changed the energy per photon; the brightness changed the number per second. Two knobs. This is the single most important conceptual point in the episode.

NARRATOR: And here is the trap, the exact trap the marker is hunting for. A weak answer says: brighter light has more energetic photons. That is wrong, and it's the most common mistake in the whole topic. Brighter light of the same colour does not have more energetic photons. It has more photons, each of exactly the same energy. The energy per photon is fixed by frequency alone. Write "brighter means more photons, not more energetic photons" and you've protected the mark. This misconception is not a small thing. It is the precise idea that the photoelectric effect destroys, and if you carry it into the photoelectric episode you will get every conceptual question wrong. So fix it now.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the strong extended-response answer in full, because this dot-point is worded almost exactly as the exam will word it: "use quantum theory to relate the frequency of light, the intensity of light, the energy of the photons, and the number of photons." Here is the band-six answer. In the quantum model, light travels as discrete packets called photons. The energy of each photon is fixed by the frequency of the light through the relation energy equals Planck's constant times frequency, so a higher frequency means a higher-energy photon, and this is independent of the intensity. The intensity, the brightness, of the beam is determined by the number of photons arriving per second, not by the energy of each photon. Since the total power of the beam equals the number of photons per second multiplied by the energy of each photon, increasing the intensity at a fixed frequency increases the number of photons per second but does not change the energy of any individual photon. And the clean closing line that lifts it to full marks: equivalently, for two beams of equal power, the lower-frequency, longer-wavelength beam must contain more photons per second, because each of its photons carries less energy. That last sentence is doing real work; we're going to prove it numerically in a minute.

NARRATOR: Now contrast that with the weak answer, so you can hear the marking boundary. A weak answer says: the brighter the light, the more energy it has, and higher frequency light is also more energetic. That sentence blurs the two ideas together into one vague mush. It never says that frequency sets the energy per photon while intensity sets the number of photons. It never names the relationship total power equals number per second times energy per photon. The marker cannot award the analysis marks, because the candidate hasn't analysed anything, they've just asserted that more is more. The strong answer keeps the two variables strictly separate and links them with the power relationship. Hear the difference? The marks live in the separation.

NARRATOR: Before the worked examples, one more tool you need: the electron-volt. Photon energies in joules are absurdly tiny numbers, ten to the minus nineteen and smaller, which are awkward. So physicists use a more convenient unit at this scale, the electron-volt. One electron-volt is the energy an electron gains when it accelerates through a potential difference of one volt, and it equals one point six times ten to the minus nineteen joules. That number is the same as the charge on the electron, and that's not a coincidence, it falls straight out of energy equals charge times voltage. To convert from joules to electron-volts, you divide by one point six times ten to the minus nineteen. To go the other way, electron-volts to joules, you multiply by it. Photon energies and, later, work functions are routinely quoted in electron-volts, so get fluent with that conversion both directions. I've put it in Listing 2.

NARRATOR: Exactly right, and this is genuinely examinable. The logic is bookkeeping. The total energy of a flash of light equals the number of photons times the energy of each photon. NARRATOR: Now worked example D, which is the one the spec specifically asks you to rehearse, comparing two lasers of equal power but different colour. NARRATOR: Let me anchor all of this in a real story you'll want to remember, because it's the case study that makes the photon physically real. Remember the photoelectric effect story, the one where shining light on a metal kicks electrons out of it. The puzzle that broke classical physics was exactly today's distinction. Turn up the brightness of dim red light as much as you like, and not a single electron comes out, no matter how long you wait. But the faintest flicker of ultraviolet ejects electrons instantly. The wave model couldn't explain that, because the wave model said brighter equals more energy, so enough red light should eventually do it. But it never does. Einstein's resolution was today's idea: each photon acts alone, and what matters is the energy of the individual photon, set by frequency, not the brightness of the beam. Red photons, low frequency, each too feeble to free an electron, and a million feeble photons is still a million feeble taps, none of them enough. One ultraviolet photon, high frequency, carries enough energy in a single hit. That is the energy-versus-number distinction, made visible, and it's why we drill it so hard today. The deep idea, the one that runs from here all the way through the quantum world, is that energy and matter both come in discrete lumps, and Planck's constant is the universal exchange rate between the wave description and the particle description. You're meeting that exchange rate today, for the first time, with light.

NARRATOR: One more subtlety the syllabus wants, the meaning of "quantised". Energy is exchanged only in whole-number multiples of one photon's worth. A photon is all-or-nothing. It delivers its full Planck's-constant-times-frequency, or it delivers nothing. There's no such thing as half a photon's worth of energy being handed over. That indivisibility is what "quantum" means, a discrete lump, and it becomes "one photon, one electron" when we reach the photoelectric effect. So don't ever say a photon gives up a fraction of its energy in this context. Whole lumps only.
