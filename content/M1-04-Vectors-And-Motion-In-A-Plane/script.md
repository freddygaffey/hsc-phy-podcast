NARRATOR: For three episodes we've kept motion on a straight line, where a vector was just a signed number. Today we lift it into two dimensions, a plane, where vectors get directions that aren't simply plus or minus, and we learn the two skills that unlock the rest of mechanics: resolving a vector into components, and adding vectors to find a resultant. Then we apply them to relative velocity in two dimensions, the boat crossing a river, the plane in a crosswind. This episode is the gateway to projectile motion next year, so the method matters. But first, the recap, because today builds directly on everything so far.

NARRATOR: Episode one set the foundation: a vector has magnitude and direction, the vector trio is D-V-A, displacement, velocity, acceleration, and we handled direction in one dimension with a positive sign and a negative sign. Episode two put motion on graphs: gradient of a displacement-time graph is velocity, gradient of a velocity-time graph is acceleration, area under a velocity-time graph is displacement. And last episode gave us the four equations of uniformly accelerated motion, the suvat equations, chosen by spotting the missing quantity, and applied to motion under gravity. Today we take the vector idea from episode one and finally let directions be genuine angles, not just plus or minus, because once an object moves in a plane, "left or right" isn't enough, we need "this fast, in that direction".

QUESTION: Quick check before the new material. What is the difference between a scalar and a vector, and which of velocity and speed is which? Pause the player, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: A scalar has magnitude only; a vector has magnitude and direction. Speed is the scalar, velocity is the vector. Hold that, because today every quantity we handle is a vector with a real direction in a plane, and the whole episode is about how to add and break apart those directioned quantities. Let's start with breaking them apart: resolving into components.

NARRATOR: Here's the central idea. Any vector pointing at an angle can be replaced by two perpendicular vectors, one horizontal and one vertical, that together have exactly the same effect. Those two are called the components of the vector. Think of a vector as the diagonal of a rectangle: the horizontal side and the vertical side are its components, and the diagonal is the vector itself. Why bother? Because perpendicular components are independent, they don't affect each other, so a hard two-dimensional problem splits into two easy one-dimensional problems, one horizontal, one vertical, each of which you already know how to handle. That independence is the single most powerful idea in this part of the course, and it's the whole secret of projectile motion next year.

NARRATOR: Now the trigonometry, and it's just right-angled triangle trig. Take a vector of magnitude, call it V, pointing at an angle theta above the horizontal. Drop it onto the rectangle: the horizontal component is V times the cosine of theta, and the vertical component is V times the sine of theta. Horizontal is cosine, vertical is sine, when the angle is measured from the horizontal. Here's the memory hook, because students swap sine and cosine under pressure: the component the angle "opens onto", the one adjacent to the angle, gets the cosine, and the component across from the angle, opposite to it, gets the sine. Adjacent is cosine, opposite is sine, straight from SOH CAH TOA. If your angle is measured from the horizontal, the horizontal component is adjacent, so it takes the cosine; the vertical is opposite, so it takes the sine. Get the angle's reference direction right and the rest follows. The resolution rules are in Listing one.

NARRATOR: One subtlety on the angle that catches people, so let me flag it. The rule "horizontal is cosine, vertical is sine" is only true when the angle is measured from the horizontal. If a problem gives you the angle from the vertical instead, the two swap: the horizontal component becomes the magnitude times the sine, and the vertical becomes the magnitude times the cosine. This is why I keep saying "adjacent is cosine, opposite is sine" rather than memorising "horizontal is cosine", because adjacent and opposite are defined relative to wherever the angle actually is. So before you write anything, look at the diagram and ask: which side is the angle sitting against? That side, the adjacent one, takes the cosine; the side across the triangle from the angle takes the sine. Get the reference direction right and you'll never swap them, whichever way the angle is quoted. It's the single most common resolution error in the exam.

NARRATOR: Now the reverse operation: adding vectors, combining components back into a single resultant. If you have two perpendicular components, a horizontal one and a vertical one, the single vector they make, the resultant, has a magnitude you get from Pythagoras, the square root of the sum of the squares of the two components, and a direction you get from trigonometry, the angle whose tangent is the vertical component over the horizontal component. So resolving and recombining are inverse operations: resolve splits a vector into perpendicular parts using cosine and sine; recombine fuses perpendicular parts back into one using Pythagoras and the inverse tangent. Between them, they handle any vector problem in a plane.

NARRATOR: For adding vectors that aren't already perpendicular, there are two equivalent pictures. The graphical one is head-to-tail: draw the first vector, then draw the second starting from the head of the first, and the resultant is the arrow from the very start to the very end. The algebraic one, which is more accurate, is to resolve every vector into horizontal and vertical components, add all the horizontal components together and all the vertical components together to get the components of the resultant, then recombine with Pythagoras and tangent. For the perpendicular cases the syllabus focuses on, the head-to-tail picture is a right-angled triangle, and Pythagoras plus tangent finishes it. The key discipline the marker rewards: never just add the magnitudes as if they were numbers. Two vectors of size three and four don't make seven unless they point the same way; if they're perpendicular they make five, by Pythagoras. Direction always matters.

QUESTION: Let me make you do one. A vector of magnitude ten units points at an angle of thirty degrees above the horizontal. What are its horizontal and vertical components? Pause the player, work both out, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer. The angle is measured from the horizontal, so the horizontal component, adjacent to the angle, is ten times the cosine of thirty degrees. Cosine of thirty is about zero point eight seven, so the horizontal component is about eight point seven units. The vertical component, opposite the angle, is ten times the sine of thirty degrees. Sine of thirty is zero point five, so the vertical component is five units. So the single ten-unit vector at thirty degrees is exactly equivalent to eight point seven units horizontally plus five units vertically. If you got both, with horizontal taking the cosine, you've got the core skill. If you swapped them, remember: from the horizontal, horizontal is adjacent, adjacent is cosine. The working is in Listing two.

NARRATOR: Now the headline application, relative velocity in two dimensions, because this is where the exam lives. Relative velocity is about how fast one object appears to move from the point of view of another. In one dimension you've done this since junior science: two cars approaching, you add the speeds; two cars in the same direction, you subtract. The rule, stated properly, is this: the velocity of object A relative to object B is the velocity of A minus the velocity of B, as a vector subtraction. That's the master rule, and it's in Listing four. In two dimensions the subtraction is a vector subtraction, done with components or a vector triangle, but the rule is identical: A relative to B equals velocity of A minus velocity of B.

NARRATOR: Let me do the classic boat-and-river problem, because it's the syllabus's favourite and it shows the vectors beautifully. The full working is in Listing three. A boat points straight across a river and travels at four metres per second relative to the water. The river itself flows at three metres per second downstream. Find the boat's resultant velocity relative to the ground, the bank. The boat's velocity has two parts that are already perpendicular: four metres per second straight across, and three metres per second downstream, carried along by the current. These add as a right-angled triangle. The magnitude of the resultant is the square root of four squared plus three squared, the square root of sixteen plus nine, the square root of twenty-five, which is five metres per second. The direction: the angle downstream from the straight-across direction has a tangent of three over four, the downstream component over the across component, which gives an angle of about thirty-seven degrees. So the boat actually travels at five metres per second, at about thirty-seven degrees downstream of straight across. Notice it ends up downstream of where it was pointing, because the current carries it, and notice we never added four and three to get seven, we combined them as perpendicular vectors to get five.

QUESTION: Here's a retrieval one worth stopping for. In that boat problem, the boat's engine pushes it across the river at four metres per second, yet it crosses the river in the same time whether or not the river is flowing. Why does the downstream current not change how long the crossing takes? Pause the player, think it through, then play.

NARRATOR: Here's the answer, and it's the deep idea of the whole episode. The crossing time depends only on the component of velocity across the river, the four metres per second, and the width of the river. The current acts purely downstream, perpendicular to that, so it has no across-the-river component at all, it adds nothing to the across motion. Perpendicular components are independent: the downstream current moves the boat downstream but does absolutely nothing to its progress across, so the crossing takes exactly the same time as in still water. The current changes where the boat lands, further downstream, but not when it arrives at the far bank. That independence of perpendicular components is exactly the principle that makes projectile motion work next year, where the horizontal motion and the vertical motion proceed completely independently. If you saw that, you've understood the most important idea in two-dimensional motion.

NARRATOR: Let me give you a second relative-velocity scenario, the crosswind, because the syllabus names it. An aeroplane heads north with a certain airspeed, the speed relative to the air. A wind blows from the west, pushing the plane eastward. The plane's velocity relative to the ground is the vector sum of its velocity relative to the air and the velocity of the air relative to the ground, the wind. So you add the plane's northward velocity and the wind's eastward velocity as perpendicular vectors, Pythagoras for the ground speed, tangent for the angle the plane is pushed off course to the east. This is why pilots have to "aim upwind", point the plane partly into the wind, so that the wind's push and the aiming cancel and the resultant ground velocity points where they actually want to go. Same physics as the boat: a velocity relative to a medium, plus the medium's velocity, gives the velocity relative to the ground.

NARRATOR: And the two-cars scenario, to nail the subtraction. Two cars drive toward an intersection at right angles, one heading north at twenty metres per second, the other heading east at twenty metres per second. What's the velocity of the first car relative to the second? Apply the rule: velocity of A minus velocity of B. A is twenty north; B is twenty east; minus twenty east is twenty west. So A relative to B is twenty north plus twenty west, which by Pythagoras is the square root of twenty squared plus twenty squared, about twenty-eight metres per second, directed north-west. To the driver of the second car, the first car appears to approach at about twenty-eight metres per second from the north-west, faster and from a different direction than either car's road speed. That's why relative velocity matters in collisions, and it's a guaranteed exam type. The rule, A minus B, did all the work.

NARRATOR: Let me make the subtraction in the relative-velocity rule concrete, because "subtract a vector" confuses people. To work out velocity of A minus velocity of B, you reverse vector B, flip its direction by one hundred and eighty degrees, and then add it to vector A head-to-tail. So "minus twenty east" becomes "plus twenty west", which is exactly what we did with the two cars. Geometrically, the velocity of A relative to B is the vector you'd draw from the head of B to the head of A when both are drawn from the same starting point. The practical recipe: write each velocity in components, north and east say, subtract the components of B from the components of A, then recombine the resulting components with Pythagoras and tangent. Reverse and add, or subtract component by component, same result. That recipe never fails, even when the two velocities point at awkward angles.

NARRATOR: And let me show the general component method on vectors that aren't perpendicular, because it's the technique that handles anything. Suppose you walk five hundred metres on a bearing, at an angle, then another vector at a different angle, and you want the total displacement. You don't try to draw it to scale and measure. Instead you resolve each leg into its east component and its north component using cosine and sine, add all the east components to get the total east, add all the north components to get the total north, and then recombine those two totals with Pythagoras and the inverse tangent to get the single resultant. So even a messy chain of vectors at all sorts of angles reduces to: resolve every one into east and north, add the easts, add the norths, recombine. The reason the boat and plane problems looked so clean is that their two vectors were already perpendicular, one purely across and one purely downstream, so each had only one non-zero component and the triangle was already right-angled. The component method is the general engine; the right-angled triangle is just the easy special case. This is laid out in Listing one.

NARRATOR: Let me make the component method concrete with numbers. A hiker walks one hundred metres due east, then one hundred and twenty metres on a bearing thirty degrees north of east. Find the resultant displacement. Resolve each leg into east and north. The first leg is one hundred east, zero north. The second leg: its east component is one hundred and twenty times the cosine of thirty, about one hundred and four metres east; its north component is one hundred and twenty times the sine of thirty, sixty metres north. Now add the components: total east is one hundred plus one hundred and four, two hundred and four metres; total north is zero plus sixty, sixty metres. Recombine: the magnitude is the square root of two hundred and four squared plus sixty squared, about two hundred and thirteen metres; the direction is the angle north of east whose tangent is sixty over two hundred and four, about sixteen degrees north of east. So the resultant is about two hundred and thirteen metres, sixteen degrees north of east. Two legs at different angles, reduced to one answer, purely by resolving, adding components, and recombining, no scale drawing needed. That's the method that handles every vector-addition problem you'll meet.

NARRATOR: A practical note, because the syllabus expects you to represent vectors in investigations too. When you add vectors by scale diagram, you draw each one as an arrow whose length is proportional to its magnitude and whose direction is the true direction, placed head-to-tail, then measure the resultant arrow with a ruler and protractor. It's a useful check and it builds intuition, but it's limited by drawing accuracy, which is exactly why the component method, resolve, add, recombine, is preferred for a precise answer. Know both: the scale diagram for understanding and a quick check, the components for the marks.

NARRATOR: Let me give the strong extended-response answer for the boat-type question, because there's a structure markers reward. For "a boat crosses a flowing river; determine its resultant velocity", the strong answer: state that the boat's velocity across the water and the river's velocity downstream are perpendicular; draw or describe the vector triangle; find the magnitude of the resultant using Pythagoras; find the direction using the inverse tangent of the downstream component over the across component; and state both the magnitude and the direction in the answer, because velocity is a vector. The marks are awarded for treating the two velocities as perpendicular vectors, for Pythagoras, for the angle, and for giving a directioned final answer, not a bare number.

NARRATOR: And the weak answer, so you hear the gap. A weak answer says "the boat goes four across and three down, so it goes seven", adding the magnitudes as scalars, or gives a speed with no direction. That earns almost nothing: it ignores that the velocities are perpendicular vectors, it produces a physically wrong magnitude, and it drops the direction that a velocity answer requires. The strong answer draws the triangle, uses Pythagoras to get five, finds the angle, and states "five metres per second at thirty-seven degrees downstream". Vectors add as vectors, and the answer carries a direction. That's the whole difference.

NARRATOR: A few traps gathered, because each is a mark. Trap one: adding vector magnitudes as if they were scalars, three and four make five when perpendicular, not seven. Trap two: swapping sine and cosine when resolving, from the horizontal, horizontal is cosine. Trap three: giving a vector answer with no direction, a resultant velocity needs its angle stated. Trap four: in relative velocity, adding when you should subtract, the rule is A minus B, always. Trap five: forgetting that perpendicular components are independent, so the current or wind perpendicular to the crossing doesn't change the crossing time. Avoid those and two-dimensional motion is just two one-dimensional problems side by side.

NARRATOR: Let me draw the threads together, because this episode is a hinge. Back to episode one: this is the vector idea, magnitude and direction, finally handled in a plane instead of on a line. And forward, hugely, to Module Five, Advanced Mechanics: projectile motion is exactly this episode plus last episode combined. You resolve the launch velocity into horizontal and vertical components, today's skill; then you treat the vertical motion with the suvat equations under gravity, last episode's skill, and the horizontal motion as constant velocity; and because the components are independent, today's key insight, the two run side by side without interfering. Every projectile problem you'll ever do is resolve, then apply suvat to the vertical. There's a link to the next module too: in Dynamics you resolve forces into components by the identical cosine-and-sine method, especially on an inclined plane, where you split the weight into a component down the slope and a component into the slope. Master resolving here and you've built the tool for forces, projectiles, and circular motion.

NARRATOR: Let me recap the takeaways, phrased for marks. One: any vector can be resolved into perpendicular components; measured from the horizontal, the horizontal component is the magnitude times the cosine of the angle, the vertical is the magnitude times the sine. Two: perpendicular components are independent, which is why a two-dimensional problem splits into two one-dimensional ones. Three: to combine perpendicular components into a resultant, use Pythagoras for the magnitude and the inverse tangent for the direction, and never add magnitudes as scalars. Four: the velocity of A relative to B is the velocity of A minus the velocity of B, a vector subtraction, and in the boat, plane and two-car scenarios you build a vector triangle. Five: always state both magnitude and direction for a vector answer. Hold those and you can handle any motion in a plane.

NARRATOR: Now let's finish with exam-style questions in real NESA format. Get a pen, because you learn this by retrieving it, not by hearing it. Five questions, easy to hard.

QUESTION: Question one. A vector of magnitude twenty units points at sixty degrees above the horizontal. Calculate its horizontal and vertical components. Two marks. Pause the player, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing two. The horizontal component, adjacent to the angle measured from the horizontal, is twenty times the cosine of sixty degrees. Cosine of sixty is zero point five, so the horizontal component is ten units. The vertical component, opposite the angle, is twenty times the sine of sixty degrees. Sine of sixty is about zero point eight seven, so the vertical component is about seventeen point three units. Two marks: horizontal using cosine, vertical using sine. The trap is swapping them, from the horizontal, horizontal is cosine.

QUESTION: Question two. A person walks three hundred metres east, then four hundred metres north. Calculate the magnitude and direction of their displacement. Three marks. Pause, work it, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The two legs are perpendicular, so the resultant displacement is the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle. The magnitude is the square root of three hundred squared plus four hundred squared, the square root of ninety thousand plus one hundred and sixty thousand, the square root of two hundred and fifty thousand, which is five hundred metres. The direction is the angle north of east whose tangent is four hundred over three hundred, giving about fifty-three degrees north of east. So the displacement is five hundred metres, at fifty-three degrees north of east. Three marks: Pythagoras for the five hundred metres, the angle, and stating the direction. The trap is answering "seven hundred metres", that's the distance walked, not the displacement, and adding magnitudes ignores that the legs are perpendicular.

QUESTION: Question three. A swimmer swims straight across a river at one point five metres per second relative to the water. The river flows at two metres per second. Calculate the swimmer's resultant velocity relative to the bank. Three marks. Pause, work it fully, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer, in Listing three. The across velocity, one point five metres per second, and the downstream velocity, two metres per second, are perpendicular. The magnitude of the resultant is the square root of one point five squared plus two squared, the square root of two point two five plus four, the square root of six point two five, which is two point five metres per second. The direction, downstream from straight across, has a tangent of two over one point five, giving about fifty-three degrees downstream. So the swimmer moves at two point five metres per second, about fifty-three degrees downstream of straight across. Three marks: recognising the velocities are perpendicular, Pythagoras for two point five, and the direction. The trap is adding one point five and two to get three point five.

QUESTION: Question four. Explain why a boat pointed straight across a flowing river takes the same time to cross whether or not the river is flowing. Three marks. Pause, answer, then play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The time to cross depends only on the component of the boat's velocity directed across the river and the width of the river. The river's current flows downstream, which is perpendicular to the across direction, so it has no component across the river and therefore does not affect the across velocity. Because perpendicular components of velocity are independent, the current moves the boat downstream but does not change how quickly it covers the width, so the crossing time is unchanged. Three marks: crossing time depends on the across component; the current is perpendicular and so has no across component; perpendicular components are independent. This independence is the foundation of projectile motion.

QUESTION: Question five, the harder one. An aeroplane flies with an airspeed of two hundred metres per second due north. A wind blows from the west at fifty metres per second, pushing the plane east. Calculate the plane's resultant velocity relative to the ground, and explain what the pilot must do to actually travel due north. Four marks. This is an extended response, so pause the player and write a full answer before you play.

NARRATOR: Model answer. The plane's velocity relative to the air, two hundred metres per second north, and the wind's velocity, fifty metres per second east, are perpendicular, so the ground velocity is their vector sum. The magnitude is the square root of two hundred squared plus fifty squared, the square root of forty thousand plus two thousand five hundred, the square root of forty-two thousand five hundred, about two hundred and six metres per second. The direction is the angle east of north whose tangent is fifty over two hundred, about fourteen degrees east of north. So without correcting, the plane travels at about two hundred and six metres per second, fourteen degrees east of north, pushed off course by the wind. To travel due north over the ground, the pilot must aim the plane partly into the wind, to the west of north, so that the westward component of the aiming cancels the eastward push of the wind, leaving a resultant ground velocity pointing due north. Four marks: treating the velocities as perpendicular vectors; the magnitude by Pythagoras; the angle off course; and explaining that the pilot aims upwind so the components cancel. Notice the whole solution was resolve, add as perpendicular vectors, recombine, the method of the entire episode.
