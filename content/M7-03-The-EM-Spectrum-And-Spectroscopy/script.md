NARRATOR: Now we arrive at the centrepiece. When you point a spectroscope at different light sources, you don't always get the same kind of spectrum. You get one of three distinct types, and which one you get tells you something profound about the source. These three types, and the conditions that produce them, were stated as laws by Gustav Kirchhoff and Robert Bunsen in eighteen fifty-nine, working with a Bunsen burner and flame tests. This is the most examinable content in the episode, so we go slowly and we build a cause-and-effect chain for each, because that chain is exactly what a marker is hunting for.

NARRATOR: Type one: the continuous spectrum. It looks like an unbroken band of colour, a full rainbow with no gaps, every wavelength present. What produces it? A hot, dense, glowing object: a solid, a liquid, or a dense gas. The standard example is an incandescent filament, a tungsten wire heated to around three thousand kelvin in a light bulb. Tungsten, by the way, because it has an enormously high melting point and can be run that hot without vaporising. Why does a dense, hot source give every wavelength? Because in a solid or a dense gas the atoms are jammed together and the electrons are not confined to the sharp, separate energy levels of an isolated atom, they're jostled and shared and can move relatively freely. With a continuous range of possible energies available, they emit a continuous range of frequencies, so you get every colour blended together. A hot, dense source behaves as a black body, and we'll meet black-body radiation properly later in the module, the curve of intensity against wavelength. For now: hot and dense gives continuous.

NARRATOR: Type two: the emission spectrum. This looks completely different, it's a black background crossed by a few bright, sharp coloured lines, and nothing in between. What produces it? A hot gas at low density, a rarefied gas, the classic example being a discharge tube, the kind of tube behind a neon sign. Here's the cause-and-effect chain the marker wants, link by link. The electric discharge pumps energy into the gas and excites its atoms, meaning it lifts their electrons up to higher energy levels. Those excited electrons are unstable, and they fall back down, they de-excite, to lower levels. Each time an electron drops, it releases the energy difference as a single photon. And because the atom's energy levels are at fixed, specific values, the energy differences are specific, so the photons come out at specific energies, which means specific frequencies, which means specific wavelengths. You see bright lines only at those particular wavelengths, and nowhere else. The key phrase: bright lines at wavelengths characteristic of the element. The gas isn't coloured and shining its colour at you, it's emitting photons as electrons fall between defined energy levels. That mechanism is the mark.

NARRATOR: Type three: the absorption spectrum. This one is the trickiest and the most rewarding. It looks like a full continuous rainbow, but with a set of thin dark lines cut into it, like missing teeth. What produces it? Light from a continuous source passing through a cooler gas on its way to the spectroscope. So you need two things: a hot dense source behind, making a continuous spectrum, and a cooler, thinner gas in front of it. Here's the chain. The continuous light contains all wavelengths. As it passes through the cool gas, the atoms of that gas absorb photons, but only at exactly the wavelengths they would themselves emit, because those are the energies that match their electron transitions, the electrons absorb those photons and jump up. Then the atoms re-emit those photons, but in all directions, randomly, not just forward along the original beam. So along your line of sight, those particular wavelengths are depleted, scattered away out of the beam, and you see a dark gap where each one used to be. Dark lines on a continuous background. The standard example is the Sun: its hot dense interior makes a continuous spectrum, and the cooler gas of its outer atmosphere absorbs specific wavelengths on the way out, leaving dark lines. Those solar dark lines are called the Fraunhofer lines, after Joseph von Fraunhofer who mapped hundreds of them in eighteen fourteen.

NARRATOR: Now the hook to keep the three sources straight, because mixing them up is the single biggest error students make on this topic. Dense and hot equals continuous. Thin and hot equals emission. Cool gas in front of a continuous source equals absorption. Say it as a rhythm: dense-hot continuous, thin-hot emission, cool-in-front absorption. Three conditions, three spectra. If you can recite that one line, you've protected yourself against the most common mistake on the whole topic, which is naming the wrong source for a spectrum or the wrong spectrum for a source.

NARRATOR: Which brings us to why any of this matters, the second dot-point this episode owns: how spectroscopy identifies elements. The foundational discovery, from Kirchhoff and Bunsen, was this: no two elements produce the same spectrum, but every atom of a given element produces the same spectrum. Each element has its own unique set of spectral lines, a fixed pattern of wavelengths, its spectral fingerprint. Hydrogen has its lines, sodium has its, helium has its, and they never overlap into a copy of one another. So the procedure is simple and powerful: take the light from an unknown gas, spread it with a spectroscope, measure the wavelengths of its lines, and compare them against the known laboratory spectra of the elements. Whichever element's fingerprint matches, that's what's in the gas. And because every atom of an element gives the same lines whether it's in a flame on your bench or in a star, you can read the composition of something light-years away that you will never touch.
