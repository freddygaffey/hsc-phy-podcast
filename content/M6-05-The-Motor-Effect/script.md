NARRATOR: Before we touch today's topic, let's walk back through the last few lessons, because every single one of them feeds straight into what a wire does in a magnetic field. This is deliberate. The more times you meet these ideas, spread out over days, the more they stick — so I'm going to keep re-surfacing them, and you should let them wash over you again even if they feel familiar.

NARRATOR: Go back to the start of this module. Lesson one was charged particles in electric fields, the conceptual picture. The headline there: a uniform electric field exerts a constant force on a charge — the charge q multiplied by the field strength E. Constant force means constant acceleration, and constant acceleration sideways while something drifts along gives you a parabola — exactly the same shape as a projectile under gravity. And the energy line you must be able to write: the work done on a charge as it falls through a potential difference equals the charge q times the voltage V, and that shows up as kinetic energy, one half m v squared. A charge "falls through a potential difference" the way a ball falls through a height.

NARRATOR: Lesson two flipped from electric to magnetic, and this is the one that matters most today, so lean in. Charged particles in magnetic fields. The magnetic force on a moving charge is the charge q, times its speed v, times the magnetic field strength B, times the sine of the angle between the velocity and the field. And the crucial, almost philosophical fact: that force is always perpendicular to the velocity. A force at right angles to motion can never speed the thing up or slow it down — it does no work — it only bends the path. So a charge in a magnetic field goes in a circle, radius equal to m v over q B, and remarkably the period of that circle doesn't depend on how fast the particle is going. That was the cyclotron principle. Hold on tight to one phrase from that lesson: a moving charge in a magnetic field feels a sideways force. Today's entire topic is that sentence, scaled up.

NARRATOR: Lesson three was the great comparison — fields, forces and paths side by side. Gravitational and electric fields give a constant force and parabolic paths, and they can do work on something even if it starts at rest. The magnetic force is the odd one out: it needs the charge to already be moving, it's always perpendicular to that motion, it does no work, and it gives circles, not parabolas. And the exam skill from that lesson was structural — when a question says "compare" or "distinguish", you build the answer point by point and you use the word "whereas". Gravity does work on a stationary mass, whereas the magnetic force does no work and acts only on moving charge. That linking word is worth marks.

NARRATOR: And lesson four put it all to work in real devices — mass spectrometers and electron guns. Remember the mnemonic: A.S.D. Accelerate, Select, Deflect. An electron gun accelerates charges through a potential difference, using q V equals one half m v squared to set their speed. A velocity selector then balances an electric force against a magnetic force, so only particles with one exact speed — the field E over the field B — pass straight through. Then magnetic deflection curves the survivors on a circle of radius m v over q B, so heavier isotopes swing out to a bigger radius and land further along the detector. That whole device is just lessons one and two bolted together.

QUESTION: So all four of those were about loose, free particles — single charges flying through a vacuum. What changes when the charges are stuck inside a wire?

NARRATOR: That is exactly the right question, and it is the doorway into today. Almost nothing changes about the physics — and that's the beautiful part. A current is nothing more than a stream of moving charges that happen to be confined to a wire. Each one of those charges is a moving charge in a magnetic field, so each one feels that sideways force from lesson two. The wire is just the bus they're all riding in. Today's topic — the motor effect — is the force on the whole wire, and it is simply the sum of the force on every single moving charge inside it. If you understood lesson two, you already understand the cause of the motor effect. What's new is the language, the explanation the marker wants, the direction rule, and one equation. Let's get into it.

NARRATOR: Let me give you the objectives up front, because knowing the questions sharpens the listening. By the end you'll be able to do four things. Two: find the direction of the force using the right-hand push rule, and know that it's only a memory aid, not an explanation. Three: state and use the equation for the force — force equals the field B, times the current I, times the length L, times the sine of the angle theta — and reason about how the force changes when you change each of those four things. And four: explain why the force is maximum when the wire is perpendicular to the field and zero when it's parallel. Keep those four in mind.

NARRATOR: First, the definition, word for word, because the textbook phrasing is worth memorising. The motor effect is the action of a force experienced by a current-carrying conductor in an external magnetic field. Say that back to yourself — a current-carrying conductor, in an external magnetic field, experiences a force. Historically the textbook credits the discovery to Michael Faraday, in eighteen twenty-one, though for the exam the effect itself doesn't need a name attached. What needs a name is the mechanism.

NARRATOR: So let's build the mechanism from the ground up, because this is the part that earns the marks. Start with what a current actually is. An electric current is a flow of moving charges. By convention, the direction of conventional current is the direction in which positive charges would move — which, file this away now, is the opposite direction to the way the electrons actually drift, because electrons are negative. We'll come back to that, because it's a classic trap.

NARRATOR: Now, each of those moving charges is sitting inside the external magnetic field, and from lesson two we know a moving charge in a magnetic field feels a force, perpendicular to both its velocity and the field. Multiply that by the enormous number of charges flowing every second, add up all their little sideways forces, and you get one substantial force on the wire as a whole. That is the deepest "why": the wire feels a force because it is full of moving charges, and every moving charge in a magnetic field feels a force. The motor effect is the macroscopic version of the single-charge force from lesson two. If you remember nothing else about causation, remember that the force on the wire is the sum of the forces on all the charges inside it.

QUESTION: That's a satisfying explanation. But you said earlier there's a specific picture the HSC wants — the "two fields" picture. What is that, and why isn't the moving-charges story enough on its own?

NARRATOR: Good — both pictures are correct, and a strong answer can use either or both, but the two-field interaction picture is the one the syllabus phrasing points at, so let's nail it. Here's the chain. A wire carrying a current produces its own magnetic field. Not a straight field — a circular one. The field lines form concentric circles wrapping around the wire, like the rings of a tree trunk seen end-on. The direction of those circles you get from the right-hand grip rule: point your right thumb along the conventional current, and your curled fingers show the way the circular field wraps around. That grip rule is for the wire's own field. Keep it separate in your head from the force rule we'll meet in a minute — confusing the two is a top-five trap.

NARRATOR: Now superimpose that circular field onto the external uniform field from the magnet. Two fields now occupy the same space, and fields add as vectors. On one side of the wire, the wire's own circular field points the same way as the external field — they reinforce, and the combined field there is stronger. The field lines bunch up close together. On the other side of the wire, the wire's circular field points against the external field — they partially cancel, and the combined field there is weaker. The field lines spread apart. So the once-uniform field has been distorted into something lopsided: strong on one side of the wire, weak on the other.

NARRATOR: And here is the punchline, the sentence that earns the mark. The conductor experiences a net force directed from the region of stronger combined field toward the region of weaker combined field. The wire is squeezed out of the high-field region and pushed into the low-field region. A nice mental image the textbooks use: the bunched-up field lines behave like stretched elastic bands that want to straighten out, and in straightening they shove the wire toward the slack side. Strong side pushes, weak side yields, wire moves from strong to weak.

NARRATOR: Let me put the weak answer and the strong answer right next to each other, because hearing the gap is the fastest way to learn the marking boundary. Suppose the question is: "Use magnetic field interactions to explain why a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force." The weak answer — the band-four answer — is: "A force acts because F equals B I L." That's it. It quotes a formula. It does not mention an interaction, it does not explain anything, and on a four-mark "explain why" question it might scrape one mark for relevance. Now the strong answer: "The current-carrying conductor produces a circular magnetic field around itself. This field interacts with the external uniform magnetic field, producing a region of stronger combined field on one side of the conductor and a weaker combined field on the other. The conductor experiences a net force directed from the region of stronger field toward the region of weaker field." Hear the difference? The strong answer never quotes the formula, because the formula isn't an explanation — it's a calculation tool. The strong answer describes an interaction of two fields and names the resulting asymmetry. That's the mechanism. That's the marks.

NARRATOR: A quick but important warning about pole-pushing language, because it's a trap. Don't try to explain this by saying "the field lines push because like poles repel" or anything woolly about magnets shoving each other. That's not the right physics and markers see straight through it. The correct picture is superposition — two fields adding to give an asymmetric resultant — and the net force pointing from high field to low field. Use those words: superimposed, combined, stronger side, weaker side, net force from strong to weak.

NARRATOR: Right — now the direction. Once you know a wire feels a force, which way does it go? The force is always perpendicular to both the current and the field. Always. It sticks out at right angles to the flat plane that contains the current arrow and the field arrow. So if the current and the field both lie in the plane of your page, the force must point either into the page or out of the page — never along the page. Get that into your bones, because a classic error is drawing the force arrow parallel to the field or parallel to the current. It is parallel to neither. It is perpendicular to both.

NARRATOR: To pin down which way — into or out of the page — use the right-hand push rule, also called the right-hand palm rule. Here's the convention, and get it exactly right. Hold your right hand flat. Your fingers point in the direction of the magnetic field B — that means from north to south, the external field direction. Your thumb, stuck out to the side, points in the direction of the conventional current I. And then your palm pushes in the direction of the force F — the force comes straight out of your palm. Fingers field, thumb current, palm pushes force. Practise it once or twice with your actual right hand; it's much easier to do than to describe.

NARRATOR: There's an alternative mnemonic some teachers use, the "F.I.B." rule — F, I, B from top to bottom standing for Force, Current, Field, set on three perpendicular fingers. If that one already lives in your head, fine, keep using it. But the textbook's primary tool is the right-hand push rule, so that's the one I'd default to, and whichever you use, here's the non-negotiable point.

NARRATOR: The hand rule is a memory aid. It is not a physical law, and it is not an explanation. The course materials are blunt about this — you use it to get the direction, but you never quote it as the reason a force exists. If an "explain why" question comes up and you write "because of the right-hand rule", that's the same dead end as writing "because F equals B I L". The why is always, always the two-field interaction. The hand rule just tells you which way the result points once you already know there's a force. Direction tool, not explanation. I'll say it a third time later, because at high speed this is the kind of thing that slips past.

QUESTION: You mentioned conventional current versus electron flow as a trap. Can you spell that out, because I can feel it being a problem in the exam.

NARRATOR: Yes, and it's a sneaky one. The right-hand push rule is defined using conventional current — the direction positive charge would move. But the things actually moving in a metal wire are electrons, which are negative, and they drift the opposite way. So if a question hands you the direction of electron flow — and some deliberately do, to catch you — you must flip it before you apply the rule. Reverse the electron direction to get the conventional current, then point your thumb that way. Forget that, and your force comes out exactly backwards — wrong direction, lost mark. Same logic applies if you ever reason at the level of a single charge: a negative charge feels a force opposite to the right-hand prediction for a positive one. The safe habit, every time: convert to conventional current first, then use the push rule.

NARRATOR: One more piece of notation you'll meet in diagrams. A dot in a circle means the field or the current is coming out of the page, toward you — think of it as the tip of an arrow flying at your eye. A cross in a circle means it's going into the page, away from you — the tail feathers, the fletching, of an arrow flying away. Dot out, cross in. You'll need that constantly for reading and drawing motor-effect setups.

NARRATOR: Now the equation — the second dot-point, the one about how the magnitude of the force varies. In words: the force on a current-carrying conductor equals the magnetic field strength, times the current, times the length of conductor that's inside the field, times the sine of the angle theta between the conductor and the field. In symbols, on the data sheet, it's written F equals B I L sine theta. Let me take the four quantities one at a time, because the units and meanings are examinable.

NARRATOR: F is the force on the conductor, in newtons. B is the strength of the external magnetic field — properly called the magnetic flux density — measured in tesla. A tesla, by the way, is a newton per ampere per metre, which you can read straight off this equation. I is the current in the conductor, in amperes. L is the length of the conductor — but specifically the length that lies within the field region, in metres. A small note on letters: the textbook tends to write a lowercase l for length, while the data sheet uses a capital L. Same quantity — the length of wire in the field — don't let the two letters confuse you. And theta is the angle between the conductor and the magnetic field lines.

NARRATOR: That angle is where students lose marks, so I'm going to hammer it. Theta is the angle between the wire and the field. It is not the angle between the wire and the force, and it is not the angle between the field and the force. Wire to field. Get the wrong angle in there and the whole calculation collapses. And here's the counter-intuitive bit that trips people: the force is maximum when theta is ninety degrees — when the wire is perpendicular to the field. Because the sine of ninety degrees is one. And the force is zero when theta is zero, when the wire lies parallel to the field, because the sine of zero is zero. Students often guess the opposite — they think "parallel, lined up, that must be maximum" — but it's exactly backwards. Perpendicular is maximum, parallel is zero.

QUESTION: Why is it zero when the wire is parallel to the field? Intuitively, the wire is still sitting in a strong field — why no force at all?

NARRATOR: Two ways to see it, and both are worth knowing. The first goes back to lesson two — the single charge. The force on a moving charge depends on the component of its velocity that's perpendicular to the field. If the wire lies along the field, the charges are drifting straight along the field lines. Their velocity is parallel to B, so there's no perpendicular component at all — nothing for the field to push sideways against. In the language of the cross product, velocity cross field is zero when they're parallel. No perpendicular velocity, no force. The second way uses today's two-field picture: if the current runs parallel to the external field, the wire's circular field doesn't create that front-to-back, strong-side-weak-side asymmetry across the field direction — there's no lopsidedness to drive the wire sideways. Either explanation earns the mark; pick the one that sticks for you. And notice it's the same deep reason as the sine in lesson two's q v B sine theta — the motor effect equation is literally that single-charge force, summed over the whole wire.

NARRATOR: Let me make the four factors concrete, because the dot-point is "analyse how the force varies by changing the conductor length, current, field strength, and angle". Four knobs you can turn. Turn up the current I, and the force goes up in direct proportion — double the current, double the force — because there are more charges flowing per second, so more individual little forces to add up. Turn up the length L of wire in the field, force goes up in proportion — more length means more current-carrying charges actually sitting inside the field region. Turn up the field strength B, force goes up in proportion — a stronger field means a bigger force on each moving charge. And turn the angle theta from zero up toward ninety degrees, and the force grows as the sine of theta — zero when parallel, climbing to its maximum when perpendicular. Three of the four are simple direct proportions; only the angle behaves as a sine. You don't even need a separate mnemonic — the relationships are written right into the equation: force equals B I L sine theta, so force is proportional to B, to I, to L, and to sine theta. Read the formula and you've read the physics.

NARRATOR: There's one more reading of that equation that examiners like, and it's worth ten seconds. You can group it as B, times I, times the quantity L sine theta — and L sine theta is the component of the wire's length that's perpendicular to the field. Or you can group it as the quantity B sine theta, times I, times L — and B sine theta is the component of the field that's perpendicular to the wire. Either reading is fine: it's the field times the current times the perpendicular length, or the perpendicular field times the current times the length. Both say the same thing — only the perpendicular parts contribute.

NARRATOR: Worked example B is a quick order-of-magnitude one, good for the "maximum force" phrasing.
