NARRATOR: In the spring of 1905, in a rented apartment in Bern, Switzerland, a twenty-six-year-old patent clerk sat down and, over the course of a single year, broke physics open in four different places. He had no laboratory. He had no university position. He had no research funding, no graduate students, no telescope, no vacuum pump. His official title was Technical Expert, Third Class, at the Swiss Federal Patent Office. He spent six days a week reading applications for things like electromagnetic switches and gravel sorters, and he did his real work in the gaps. His name was Albert Einstein.

NARRATOR: That year, nineteen oh five, is now called his Annus Mirabilis. His miraculous year. He published four papers, and the staggering thing is not that they were good. It is that each one of them, on its own, would have made him the greatest physicist of his generation. One paper proved that atoms are real, by explaining the jittering dance of pollen grains in water. One paper introduced special relativity, and rewrote our understanding of space and time. One paper, almost a footnote to relativity, gave us the most famous equation in all of science, energy equals mass times the speed of light squared. And one paper, the very first of the four, posted in March, explained a strange little laboratory curiosity about ultraviolet light knocking electrons off a metal plate.

NARRATOR: Now, here is the part that should stop you. If you had asked Einstein himself which of these four papers was the radical one, the dangerous one, the one that would keep him up at night, he would not have said relativity. In a letter to his friend Conrad Habicht, written in May of nineteen oh five, he ran through what he was working on. And when he got to that first paper, the one about light and electrons, he described it with a single word that he used for none of the others. He called it, in his words, very revolutionary.

NARRATOR: Not relativity. This one. The little paper about light hitting metal. Today we call the phenomenon the photoelectric effect, and the idea Einstein invented to explain it, the idea he himself found so unsettling, is the foundation of quantum physics. This is the story of how a patent clerk looked at a problem that had stumped every physicist in Europe, and solved it by proposing something so strange that even the man who had laid the groundwork for it refused to believe him. Even the man whose own experiments proved Einstein right spent ten years of his life trying to prove him wrong.

NARRATOR: To understand why it was revolutionary, you first have to understand how certain everyone was, in nineteen oh five, that they already knew exactly what light was.

NARRATOR: For most of the nineteenth century, the question of light's nature had been settled, and settled hard, in favour of the wave. Thomas Young had shown, around eighteen oh three, that when light passes through two narrow slits it produces an interference pattern, bright bands and dark bands, exactly the way ripples on a pond reinforce and cancel each other. Particles do not do that. Only waves do that. Then, in eighteen sixty-four, the Scottish physicist James Clerk Maxwell wrote down four equations that unified electricity and magnetism, and out of those equations fell a prediction so beautiful it felt like a revelation. The equations said that a changing electric field creates a magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field creates an electric field, and the two could chase each other through empty space as a self-sustaining wave, travelling at a very particular speed. When Maxwell calculated that speed, it came out to be the speed of light. Light was an electromagnetic wave. The matter was closed.

NARRATOR: And then, in eighteen eighty-seven, a German physicist named Heinrich Hertz set out to prove Maxwell right, and accidentally discovered the very phenomenon that would eventually prove Maxwell incomplete.

NARRATOR: Hertz was working at Karlsruhe Polytechnic, building an apparatus to generate and detect Maxwell's predicted electromagnetic waves. The idea was simple in principle. You make a spark jump across a gap in one circuit, the transmitter, and that spark sends out an electromagnetic wave. Across the room, you have a second circuit, a loop of wire with its own tiny gap, the detector. If Maxwell was right, the arriving wave would induce a current in that loop, and you would see a faint spark jump across the detector gap. Hertz built it. It worked. He had produced and detected radio waves for the first time in human history, confirming Maxwell's theory in the most direct way imaginable. That alone made him famous.

NARRATOR: But Hertz was a careful experimenter, and careful experimenters notice things. The detector spark was faint, and to see it better he enclosed the detector in a dark case. And the moment he did that, the spark got weaker. Something about putting the detector in a box was making the effect harder to produce. He started removing parts of the case one at a time to find the culprit, and he discovered that the critical piece was the panel that blocked the detector from seeing the light of the transmitter spark. When ultraviolet light from the transmitter could shine directly onto the metal of the detector gap, the detector sparked more easily. Block that light, and it sparked less. The ultraviolet light was somehow helping electrons jump the gap.

NARRATOR: Hertz wrote it up. He described it precisely, honestly, in detail. And then he did the most human thing imaginable. He had no idea why it happened, it had nothing to do with the radio waves he actually cared about, so he set it aside and moved on. He never investigated it further. Seven years later, in eighteen ninety-four, Heinrich Hertz died of a blood vessel disease, aged just thirty-six. He had opened a door and walked straight past it.

QUESTION: So if Hertz didn't follow it up, who actually figured out what was being knocked loose?

NARRATOR: That took a chain of people. The next year, in eighteen eighty-eight, a German physicist named Wilhelm Hallwachs, working in Dresden, ran the cleanest version of the experiment yet. He took a plate of zinc and connected it to a gold-leaf electroscope, which is just a device that shows you electric charge by how far apart two thin leaves of gold are pushed. He charged the zinc plate negatively, so the leaves splayed apart, and then he shone ultraviolet light on the plate. The leaves collapsed. The charge drained away. Whatever the ultraviolet light was doing, it was expelling negative charge from the metal. And here is the crucial control. When he charged the plate positively instead, and shone the same light on it, nothing happened. The charge stayed put. So the light was knocking out negative charges specifically, and a positively charged plate just pulled them straight back. This became known as the Hallwachs effect.

NARRATOR: But what were those negative charges? That answer came from Cambridge, from the Cavendish Laboratory, where in eighteen ninety-seven J. J. Thomson had just discovered the electron. Two years later, in eighteen ninety-nine, Thomson turned that discovery on the photoelectric problem. He enclosed the metal surface in an evacuated tube, shone ultraviolet light on it, and showed that the particles being knocked out of the metal were electrons, the very same particles he had found in cathode rays. And here is the crucial new feature of his experiment. In a cathode-ray tube the electrons are torn out by a strong electric field. Here, there was no such field. The electrons were being ejected by the radiation itself, by the light alone. So now the picture had a name. Light strikes a metal. Electrons come flying off. We call them photoelectrons, and we call the whole business the photoelectric effect. The phenomenon was real, it was repeatable, and it had a particle identity. What it did not have was an explanation.

NARRATOR: And that brings us to the man who did the hard, patient, decisive experimental work, the man who measured exactly what the photoelectric effect does, and who has one of the strangest and darkest stories in the history of science. His name was Philipp Lenard.

NARRATOR: Lenard was Hungarian-German, and he had been Hertz's assistant, so the photoelectric effect was almost a family inheritance. Between eighteen ninety-nine and nineteen oh two, he built a beautiful apparatus to study it properly. He put a metal plate inside a vacuum tube, shone light on it, and collected the ejected electrons on a second plate, the collector. When the electrons hit the collector, they made a tiny current that he could measure. That gave him the number of electrons. To measure their energy, he did something clever. He charged the collector negatively, so it pushed back against the incoming electrons, like running uphill. Only electrons with enough kinetic energy could fight their way up that hill and reach the collector. By turning up the opposing voltage until even the fastest electrons could no longer make it, until the current dropped to exactly zero, he could measure the energy of the most energetic electrons. That critical voltage is called the stopping voltage, and it is one of the most important measurements in this whole story. Remember it.

NARRATOR: By nineteen oh two, Lenard had results. And his results were quietly, completely devastating to the wave theory of light. He found four things, and you should hold each one in your mind, because everything turns on them.

NARRATOR: First. There was no time delay. The instant the light hit the metal, electrons came off. Even when he turned the light down very dim, so that very little energy was arriving per second, the electrons still came off immediately. There was no warm-up period.

NARRATOR: Second. There was a threshold frequency. Below a certain frequency of light, a certain colour, no electrons came off at all. None. And it did not matter how bright the light was. You could take a feeble violet light and get electrons, and take a blindingly intense red light and get nothing whatsoever. Below the threshold, zero, no matter the intensity.

NARRATOR: Third. Turning up the intensity, making the light brighter, increased the number of electrons emitted. More light, more electrons. That one, at least, made sense.

NARRATOR: And fourth, the strangest of all. The energy of each ejected electron did not depend on the brightness of the light at all. It depended only on the frequency. Higher frequency light, light shifted towards the blue and the ultraviolet, produced more energetic electrons. But making the light brighter, while keeping its colour the same, did absolutely nothing to the energy of the individual electrons. It just made more of them, all coming off at the same energy.

QUESTION: Hang on. Why is that fourth result such a problem? Why can't a wave just explain that?

NARRATOR: Because it cuts against the entire intuition of what a wave is. Think about what intensity means for a wave. A brighter light is a bigger wave, a wave carrying more energy. So in the wave picture, when you shine a brighter light on the metal, you are pouring more energy onto those electrons. They should come off faster, with more kinetic energy. That is what waves do. A bigger ocean wave hits you harder than a small one. But that is not what happens. Crank the brightness all the way up, and each electron comes off with exactly the same energy as before. There are just more of them. The wave theory had no answer for that. None at all.

NARRATOR: And the threshold was just as damning. In the wave picture, energy arrives continuously and the metal soaks it up. So even dim red light, given enough time, should eventually deliver enough energy to free an electron. It might take a while at low intensity, the electron slowly filling up like a bucket under a dripping tap, but eventually the bucket overflows and out comes the electron. So the wave theory predicted two things that turned out to be flatly false. It predicted that any colour of light would work if you waited long enough or made it bright enough, and there should be no hard threshold. And it predicted a measurable time delay at low intensity, the bucket slowly filling. Lenard saw neither. There was an absolute threshold, and there was no delay. The classical wave theory of light, Maxwell's magnificent, triumphant, seemingly unassailable theory, simply could not account for what was happening on that little metal plate.

NARRATOR: So the stage was set. By nineteen oh two there was a clean, careful, beautifully measured set of experimental facts that nobody could explain. The data sat there for three years, like a locked door, waiting for someone to find the key. And in nineteen oh five, the patent clerk in Bern found it.

NARRATOR: Einstein's move was breathtakingly bold, and to understand just how bold, you need to know what was lurking in the background, a clue that had been hiding in a completely different problem for five years.

NARRATOR: Back in nineteen hundred, the German physicist Max Planck had been wrestling with an entirely separate puzzle, the colour of light given off by hot objects, what physicists call black body radiation. The classical theory of that problem had failed catastrophically, predicting that a hot object should radiate infinite energy at short wavelengths, an absurdity nicknamed the ultraviolet catastrophe. Planck found that he could fix the maths, he could make the theory match reality perfectly, only if he made one bizarre assumption. He had to assume that the hot atoms could only emit energy in discrete lumps, little packets, and that the size of each packet was proportional to the frequency of the light. The energy of a packet equalled a tiny new constant, multiplied by the frequency. That constant is now called Planck's constant, and its value is about six point six three times ten to the minus thirty-four joule seconds. An absurdly small number, which is exactly why nobody had ever noticed these lumps before.

NARRATOR: But, and this is the key, Planck did not believe his own lumps were real. He thought it was a mathematical trick, a bookkeeping device that happened to give the right answer. He assumed the energy of light itself was still a smooth, continuous wave, and that the lumpiness was just something odd about how atoms happened to emit it. Planck had quantised the exchange of energy. He had not, in his own mind, quantised light.

NARRATOR: Einstein took the lump, and did the thing Planck had refused to do. He said, what if the lumps are real? What if light itself, out there travelling through space, is not a continuous wave at all, but actually comes in discrete, indivisible particles, each one carrying a packet of energy equal to Planck's constant times its frequency? That is the equation. The energy of one particle of light equals Planck's constant times frequency. Energy equals h f. We call those particles photons now, though that word would not be coined for another twenty-one years.

NARRATOR: And watch how this single idea, this one assumption, unlocks every single one of Lenard's stubborn results at once. It is one of the most satisfying moments in all of physics.

NARRATOR: Einstein said, picture the photon as a tiny billiard ball of light. When it strikes the metal, it does not spread its energy across the whole surface like a wave. It collides with exactly one electron, and it hands over all of its energy to that one electron, all or nothing, in a single instantaneous transaction. In Einstein's own words, the simplest conception is that a light quantum transfers its entire energy to a single electron. One photon, one electron. And an individual electron can never accept energy from more than one photon. Now, that electron is bound inside the metal. It takes a certain minimum amount of energy to break it free from the surface, and that minimum energy is a property of the particular metal. We call it the work function, and in your formulas it carries the symbol capital W. So the electron takes the photon's energy, spends some of it, the work function, just escaping the metal, and keeps whatever is left over as its kinetic energy, the energy of its motion. In plain words. The maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electron equals the photon's energy, which is Planck's constant times frequency, minus the work function of the metal. That is Einstein's photoelectric equation, and notice what it really is. It is nothing but the law of conservation of energy, applied to a single collision. Energy in, equals escape cost, plus energy out. This is one of the dot-points you are responsible for.

NARRATOR: Now feed Lenard's four puzzles into it, one at a time, and watch them dissolve.

NARRATOR: Why is there no time delay? Because the energy transfer is a single collision, not a slow accumulation. The first photon to arrive can free an electron immediately. There is no bucket slowly filling. One photon, one electron, one instant.

NARRATOR: Why is there a threshold frequency? Because the photon's energy is set entirely by its frequency. If the frequency is too low, then Planck's constant times that frequency is less than the work function, the photon simply does not carry enough energy to pay the escape cost, and the electron stays put. And making the light brighter does not help one bit, because brighter just means more photons, and if no single photon has enough energy, then a billion of them, each individually too weak, still cannot free an electron. They cannot pool their energy, because each electron only ever interacts with one photon at a time. That is why a million-watt red laser produces nothing, while a faint violet glow produces electrons. It is the energy per photon that matters, not the total.

NARRATOR: Why does brightness increase the number of electrons but not their energy? Because brightness is the number of photons arriving per second. More photons means more individual collisions means more electrons knocked out. But every single photon of a given colour carries the exact same energy, Planck's constant times that one frequency. So every electron is freed by an identical packet, and they all come off with the same kinetic energy. Turning up the brightness just sends in more identical packets. More electrons, same energy each. Exactly what Lenard saw.

NARRATOR: And why does the electron energy go up with frequency? Because higher frequency means each photon carries more energy, Planck's constant times a bigger frequency. More energy in, the same work function spent on escape, so more kinetic energy left over. The relationship is beautifully simple and linear. Einstein predicted that if you plotted the maximum kinetic energy of the electrons against the frequency of the light, you would get a straight line, and the slope of that line would be Planck's constant itself, the very same number Planck had pulled out of the black body problem five years earlier. The same constant, surfacing in two completely unrelated phenomena. That is the signature of a deep truth.

NARRATOR: One assumption. Every result explained. It should have been a triumph. It was met, instead, with near-total silence and, where it was noticed at all, with disbelief.

QUESTION: Wait, why? If it explained everything, why didn't people accept it immediately?

NARRATOR: Because of the cost. To accept Einstein's photon, you had to throw away a hundred years of unshakeable evidence that light is a wave. The interference of Young, the diffraction patterns, the polarisation, Maxwell's equations, all of it screams that light is a wave, and waves do not come in particles. Light bends around corners and forms interference fringes precisely because it is a continuous wave. You cannot have it both ways. Or so everyone thought. Einstein was not just adding a new idea, he was appearing to contradict the most well-established theory in physics. And remember who was rejecting it. Max Planck himself, the man who had invented the quantum, did not believe Einstein. When Planck recommended Einstein for membership of the Prussian Academy of Sciences a few years later, glowing about his genius, he actually apologised for the photon idea, saying that Einstein may sometimes have missed the target, as for example with his hypothesis of light quanta. The inventor of the quantum thought Einstein had gone too far with it.

NARRATOR: Einstein himself knew exactly how provocative he was being, and he flagged it right in the title of the paper. The German title translates as, On a Heuristic Viewpoint Concerning the Production and Transformation of Light. Heuristic. That word is a deliberate signal. It means a useful working model, a tool for finding things, not a finished, proven theory. Einstein was saying, in effect, I know this seems to contradict the wave nature of light, I am not claiming I have resolved that, I am offering this as a productive way of thinking that explains the facts. He was being honest about the tension. The full reconciliation, the idea that light is genuinely both wave and particle at once, what we call wave-particle duality, would not arrive for another twenty years, with de Broglie in nineteen twenty-four and the birth of quantum mechanics in nineteen twenty-five and twenty-six.

NARRATOR: And this, finally, is why Einstein called this paper, and not relativity, the revolutionary one. Special relativity was radical, yes, but it extended classical physics. It kept a universe that was continuous, deterministic, smooth. The photon did something far more disturbing. It said that at the deepest level, nature is grainy, lumpy, discontinuous. It said the smooth world is an illusion that breaks down when you look closely enough. Einstein, who had thrown the first stone, spent the rest of his life uneasy about where it led. Decades later, as quantum mechanics revealed a universe governed by probability and chance, he famously protested that God does not play dice with the universe. Which is a remarkable thing to say, when you are the man who first put the dice on the table.

NARRATOR: So in nineteen oh five the theory existed, elegant and complete, and almost nobody believed it. What it needed was proof. Hard, undeniable, experimental proof. And the proof, when it came, arrived from the most unlikely source imaginable. It came from a man who set out, deliberately and with great determination, to destroy Einstein's equation.

NARRATOR: His name was Robert Millikan, an American experimental physicist at the University of Chicago, and he was one of the finest measurers who ever lived. Millikan thought Einstein's photon hypothesis was, in his own words, wholly untenable. Reckless. A betrayal of everything physics knew about the wave nature of light. He found the idea so offensive that he resolved to prove the equation wrong by measuring it to death. He expected the data to disagree with Einstein, and he set out to show exactly how.

NARRATOR: It took him ten years. And the apparatus he built to do it was a masterpiece. The great enemy of this kind of measurement is contamination. Metals, especially the reactive ones with low work functions, form a thin oxide skin the moment they meet air, and that skin ruins the measurement. So Millikan built what he half-jokingly called a machine shop in vacuo, a machine shop inside a vacuum. Inside a sealed, evacuated glass chamber, he installed a sharp knife on a magnetically operated arm, so that he could shave a fresh, clean, mirror-bright surface off the metal sample without ever exposing it to air. A pristine surface, every single time, in a perfect vacuum.

NARRATOR: Then he did the experiment Einstein's equation demanded. He shone light of a known frequency on the freshly cut metal, and measured the stopping voltage, the reverse voltage needed to bring the photoelectron current to exactly zero, which gives you the maximum kinetic energy of the electrons. He did this for many different frequencies of light, and for several different alkali metals, sodium and the like, which have nice low work functions. And then he plotted his graph, stopping voltage against frequency, the exact graph Einstein had predicted nine years earlier.

NARRATOR: The points fell on a straight line. A perfectly straight line. For every metal. And the lines for the different metals were all parallel to each other, all with the same slope, just as Einstein had said. And that slope, when Millikan worked it out, gave a value for Planck's constant of six point five seven times ten to the minus thirty-four joule seconds. Now here is why Millikan, of all people, could nail this so precisely. He had already, in nineteen thirteen, performed his famous oil-drop experiment, the one that measured the charge on a single electron to extraordinary precision. So he knew the charge of the electron better than anyone alive. And the slope of his photoelectric graph gave him Planck's constant divided by that charge. Knowing the charge, he could extract Planck's constant alone, to within about half a percent. It matched Planck's own value from black body radiation almost exactly. Einstein's equation was not approximately right. It was exactly right, dead on, the most precisely confirmed equation of its kind.

NARRATOR: Millikan had spent a decade trying to disprove Einstein, and instead he had produced the most beautiful confirmation of the photoelectric equation ever made. And how did he take it? Badly, at first. Even as he published his triumphant results in nineteen sixteen, even as he declared that Einstein's equation, in his words, accurately represents the energy of electron emission, he simultaneously insisted that the physical theory behind it, the photon, was, again his words, totally unreasonable. He confirmed the equation while explicitly rejecting the idea that made the equation work. He once wrote that he had been compelled, contrary to all his expectations, to assert the equation's unambiguous experimental verification, in spite of its unreasonableness, since it seemed to violate everything we knew about the interference of light.

NARRATOR: Think about the integrity in that, and the agony of it. A man so committed to the data that he announced a result he hated, a result that demolished his own deeply held belief, simply because the experiment said so. That is science working exactly as it should. The universe does not care what you want to be true. Millikan came around eventually. Decades later, in his nineteen fifty autobiography, he wrote a chapter on this very work, and he titled it, the experimental proof of the existence of the photon. The man who set out to bury the photon ended up writing its proof.

QUESTION: So after Millikan, was that it? Was everyone finally convinced light came in particles?

NARRATOR: Not quite everyone, not yet. There was a hard core of physicists who accepted that Einstein's equation worked, but still could not swallow the photon as a real, physical particle. They needed one more thing. They needed to see a photon behave like a particle in a way that had nothing to do with energy and electrons. They needed to see it carry momentum and collide. And that came in nineteen twenty-three, from the American physicist Arthur Compton. Compton fired X-rays, which are just very high frequency light, at electrons, and he found that the X-rays bounced off and came away with a longer wavelength, a lower frequency, having lost energy in the collision, exactly as if they were particles ricocheting off other particles and transferring momentum, like two billiard balls clicking together. This is the Compton effect, and it was the final blow. After Compton, the photon was real. The debate was over. Light was a wave, and light was a particle, and physics would have to learn to live with both.

NARRATOR: The recognition for Einstein arrived in stages, and in a way that has its own irony. In nineteen twenty-one, he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics, the prize actually presented the following year. But read the citation carefully, because students get this wrong all the time. He did not win it for relativity. The official citation reads, for his services to theoretical physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect. Not relativity. The Nobel Committee was still uneasy about whether relativity had been experimentally confirmed, and they wanted something solid, something Millikan had measured to half a percent. So the most famous scientist in human history won his only Nobel Prize for this, for the photon, for the little paper he himself had called revolutionary. And the word photon, by the way, was finally coined in nineteen twenty-six, not by Einstein, but by the American chemist Gilbert Lewis.

NARRATOR: There is one more thread in this story, and it is the darkest one, because it shows what can happen when scientific resentment curdles into something monstrous. Remember Philipp Lenard, the man whose careful experiments had laid out all the facts that Einstein explained? Lenard believed, with some justification, that he had done the real work, the hard laboratory work, the measuring. And he watched as a Jewish theorist who had never spent a day in a laboratory took the data and claimed the glory of explaining it. Lenard had won his own Nobel Prize in nineteen oh five, for his work on cathode rays, but the resentment over the photoelectric effect festered in him for decades. And it grew into something terrible. As the Nazis rose, Lenard became one of the leading champions of what he called Deutsche Physik, Aryan Physics. He denounced relativity and quantum theory as Jewish physics, decadent and false. He worked actively to drive Jewish scientists out of German universities. And when Einstein was forced to flee Germany in nineteen thirty-three, Lenard, by accounts, celebrated. It is one of the bitterest ironies in the history of science. The man whose own experiments proved the quantum nature of light became a sworn enemy of the man who understood what those experiments meant.

NARRATOR: And while all of that human drama played out, the physics went quietly to work, and it built the modern world. Because once you understand that light can knock electrons loose, and you can engineer materials to do it efficiently, you have a way of turning light directly into electricity, and into information.

NARRATOR: The photoelectric effect is, quite literally, how your devices see. Start with the early phototubes and photocells of the nineteen-tens and twenties, vacuum tubes where light striking a photocathode released electrons and made a current. They were the eyes of early technology. They read the soundtrack printed on the edge of a film reel, which is how the talkies got their sound. They opened automatic doors and triggered burglar alarms. The photomultiplier tube, which amplifies the electrons from even a single photon into a measurable pulse, became the workhorse of particle physics detectors, medical scanners, and astronomy, letting us catch individual particles of light from the faintest sources in the universe.

NARRATOR: Then came the solar cell. The first practical silicon solar cell was built at Bell Labs and announced on the twenty-fifth of April, nineteen fifty-four, by three researchers, Daryl Chapin, Calvin Fuller, and Gerald Pearson. Its first public version converted about six percent of the sunlight that hit it into electricity, and within that same year they pushed it past ten percent. The underlying physics is the photoelectric effect at work inside a semiconductor. A photon arrives, and if its energy exceeds the energy gap of the silicon, it liberates an electron, just as it would from a metal surface, except here the freed electrons are swept into a circuit to do useful work. Today, the panels on rooftops and solar farms run at around twenty-two to twenty-four percent efficiency in commercial cells, with laboratory records above twenty-nine percent for single-junction silicon. Every one of them is Einstein's nineteen oh five paper, turned into hardware, generating clean power for the planet. And this is exactly the application your syllabus singles out, when it asks you to explain how the photoelectric effect is used in photovoltaics.

NARRATOR: And then, the device that may have changed daily life most of all. In nineteen sixty-nine, also at Bell Labs, Willard Boyle and George Smith invented the charge-coupled device, the CCD. In a CCD, the surface is divided into millions of tiny pixels, and in each pixel, incoming photons knock electrons loose, the photoelectric effect again, and those electrons are caught and counted as a packet of charge. Count the charge in every pixel, and you have reconstructed the image, photon by photon. The CCD became the sensor inside digital cameras, the eye of the Hubble Space Telescope, the heart of medical imaging. Boyle and Smith won the Nobel Prize in Physics in two thousand nine for it. Every digital photograph ever taken, every selfie, every image of a distant galaxy, exists because light comes in countable packets that knock out countable electrons. Einstein's revolutionary little idea is sitting in your pocket right now, looking back at you through your phone's camera.

NARRATOR: That is the arc of it. A spark Hertz could not explain, a discharge Hallwachs measured, an identity Thomson supplied, four impossible facts Lenard pinned down, one revolutionary assumption from a patent clerk who had no laboratory, ten years of a hostile genius trying and failing to tear it down, and finally a Nobel Prize, the birth of quantum mechanics, and the silicon eyes through which our whole civilisation now sees. All of it from ultraviolet light, and a clean plate of metal, and the simple, world-breaking realisation that light is not smooth. Light comes in lumps.

NARRATOR: For your New South Wales HSC Physics course, this story is the evidence behind the nature of light, and specifically the quantum model of light. At its centre is one idea the syllabus wants you to be able to explain in your own words. The particle model of light says that light comes in photons, each one carrying a discrete, indivisible amount of energy set entirely by its frequency, Planck's constant times that frequency. Hold on to that, because everything else follows from it. When the syllabus asks you to investigate the evidence from photoelectric effect investigations that demonstrated inconsistency with the wave model of light, this is that evidence. The no time delay, the threshold frequency, the energy depending on frequency and not intensity, those are the observations the wave model cannot explain, and the photon model can. Notice that one of the four, that brighter light gives more electrons, the wave model actually does get right, so be careful in an exam to say that the wave model fails on the other three. When it asks you to relate the law of conservation of energy to the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons, that is Einstein's equation, the maximum kinetic energy of the electron equals the photon energy, Planck's constant times frequency, minus the work function W. The photon arrives with its energy, pays the work function to escape, and keeps the rest as kinetic energy. Conservation of energy, one collision at a time. The same equation describes thermionic emission, where the escape energy is supplied by heat rather than a photon, and you may be asked to analyse data from either. And remember where the photon came from in the first place. It came from Planck wrestling with black body radiation, with the way the wavelength of peak emission shifts as an object heats up, which you handle with Wien's law, and with the ultraviolet catastrophe that the classical wave theory could not avoid. That is the line that runs from the black body problem, through Einstein, through Millikan's reluctant proof, straight to the panel on the roof and the camera in your hand. Those last two, the solar cell and the digital sensor, are your photoelectric effect used in photovoltaics, and your one other real-world application, both of which the syllabus asks you to be able to explain. Know the observations, know that the slope of the kinetic-energy-versus-frequency graph is Planck's constant, and know that the line crosses the frequency axis at the threshold frequency and the energy axis at minus the work function. That is the heart of what they will ask you. The full equation and a complete worked example are in the supplementary materials.
