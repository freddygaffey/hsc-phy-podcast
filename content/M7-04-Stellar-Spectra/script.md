NARRATOR: And the size of the shift gives you the speed, specifically the radial velocity, the component of the star's velocity straight along the line of sight between Earth and the star. NARRATOR: First, rotational broadening. A star spins on an axis. So one edge of its disc, the limb rotating toward us, is moving toward us and blueshifts the light coming from that side. The opposite edge, rotating away, redshifts its light. But we cannot resolve the two edges, remember the star is a single point, so the light from the whole spinning disc arrives at once, all mixed together. One side's lines nudged blue, the other side's nudged red, the middle unshifted, all superimposed. The result is that each absorption line is smeared across a small range of wavelengths. The line does not move, it widens. And the faster the star spins, the wider the smear. Faster rotation, broader lines. That is how the spectrum gives rotational velocity.

NARRATOR: Second, density broadening, which has a proper name, pressure broadening, sometimes called collision broadening. In a denser outer layer, the atoms are packed closer together, so they collide with each other far more often. Every collision jolts the energy levels of an absorbing atom slightly, so it ends up absorbing over a slightly wider band of wavelengths instead of one precise wavelength. Add up all those jostled atoms and the absorption line broadens. So a denser star shows broader lines, and a low-density star shows narrow, sharp lines. This is genuinely useful: a red giant, near the end of its life, has puffed its outer layers out to enormous size so they are extremely thin and low-density, and its lines are correspondingly narrow. A dense, compact main-sequence star shows broader lines. Pressure broadening is most pronounced in the smaller, denser stars. So line width is a clue to where a star sits in its life cycle, whether it is on the main sequence or has evolved off it.
