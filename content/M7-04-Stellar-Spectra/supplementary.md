# Supplementary Materials

Key relationships, the four-property map, and model extended-response answers for this episode. Nothing here is spoken in the audio — it is the read-along reference.

### Listing 1 — Wien's displacement law (qualitative use here; quantitative in M7-10)

$$
\lambda_{\max} = \frac{b}{T}
$$

- \(\lambda_{\max}\) = wavelength of peak intensity of the continuous (black-body) spectrum [metres, \(\mathrm{m}\)]
- \(T\) = absolute SURFACE temperature of the star [kelvin, \(\mathrm{K}\)]
- \(b\) = Wien's displacement constant \(= 2.898 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m\cdot K}\) (lesson rounds to \(2.9 \times 10^{-3}\))

**Direction** (the only thing needed in M7-04):

- \(T\) larger \(\to \lambda_{\max}\) smaller \(\to\) peak shifts toward BLUE \(\to\) hotter, bluer star
- \(T\) smaller \(\to \lambda_{\max}\) larger \(\to\) peak shifts toward RED \(\to\) cooler, redder star

Colour–temperature scale (hottest \(\to\) coolest): blue \(\to\) white (Sirius) \(\to\) yellow (the Sun) \(\to\) orange \(\to\) red (Betelgeuse).

Memory hook: "hot and blue, short and true." Always quote stellar temperature in KELVIN. Colour gives the SURFACE temperature, not the core.

Note: \(1\ \mathrm{nm} = 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m}\); \(1\) ångström (Å) \(= 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{m}\). Absolute zero: \(0\ \mathrm{K} = -273.15\ \mathrm{°C}\). The quantitative substitution practice (numbers into \(\lambda_{\max} = b/T\)) belongs to M7-10.

### Listing 2 — The four properties and their four spectral features (the master map)
| Property (syllabus) | Spectral feature | What you read it from | Key rule / trap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **T**emperature | **Peak** | Wavelength of peak intensity of the continuous spectrum | Wien: hotter = shorter peak = bluer; quote in K; surface not core |
| **C**omposition | **Pattern** | Which absorption lines are present, matched to lab element fingerprints | Mostly hydrogen + helium; line *positions*, not the peak |
| **V**elocity (translational/radial) | **Shift** | Doppler shift of the *whole* line pattern | Toward = blueshift (shorter λ); away = redshift (longer λ); radial component only |
| **Velocity (rotational)** | **Spread** | Broadening of each line as spinning limbs blue/red-shift | Faster spin = broader lines; line widens, does not move |
| **D**ensity | **Spread** | Pressure (collision) broadening of each line | Denser = more collisions = broader; red giants = narrow lines |

Hook: T-C-V-D \(\to\) Peak, Pattern, Shift, Spread.

The two broadening causes share one symptom — a wider line: "same Spread, spin or squeeze, argue which."

- spin = rotational (Doppler) broadening
- squeeze = density / pressure (collision) broadening

A broad line ALONE is ambiguous — the cause must be distinguished.

The #1 marker trap: a SHIFT moves the whole pattern (translational velocity); a BROADENING widens each line but keeps it centred (rotation or density). Never say rotation "shifts" the lines or that radial motion "broadens" them.

### Listing 3 — Model 6-mark answer: temperature AND composition from a spectrum

Q: "Explain how the spectrum of a star can be used to determine its surface temperature and its chemical composition." (6 marks)

A stellar spectrum is a continuous black-body spectrum (from the hot, dense inner layers) crossed by dark absorption lines (from the cooler outer atmosphere). These two features carry two INDEPENDENT pieces of information.

**Surface temperature — from the CONTINUOUS part (the Peak):**

- A star radiates approximately as a black body, so \(T\) sets the shape of the curve.
- By Wien's law, \(\lambda_{\max} = b/T\), the peak wavelength is inversely proportional to \(T\).
- Hotter stars peak at shorter (bluer) \(\lambda\); cooler stars at longer (redder) \(\lambda\).
- Blue = hot, yellow (Sun) = middling, red = cool. Measuring \(\lambda_{\max}\) gives \(T\) in kelvin.
- This is the SURFACE temperature (sets the colour), not the much hotter core.

**Chemical composition — from the ABSORPTION-LINE PATTERN (not the continuum):**

- Each element absorbs at a unique, fixed set of wavelengths — its fingerprint.
- Match the dark-line wavelengths to laboratory reference spectra of known elements.
- Identifies which elements are present; line strengths give relative abundances.
- Result: stars are mostly hydrogen and helium (helium found in the Sun first — Lockyer 1868 — before Ramsay isolated it on Earth, 1895; Payne-Gaposchkin, 1920s).

**Keep separate (the marks are here):**

- Temperature \(\leftarrow\) Peak of the continuous spectrum (Wien)
- Composition \(\leftarrow\) Pattern of the absorption lines (element matching)

### Listing 4 — Model answer: velocity from the Doppler effect

Q: "Explain how the Doppler effect allows astronomers to determine the velocity of a star."

- If a star moves relative to us, the Doppler effect shifts the ENTIRE line pattern.
- Moving TOWARD the observer \(\to\) wavelengths shortened \(\to\) lines shift to BLUE = blueshift.
- Moving AWAY from the observer \(\to\) wavelengths lengthened \(\to\) lines shift to RED = redshift.
- The SIZE of the shift gives the RADIAL velocity (component along the line of sight); a bigger shift = a faster radial speed.
- The Doppler shift gives ONLY the radial component. The TANGENTIAL component (motion across the sky) must be found another way (proper motion).
- TRUE translational velocity = vector sum of radial + tangential velocities.

Hook: "approaching is bluer." (toward = blue = shorter \(\lambda\); away = red = longer \(\lambda\))

### Listing 5 — Names and dates worth quoting for a mark
| Name | Date | Contribution |
