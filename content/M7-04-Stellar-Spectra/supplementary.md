# Supplementary Materials

Key relationships, the four-property map, and model extended-response answers for this episode. Nothing here is spoken in the audio — it is the read-along reference.

### Listing 1 — Wien's displacement law (qualitative use here; quantitative in M7-10)
```text
λ_max = b / T

λ_max = wavelength of peak intensity of the continuous (black-body) spectrum   [metres, m]
T      = absolute SURFACE temperature of the star                              [kelvin, K]
b      = Wien's displacement constant = 2.898 × 10^-3 m·K   (lesson rounds to 2.9 × 10^-3)

Direction (the only thing needed in M7-04):
  T larger  →  λ_max smaller  →  peak shifts toward BLUE  →  hotter, bluer star
  T smaller →  λ_max larger   →  peak shifts toward RED   →  cooler, redder star

Colour–temperature scale (hottest → coolest):
  blue  →  white (Sirius)  →  yellow (the Sun)  →  orange  →  red (Betelgeuse)

Memory hook: "hot and blue, short and true."
Always quote stellar temperature in KELVIN. Colour gives the SURFACE temperature, not the core.

Note: 1 nm = 10^-9 m ;  1 ångström (Å) = 10^-10 m.  Absolute zero: 0 K = −273.15 °C.
The quantitative substitution practice (numbers into λ_max = b/T) belongs to M7-10.
```

### Listing 2 — The four properties and their four spectral features (the master map)
| Property (syllabus) | Spectral feature | What you read it from | Key rule / trap |
|---|---|---|---|
| **T**emperature | **Peak** | Wavelength of peak intensity of the continuous spectrum | Wien: hotter = shorter peak = bluer; quote in K; surface not core |
| **C**omposition | **Pattern** | Which absorption lines are present, matched to lab element fingerprints | Mostly hydrogen + helium; line *positions*, not the peak |
| **V**elocity (translational/radial) | **Shift** | Doppler shift of the *whole* line pattern | Toward = blueshift (shorter λ); away = redshift (longer λ); radial component only |
| **Velocity (rotational)** | **Spread** | Broadening of each line as spinning limbs blue/red-shift | Faster spin = broader lines; line widens, does not move |
| **D**ensity | **Spread** | Pressure (collision) broadening of each line | Denser = more collisions = broader; red giants = narrow lines |

```text
Hook: T-C-V-D  →  Peak, Pattern, Shift, Spread.

The two broadening causes share one symptom — a wider line:
  "same Spread, spin or squeeze, argue which."
  spin   = rotational (Doppler) broadening
  squeeze= density / pressure (collision) broadening
A broad line ALONE is ambiguous — the cause must be distinguished.

The #1 marker trap: a SHIFT moves the whole pattern (translational velocity);
a BROADENING widens each line but keeps it centred (rotation or density).
Never say rotation "shifts" the lines or that radial motion "broadens" them.
```

### Listing 3 — Model 6-mark answer: temperature AND composition from a spectrum
```text
Q: "Explain how the spectrum of a star can be used to determine its surface
    temperature and its chemical composition." (6 marks)

A stellar spectrum is a continuous black-body spectrum (from the hot, dense inner
layers) crossed by dark absorption lines (from the cooler outer atmosphere). These
two features carry two INDEPENDENT pieces of information.

SURFACE TEMPERATURE  —  from the CONTINUOUS part (the Peak):
  • A star radiates approximately as a black body, so T sets the shape of the curve.
  • By Wien's law, λ_max = b/T, the peak wavelength is inversely proportional to T.
  • Hotter stars peak at shorter (bluer) λ; cooler stars at longer (redder) λ.
  • Blue = hot, yellow (Sun) = middling, red = cool. Measuring λ_max gives T in kelvin.
  • This is the SURFACE temperature (sets the colour), not the much hotter core.

CHEMICAL COMPOSITION  —  from the ABSORPTION-LINE PATTERN (not the continuum):
  • Each element absorbs at a unique, fixed set of wavelengths — its fingerprint.
  • Match the dark-line wavelengths to laboratory reference spectra of known elements.
  • Identifies which elements are present; line strengths give relative abundances.
  • Result: stars are mostly hydrogen and helium (helium found in the Sun first —
    Lockyer 1868 — before Ramsay isolated it on Earth, 1895; Payne-Gaposchkin, 1920s).

KEEP SEPARATE (the marks are here):
  Temperature ← Peak of the continuous spectrum (Wien)
  Composition ← Pattern of the absorption lines (element matching)
```

### Listing 4 — Model answer: velocity from the Doppler effect
```text
Q: "Explain how the Doppler effect allows astronomers to determine the velocity
    of a star."

• If a star moves relative to us, the Doppler effect shifts the ENTIRE line pattern.
• Moving TOWARD the observer  → wavelengths shortened → lines shift to BLUE = blueshift.
• Moving AWAY from the observer → wavelengths lengthened → lines shift to RED  = redshift.
• The SIZE of the shift gives the RADIAL velocity (component along the line of sight);
  a bigger shift = a faster radial speed.
• The Doppler shift gives ONLY the radial component. The TANGENTIAL component (motion
  across the sky) must be found another way (proper motion).
• TRUE translational velocity = vector sum of radial + tangential velocities.

Hook: "approaching is bluer." (toward = blue = shorter λ ; away = red = longer λ)
```

### Listing 5 — Names and dates worth quoting for a mark
| Name | Date | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| William Wollaston | 1802 | First noticed dark lines in the solar spectrum |
| Joseph von Fraunhofer | 1814 | Mapped 576 dark solar lines — the Fraunhofer lines |
| Kirchhoff & Bunsen | 1859 | Three rules of spectra; stellar spectrum = absorption on a continuum |
| Norman Lockyer | 1868 | Predicted helium from unmatched solar lines (named after *helios*, the Sun) |
| William Ramsay | 1895 | Isolated helium on Earth, confirming Lockyer |
| Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin | 1920s | Showed stars are mostly hydrogen and helium from spectral analysis |
