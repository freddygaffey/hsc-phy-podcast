# Teaching Lesson Plan — Module 1: Kinematics
## Theory-First, Exam-Ready (Year 11)

**What these episodes are:** Standard teaching episodes that build the *language and method* of
describing motion — the difference between scalars and vectors, how to read motion graphs, when to
reach for each equation of motion, and how to handle motion and relative velocity in two dimensions.

**What they are NOT:** Drill sheets. The algebra and worked numbers live in the supplementary; the
spoken lesson is about *which tool, why, and the exam phrasing* that earns marks.

**The problem this fixes:** Year 11 students can often plug into `v = u + at` but lose marks for
calling displacement "distance", quoting a velocity with no direction, misreading a velocity–time
graph (gradient vs area), or botching relative velocity by adding when they should subtract. These
episodes drill the distinctions the markers reward.

**Target listener:** Year 11 Physics student, comfortable with the maths, weak on the syllabus
wording, the scalar/vector discipline, graph interpretation, and 2D vector method.

## Episode Sequence

### M1-01 — Motion in a Straight Line: Distance, Displacement, Velocity, Acceleration
**Home topic:** The core quantities of rectilinear motion and the scalar-vs-vector discipline that
underpins all of kinematics.

**Theory focus:**
- Scalar vs vector: distance (scalar, total path) vs displacement (vector, straight-line change in
  position with direction); speed (scalar) vs velocity (vector). The classic trap: a runner doing a
  full lap has travelled a distance but has zero displacement.
- Average vs instantaneous: average velocity = displacement / time; instantaneous velocity = the
  velocity at one instant (the speedometer reading). Average speed = distance / time, and is *not*
  the magnitude of average velocity in general.
- Acceleration as the rate of change of velocity (a vector); a negative acceleration is not always
  "slowing down" — it depends on the direction of motion (deceleration vs speeding up in the
  negative direction). Units: metres per second per second.
- Sign conventions: choose a positive direction and stick to it; this is what makes 1D vectors
  tractable.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Distinguish between distance and displacement, using an example." Strong answer names distance as
  the scalar total path length, displacement as the vector from start to finish with direction, and
  gives the lap/return example where distance ≠ 0 but displacement = 0.
- Weak answer: "distance and displacement are how far you go." Strong answer: names scalar/vector and
  the direction, with a worked example.
- Common trap: dropping the direction off a vector answer, or equating average speed with the size of
  average velocity.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** scalar-vs-vector pairs — a stable hook for which of
{distance, speed} are scalars and which of {displacement, velocity, acceleration} are vectors.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "describe uniform straight-line (rectilinear) motion and uniformly
accelerated motion through qualitative descriptions and the use of scalar and vector quantities
(ACSPH060)"; analyse instantaneous and average velocity (ACSPH061).

**Cross-links:** M1-02 (these quantities become graph gradients); M1-03 (they are the suvat symbols);
M2 Dynamics (velocity/acceleration feed Newton's second law); forward to M5 projectile/circular motion.

### M1-02 — Velocity-Time and Displacement-Time Graphs
**Home topic:** Reading motion from graphs — gradients and areas — the most examined kinematics skill.

**Theory focus:**
- Displacement–time graph: the *gradient* is the velocity; a curve means changing velocity; a
  horizontal line means at rest.
- Velocity–time graph: the *gradient* is the acceleration; the *area under the line* is the
  displacement. This gradient-vs-area distinction is the headline trap.
- Reading the shape: straight sloping line = constant acceleration; horizontal line = constant
  velocity; line crossing the time axis = reversing direction.
- Tangents for instantaneous velocity on a curved displacement–time graph; counting area (triangles +
  rectangles) for displacement on a v–t graph.

**Extended response prep:**
- "From the velocity–time graph, determine the acceleration and the total displacement." Strong answer
  reads gradient for acceleration and area for displacement, with units.
- Weak answer: reads the value off the axis instead of the gradient/area. Strong answer states
  "gradient = acceleration, area = displacement" explicitly.
- Common trap: confusing displacement–time gradient (velocity) with velocity–time gradient
  (acceleration); forgetting that area below the time axis is negative displacement.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** which graph gives what by gradient and by area —
a stable hook (e.g. "slope of d–t is v; slope of v–t is a; area of v–t is s").

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "the graphical representation and interpretation of data (ACSPH061)";
use graphs to analyse relationships between time, distance, displacement, speed, velocity, acceleration.

**Cross-links:** M1-01 (the quantities being plotted); M1-03 (the v–t area derivation underpins the
equations of motion); M2-03 (work = area under a force–displacement graph — same "area" idea).

### M1-03 — Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion (SUVAT)
**Home topic:** The equations of motion for constant acceleration — which to use, why, and the method.

**Theory focus:**
- The five SUVAT variables: s (displacement), u (initial velocity), v (final velocity), a
  (acceleration), t (time). Every constant-acceleration problem is "I know three, I want a fourth."
- The equations and the variable each omits: v = u + at (no s); s = ut + ½at² (no v);
  v² = u² + 2as (no t); s = ½(u + v)t (no a). The method: list s, u, v, a, t; mark the one you don't
  have and don't need; pick the equation that omits it.
- Only valid for *constant* acceleration. Sign discipline from M1-01 carries straight in (e.g. taking
  up as positive, g as negative).
- Vertical motion under gravity as the headline application: acceleration = g ≈ nine point eight
  metres per second per second, downward.

**Extended response prep:**
- "A ball is thrown upward at … ; calculate the maximum height / time of flight." Strong answer lists
  the knowns with correct signs, selects the equation by the missing variable, and quotes units.
- Weak answer: grabs an equation at random or mismanages the sign of g. Strong answer shows the
  variable list and the selection logic.
- Common trap: using a suvat equation when acceleration isn't constant; sign errors on g.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** SUVAT itself (the five variables) plus the
"identify the missing variable to choose the equation" technique.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "use mathematical modelling and graphs to derive relationships …
including s = ut + ½at², v = u + at, v² = u² + 2as (ACSPH061)."

**Cross-links:** M1-02 (the equations come from the v–t graph area and gradient); M5 projectile motion
(suvat applied to the vertical component — a direct forward link); M6-01 (charged particle in a field
uses the same uniformly-accelerated equations).

### M1-04 — Vectors and Motion in a Plane
**Home topic:** Vector addition and resolution, and relative velocity in two dimensions.

**Theory focus:**
- Resolving a vector into two perpendicular components (horizontal and vertical) and recombining
  components into a single resultant (magnitude by Pythagoras, direction by trigonometry).
- Adding vectors head-to-tail; the resultant displacement of a multi-leg journey.
- Relative velocity in 2D: the velocity of A relative to B is the vector difference v(A) − v(B). The
  syllabus scenarios: a boat crossing a flowing river, two moving cars, an aeroplane in a crosswind.
- The exam discipline: draw the vector diagram, resolve, then compute — never just add magnitudes.

**Extended response prep:**
- "A boat heads straight across a river that is flowing; find its resultant velocity and the angle of
  travel." Strong answer draws the vector triangle, adds the boat and current velocities as vectors,
  and gives magnitude and direction.
- Weak answer: adds or subtracts the speeds as scalars. Strong answer uses vector addition with a
  diagram and resolves components.
- Common trap: treating relative velocity as a scalar; adding when the situation requires subtraction
  (or vice versa); forgetting the direction in the final answer.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** relative-velocity rule — a stable hook for "A
relative to B = v(A) − v(B)" and the resolve-then-recombine method.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "resolve a vector into two perpendicular components; add two
perpendicular vector components (ACSPH061)"; relative positions and motions in two dimensions
(boat on a river, two cars, aeroplane in a crosswind) (ACSPH060, ACSPH132).

**Cross-links:** M1-01 (vectors, now in 2D); M5 projectile motion (independence of horizontal and
vertical components is the direct payoff); M2 (resolving forces on an inclined plane uses the same
component method).

### M1-99 — Module Review: Kinematics
**Home topic:** Tie the module together — the scalar/vector discipline, graph reading, the SUVAT
method, and 2D vectors — through integrated, multi-part exam questions (STYLE §5.3).

**Theory focus (interleaving at full scale):** the through-line is *describe motion precisely, then
predict it*. Re-surface every distinction (distance/displacement, speed/velocity, average/instantaneous,
gradient/area, the missing-variable method, relative velocity = vector difference) and every mnemonic,
and flag the cross-module bridges to Dynamics (M2) and Advanced Mechanics (M5: projectiles, circular).

**Extended response prep:** lean on integrated questions that combine a graph, a SUVAT calculation, and
a vector/relative-velocity part; show a weak-vs-strong answer for the headline "interpret this v–t graph"
question; pose a "pause the player" retrieval of all four SUVAT equations and which variable each omits.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** integrative review of all M1 dot-points (no new dot-point;
consolidation per STYLE §5.3).

**Cross-links:** every M1 episode; forward to M2 (forces cause the acceleration), M5 (projectile and
circular motion), and M6 (charged particles in fields).
