# Teaching Lesson Plan — Module 4: Electricity and Magnetism
## Theory-First, Exam-Ready (Year 11)

**What these episodes are:** Standard teaching episodes covering charge and electric fields, the energy
story of electric circuits, series and parallel networks, and magnetism — the syllabus language,
field-line reasoning, and circuit method that earn marks, and the direct foundation for Module 6.

**What they are NOT:** Circuit-solving drills. The algebra and worked numbers live in the supplementary;
the spoken lesson builds the *field* and *conservation* reasoning behind the equations.

**The problem this fixes:** students confuse charge with current, voltage with current, and field with
force; they mis-draw field lines, mix up series and parallel rules, and treat Ohm's law as universal
when many components are non-ohmic. These episodes drill the distinctions and the field/conservation logic.

**Target listener:** Year 11 student comfortable with the maths and with M1/M2, weak on field-line
representation, the precise circuit quantities, and the right-hand rules for magnetism.

## Episode Sequence

### M4-01 — Electrostatics: Charge, Coulomb's Law and Electric Fields
**Home topic:** Electric charge, the force between charges, and the electric field that mediates it.

**Theory focus:**
- Charge: two kinds (positive, negative); like charges repel, unlike attract; charge is conserved and
  quantised. How objects become charged — friction, conduction, induction.
- Coulomb's law: the electrostatic force between two point charges is proportional to the product of the
  charges and inversely proportional to the square of their separation (an inverse-square law, like
  gravity and light intensity). The variables that change the force: charge magnitudes and distance.
- The electric field: the region where a charge experiences a force; field strength E = force per unit
  charge, E = F/q (newtons per coulomb). The field is a vector, pointing the way a positive test charge
  would be pushed — away from positive, toward negative.
- Field-line representation (a named dot-point): lines from positive to negative; density shows strength;
  the patterns for a point charge, a pair of charges, a dipole, and parallel charged plates. Between
  parallel plates the field is uniform, E = V/d (voltage over plate separation) — the direct setup for
  M6's charged particle in a field.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Describe the electric field around a single positive point charge and between two parallel plates."
  Strong answer: radial lines pointing outward (point charge); uniform, evenly spaced parallel lines
  from positive to negative plate (parallel plates).
- "Distinguish between electric field strength and electric force." Strong answer: field is force per
  unit charge (a property of the location, E = F/q); force is what a particular charge experiences in it.
- Weak answer: "the field is where the charge pushes things." Strong answer names E = F/q and the
  direction convention.
- Common trap: drawing field lines that cross or that point the wrong way; confusing E (field) with F
  (force); dropping the inverse-square in Coulomb's law.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** like-repel/unlike-attract; field-line rules (out of +,
into −, never cross); E = F/q and uniform-field E = V/d.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** charging processes (ACSPH002); electrostatic forces and the variables
that affect them (ACSPH103); field-line models for point charges, pairs, dipoles, parallel plates;
E = F/q, E = V/d, Coulomb's law (ACSPH103, ACSPH104).

**Cross-links:** M2-01 (force as a vector; field-mediated forces); M3-04 (inverse-square law — same form
as light intensity); M6-01 (charged particle accelerated through E = V/d — the direct continuation);
M7/M8 (fields and charges throughout).

### M4-02 — Electric Circuits: Current, Voltage, Resistance and Power
**Home topic:** The quantities of a circuit and the energy conversions in it — Ohm's law and electrical power.

**Theory focus:**
- Current: the rate of flow of charge, I = q/t (amperes); conventional current flows from positive to
  negative (opposite to electron flow in a metal). Charge is conserved.
- Voltage (potential difference): the energy transferred per unit charge, V = W/q (volts); EMF as the
  energy supplied per unit charge by a source. The crucial distinction students blur: current is *flow*,
  voltage is *energy per charge* — they are not the same thing.
- Resistance and Ohm's law: R = V/I (ohms). Ohmic conductors have constant resistance (a straight
  current–voltage line); non-ohmic components (filament lamp, diode) do not — the I–V graph curves. Know
  the limitation: Ohm's law holds only for ohmic conductors at constant temperature.
- Electrical power and energy: the rate of energy conversion P = VI (and, via Ohm's law, P = I²R = V²/R),
  energy W = Pt; the production of heat and light in components.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Distinguish between current and voltage." Strong answer: current is the rate of flow of charge
  (I = q/t); voltage is the energy transferred per unit charge (V = W/q).
- "Explain, using an I–V graph, why a filament lamp is non-ohmic." Strong answer: as current rises the
  filament heats, its resistance increases, so the graph curves — resistance is not constant, so Ohm's
  law does not apply.
- Weak answer: "voltage pushes the current." Strong answer names V = W/q and I = q/t precisely.
- Common trap: confusing current and voltage; assuming every component is ohmic; power-formula slips.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** current = charge-flow (I = q/t) vs voltage =
energy-per-charge (V = W/q); the Ohm's-law trio R = V/I; ohmic vs non-ohmic.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** current I = q/t (ACSPH038); V = W/q; ohmic/non-ohmic and Ohm's law
R = V/I (ACSPH003, ACSPH041, ACSPH043); power P = VI, W = Pt and variations with Ohm's law (ACSPH042).

**Cross-links:** M2-03 (energy and power — same P = energy/time idea); M4-03 (these quantities in series
and parallel networks); M6 (induced EMF and electrical power in generators/transformers).

### M4-03 — Series and Parallel Circuits (Kirchhoff's Laws)
**Home topic:** How current, voltage and resistance behave in series and parallel networks, from the
conservation of charge and energy.

**Theory focus:**
- Kirchhoff's current law: the sum of currents into a junction equals the sum out, Σ I = 0 — this is
  conservation of charge. Kirchhoff's voltage law: around any loop the voltages sum to zero, Σ V = 0 —
  this is conservation of energy. The two laws are the foundation, and naming the conservation principle
  is the mark-earning move.
- Series circuits: one path, so the current is the same everywhere; the voltage divides across the
  components; total resistance adds, R-series = R1 + R2 + … . A break anywhere stops everything.
- Parallel circuits: multiple paths, so the voltage across each branch is the same; the current divides
  between branches; the total resistance is found from the reciprocal sum (one over R-total equals the
  sum of one over each R), so the total is *less* than the smallest branch resistance.
- Solving networks: reduce to an equivalent resistance, then apply Ohm's law; track current and voltage
  through each component.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Compare how current and voltage behave in series versus parallel circuits." Strong answer: series —
  current the same throughout, voltage divides, resistances add; parallel — voltage the same across
  branches, current divides, total resistance from the reciprocal sum.
- "Explain, using conservation laws, Kirchhoff's two rules." Strong answer ties Σ I = 0 to conservation
  of charge and Σ V = 0 to conservation of energy.
- Weak answer: "in series it adds up." Strong answer states which quantity is constant and which divides,
  for each configuration.
- Common trap: swapping the series and parallel rules; thinking parallel resistance is larger; forgetting
  total parallel resistance is below the smallest branch.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** series-vs-parallel rules — a stable hook (e.g. "series:
same current, voltage shares, R adds; parallel: same voltage, current shares, R drops"); Kirchhoff =
charge (current law) and energy (voltage law).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** series and parallel circuits relating current, potential difference and
energy conversion to conservation of charge and energy; Σ I = 0 (Kirchhoff current law), Σ V = 0
(Kirchhoff voltage law), R-series = R1 + R2 + …, parallel reciprocal sum (ACSPH038, ACSPH039, ACSPH044).

**Cross-links:** M4-02 (the circuit quantities and Ohm's law applied here); M2-03 (conservation of
energy — the voltage law); M6 (circuit analysis of generators, transformers, induced currents).

### M4-04 — Magnetism and Magnetic Fields
**Home topic:** Magnetic poles and materials, and the magnetic fields produced by magnets, wires and
solenoids.

**Theory focus:**
- Magnetic poles: north and south; like poles repel, unlike attract; poles always come in pairs (no
  magnetic monopole). Ferromagnetic materials (iron, nickel, cobalt) and how they become magnetised
  (alignment of domains).
- Magnetic field lines: from north to south outside a magnet; density shows strength; the patterns for a
  bar magnet, a current-carrying wire (concentric circles, direction by the right-hand grip rule), and a
  solenoid (like a bar magnet, with a defined north end).
- Quantitative fields: the field around a long straight wire, B = mu-nought I over two pi r (proportional
  to current, inversely proportional to distance); the field inside a solenoid, B = mu-nought N I over L
  (proportional to current and turns per length). mu-nought is the permeability of free space.
- The right-hand rules: grip rule for the field around a wire; the field direction for a solenoid. Getting
  the direction right is a recurring exam mark and is essential for Module 6.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Describe the magnetic field around a long straight current-carrying wire." Strong answer: concentric
  circles centred on the wire, direction given by the right-hand grip rule, strength falling with distance
  (B ∝ 1/r).
- "Explain how a ferromagnetic material becomes magnetised." Strong answer: its magnetic domains align
  with an applied field, so their fields add to give a net magnetisation.
- Weak answer: "the wire makes a magnetic field." Strong answer names the circular pattern, the grip rule,
  and the 1/r dependence.
- Common trap: getting the field direction wrong (right-hand rule); confusing the wire field (circles)
  with the solenoid field (bar-magnet-like); forgetting B depends on current and distance/turns.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** like-poles-repel; the right-hand grip rule for a wire;
solenoid field direction; B = μ₀I/2πr (wire) vs B = μ₀NI/L (solenoid).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** forces between magnetised/magnetic materials, ferromagnetism (ACSPH079);
magnetic field lines for magnets, wires and solenoids (ACSPH083); B = μ₀I/2πr and B = μ₀NI/L (ACSPH106,
ACSPH107); magnetisation of ferromagnetic materials (ACSPH083).

**Cross-links:** M4-01 (field-line reasoning, now magnetic); M6-02/05 (the motor effect — a current in a
magnetic field feels a force — is the direct continuation; the solenoid field underpins motors,
generators and transformers); M7-01 (changing electric and magnetic fields make light).

### M4-99 — Module Review: Electricity and Magnetism
**Home topic:** Tie the module together — charge and electric fields, the circuit energy story, series
and parallel networks, and magnetism — through integrated, multi-part exam questions (STYLE §5.3), and
explicitly set up Module 6.

**Theory focus (interleaving at full scale):** the through-line is *fields mediate forces, and circuits
move energy under conservation laws*. Re-surface every distinction (charge vs current vs voltage, field
vs force, ohmic vs non-ohmic, series vs parallel, wire field vs solenoid field) and every mnemonic and
right-hand rule; flag hard that all of this is the foundation of Module 6 (charged particles in fields,
the motor effect, electromagnetic induction).

**Extended response prep:** lean on integrated questions — e.g. a field/Coulomb problem, a mixed
series–parallel network with power, a magnetic-field-direction question; show a weak-vs-strong answer for
"compare series and parallel circuits"; pose a "pause the player" retrieval of Kirchhoff's two laws and
the right-hand grip rule.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** integrative review of all M4 dot-points (consolidation per STYLE §5.3).

**Cross-links:** every M4 episode; forward, emphatically, to M6 Electromagnetism (the whole of Year 12's
fields-and-motion module builds on this); back to M2 (force, energy).
