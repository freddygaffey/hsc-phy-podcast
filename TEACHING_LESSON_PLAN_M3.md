# Teaching Lesson Plan — Module 3: Waves and Thermodynamics
## Theory-First, Exam-Ready (Year 11)

**What these episodes are:** Standard teaching episodes covering the physics of waves (mechanical and
the ray model of light) and of heat — the properties common to all waves, how waves behave, sound,
light by rays, and thermal energy — with the syllabus language and worked method that earn marks.

**What they are NOT:** Formula drills. The equations and worked numbers live in the supplementary; the
spoken lesson builds the *mechanism* (why a wave diffracts, why a standing wave forms, why heat flows)
and the exam phrasing.

**The problem this fixes:** students confuse transverse with longitudinal, frequency with wavelength,
progressive with standing waves; they misuse Snell's law or forget total internal reflection's
condition; and in thermodynamics they conflate heat with temperature, or say a hot object "has heat".
These episodes drill the precise distinctions.

**Target listener:** Year 11 student comfortable with the maths and with M1/M2, weak on wave vocabulary,
graph interpretation, the ray-model relationships, and thermal language.

## Episode Sequence

### M3-01 — Wave Properties and the Wave Equation
**Home topic:** The quantities common to all waves and the relationship v = f λ.

**Theory focus:**
- A wave transfers energy (and information) through a medium (or, for EM waves, through space) WITHOUT
  transferring matter — the particles oscillate about fixed points. This "energy not matter" point is
  the headline.
- Transverse (oscillation perpendicular to propagation — e.g. waves on a string, all EM waves) vs
  longitudinal (oscillation parallel to propagation — e.g. sound, with compressions and rarefactions).
  Mechanical (need a medium) vs electromagnetic (no medium — preview of M7).
- The wave quantities: wavelength (distance for one full cycle), frequency (cycles per second, hertz),
  period (time for one cycle, T = 1/f), amplitude (maximum displacement → energy), velocity, and wave
  number. Reading displacement–time graphs (gives period/frequency) vs displacement–position graphs
  (gives wavelength) — a guaranteed exam distinction.
- The wave equation: v = f λ (speed = frequency × wavelength), and f = 1/T. Modelling and problem-solving.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Distinguish between transverse and longitudinal waves, with an example of each." Strong answer:
  transverse oscillates perpendicular to propagation (string, light); longitudinal oscillates parallel
  (sound, with compressions and rarefactions).
- "A displacement–time graph and a displacement–position graph are given; find the wave speed." Strong
  answer reads period (or frequency) from the time graph and wavelength from the position graph, then
  v = f λ.
- Weak answer: "a wave carries energy along." Strong answer names "energy not matter" and the medium.
- Common trap: reading wavelength off a time graph (it gives period, not wavelength) and vice versa;
  confusing amplitude with wavelength.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** transverse-vs-longitudinal (perpendicular vs parallel);
which graph gives period vs wavelength; the wave-quantity set with units.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** role of the medium and energy transfer (ACSPH067, ACSPH070); transverse
vs longitudinal (ACSPH068), mechanical vs electromagnetic (ACSPH070, ACSPH074); displacement graphs and
velocity/frequency/period/wavelength/wave number/amplitude (ACSPH069); v = f λ, f = 1/T.

**Cross-links:** M3-02/03 (these properties underlie all wave behaviour and sound); M7 (the wave model
of light, the EM spectrum, c = f λ is the same equation — explicit forward link); M2-03 (energy).

### M3-02 — Wave Behaviour: Reflection, Refraction, Diffraction and Superposition
**Home topic:** How waves behave when they meet boundaries and each other — and standing waves and resonance.

**Theory focus:**
- Reflection (bouncing off a boundary, angle in = angle out); refraction (changing speed, and so
  direction, on entering a new medium); diffraction (spreading around edges/through gaps, most marked
  when the gap is comparable to the wavelength — preview of M7's slit work).
- Superposition: when two waves overlap, the resultant displacement is the sum of the individual
  displacements; constructive (in phase, reinforce) and destructive (out of phase, cancel) interference.
- Progressive vs standing waves: a standing wave forms when two identical waves travel in opposite
  directions and superpose, producing fixed nodes (no displacement) and antinodes (maximum) — it stores
  energy rather than transporting it.
- Resonance: every system has a natural frequency; driving it at that frequency transfers energy
  efficiently and the amplitude grows large. The relationships between driving frequency, natural
  frequency, and amplitude. (The Tacoma-Narrows-style consequences belong to later case-study tie-ins.)

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain, in terms of superposition, how a standing wave forms." Strong answer: two identical waves
  travelling in opposite directions superpose; where they are always out of phase, nodes form; where
  always in phase, antinodes form.
- "Explain why diffraction is more pronounced for a narrow gap." Strong answer: diffraction is greatest
  when the gap is comparable to the wavelength.
- Weak answer: "the waves add up." Strong answer states the in-phase/out-of-phase condition and
  node/antinode result.
- Common trap: confusing a standing wave (energy stored, fixed nodes) with a progressive wave (energy
  transported); forgetting diffraction depends on gap-to-wavelength ratio.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** the four behaviours (reflection, refraction,
diffraction, superposition) — a stable hook; nodes-vs-antinodes; constructive vs destructive (in/out of phase).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** reflection, refraction, diffraction, wave superposition (ACSPH071,
ACSPH072); progressive vs standing waves (ACSPH072); resonance — driving/natural frequency, amplitude,
energy transfer (ACSPH073).

**Cross-links:** M3-01 (wave properties); M3-03 (standing waves on strings/in pipes; resonance in sound);
M7-05/06 (diffraction and interference of light — the same superposition physics, examined harder).

### M3-03 — Sound Waves, Resonance and Standing Waves
**Home topic:** Sound as a longitudinal mechanical wave — pitch and loudness, standing waves in
strings and pipes, beats, and the Doppler effect.

**Theory focus:**
- Sound is a longitudinal wave of pressure variations (compressions and rarefactions) in a medium;
  it needs a medium (no sound in vacuum). Pitch ↔ frequency; loudness ↔ amplitude/intensity. Intensity
  falls with distance (inverse-square behaviour).
- Standing waves on strings and in pipes: the fundamental (lowest) frequency and its harmonics, set by
  the length and the boundary conditions (fixed/open/closed ends), the wave speed, tension, and mass —
  this is the physics of musical instruments.
- Beats: when two notes of slightly different frequency superpose, the loudness pulses at the beat
  frequency, f-beat = the magnitude of the difference of the two frequencies.
- The Doppler effect: relative motion between source and observer shifts the observed frequency — higher
  as they approach, lower as they recede (the passing-siren example). Qualitative cause and the
  quantitative relationship.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain how a standing wave on a guitar string produces a note of definite pitch." Strong answer:
  reflections at the fixed ends superpose to form a standing wave whose fundamental frequency is set by
  the string length, tension and mass.
- "Explain, using the Doppler effect, why a siren's pitch drops as it passes." Strong answer: approaching,
  the waves are compressed → higher observed frequency; receding, stretched → lower frequency.
- Weak answer: "the sound changes because it moves." Strong answer names the compression/stretching of
  wavefronts and the frequency shift.
- Common trap: saying the source frequency changes (it doesn't — the *observed* frequency does);
  confusing beats (two close frequencies) with the Doppler effect (relative motion).

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** pitch↔frequency / loudness↔amplitude; Doppler direction
(approach = higher, recede = lower); beats f-beat = |f2 − f1|.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** sound as a longitudinal wave; displacement↔pressure (ACSPH070);
intensity vs distance; reflection/diffraction/resonance/superposition of sound (ACSPH071); standing
waves on strings/pipes — fundamental and harmonics (ACSPH072); beats (f-beat = |f2 − f1|); Doppler effect.

**Cross-links:** M3-01/02 (longitudinal waves, superposition, resonance); M7-04 (the Doppler effect of
*light* gives stellar radial velocity — the same idea, redshift/blueshift); M3-04 (waves vs rays for light).

### M3-04 — The Ray Model of Light: Reflection, Refraction and Total Internal Reflection
**Home topic:** Treating light as rays — reflection, refraction via Snell's law, total internal
reflection, dispersion, and the inverse-square law for intensity.

**Theory focus:**
- The ray model: light travels in straight lines (rays); reflection (angle of incidence = angle of
  reflection) and image formation in mirrors and lenses.
- Refraction: light changes speed when it enters a new medium, bending toward the normal entering a
  denser (higher-index) medium and away when leaving. Refractive index n = c / v (speed of light in
  vacuum over speed in the medium). Snell's law: n1 sine i = n2 sine r.
- Total internal reflection: when light travels from a denser to a less dense medium beyond the critical
  angle, it reflects entirely; the critical angle satisfies sine of the critical angle = 1 / n. The
  basis of optical fibres.
- Dispersion: different wavelengths refract by different amounts, splitting white light into colours
  (the rainbow / prism) — bridges to the spectrum in M7.
- Intensity and the inverse-square law: intensity falls as one over distance squared; I1 times r1
  squared equals I2 times r2 squared.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Use Snell's law to predict whether light entering a medium bends toward or away from the normal."
  Strong answer: entering a higher-index (denser) medium, light slows and bends toward the normal.
- "Explain the condition for total internal reflection." Strong answer: light must travel from a denser
  to a less dense medium and strike the boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle, sine ic = 1/n.
- Weak answer: "light bends in glass." Strong answer names the index, the direction of bending, and Snell's law.
- Common trap: forgetting TIR requires dense-to-less-dense AND angle > critical; mixing i and r in
  Snell's law; dropping the squares in the inverse-square law.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** "denser → bend toward the normal"; the TIR conditions;
the inverse-square relationship.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** images in mirrors/lenses (ACSPH075); refraction and total internal
reflection (ACSPH075, ACSPH076); Snell's law n1 sin i = n2 sin r; n = c/v; sin ic = 1/n; dispersion;
inverse square law I1 r1² = I2 r2² (ACSPH077).

**Cross-links:** M3-02 (refraction and reflection as wave behaviours — ray vs wave model); M7 (the wave
model of light, dispersion into the spectrum, and the eventual wave-vs-particle story); M3-03 (intensity
vs distance — same inverse-square idea as sound).

### M3-05 — Thermodynamics: Temperature, Thermal Equilibrium and Specific Heat
**Home topic:** What temperature really is, thermal equilibrium, and the specific-heat relationship.

**Theory focus:**
- Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance — NOT the same
  as heat. Heat (thermal energy) is energy transferred because of a temperature difference. The crucial
  distinction: an object doesn't "contain heat"; it contains thermal/internal energy, and heat is energy
  *in transit*. (This sets up M7-10's black-body "distribution of particle kinetic energies".)
- Thermal equilibrium: two objects in contact reach the same temperature when there is no net transfer
  of thermal energy between them; heat flows from hot to cold until equilibrium.
- Specific heat capacity: the energy needed to raise one kilogram by one kelvin; ΔQ = m c ΔT. Why water's
  high specific heat matters (coastal climates, cooling systems). Worked problems are standard.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Distinguish between heat and temperature." Strong answer: temperature is the average kinetic energy of
  the particles; heat is the energy transferred due to a temperature difference (energy in transit).
- "Calculate the energy to heat a mass of water through a temperature rise." Strong answer applies
  ΔQ = m c ΔT with the correct specific heat and units.
- Weak answer: "heat and temperature are how hot something is." Strong answer separates average particle
  KE (temperature) from transferred energy (heat).
- Common trap: using heat and temperature interchangeably; forgetting ΔT is a change, not an absolute.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** heat-vs-temperature (transit vs average-KE); ΔQ = mcΔT.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** temperature ↔ particle kinetic energy (ACSPH018); thermal equilibrium
(ACSPH022); specific heat capacity ΔQ = mcΔT (ACSPH020).

**Cross-links:** M2-03 (energy and its conservation/transformation); M3-06 (heat transfer and latent
heat continue the thermal story); M7-10 (temperature as the distribution of particle kinetic energies —
the direct foundation of black-body radiation and Wien's law).

### M3-06 — Heat Transfer and Latent Heat
**Home topic:** How thermal energy moves — conduction, convection, radiation — plus latent heat of a
change of state and thermal conductivity.

**Theory focus:**
- The three modes of heat transfer: conduction (energy passed particle-to-particle, mainly in solids);
  convection (bulk movement of a heated fluid, hot rises / cool sinks); radiation (energy carried by
  electromagnetic waves, needs no medium — links to M7). Each with everyday examples.
- Thermal conductivity: the rate of conduction through a material, Q over t = k A ΔT over d (conductivity
  times area times temperature difference, divided by thickness). Why metals conduct well and insulators don't.
- Latent heat: the energy absorbed or released during a change of state (melting/freezing,
  boiling/condensing) at constant temperature — the energy goes into breaking/forming bonds, not raising
  temperature. This is why a temperature-vs-time heating curve has flat plateaus at the phase changes.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain why the temperature stays constant while ice melts, even though heat is added." Strong answer:
  the added energy is the latent heat, used to break the bonds of the solid structure rather than to
  increase particle kinetic energy, so the temperature is constant during the change of state.
- "Compare conduction, convection and radiation." Strong answer gives the mechanism of each and whether
  a medium is required (radiation needs none).
- Weak answer: "heat moves three ways." Strong answer names the mechanism and medium for each.
- Common trap: thinking temperature rises during a phase change; confusing convection (fluids) with
  conduction (solids).

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** the three transfer modes (conduction / convection /
radiation) with their mechanisms — a stable hook; latent-heat "energy goes to bonds, not temperature".

**Syllabus dot-points served:** energy transfer by conduction, convection, radiation (ACSPH016); latent
heat in a change of state; thermal conductivity Q/t = kAΔT/d.

**Cross-links:** M3-05 (specific heat and temperature); M7-01/M7-10 (radiation is electromagnetic — the
bridge to the nature of light and black-body emission); M2-03 (energy conservation across transfers).

### M3-99 — Module Review: Waves and Thermodynamics
**Home topic:** Tie the module together — the common wave properties, wave behaviour, sound, the ray
model, and the thermal story — through integrated, multi-part exam questions (STYLE §5.3).

**Theory focus (interleaving at full scale):** two threads — *all waves share properties and behaviours,
whether sound, light or on a string* and *thermal energy is particle motion that flows from hot to cold*.
Re-surface every distinction (transverse/longitudinal, progressive/standing, reflection/refraction/
diffraction, Snell's law and TIR conditions, heat vs temperature, the latent-heat plateau) and every
mnemonic; flag the strong bridges forward to M7 (wave model of light, the EM spectrum, dispersion,
black-body radiation) and to M8.

**Extended response prep:** lean on integrated questions — e.g. a wave-speed problem combining graphs and
v = f λ, a Snell's-law/TIR scenario, a calorimetry question (ΔQ = mcΔT then a phase change); show a
weak-vs-strong answer for "explain how a standing wave forms"; pose a "pause the player" retrieval of the
four wave behaviours and the three heat-transfer modes.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** integrative review of all M3 dot-points (consolidation per STYLE §5.3).

**Cross-links:** every M3 episode; forward to M7 (nature of light) and M8; back to M2 (energy).
