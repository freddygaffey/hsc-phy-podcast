# Teaching Lesson Plan — Module 6: Electromagnetism
## Theory-First, Extended-Response Focused

**What these episodes are:** Standard teaching episodes (not case studies). Each one teaches the conceptual *why* behind a topic — the mechanism, the cause-and-effect chain, the syllabus language — so students can write strong extended responses, not just calculate.

**What they are NOT:** Equation-manipulation walkthroughs. The maths is in the supplementary. The spoken lesson is all about understanding why the physics works the way it does.

**The extended response problem this fixes:** HSC markers report that most students can calculate the right number but cannot explain the mechanism. "The force is 2.4 N" gets no marks. "A force acts because the external magnetic field interacts with the magnetic field produced by the current-carrying conductor, resulting in a net force in the region of stronger combined field" gets marks. These episodes are entirely about building that explanatory vocabulary.

**Target listener:** HSC Year 12 Physics student. Knows the basics. Weak on the exact syllabus wording, the direction of cause and effect, and how to structure a multi-sentence extended response.

## Episode Sequence

### M6-01 — Charged Particles in Electric Fields: The Conceptual Picture
**Home topic:** Why a charged particle in a uniform electric field follows a parabolic path, and how to describe its motion and energy changes in extended-response language.

**Theory focus:**
- The electric field as a region where a force acts on a charge — the field exists independently of whether a charge is there
- Why the force is constant (uniform field = constant E = constant F = constant acceleration)
- The horizontal/vertical independence: exactly like projectile motion, but the "gravity" is electrical and can point any direction
- Energy: why work done by the field equals the gain in kinetic energy, and what it means to say the particle "falls through a potential difference"
- The critical distinction: electric field gives constant force regardless of velocity; magnetic field gives a force that depends on velocity

**Extended response prep:**
- Weak answer: "The particle accelerates because of the electric field."
- Strong answer: "The uniform electric field exerts a constant electrostatic force on the charge in the direction of the field (for a positive charge), producing a constant acceleration. The component of velocity parallel to the field increases uniformly while the component perpendicular to it remains constant, resulting in a parabolic trajectory — analogous to projectile motion in a gravitational field."
- Common trap: students say "the particle moves toward the positive plate" — wrong for negative charges; always specify the charge sign and field direction

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — Apply conventions to determine direction of electrostatic forces; Analyse motion using kinematic equations; Analyse energy changes using work and electric potential energy

**Cross-links:** M5 projectile motion (same kinematic structure); M6 electron gun (application of this exact scenario); M6 mass spectrometer (next episode)

### M6-02 — Charged Particles in Magnetic Fields: Circular Motion and the Cyclotron
**Home topic:** Why a moving charge in a magnetic field travels in a circle, why the magnetic force does no work, and how the radius of the path depends on mass, charge, speed and field strength.

**Theory focus:**
- Why the force is always perpendicular to velocity: the cross product of velocity and field. You can't memorise this — you have to feel why "always perpendicular" means "always changing direction but never changing speed"
- The consequence: a magnetic force can never do work on a charge. It can only change direction. Kinetic energy is unchanged. This is one of the most important conceptual facts in the module.
- Why "always perpendicular to velocity" produces a circle: the force is centripetal
- The radius equation (r = mv/qB): what each variable does. Heavier particles curve less. Faster particles curve less. Stronger field curves more.
- The cyclotron equation and what it reveals: the period of circular motion in a magnetic field does NOT depend on the speed. Faster particles have bigger circles but complete them in the same time. This is the principle behind the cyclotron accelerator.

**Extended response prep:**
- Weak answer: "The particle moves in a circle because of the magnetic force."
- Strong answer: "The magnetic force on a moving charge acts perpendicular to both the velocity and the field at every instant. Because the force is always perpendicular to the velocity, it does no work — the particle's speed remains constant. However, the direction of velocity continuously changes, causing the particle to follow a circular path. The magnetic force provides the centripetal force required for this circular motion."
- Common trap: students say the magnetic force "accelerates" the particle — it does change direction (which is acceleration) but does not change speed. Markers want this distinction.
- Common trap: confusing the direction rule for positive vs negative charges

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — Account for the force on a charged particle in a magnetic field; Analyse circular motion of charged particles; Derive the cyclotron equation

**Cross-links:** M5 circular motion (centripetal force equation is identical); M6 mass spectrometer; M8 particle accelerators

### M6-03 — The Great Comparison: Fields, Forces and Paths
**Home topic:** The single most-examined extended response topic in this module — comparing the motion of masses in gravitational fields, satellites in orbit, charges in electric fields, and moving charges in magnetic fields.

**Theory focus — build a comparison table in the listener's head:**
- Gravitational field: force proportional to mass, always attractive, constant in uniform field → parabolic path or orbit
- Electric field: force proportional to charge, can attract or repel, constant in uniform field → parabolic path (same maths as gravity)
- Magnetic field: force on moving charges only, always perpendicular to velocity, depends on speed → circular path, no work done
- Satellite orbit: gravity provides centripetal force, circular (approximately) → period depends on altitude
- The key similarities: gravity and electric fields both give constant force in a uniform field, both give parabolic trajectories for a particle projected across the field
- The key differences: magnetic force requires motion, does no work, gives circles; gravity and E fields work on stationary charges/masses, can do work, give parabolas

**Extended response prep:**
- This exact comparison is a common 5–7 mark extended response. Structure it: one paragraph per comparison point, not one paragraph per field type.
- "Both gravitational and electric fields exert a force proportional to a property of the particle (mass and charge respectively) that acts along the field direction, regardless of the particle's velocity. In contrast, the magnetic force acts only on a moving charge and is always perpendicular to the velocity..."
- The strongest answers explicitly use the word "whereas" or "in contrast" — markers reward structured comparison language

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Account for the similarities and differences between the paths of masses in uniform gravitational fields, satellites in orbit, charged particles in electric fields, and moving charged particles in magnetic fields"

**Cross-links:** M5 circular motion and orbital mechanics; M6 all field topics; M8 particle accelerators

### M6-04 — Mass Spectrometers and Electron Guns: Fields Applied
**Home topic:** How electric and magnetic fields are combined in devices, and how to explain their operation from first principles rather than memorising a diagram.

**Theory focus:**
- Electron gun: electric field accelerates electrons (converts electric potential energy to kinetic), gives them a known speed. The maths: qV = ½mv². If you know the voltage, you know the speed.
- Velocity selector (inside a mass spectrometer): electric and magnetic forces balance. Only one speed passes straight through. The rest curve away. This is the elegance of the device — it filters by speed using two opposing forces.
- Magnetic deflection: the selected-speed ions enter a uniform magnetic field and curve in a circle. The radius tells you the mass-to-charge ratio. Different isotopes → different radii → different positions on the detector.
- The point the HSC loves: this is a direct application of M6-01 and M6-02 combined. The electron gun is "E field accelerates charge." The velocity selector is "E and B forces balance." The deflection chamber is "B field gives circular motion." Tell the story in that order.

**Extended response prep:**
- The HSC question pattern: "Explain how a mass spectrometer separates isotopes of different mass." Students must explain three stages in order, using force concepts, not just labelling a diagram.
- Weak answer: "Ions enter and are deflected by a magnetic field."
- Strong answer structured as three stages: acceleration (E field does work), velocity selection (F_E = F_B, only v = E/B passes), deflection (F_B provides centripetal force, r = mv/qB — heavier isotope, larger radius, different position).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Outline the functions and effects of electric fields and magnetic fields in mass spectrometers and electron guns"

**Cross-links:** M6-01, M6-02; M8 cathode ray tube (J.J. Thomson case study — same electron gun principle)

### M6-05 — The Motor Effect: Why a Wire Moves
**Home topic:** The mechanism by which a current-carrying conductor experiences a force in a magnetic field — building from first principles so the student can explain it in their own words.

**Theory focus:**
- First: the current is moving charges. Moving charges in a magnetic field experience a force (M6-02). The force on the wire is just the sum of all the forces on all the moving charges inside it.
- The two-field picture: the external magnet creates a field. The current in the wire creates its own field (concentric circles around the wire). Where these two fields add together, the field is stronger. Where they oppose, the field is weaker. The wire is pushed from the region of stronger combined field toward the region of weaker combined field. This is the mechanism the HSC expects you to describe.
- The factors that change the force magnitude: current (more charges moving), length (more charges), field strength (stronger force per charge), and the angle between wire and field (maximum when perpendicular, zero when parallel — and the intuition for why)
- Why zero force when the wire is parallel to the field: the velocity of the charges is along the field direction, so the cross product is zero — there is no perpendicular component to push against

**Extended response prep:**
- The syllabus says "Use magnetic field interactions to explain why..." — this is the mechanism question. Students must describe the two-field interaction, not just quote F = BIL.
- Weak answer: "A force acts because F = BIL."
- Strong answer: "The current-carrying conductor produces a circular magnetic field around itself. This field interacts with the external uniform magnetic field, resulting in a region of stronger combined field on one side of the conductor and weaker combined field on the other. The conductor experiences a net force directed from the region of stronger field toward the region of weaker field."
- Right hand rule: explain it as a tool for finding the direction, not the explanation of why. The why is always the two-field interaction.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Use magnetic field interactions to explain why a current-carrying conductor experiences a force"; F = BIL direction and magnitude

**Cross-links:** M6-02 (force on moving charges is the underlying principle); M6-06 (torque on a loop uses this force); M6-07 (DC motor is this force applied to a coil)

### M6-06 — Torque on a Current Loop: Why the Motor Spins
**Home topic:** How the motor effect on two sides of a rectangular coil produces a turning force (torque), and why torque varies as the coil rotates.

**Theory focus:**
- A rectangular coil in a magnetic field: two sides carry current perpendicular to the field (these experience a force). Two sides are parallel to the field (these experience no force, because the current is parallel to B).
- The two forces on the active sides are in opposite directions (the current flows in opposite directions on those sides), so they form a couple — a pair of forces that create rotation without translation.
- Why torque changes with angle: when the coil plane is parallel to the field, the forces act at the maximum perpendicular distance from the axis → maximum torque. When the coil plane is perpendicular to the field (normal to the coil is parallel to B), the forces still exist but they are pulling directly outward along the axis → zero torque, the coil is not being turned.
- The practical problem: without modification, the coil reaches the zero-torque position and stops. The commutator solves this — which is M6-07.
- Factors that increase torque: more turns (more current-carrying segments), stronger field, larger coil area (larger perpendicular distance from axis), larger current

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain why the torque on a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field varies as the coil rotates"
- Strong answer: must describe that torque is maximum when coil plane is parallel to field (forces act at maximum distance), drops to zero when coil is perpendicular to field (forces act along the rotation axis), and increases again as the coil continues rotating — with the reason for each position.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — torque on a current-carrying loop; factors that affect torque; τ = nBIA cos θ (the cosine factor is the conceptual heart of this lesson)

**Cross-links:** M5 circular motion (torque concept); M6-05 (torque arises from motor-effect forces on the coil sides); M6-07 (DC motor uses this coil, adds commutator to solve the zero-torque problem)

### M6-07 — The DC Motor: Components, Function, and the Commutator's Role
**Home topic:** The full operation cycle of a DC motor — all components, what each one does, and the critical role of the split-ring commutator.

**Theory focus:**
- Components: rotor coil (carries current, rotates), field magnets (provide external B field), brushes (stationary contacts that maintain connection to the rotating coil), split-ring commutator (rotates with the coil, reverses the current direction at the right moment)
- The commutator's job: without it, the coil reaches the zero-torque position and the coil's own momentum carries it past — but then the torque would act to push it back the wrong way. The commutator reverses the current at exactly the zero-torque point (when the gap in the commutator aligns with the brushes), so the torque always acts in the same rotational direction. This is the key conceptual point most students miss.
- The full cycle: describe one 360° rotation step by step — where the torque is maximum, where it's zero, when the commutator reverses, and why the net effect is continuous rotation in one direction
- How to increase torque: more turns of wire (more current-carrying segments), stronger field magnets (permanent magnets or stronger electromagnets), larger coil area, larger current

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain the role of the split-ring commutator in a simple DC motor" — worth 3–4 marks
- Weak answer: "The commutator reverses the current."
- Strong answer: "As the coil rotates past the position where its plane is perpendicular to the magnetic field — the zero-torque position — the gap in the split-ring commutator aligns with the brushes, momentarily breaking the circuit. The two halves of the commutator then swap contact with the brushes, reversing the direction of current through the coil. This ensures that the force on each side of the coil always acts in the same rotational direction, allowing the motor to continue rotating continuously rather than oscillating about the zero-torque position."
- The HSC also loves: "compare the structure and function of a DC motor with an AC motor" — the answer hinges on commutator vs slip rings

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — structure and function of DC motor components; role of split-ring commutator; factors that increase torque in a brushed motor

**Cross-links:** M6-06 (torque on coil is the force that drives the motor); M6-08 (back-EMF is generated by the same coil spinning); M6-15 (comparing DC motor with AC induction motor)

### M6-08 — Back-EMF: The Motor That Fights Itself
**Home topic:** What back-EMF is, why it exists, and what it means for the current through a motor and the motor's speed — including what happens when a motor is stalled.

**Theory focus:**
- When a motor coil spins, it is also a coil moving through a magnetic field — so by Faraday's law, it generates an EMF. This induced EMF opposes the supply voltage (Lenz's law). It is called back-EMF, or counter-EMF.
- The back-EMF grows as the motor speeds up, because a faster-spinning coil cuts through flux more rapidly.
- The current through the motor at any speed: I = (V_supply − V_back) / R. When the motor first starts, back-EMF = 0, so current is very high — often high enough to trip a circuit breaker if not managed.
- At full operating speed, back-EMF is large, so current is small — the motor is efficient.
- If the motor is mechanically stalled (held still by a load), back-EMF = 0 again, current surges, the motor overheats. This is why motors burn out when stalled.
- The self-regulating mechanism: if load increases, motor slows, back-EMF falls, current rises, force rises — the motor automatically adjusts to carry more load.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain the effect of back-EMF on the current through and speed of a DC motor"
- Must describe: why back-EMF is generated (Faraday/Lenz), how it relates to speed (back-EMF rises with speed), and the effect on current (current = (V − back-EMF)/R, so current falls as speed rises, stabilising the motor)
- Common trap: students say back-EMF "reduces the current" without explaining the mechanism. Marks require: back-EMF is an induced EMF that opposes the supply (Lenz's law), which reduces the net voltage driving current through the motor's resistance.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Explain the production of back-EMF in simple DC motors"; "Explain the effects of back-EMF on the current flowing through and speed of a simple DC motor"

**Cross-links:** M6-07 (DC motor operation — the coil spinning IS the source of back-EMF); M6-10 (Faraday's law explains why the spinning coil generates an EMF); M6-11 (Lenz's law explains why the induced EMF opposes the supply)

### M6-09 — Magnetic Flux: What It Actually Means
**Home topic:** Building an intuitive understanding of magnetic flux before Faraday's law — so Faraday's law makes sense rather than being a formula students apply without understanding.

**Theory focus:**
- Flux is not the field strength. Flux is the total amount of field passing through a surface — field strength times area, adjusted for angle.
- The area vector concept: every flat surface has a normal (perpendicular) direction. Flux is maximised when the field is parallel to this normal (perpendicular to the surface), and is zero when the field is parallel to the surface (skimming across it, passing no field lines through).
- The angle: Φ = BA cos θ where θ is the angle between the field and the area vector (the normal). When the field lines go straight through — θ = 0 — cos θ = 1, maximum flux. When the field is parallel to the surface — θ = 90° — cos θ = 0, zero flux.
- Why this matters for the motor: as the coil rotates, the angle between the field and the coil's normal changes → flux changes continuously → by Faraday's law, this changing flux induces an EMF. The coil is simultaneously a motor (converting electrical to mechanical energy) and a generator (converting mechanical back to electrical — that's back-EMF).
- Everyday intuition: think of the field lines as rain. Flux is the rate at which rain falls through a window. Tilt the window: less rain gets through. Lay it flat (horizontal): maximum rain gets through.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Describe magnetic flux and explain how it changes as a coil rotates in a magnetic field"
- Flux must be defined precisely: the product of the magnetic field strength and the component of area perpendicular to the field, or equivalently BA cos θ where θ is the angle between the field direction and the area vector normal to the coil.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Describe magnetic flux as the measure of the total magnetic field passing through a given area"; relationships between Φ, B, A, and θ

**Cross-links:** M6-10 (Faraday's law is literally "the rate of change of this"); M6-11 (Lenz's law determines the direction of the induced current when flux changes); M6-12 (eddy currents form whenever flux through a conductor changes)

### M6-10 — Faraday's Law: Why Change Is Everything
**Home topic:** The conceptual meaning of Faraday's law — that it is the *change* in flux, not the flux itself, that induces an EMF — and how to explain induction in straight conductors, solenoids, and metal plates.

**Theory focus:**
- The central insight: a steady magnetic field through a coil does nothing. Only a *changing* flux induces an EMF. This is counterintuitive — students expect that a stronger field means more induced current. But a strong, constant field induces nothing.
- Three ways to change flux: change the field strength (vary the magnet), change the area (move the conductor), change the angle (rotate the coil). All three produce identical results per Faraday's law because all three change Φ.
- Straight conductor moving through a field: the conductor cuts through field lines (changes the area of the circuit). Each charge in the conductor experiences a force (F = qvB) in the direction along the wire → charge separation → EMF.
- Solenoid with magnet approaching: the flux through the solenoid increases → EMF is induced → current flows in the coil.
- Metal plate moving through a field: currents are induced in the bulk of the metal (these are eddy currents, M6-12). There are no discrete loops, but the changing flux still drives currents.
- The more turns, the larger the EMF: each turn adds its induced EMF in series.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain why an EMF is induced when a magnet is moved into a solenoid"
- Must explain: the motion of the magnet changes the magnetic flux through the solenoid (more field lines pass through the coil). By Faraday's law, any change in flux induces an EMF proportional to the rate of change of flux and the number of turns.
- Common trap: saying "the magnet causes a current." The magnet causes a change in flux. The change in flux causes an EMF. The EMF drives a current if there is a complete circuit. Three-step chain — mark-earners state all three.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — Faraday's law; how EMF size is affected by turns and rate of flux change; effects of induction in straight conductors, metal plates, and solenoids

**Cross-links:** M6-09 (flux is the thing that's changing); M6-11 (Lenz's law gives the direction); M6-08 (back-EMF is Faraday's law applied to a spinning motor coil)

### M6-11 — Lenz's Law: Conservation of Energy in Disguise
**Home topic:** Lenz's law as a consequence of conservation of energy — not just a rule about directions, but a fundamental physical necessity.

**Theory focus:**
- Lenz's law stated simply: the induced current always flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it.
- The conservation of energy argument: if the induced current reinforced the change in flux instead of opposing it, we would get a runaway — the changing flux would increase the induced current, which would increase the flux, which would increase the induced current, all without any energy input. That would be a perpetual motion machine. It is impossible. Therefore, the induced current must always oppose its cause.
- The practical consequences: a magnet falling into a solenoid is slowed by the opposing force. A conductor plate moving into a magnetic field feels a braking force. A motor's spinning coil generates an opposing EMF (back-EMF). All of these are the same Lenz's law.
- The direction rule derived from Lenz's law: if flux through a coil is increasing, the induced current creates a field that opposes the increase (pushes back against the incoming flux). If flux is decreasing, the induced current creates a field that tries to maintain it.
- How to apply it to find current direction: determine how flux is changing → determine which direction of induced current would oppose that change → use the right-hand rule to find which way current flows in the coil.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain how Lenz's law is consistent with the law of conservation of energy"
- Weak answer: "Lenz's law says the current opposes the change."
- Strong answer: "If the induced current reinforced rather than opposed the change in flux, the increasing flux would drive a larger current, which would further increase the flux, creating unlimited energy from no external input — violating conservation of energy. Therefore, by conservation of energy, the induced current must always act to oppose the change in flux that produced it. This is Lenz's law."

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Explain how ε and the direction of an induced current are consistent with the law of conservation of energy"

**Cross-links:** M6-10 (Faraday gives the magnitude; Lenz gives the direction); M6-12 (eddy current braking force is Lenz's law in a conductor); M6-08 (back-EMF in a motor is Lenz's law applied to the spinning coil)

### M6-12 — Eddy Currents: Lenz's Law in Bulk Conductors
**Home topic:** How eddy currents form in solid conductors, why they always create a braking force, and where this is useful and where it is a problem.

**Theory focus:**
- When a solid conductor moves through a magnetic field (or has a changing flux through it), currents are induced in the bulk of the material — not in discrete loops but in swirling patterns, like eddies in water. These are eddy currents.
- By Lenz's law, these currents create magnetic fields that oppose the change causing them → they always produce a force that opposes the motion. Always a brake, never a boost.
- The energy story: kinetic energy of the conductor → electrical energy of eddy currents → thermal energy (resistive heating in the conductor). This is irreversible — it always heats up the conductor.
- Where eddy currents are useful:
  - Electromagnetic braking (trains, roller coasters, fairground rides): smooth, wear-free, contactless braking. The faster the motion, the stronger the eddy current braking force.
  - Induction cooktops: the changing magnetic field from the coil drives eddy currents in the pot, heating it directly. The cooktop surface stays cool — only the pot is heated.
  - Electricity meters (older moving-disc type): the disc is braked proportionally to current flow, measuring energy use.
- Where eddy currents are a problem:
  - Transformer cores: eddy currents waste energy as heat. Solved by laminating the core — thin insulated sheets of iron reduce the size of eddy current loops and hence their magnitude.
  - Electric motors: same issue in the rotor and stator cores, same lamination solution.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain how electromagnetic braking works with reference to eddy currents and Lenz's law"
- Must describe: motion through a field induces eddy currents in the conductor (Faraday). By Lenz's law, these currents produce a force opposing the motion (conservation of energy). The kinetic energy is dissipated as thermal energy. Braking force increases with speed.
- Common trap: students describe eddy currents as "opposing" without explaining what they are. State clearly: eddy currents are electrical currents induced in bulk conductors (not in discrete circuit loops) by a changing magnetic flux through the material.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — "Account for the production of eddy currents in conductive materials"; effects of electromagnetic induction in metal plates; electromagnetic braking; real transformer losses

**Cross-links:** M6-10 (eddy currents arise from Faraday's law); M6-11 (eddy current direction from Lenz's law); M6-14 (transformer core losses from eddy currents)

### M6-13 — AC and DC Generators: How Rotation Becomes Current
**Home topic:** The structure and operation of AC and DC generators — why the output is sinusoidal, what the commutator does differently, and how to compare them.

**Theory focus:**
- Both generators: a coil rotates in a magnetic field. As it rotates, the flux changes → Faraday's law → EMF is induced. This is the fundamental mechanism.
- Why the output is sinusoidal: the rate of change of flux varies as the coil rotates. When the coil is parallel to the field (coil plane parallel to B), the coil sides cut field lines at maximum rate → maximum EMF. When the coil is perpendicular to the field, the coil sides move parallel to the field lines → zero rate of flux change → zero EMF. The output is EMF = ε_max sin(ωt), which is by definition sinusoidal.
- AC generator: slip rings connect the coil to the external circuit. The slip rings rotate with the coil and maintain a constant connection. The output alternates direction with each half-rotation, giving a true AC sinusoidal output.
- DC generator: split-ring commutator, same as in the DC motor. The commutator reverses the external connection every half rotation, so the external circuit always gets current in the same direction — but the magnitude still pulsates, going from zero to maximum to zero (a rectified sinusoid, not a flat DC line).
- The structural difference: AC has slip rings (continuous rings), DC has a split-ring commutator (two separate half-rings). The function of the coil is identical. The output difference comes entirely from the commutator vs slip rings.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain the variations in current produced by an AC generator compared with a DC generator"
- "Compare the structure and function of an AC generator and a DC generator"
- The key: same mechanism (Faraday's law), same coil rotation, same sinusoidal EMF generation internally. The only structural difference is slip rings vs split-ring commutator. The output difference: AC is a full sinusoidal waveform, DC is a pulsating unidirectional output (both halves positive, but still varying from zero to maximum).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — structure and function of AC and DC generators; why output varies; slip rings vs split-ring commutators

**Cross-links:** M6-07 (DC motor structure is essentially a DC generator run in reverse — same components); M6-10 (Faraday's law explains why the rotating coil generates an EMF); M6-14 (transformers step up or down the AC output of generators)

### M6-14 — Transformers: Why AC and How They Work
**Home topic:** The full operation of a transformer — mutual induction, why AC is essential, and what makes a real transformer less efficient than an ideal one.

**Theory focus:**
- The mechanism: an AC current in the primary coil creates a constantly changing magnetic flux in the iron core. This changing flux passes through the secondary coil. By Faraday's law, the changing flux induces an EMF in the secondary. No electrical connection between primary and secondary — the energy is transferred by the changing magnetic field through the core.
- Why AC is essential: a DC current in the primary would create a constant flux → no change → no EMF induced in the secondary. A transformer requires a constantly changing flux. Only AC provides this. This is one of the most important "explain why" questions in the module.
- The turns ratio: the same flux passes through both coils. More turns on the secondary → each turn adds its induced EMF in series → larger output voltage. Fewer turns → smaller output voltage. V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p.
- Conservation of energy: an ideal transformer conserves power. V_s × I_s = V_p × I_p. If voltage steps up, current steps down proportionally. You cannot get more power out than you put in.
- Real transformer losses — the three the syllabus names:
  - Incomplete flux linkage: some flux from the primary doesn't pass through the secondary core — it leaks through the air. Reduced by wrapping coils tightly together on a closed iron core.
  - Eddy currents in the core: the alternating flux induces eddy currents in the iron core, generating heat. Reduced by laminating the core.
  - Resistive heating: the wire in the coils has resistance → current flowing through them dissipates energy as heat (P = I²R). Reduced by using thicker, lower-resistance wire.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain why a transformer requires an AC input rather than a DC input"
- "Explain the differences in power output between ideal and real transformers"
- Both questions require the mechanism first (mutual induction / changing flux / Faraday's law), then the specific answer. Students who jump straight to "AC keeps changing" without explaining WHY that matters (constant DC → constant flux → no change → no induced EMF) lose marks.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — structure of step-up and step-down transformers; why primary requires AC; V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p; losses in real transformers (incomplete flux linkage, eddy currents, resistive heating)

**Cross-links:** M6-10 (Faraday's law is the transformer's working principle); M6-12 (eddy currents in the core are a real transformer loss); M6-13 (generators produce AC, which is why the grid uses AC and transformers work)

### M6-15 — AC Induction Motors and Electromagnetic Braking
**Home topic:** How an AC induction motor works without any electrical connection to the rotor, and how electromagnetic braking uses the same principle.

**Theory focus:**
- The AC induction motor is the most common motor in the world (washing machines, fans, industry). Unlike the DC motor, nothing physically touches the rotor — no brushes, no commutator. This is why it is so reliable.
- How the rotating magnetic field is created: three pairs of coils arranged 120° apart, each fed by AC that is 120° out of phase. At any instant, the current in the three coils is different → the combined field points in a different direction → as the AC cycles, the field direction rotates continuously. This is the "rotating magnetic field."
- What the rotor sees: it is sitting inside a continuously rotating magnetic field. From the rotor's perspective (if it were stationary), the field is changing — which means the flux through the rotor is changing → Faraday's law → eddy currents are induced in the rotor → by Lenz's law, these eddy currents create forces opposing the relative motion between field and rotor → the rotor is dragged around by the rotating field.
- The slip: the rotor can never quite catch the rotating field — if it did, there would be no relative motion, no flux change, no induced currents, no force. The rotor always runs slightly slower than the field rotation speed. This speed difference is called the slip.
- Electromagnetic braking: same principle in reverse. A conductor moving through a field (or in a changing field) develops eddy currents → Lenz's law → braking force. Used in trains, roller coasters, fairground rides. Smooth, contactless, wear-free. Braking force proportional to speed.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain how electromagnetic induction causes a torque on the armature of an AC induction motor"
- Must include: rotating field, relative motion between field and rotor, changing flux through rotor, induced eddy currents (Faraday), eddy currents create a field opposing relative motion (Lenz), this produces a torque dragging the rotor in the direction of field rotation.
- "Explain how electromagnetic braking generates a force opposing motion"
- Same chain: motion → changing flux → induced currents (Faraday) → opposing force (Lenz) → braking.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** M6 — AC induction motor structure and function; "Explain how electromagnetic induction causes a torque on the armature of a brushless DC motor"; electromagnetic braking; "Outline the differences between a motor and a generator in terms of energy transformation"

**Cross-links:** M6-10 (Faraday's law is the mechanism for both induction motor and braking); M6-11 (Lenz's law provides the direction and the braking); M6-12 (eddy currents in the rotor are the same phenomenon as eddy currents in any conductor); M6-07 (comparison: DC motor needs brushes and commutator; AC induction motor needs neither)
