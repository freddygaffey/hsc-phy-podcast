# Teaching Lesson Plan — Module 2: Dynamics
## Theory-First, Exam-Ready (Year 11)

**What these episodes are:** Standard teaching episodes that explain *why things move the way they
do* — Newton's three laws, how to resolve and add forces, the work–energy story, and the conservation
of momentum — and the exam phrasing that earns marks.

**What they are NOT:** Plug-and-chug. The algebra and worked numbers live in the supplementary; the
spoken lesson is about the *cause-and-effect* reasoning (net force → acceleration; impulse → change
in momentum) the markers reward.

**The problem this fixes:** Year 11 students can quote `F = ma` but lose marks for confusing mass with
weight, drawing incomplete force diagrams, mishandling the components of a force on an incline, treating
momentum as a scalar, or saying "energy is used up" instead of "transformed". Dynamics is the module
where precise force/energy/momentum language is everything.

**Target listener:** Year 11 student comfortable with the maths and with kinematics (M1), weak on the
syllabus wording, force diagrams, the resolve-into-components method, and the conservation arguments.

## Episode Sequence

### M2-01 — Forces and Newton's Three Laws
**Home topic:** What a force is, the three laws of motion, and net force as the cause of acceleration.

**Theory focus:**
- A force is a push or pull; a vector (cash-in of M1's D-V-A). Contact forces vs forces mediated by
  fields (gravity, electrostatic, magnetic). Weight = mg is a force (newtons); mass (kilograms) is the
  amount of matter — the headline distinction students blur.
- Newton's first law (inertia): an object stays at rest or in uniform motion unless acted on by a net
  (unbalanced) force. Equilibrium = zero net force = at rest or constant velocity.
- Newton's second law: net force equals mass times acceleration, F-net = ma; acceleration is in the
  direction of the net force and inversely proportional to mass. Deriving it qualitatively and with
  graphs/vectors; tie back to M1's uniformly-accelerated motion.
- Newton's third law: every action has an equal and opposite reaction, F(A on B) = minus F(B on A).
  The crucial point markers test: the action–reaction pair acts on *different* objects, so they never
  cancel; the two forces are equal in size, opposite in direction, same type.
- Net force and force diagrams: draw every force as a labelled arrow on the object; the vector sum is
  the net force.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Explain, using Newton's laws, why a passenger lurches forward when a car brakes." Strong answer
  names the first law (inertia): the passenger continues at constant velocity while the car decelerates.
- "Distinguish between mass and weight." Strong answer: mass is the scalar amount of matter in kg,
  constant everywhere; weight is the gravitational force on that mass, a vector in newtons, equal to mg.
- Weak answer: "weight is how heavy something is." Strong answer names the force/vector and W = mg.
- Common trap: thinking action–reaction forces cancel (they act on different bodies); confusing mass
  and weight.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** Newton's three laws — a stable hook (e.g. "Inertia,
F = ma, Action–Reaction"); and the mass-vs-weight distinction.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "using Newton's Laws of Motion, describe static and dynamic
interactions… contact force / force mediated by fields"; "apply Newton's first two laws… including
the role played by friction (ACSPH063)"; "relate motion to Newton's Second Law… Fnet = ma
(ACSPH062, ACSPH063)"; FAB = −FBA.

**Cross-links:** M1 (force causes the acceleration that kinematics describes — the bridge between the
two modules); M2-02 (net force on an incline); M4/M6 (forces mediated by fields — electric, magnetic);
M5 (Fnet = ma drives projectile and circular motion).

### M2-02 — Friction, Inclined Planes and Resolving Forces
**Home topic:** Net force in two dimensions — resolving forces into components, equilibrium, friction,
and the inclined plane.

**Theory focus:**
- Resolving a force into perpendicular components: the horizontal component is F cosine theta and the
  vertical component is F sine theta (relative to the chosen axes) — the same resolve-and-recombine
  method as M1-04 vectors, now for forces.
- Equilibrium in 2D: the components in each direction sum to zero; this is how you solve "find the
  tension / the supporting force" problems.
- Friction: a contact force opposing relative motion (or tendency to move); static vs kinetic;
  always acts along the surface, opposing motion. It is what makes real Newton's-law problems realistic.
- The inclined plane (a guaranteed exam scenario): choose axes *along* and *perpendicular* to the slope;
  the weight resolves into a component down the slope (mg sine theta, which drives the acceleration)
  and a component into the slope (mg cosine theta, balanced by the normal force). Friction acts up the
  slope if the object slides down.
- Net force then feeds F-net = ma to get the acceleration along the slope, linking to M1's equations.

**Extended response prep:**
- "A block slides down a frictionless incline at angle theta; derive its acceleration." Strong answer
  resolves the weight along the slope (mg sine theta), sets F-net = ma, gives a = g sine theta.
- "Explain the role of friction when the block is on the point of sliding." Strong answer: static
  friction balances the down-slope component until it exceeds the maximum, then motion begins.
- Weak answer: uses mg (not its component) along the slope. Strong answer resolves correctly with the
  sine/cosine and states which axis each component lies on.
- Common trap: using the full weight instead of its slope component; putting sine and cosine on the
  wrong components; forgetting friction direction.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** the inclined-plane resolution — a stable hook for
"down-slope component = mg sine theta, into-slope = mg cosine theta" (so students stop swapping sin/cos).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "explore net force and equilibrium… using algebraic addition, vector
addition, vector addition by resolution into components (ACSPH050)"; "Fx = F cos θ, Fy = F sin θ";
"explain and predict the motion of objects on inclined planes (ACSPH098)"; role of friction (ACSPH063).

**Cross-links:** M1-04 (vector resolution — same method, now forces); M2-01 (net force → F = ma);
M5 (resolving the velocity of a projectile uses the identical component method).

### M2-03 — Work, Energy and Power
**Home topic:** The work–energy story: work done, kinetic and gravitational potential energy,
conservation of mechanical energy, and power.

**Theory focus:**
- Work: done when a force moves an object in the direction of the force; work equals the net force
  times the displacement in that direction, W = F-net s (joules). A scalar. No work if there's no
  displacement, or if the force is perpendicular to the motion.
- Kinetic energy = one half m v squared; the work–energy theorem: the net work done equals the change
  in kinetic energy. Gravitational potential energy change in a uniform field = mg times the change in
  height, ΔU = mg Δh.
- Conservation of mechanical energy (the special case): with no friction/air resistance, kinetic plus
  gravitational potential energy stays constant — energy transforms between the two but the total is
  conserved. The exam phrasing: energy is *transformed*, never "used up" or "lost" (and where friction
  acts, mechanical energy is transformed to heat, not destroyed).
- Power: the rate of doing work or transferring energy, P = ΔE over time; equivalently P = F v
  (force times velocity). Watts. Applications: lifting against gravity, working against friction/air
  resistance/rolling resistance.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Use conservation of energy to find the speed of a ball at the bottom of a frictionless ramp." Strong
  answer equates the loss in gravitational potential energy to the gain in kinetic energy, mg Δh = ½ m v².
- "Explain what happens to the mechanical energy of a car braking to a stop." Strong answer: kinetic
  energy is transformed (by friction) into heat (and sound); energy is conserved overall, not destroyed.
- Weak answer: "the energy gets used up." Strong answer names the transformation and conservation.
- Common trap: saying energy is lost/used up; forgetting work needs displacement along the force;
  confusing power with energy.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** the energy transformations and the
"transformed, not destroyed" rule; the two power forms (P = ΔE/t and P = Fv).

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "conservation of mechanical energy… W = Fnet s; ΔU = mgΔh"; "average
power P = ΔE/t, P = Fv… uniformly accelerated motion, objects raised against gravity, work done against
air/rolling resistance and friction."

**Cross-links:** M1-02 (work = area under a force–displacement graph — the "area" idea again); M2-01/02
(the force doing the work); M3 (energy of waves; thermal energy in M3 thermodynamics); M7-15/M8
(mass–energy E = mc² is the ultimate extension of "energy is conserved and transforms").

### M2-04 — Momentum, Impulse and Collisions
**Home topic:** Momentum as "mass in motion", impulse as the change in it, and conservation of momentum
in collisions.

**Theory focus:**
- Momentum = mass times velocity, p = mv; a vector (direction matters — sign discipline from M1).
  Units kilogram metres per second.
- Impulse: the change in momentum, Δp = F Δt (force times the time it acts). This is why follow-through,
  crumple zones, and air bags work — they extend the time, reducing the force for a given change in
  momentum. The area under a force–time graph is the impulse.
- Conservation of momentum: in a closed system (no external net force), the total momentum before equals
  the total momentum after, Σ m v before = Σ m v after. This follows from Newton's third law (the
  equal-and-opposite forces in the collision). The headline tool for all collision problems.
- Elastic vs inelastic collisions: momentum is conserved in *both*; kinetic energy is conserved only in
  an *elastic* collision. In an inelastic collision (objects stick or deform) kinetic energy is
  transformed to heat/sound, so total KE after is less — but momentum still balances.

**Extended response prep:**
- "Two trolleys collide and stick together; find their common velocity." Strong answer applies
  conservation of momentum, Σmv before = Σmv after, with correct signs.
- "Explain how an airbag reduces injury, using impulse." Strong answer: the airbag increases the time
  over which the momentum changes, so for the same change in momentum the force is reduced (Δp = FΔt).
- "Compare elastic and inelastic collisions." Strong answer: momentum conserved in both; kinetic energy
  conserved only in elastic; in inelastic, KE is transformed to other forms.
- Weak answer: treats momentum as a scalar (drops the signs); says momentum isn't conserved in
  inelastic collisions. Strong answer keeps momentum a vector and distinguishes momentum from KE.
- Common trap: confusing conservation of momentum (always, closed system) with conservation of KE
  (elastic only); dropping direction signs.

**Memorisable lists needing mnemonics (§5.4):** the elastic-vs-inelastic rule (momentum always,
KE only-if-elastic) and the impulse relationship Δp = FΔt; a hook for "extend the time → cut the force".

**Syllabus dot-points served:** "conservation of momentum (Σmv before = Σmv after) and conservation of
kinetic energy in elastic collisions (ACSPH066)"; "impulse Δp = FΔt"; force–time graphs; "compare the
momentum and kinetic energy of elastic and inelastic collisions (ACSPH066)."

**Cross-links:** M2-01 (Newton's third law is *why* momentum is conserved); M2-03 (kinetic energy and
its transformation — the elastic/inelastic distinction); M8 (conservation laws as the spine of nuclear
physics; momentum conservation forces the two annihilation photons apart).

### M2-99 — Module Review: Dynamics
**Home topic:** Tie Dynamics together — Newton's laws, force resolution, the work–energy story, and the
conservation of momentum — through integrated, multi-part exam questions (STYLE §5.3).

**Theory focus (interleaving at full scale):** the through-line is *forces cause changes in motion, and
across an interaction energy and momentum are conserved*. Re-surface every distinction (mass vs weight,
action–reaction on different bodies, sin/cos on the incline, work needs displacement, transformed not
destroyed, momentum-always vs KE-only-if-elastic) and every mnemonic; flag the bridges to M1
(kinematics gives the acceleration), M5 (projectiles, circular motion), and M8 (conservation laws).

**Extended response prep:** lean on integrated questions — e.g. a block down an incline (resolve forces,
F = ma, then energy), or a collision (momentum, then compare KE elastic vs inelastic); show a
weak-vs-strong answer for "explain using Newton's laws"; pose a "pause the player" retrieval of the
three laws and the two conservation laws.

**Syllabus dot-points served:** integrative review of all M2 dot-points (consolidation per STYLE §5.3).

**Cross-links:** every M2 episode; back to M1; forward to M5 (advanced mechanics) and M8 (conservation).
