# Case Study Lesson Plan — HSC Physics Podcast
## Veritasium-Style Narrative Episodes

**Format philosophy:** Every episode opens with a question that most people get wrong, or a moment in history where everything was at stake. We never lead with "today we're going to learn about X." We lead with a mystery, a person, or a crisis. The physics is the payoff — not the premise.

**Episode shape (every case study follows this arc):**
1. **Cold open hook** — a question, paradox, or dramatic scene. ≤60 seconds. Forces the listener to hold a question in mind.
2. **Set the stage** — the world before the discovery. What did people believe? Why were they wrong?
3. **The problem** — what specific, concrete obstacle stood in the way.
4. **The experiment / event** — the decisive moment, described in real-time narrative.
5. **The physics payoff** — explain the mechanism. This is where the syllabus content lives.
6. **The aftermath** — what changed. Why it matters to physics, to technology, to the listener.
7. **The exam hook** — one sentence connecting this story to an HSC dot-point ("this is exactly what the syllabus means when it says…").

**Target runtime per episode:** 25–35 minutes.

---

## Episode Sequence

The episodes are ordered for maximum narrative momentum, not strict curriculum order. Earlier episodes build vocabulary and intuition that later episodes cash in.

### CASE-01 — Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment
**Hook:** *"Ernest Rutherford aimed radiation at a piece of gold thinner than human hair. Most of it passed straight through. But some bounced back. He later said it was like firing artillery shells at tissue paper — and having them bounce back at you. That day, he accidentally destroyed the model of the atom that everyone believed in."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Open in Manchester, 1909. Rutherford asks Geiger and Marsden to try something he thinks is pointless.
- The plum pudding model: Thomson's atom as raisin bread — positive "dough" with electron "raisins." Why it seemed completely reasonable.
- The alpha particle source, the gold foil, the zinc sulphide screen. The setup is described as a physics experiment, not a history lesson.
- The data comes in. Most go through. But 1 in 8,000 bounces back. Rutherford: "It was quite the most incredible event that ever happened to me."
- The nuclear model: almost all mass crammed into a tiny, dense, positive nucleus. Atoms are 99.99% empty space.
- Aftermath: the Bohr atom, energy levels, modern chemistry — all built on Rutherford's morning of disbelief.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — "Explain how the observations of the gold foil experiment led to the proposal of a nuclear model of the atom"
**Engagement lever:** Scale. Help the listener feel what 99.99% empty space means at human scale.
**Runtime target:** 28 min

### CASE-02 — J.J. Thomson and the Discovery of the Electron
**Hook:** *"In 1897, a Cambridge physicist pointed a beam of invisible rays at a magnet — and accidentally proved that atoms, which everyone agreed were the smallest things in existence, were made of something even smaller."*

**Narrative arc:**
- What are cathode rays? The decade-long argument: German physicists say waves, British physicists say particles.
- Thomson's three experiments: Maltese cross shadow (particles travel in straight lines), paddle wheel (particles have momentum), deflection by electric and magnetic fields.
- The e/m ratio calculation — deriving it with perpendicular E and B fields. The number is 1,800 times larger than hydrogen's. These "corpuscles" are tiny.
- Thomson announces to his Friday Evening Discourse audience that atoms are divisible. Nobody believes him.
- The plum pudding model as the logical but ultimately wrong next step.
- Aftermath: the electron underpins all of chemistry, electronics, and the modern world.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — cathode ray observations, e/m ratio, evidence for particles with mass and charge
**Engagement lever:** The argument between nations (German vs British physics). Science as human drama.
**Runtime target:** 27 min

### CASE-03 — The Millikan Oil-Drop Experiment
**Hook:** *"Robert Millikan spent eleven years watching oil droplets fall. Most scientists would have given up. But Millikan was looking for the single most important number in the universe — the charge on one electron. And he found it by making oil float in mid-air."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Why the fundamental charge matters: everything electromagnetic depends on it.
- The apparatus: atomiser, electric field plates, X-ray ioniser. Described as if the listener is setting it up.
- Watching a droplet: gravity pulls it down, electric field pushes it up. When they balance — the droplet hovers. 
- The maths: if you know the field strength, the droplet mass, and g — you get the charge. Every measurement is a multiple of one tiny number.
- The controversy with Ehrenhaft, and Millikan's data selection (a modern note on scientific integrity).
- What 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C actually means: the unit that defines the ampere, the volt, and every circuit ever built.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — determining the charge-to-mass ratio; evidence that charge is quantised
**Engagement lever:** The beauty of the number. Everything divisible by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹.
**Runtime target:** 25 min

### CASE-04 — Chadwick and the Discovery of the Neutron
**Hook:** *"By 1930, physicists knew atoms had electrons and protons. But the numbers didn't add up. Helium had 2 protons — but it was four times heavier than hydrogen. Something was missing. For fifteen years, nobody could find it, because it was invisible to every detector they had."*

**Narrative arc:**
- The mass problem: nuclei are too heavy. Predictions of a neutral particle go back to 1920 (Rutherford's lecture).
- Bothe and Becker, 1930: bombard beryllium with alpha particles, get a penetrating radiation. They call it gamma rays.
- The Joliot-Curies, Paris, 1932: this "gamma radiation" can knock protons out of paraffin wax at high speed. Gamma rays can't do that.
- Chadwick, Cambridge, reads the paper on a Friday. By the following Friday, he has the neutron.
- Conservation of momentum: the calculation that proved the particle had mass equal to the proton.
- Aftermath: the neutron is the key to nuclear reactions, fission, and everything that follows.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — "Analyse how the law of conservation of energy and momentum were applied in experiments to determine the existence and properties of the neutron"
**Engagement lever:** The detective story. Clues accumulate for 15 years; Chadwick solves it in 10 days.
**Runtime target:** 28 min

### CASE-05 — The Cavendish Experiment (Weighing the Earth)
**Hook:** *"Newton worked out the laws of gravity in 1687. He could tell you exactly how fast a ball would fall, how long the Moon takes to orbit, and the trajectory of every comet. But there was one thing he couldn't tell you: how heavy the Earth was. That answer had to wait 111 years — for a man with two lead balls and a twisted wire."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Henry Cavendish, 1798. Elderly, reclusive, possibly the richest scientist in England. Almost never publishes.
- The torsion balance: a 1.8m rod suspended by a wire, with two 0.73kg lead balls, and two 158kg lead spheres brought close.
- The gravitational attraction is vanishingly small — the wire twists by a fraction of a degree.
- The measurement: measuring the twist angle to get the force, then back-calculating G, then M_Earth.
- Modern value: G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻². Still the least precisely known constant in physics.
- Why G is hard: it's the weakest of the four forces. Every other constant is known 1,000x more precisely.

**Syllabus coverage:** M5 — Newton's law of universal gravitation; gravitational field strength
**Engagement lever:** The scale of patience. One man in a shed in 1798 measured the weight of a planet.
**Runtime target:** 26 min

### CASE-06 — The Hertz Experiment
**Hook:** *"James Clerk Maxwell died in 1879, convinced he'd proven something that nobody could test: that light was a wave of electricity and magnetism, travelling at a precise speed he'd calculated from first principles. He died before anyone built the machine to prove him right. Eight years later, a young German physicist accidentally created something new — and when he held up a copper ring, it glowed."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Maxwell's equations: four equations that unify electricity, magnetism, and light. The prediction of EM waves at 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
- Why nobody believed it (or tested it) for a decade.
- Hertz's spark-gap oscillator: high-voltage sparks create oscillating currents, radiating EM waves.
- The loop antenna: a copper ring with a small gap. When Hertz holds it near the transmitter, a tiny spark jumps. Radio has been invented, but nobody knows it yet.
- Measuring the wavelength and speed: standing waves between reflector and transmitter. The speed matches Maxwell's prediction exactly.
- Hertz: "It's of no use whatsoever. This is just an experiment that proves Maxwell was right." He dies at 36. Marconi reads his papers and invents radio.

**Syllabus coverage:** M7 — "Explain how a spark-gap oscillator and loop antenna verified the predicted speed of EM waves (Hertz experiment)"
**Engagement lever:** Dramatic irony. Hertz doesn't realise what he's built. Marconi does.
**Runtime target:** 27 min

### CASE-07 — The Michelson–Morley Experiment
**Hook:** *"In 1887, two American physicists ran the most precise experiment ever attempted — and got the wrong answer. Except the 'wrong answer' turned out to be one of the most important results in the history of science. It destroyed a 200-year-old idea about the universe, and it set Albert Einstein on the path to special relativity."*

**Narrative arc:**
- The aether: the invisible medium that light supposedly waves through. Logical, accepted by everyone.
- If the Earth moves through the aether, light should travel faster in the direction of Earth's motion.
- Michelson's interferometer: a beam splitter, two perpendicular arms, mirrors. Light takes two paths; any speed difference appears as a fringe shift.
- The result: no fringe shift. None. The apparatus is sensitive enough to detect 1/40th of the expected signal. It gets zero.
- Reactions: FitzGerald proposes lengths contract. Lorentz does the maths. Both are improvising to save the aether.
- Einstein, 1905: throws out the aether entirely. The speed of light is constant for all observers. Everything else follows.

**Syllabus coverage:** M7 — evidence for special relativity; historical context for Einstein's postulates
**Engagement lever:** The paradox of a failed experiment that changes everything.
**Runtime target:** 30 min

### CASE-08 — The Photoelectric Effect and the Birth of the Quantum
**Hook:** *"In 1905, Einstein published four papers that each independently would have made him the greatest physicist of the century. One of them solved a mystery about light that had stumped everyone for years. The weird thing is: it's the one he called 'revolutionary' — not relativity."*

**Narrative arc:**
- The mystery: shine UV light on a metal, and electrons fly off. Double the brightness — same energy electrons, just more of them. Use red light instead — nothing. No electrons at all, no matter how bright.
- Classical wave theory's prediction: brighter light = more energy = faster electrons. Wrong.
- Einstein's radical step: light comes in chunks. Photons. Energy = hf. The electron either gets a whole photon or none.
- The work function: the minimum energy to escape the metal. If hf < φ, nothing happens.
- Millikan spends 10 years trying to disprove Einstein. His careful measurements prove him right instead.
- Aftermath: photovoltaics, solar cells, CCDs, every digital camera. The quantum revolution begins here.

**Syllabus coverage:** M7 — photoelectric effect, photon energy E = hf, work function, solar cells (secondary-source investigation)
**Engagement lever:** Einstein calls this his most radical idea, not E = mc². Why?
**Runtime target:** 28 min

### CASE-09 — Muon Lifetime: Time Dilation Made Real
**Hook:** *"Cosmic rays hit the upper atmosphere 15 kilometres above your head and create particles called muons. Those muons decay in 2.2 microseconds — which means, at any speed, they should travel at most 660 metres before vanishing. But we detect them at sea level. How does a particle that should die in 660 metres travel 15 kilometres? Because time runs slower for things that move fast. We've measured it."*

**Narrative arc:**
- What muons are: created by cosmic ray collisions at 15 km altitude, moving at 0.998c.
- Classical prediction: 660m range. Measured reality: 15 km. Something is wrong with the calculation.
- Time dilation: from the muon's frame, the atmosphere is length-contracted to 600m. From ours, the muon's clock runs slow.
- The maths: γ factor of ~15. Muon lifetime from our frame: 2.2μs × 15 = 33μs. Distance = 0.998c × 33μs = 9.9 km. Close enough.
- Hafele–Keating 1971: atomic clocks flown around the world on jet planes tick slower than ground clocks. Measured and predicted differences match to within experimental error.
- Every GPS satellite corrects for time dilation. Without the correction, GPS drifts by 10km per day.

**Syllabus coverage:** M7 — "Analyse the evidence supporting the theory of relativity, including the muon lifetime and atomic clocks"
**Engagement lever:** GPS as daily proof of special relativity. Your phone runs on time dilation.
**Runtime target:** 27 min

### CASE-10 — Electron Diffraction and Wave–Particle Duality
**Hook:** *"In 1927, two physicists fired a beam of electrons — which everyone knew were particles — at a crystal. They expected the electrons to bounce off like billiard balls hitting a wall. Instead, they got the same pattern you get when you shine light through a grating. The electrons were interfering with each other. As waves."*

**Narrative arc:**
- De Broglie's 1924 thesis: if light can act like particles (photons), maybe particles can act like waves. λ = h/mv.
- Einstein reads it and says "de Broglie has lifted a corner of the great veil."
- Davisson and Germer, Bell Labs, 1927: scatter electrons off a nickel crystal. An accident (an oxide layer forms, they anneal the crystal) creates large crystal planes. Suddenly: diffraction rings.
- Thomson (son of J.J.) independently confirms in Cambridge. Father discovers the electron as a particle; son proves it's a wave.
- The standing-wave electron in Bohr's atom: why the orbit radii are quantised — the electron wave must fit a whole number of wavelengths.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — "Explain why the production of electron diffraction patterns provides evidence for electrons behaving as waves"; de Broglie wavelength; limitations of stationary energy level model
**Engagement lever:** Father discovers the particle. Son discovers the wave. Same object.
**Runtime target:** 26 min

### CASE-11 — Particle Accelerators and the Discovery of Quarks
**Hook:** *"In the 1960s, physicists thought protons were fundamental — the smallest things that existed. Then they aimed a two-mile-long particle accelerator at them and found they were made of something even smaller. Something that can never be seen alone, ever. We've proved these particles exist, but nobody has ever held one in isolation, and nobody ever will."*

**Narrative arc:**
- The particle zoo of the 1960s: over 100 "fundamental" particles. Too many to all be fundamental.
- Murray Gell-Mann's quark model (1964): three types, fractional charge. Dismissed as a mathematical trick.
- SLAC, Stanford, 1968: 2-mile electron accelerator fires electrons at protons at high energy. Deep inelastic scattering — the electrons bounce at wild angles, as if hitting something hard inside.
- Bjorken and Feynman: the hard scattering objects are point-like particles with fractional charge. Quarks.
- Colour confinement: why quarks can never be isolated. The force between quarks gets stronger as you pull them apart — like a rubber band, not an inverse-square law.
- The Standard Model: quarks, leptons, bosons. The periodic table of fundamental particles.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — "Explain how particle accelerators and collision experiments are used to discover and study quarks, leptons and force-carrying bosons"
**Engagement lever:** The thing that can never be directly seen, but whose existence is beyond question.
**Runtime target:** 30 min

### CASE-12 — Nuclear Fission and the Manhattan Project
**Hook:** *"On a December morning in 1938, two chemists in Berlin split a uranium atom and couldn't believe what their instruments were showing them. They wrote a letter to a colleague who had fled the Nazis to Stockholm. She read it, went for a walk in the snow with her nephew, and calculated on the back of a scrap of paper that the energy released was 200 million times larger than a chemical explosion. Within seven years, that calculation would end a world war."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Hahn and Strassmann bombard uranium with neutrons, find barium in the products. Doesn't make sense — barium is half the mass of uranium.
- Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch: the nuclear model says fission is energetically possible. Liquid drop model of the nucleus. Calculate the energy from mass defect: E = Δmc².
- The chain reaction concept: Szilard's 1933 patent. When Frisch confirms fission, Szilard immediately understands what it means.
- The letter to Roosevelt, the Manhattan Project, Chicago Pile-1.
- Controlled vs uncontrolled chain reactions: the difference between a reactor and a bomb is control rods.
- The physics of fission: binding energy per nucleon, the iron peak, why splitting uranium releases energy.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — nuclear fission, chain reactions, fission reactor components, mass defect and E = mc², "contribution of selected scientists to the discovery of nuclear fission"
**Engagement lever:** Meitner, a Jewish woman who fled the Nazis, did the calculation that ended WWII — and was not awarded the Nobel Prize.
**Runtime target:** 35 min

### CASE-13 — Chernobyl: The Physics of a Meltdown
**Hook:** *"At 1:23am on the 26th of April 1986, the operators of Reactor 4 at Chernobyl pressed a button to insert the control rods and shut the reactor down. Instead, the power spiked to 30,000% of its designed maximum in three seconds. The physics that caused this had been known for years. Nobody told the operators."*

**Narrative arc:**
- What a nuclear reactor is: controlled fission, moderator slowing neutrons, control rods absorbing them, coolant carrying heat away.
- The positive void coefficient: in the RBMK reactor design, if coolant boils to steam, it slows fewer neutrons → more fissions → more heat → more steam. A feedback loop toward disaster.
- The safety test: simulating a power failure. The test parameters required lowering power to an unstable level.
- The sequence of failures: xenon poisoning, manual override of safety systems, the fatal button press.
- The SCRAM that wasn't: the graphite tips on the control rods momentarily increased reactivity as they entered.
- Liquidators, the exclusion zone, the sarcophagus. Half-life of Caesium-137: 30 years. The physics of the cleanup.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — fission reactor structure (fuel, control rods, moderator, coolant); controlled vs uncontrolled chain reactions; radioactive decay; half-life
**Engagement lever:** This is a story about physics illiteracy causing a catastrophe. The lesson is the physics.
**Runtime target:** 35 min

### CASE-14 — PET Scans: Antimatter in Medicine
**Hook:** *"Every day, hospitals around the world use antimatter to diagnose cancer. The same kind of matter that powers science fiction — the thing that would annihilate on contact with normal matter — is injected into patients, and the gamma rays from the annihilation are used to build a 3D map of metabolism inside a living brain. This is real, it's been done since the 1970s, and the physics behind it is exactly what your HSC expects you to know."*

**Narrative arc:**
- What a PET scan is: inject a radioactive glucose analogue that emits positrons. Tumours and active brain regions consume more glucose.
- The positron: Dirac predicts antimatter in 1928 from his relativistic electron equation. Anderson discovers it in 1932 in a cloud chamber.
- Positron-electron annihilation: conservation of mass-energy. Two photons, each 0.511 MeV, emitted at exactly 180°. Why 180°? Momentum conservation.
- The PET scanner: a ring of detectors. When two 511 keV photons arrive simultaneously at opposite points in the ring, the annihilation happened on that line. Thousands of lines: a 3D image.
- Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG): radioactive fluorine-18 attached to glucose. Half-life 110 minutes — long enough for the scan, short enough to decay away quickly.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — positron–electron annihilation; conservation of mass-energy; PET scans; properties of a positron
**Engagement lever:** The most exotic physics — antimatter — is mundane medical technology.
**Runtime target:** 27 min

### CASE-15 — Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin: What Stars Are Made Of
**Hook:** *"In 1925, a 25-year-old woman submitted the most important PhD thesis in the history of astronomy. She had discovered what stars are made of. Her supervisor — one of the most respected astronomers in the world — told her to say her conclusion was probably wrong. She changed her thesis. Four years later, he published the same result and took the credit. Today he is largely forgotten. Her result is the foundation of modern astrophysics."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Harvard Observatory, 1920s: "computers" — women hired to catalogue stellar spectra by hand, paid less than clerical staff.
- Payne's insight: the different absorption line patterns of stars aren't about different compositions — they're about temperature. Hot stars ionise their atoms differently.
- Using the Saha ionisation equations: predicting which spectral lines are visible at each temperature.
- The result: all stars are overwhelmingly hydrogen and helium. The differences are temperature, not chemistry.
- Henry Norris Russell, her supervisor, says the result "is clearly impossible." She adds a caveat to her thesis.
- 1929: Russell publishes the same result. Credits Payne in a footnote.
- The aftermath: stellar classification re-done; the H-R diagram explained; the basis of nucleosynthesis theory.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — "Describe Payne-Gaposchkin's spectral analysis technique for determining the chemical composition of stars"; spectra and stellar composition; H-R diagram
**Engagement lever:** Science as a human story with a villain and a hero — and the hero was right all along.
**Runtime target:** 28 min

### CASE-16 — Stellar Nucleosynthesis: We Are Made of Stars
**Hook:** *"Every atom of calcium in your bones, every atom of iron in your blood, every atom of oxygen you are breathing right now — was forged inside a star that died before the Sun was born. You are made of dead stars. And the process that made you is happening right now, in every star you can see tonight."*

**Narrative arc:**
- The Big Bang produces only hydrogen, helium, and trace lithium. Everything else had to be made later.
- Fred Hoyle, 1946: proposes that stars are element factories. (He also coins the term "Big Bang" as a mockery.)
- The proton-proton chain: 4 protons → 1 helium-4 + energy. The Sun fuses 600 million tonnes of hydrogen per second.
- The CNO cycle: the dominant process in stars heavier than ~1.3 solar masses. Carbon acts as a catalyst.
- As a star ages and its core hydrogen runs out: helium fusion (triple-alpha process) makes carbon. Carbon fusion makes oxygen, neon, silicon.
- The iron wall: binding energy per nucleon peaks at iron-56. Fusing iron costs energy. The star loses. The core collapses.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — stellar nucleosynthesis, proton-proton chain, CNO cycle, H-R diagram, gravity vs fusion pressure; "Relate Aboriginal Peoples' Knowledge of different star colours to the type of nucleosynthesis reactions occurring in main sequence stars"
**Engagement lever:** The personal stakes. The listener is literally made of this.
**Runtime target:** 30 min

### CASE-17 — Supernovae: Making Everything Heavier than Iron
**Hook:** *"Iron is the end of the road for nuclear fusion inside a star. Once the core is iron, the star is minutes from death. What follows is the most violent event in the universe — a stellar explosion so powerful it can outshine an entire galaxy. And in those frenzied seconds, every atom heavier than iron in the universe is forged: gold, silver, platinum, uranium. Every heavy element you've ever seen was born in the death of a star."*

**Narrative arc:**
- The iron core collapse: when the core exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 solar masses), electron degeneracy pressure fails. The core collapses at 70,000 km/s in less than a second.
- The bounce: when core density reaches nuclear density, the strong force halts collapse. A shock wave explodes outward.
- The r-process: the rapid neutron capture process. In the flood of neutrons during the explosion, nuclei capture neutrons faster than they can decay — building elements all the way up to uranium.
- Supernova 1987A: the last naked-eye supernova visible from Earth. Detected first by its neutrino burst (1987, 3 hours before visible light), confirming the collapse model.
- GW170817: two neutron stars merging, detected by LIGO via gravitational waves. Followed up by gamma-ray and optical telescopes. Confirmed as a kilonova — the dominant site of r-process nucleosynthesis.

**Syllabus coverage:** M8 — "Explain how elements up to, and including iron, are synthesised by stars, and how atoms larger than iron are synthesised"; binding energy; stellar evolution past the main sequence
**Engagement lever:** LIGO appears here as a payoff for everything the listener now knows about nuclear physics and stellar evolution.
**Runtime target:** 32 min

### CASE-18 — LEO and Geostationary Satellites
**Hook:** *"In 1945, a science fiction writer calculated the exact altitude at which a satellite would orbit the Earth at the same rate the Earth rotates — appearing to hang motionless in the sky. He published it in a letter to a magazine. Nobody built anything there for another 18 years. Today, every television broadcast, every weather map, every GPS navigation relies on orbits that were designed using nothing more than Newton's law of gravitation."*

**Narrative arc:**
- Arthur C. Clarke's 1945 letter: geostationary orbit at 35,786 km altitude.
- The physics: orbital velocity from F_gravity = F_centripetal. Why altitude determines orbital period.
- Geostationary vs low Earth orbit: GEO always above the same point (comms, weather); LEO fast-moving (ISS, GPS constellation, imaging).
- GPS: not one satellite, but 24–32 in MEO (~20,200 km). Triangulation requires knowing the satellite's exact position and the signal travel time.
- The relativity correction: GEO clocks tick faster due to weaker gravity (general relativity) but slower due to orbital velocity (special relativity). Net effect: +38 μs/day. Without correction, GPS drifts 10 km/day.
- The Hubble Space Telescope: LEO at 540 km. Serviced five times by shuttle missions. Demonstrates why LEO orbital mechanics matter for access.

**Syllabus coverage:** M5 — orbital velocity, law of periods, uses of LEO and geostationary satellites (secondary-source investigation); links forward to M7 special relativity (GPS time dilation)
**Engagement lever:** Clarke's letter 18 years before anyone built it. Theoretical physics as engineering prophecy.
**Runtime target:** 26 min

### CASE-19 — LIGO: Listening to the Fabric of Spacetime
**Hook:** *"On September 14, 2015, two detectors — one in Louisiana, one in Washington state — simultaneously registered a vibration smaller than one-thousandth the diameter of a proton. It lasted 0.2 seconds. In that fifth of a second, a billion light-years away, two black holes — each thirty times the mass of the Sun — had merged in a fraction of a second, converting three solar masses of matter directly into spacetime ripples. Einstein had predicted this in 1916. Nobody thought we'd ever be able to measure it."*

**Narrative arc:**
- What gravitational waves are: ripples in spacetime curvature, caused by accelerating masses. Predicted by Einstein; he himself doubted they could be detected.
- The detector: 4 km arms with laser light bouncing between mirrors. A gravitational wave passing through changes one arm's length relative to the other by 10⁻¹⁸ m. Isolating this from every other vibration on Earth is the engineering challenge.
- The signal: the chirp. Frequency and amplitude rise as the black holes spiral closer. The waveform is an exact match to the general relativistic prediction. 
- The announcement: February 11, 2016. "Ladies and gentlemen, we have detected gravitational waves."
- GW170817, 2017: neutron star merger. Detected in gravitational waves, then gamma rays, then visible light. Confirms that short gamma-ray bursts are neutron star mergers. Confirms kilonovae as the birthplace of heavy elements (connects back to CASE-17).

**Syllabus coverage:** Extends M5 (gravity), M7 (special relativity), M8 (nuclear energy, stellar evolution). Not a single dot-point — it's the capstone that makes all the prior episodes pay off.
**Engagement lever:** Save this for last. By this point the listener understands gravitational fields, nuclear physics, stellar nucleosynthesis, and time dilation. LIGO rewards all of it at once.
**Runtime target:** 35 min
